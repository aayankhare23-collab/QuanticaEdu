Prealgebra · Lesson 11.3

Angles in Polygons

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

You can measure a single angle now, and you know how angles line up when parallel lines are involved. Time to look inward. Every closed shape has angles tucked inside its corners, and those angles are not free to be anything they want. They have to add up. Start with the triangle, the simplest shape you can close with straight sides. Its three angles always land on the same total, no matter how you stretch or tilt it. Once you see why that happens, the same idea will carry you all the way up to a shape with any number of sides.

Key idea
The angles of a triangle. The three interior angles of any triangle add to \(180^\circ\). Here is why, straight from the last lesson. Draw a line through the top vertex parallel to the base, as in the figure above. The two base angles reappear up at that vertex as alternate interior angles, and there they sit alongside the top angle, the three of them resting along a straight line. A straight line is \(180^\circ\), so the three angles of the triangle are too.
In the diagram above, a triangle has two angles marked \(47^\circ\) and \(72^\circ\). Find the third angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The three interior angles of a triangle add to \(180^\circ\), so the third is what is left after removing the other two. $$180 - 47 - 72 = \boxed{61}$$ The third angle is \(61^\circ\).
In the diagram above, an isosceles triangle has its apex angle marked \(40^\circ\). The tick marks show the two sides meeting at the apex are equal, so the two base angles are equal. Find one base angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The three angles add to \(180^\circ\), so the two base angles together take up \(180-40 = 140\) degrees. The tick marks make those base angles equal, so each one is half of that. $$\frac{180^\circ - 40^\circ}{2} = \boxed{70^\circ}$$ One base angle is \(70\) degrees.
Key idea
The exterior angle of a triangle. Extend one side of a triangle past a corner and the angle that opens up just outside is an exterior angle. It equals the sum of the two remote interior angles, the two angles it does not touch. Here is why. The exterior angle sits next to the interior angle at that corner, and together they make a straight line, so they add to \(180^\circ\). The three interior angles also add to \(180^\circ\). Both totals hit \(180^\circ\), so once you take away that shared corner angle, the exterior angle has to equal the other two interior angles added together.
In the diagram above, one side of a triangle is extended, forming an exterior angle of \(110^\circ\). One of the two remote interior angles is \(65^\circ\). Find the other remote interior angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
By the exterior angle theorem, the exterior angle equals the sum of the two remote interior angles. So the unknown angle is $$110^\circ - 65^\circ = \boxed{45}^\circ.$$ The other remote interior angle is \(45^\circ\).
Key idea
Acute, right, and obtuse triangles. A triangle is acute when all three of its angles are below \(90^\circ\), right when one angle is exactly \(90^\circ\), and obtuse when one angle is above \(90^\circ\). Since the three angles share only \(180^\circ\) between them, a triangle can hold at most one right or obtuse angle. And in a right triangle, that leaves the two acute angles to add up to exactly \(90^\circ\).
In the diagram above, the three angles of a triangle are marked \(x^\circ\), \(2x^\circ\), and \(3x^\circ\). Find the largest angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The three angles of a triangle add to \(180^\circ\), so the marked pieces satisfy $$x + 2x + 3x = 180.$$ That gives \(6x = 180\), so \(x = 30\). The largest angle is the one marked \(3x^\circ\), which is \(3 \times 30 = \boxed{90}\).
Key idea
Polygons and their parts. A polygon is a closed figure made of straight sides. The corners where two sides meet are its vertices, and the angle sitting inside the figure at each vertex is an interior angle. A segment that joins two vertices which are not neighbors, cutting across the inside, is a diagonal. In the diagram above you can see a vertex, an interior angle, and a diagonal all labelled on one shape. We name polygons by how many sides they have: a triangle has 3, a quadrilateral has 4, a pentagon has 5, a hexagon has 6, a heptagon has 7, an octagon has 8, a nonagon has 9, a decagon has 10, and a dodecagon has 12. Learn these names now, because the rest of this lesson is about the angles hiding inside each one.
Key idea
The interior angles of any polygon. Pick one vertex and draw every diagonal from it, as the figure above shows. A polygon with \(n\) sides splits into \(n-2\) triangles, and each triangle holds \(180^\circ\), so the interior angles of an \(n\)-gon add to \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\). A quadrilateral gives \(2\times 180 = 360^\circ\), a pentagon \(3\times 180 = 540^\circ\), and it climbs by \(180^\circ\) for every side you add.
In the diagram above is a hexagon. Find the sum of its interior angles. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The interior angles of a polygon add to \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\). A hexagon has 6 sides, so \(n=6\). $$(6-2)\times 180^\circ = 4\times 180^\circ = \boxed{720^\circ}$$ The sum of the interior angles is 720 degrees.
In the diagram above, a pentagon has four interior angles marked \(100^\circ\), \(110^\circ\), \(120^\circ\), and \(130^\circ\). Find the fifth angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The interior angles of an \(n\)-gon add to \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\). For a pentagon that is $$(5-2)\times 180^\circ = 540^\circ.$$ Subtract the four marked angles from the total. $$540^\circ - 100^\circ - 110^\circ - 120^\circ - 130^\circ = \boxed{80}^\circ$$ So the fifth angle is \(80^\circ\).
The interior angles of a polygon add to \(1080^\circ\). How many sides does it have? Give the number of sides.
Show solution
The interior angles of an \(n\)-sided polygon add to \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\), so set that total equal to \(1080^\circ\). $$(n-2)\times 180 = 1080$$ Divide both sides by \(180\) to get \(n-2 = 6\), then add \(2\) to both sides. The polygon has \(\boxed{8}\) sides.
Key idea
Regular polygons. A regular polygon has all sides equal and all angles equal, so each interior angle is just the total split evenly, \(\frac{(n-2)\times 180^\circ}{n}\). A regular pentagon gives \(540/5 = 108^\circ\) and a regular hexagon \(720/6 = 120^\circ\).
The diagram above shows a regular octagon with one interior angle marked. Find that angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
A regular polygon has equal angles, so each interior angle is \(\frac{(n-2)\times 180^\circ}{n}\). With \(n=8\), the eight angles together add to \((8-2)\times 180^\circ = 1080^\circ\). Sharing that evenly among the \(8\) corners gives $$\frac{1080^\circ}{8} = \boxed{135}^\circ.$$
The diagram above shows a regular 20-sided polygon with one interior angle marked. Find that angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
For any regular polygon, every interior angle equals \(\frac{(n-2)\times 180^\circ}{n}\), since the whole interior sum \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\) is split evenly among the \(n\) equal corners. Here \(n=20\), so the twenty corners together hold \((20-2)\times 180^\circ = 18\times 180^\circ = 3240^\circ\). One corner is that shared out over all twenty. $$\frac{(20-2)\times 180^\circ}{20} = \frac{3240^\circ}{20} = \boxed{162}^\circ$$ Quick check, twenty angles of \(162^\circ\) rebuild the full \(3240^\circ\), so the marked angle is \(162\) degrees.
Key idea
The exterior angles close a full turn. Put one exterior angle at each vertex of a convex polygon, then walk all the way around the outside. As the figure above shows, each exterior angle is the turn you make at that corner, and by the time you come back to where you started you have turned through one complete loop. So the exterior angles always add to \(360^\circ\), no matter how many sides the polygon has. For a regular polygon all the corners are the same, so the turns are equal and each exterior angle is \(\frac{360^\circ}{n}\). Each interior angle is that exterior angle's partner along a straight line, so it is \(180^\circ\) minus the exterior angle.
In the diagram above, a regular pentagon has one exterior angle marked. Find that exterior angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The exterior angle sum is \(360^\circ\) for every polygon. A regular pentagon has 5 equal exterior angles, so divide the total by 5. $$\frac{360^\circ}{5} = 72^\circ$$ The marked exterior angle is \(\boxed{72}\) degrees.
In the diagram above, one corner of a regular polygon is shown, and each interior angle is \(150^\circ\). How many sides does the polygon have? Give the number of sides.
Show solution
The interior and exterior angle at a corner form a straight line, so the exterior angle is \(180^\circ - 150^\circ = 30^\circ\). The exterior angles of any polygon add to \(360^\circ\), and here they are all equal, so$$n = \frac{360^\circ}{30^\circ} = \boxed{12}.$$
Fun fact
Bees build their honeycombs out of regular hexagons, and there is a reason. Only three regular shapes tile a flat surface with no gaps, the triangle, the square, and the hexagon. Of those three, the hexagon walls off the most space for the least wall, so a honeycomb full of hexagons stores the most honey using the least wax. Bees found the best answer long before anyone could prove it. Mathematicians only finished proving the hexagon really is the best possible in 1999.
In the diagram above, a regular pentagon and a regular hexagon share a side and meet at a point, leaving a gap. Find the gap angle marked at that point. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The angles around the shared point make one full turn, so they **add to \(360^\circ\)**. The pentagon's corner is \(\frac{(5-2)\times 180^\circ}{5}=108^\circ\) and the hexagon's is \(\frac{(6-2)\times 180^\circ}{6}=120^\circ\). The gap is what's left. $$360^\circ - 108^\circ - 120^\circ = \boxed{132}^\circ$$
In the diagram above, one corner of a regular polygon is shown where each interior angle is \(4\) times its exterior angle. How many sides does the polygon have? Give the number of sides.
Show solution
The interior and exterior angle at a corner form a linear pair, so they add to \(180^\circ\). The interior angle is \(4\) times the exterior angle, so with exterior angle \(e\), $$4e + e = 180^\circ,\qquad 5e = 180^\circ,\qquad e = 36^\circ.$$ Every exterior angle of a regular polygon is \(\frac{360^\circ}{n}\), so \(\frac{360^\circ}{n} = 36^\circ\), which gives \(n = \boxed{10}\).
Key idea
The polygon-angle toolkit. Start with the triangle, since every rule here grows from it. Its three angles always add to \(180^\circ\), an exterior angle equals the two remote interior angles it does not touch, and because the three must share a single \(180^\circ\), a triangle can hold at most one right or obtuse angle. Reach past three sides by slicing. Any \(n\)-gon splits into \(n-2\) triangles from one corner, and each triangle brings \(180^\circ\), so the interior angles of an \(n\)-gon add to $$(n-2)\times 180^\circ.$$ If the polygon is regular, that total is shared equally, so every interior angle is \(\frac{(n-2)\times 180^\circ}{n}\). But the fastest handle is the outside. Walk once around any convex polygon and you turn through one full circle, so the exterior angles always add to \(360^\circ\), no matter how many sides there are. On a regular \(n\)-gon that splits evenly into an exterior angle of \(\frac{360^\circ}{n}\), and reading it backward, \(n=\frac{360^\circ}{\text{exterior angle}}\) is the quickest way to go from an angle straight back to the number of sides.

Review problems

Review 1.
In the diagram above, a triangle has angles \(55^\circ\) and \(80^\circ\). Find the third angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The angles in any triangle add to \(180^\circ\). Two of them are \(55^\circ\) and \(80^\circ\), so the third is what is left after taking those away from \(180^\circ\). $$180^\circ - 55^\circ - 80^\circ = \boxed{45}$$ The third angle is \(45^\circ\).
Review 2.
In the diagram above, an isosceles triangle has an apex angle of \(100^\circ\), and the tick marks show that the two base angles are equal. Find one base angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The angles in a triangle add to \(180^\circ\), so the two base angles together make \(180^\circ - 100^\circ = 80^\circ\). The tick marks tell us those base angles are equal, so each one is half of that. $$\frac{80^\circ}{2} = \boxed{40}$$
Review 3.
In the diagram above, one side of a triangle is extended to make an exterior angle of \(125^\circ\). One of the two remote interior angles is \(60^\circ\). Find the other remote interior angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
By the exterior angle theorem, an exterior angle equals the sum of the two remote interior angles. So the two remote angles add to \(125^\circ\). One of them is \(60^\circ\), so the other is $$125^\circ - 60^\circ = \boxed{65^\circ}.$$
Review 4.
In the diagram above, a triangle has angles \(2x^\circ\), \(3x^\circ\), and \(4x^\circ\). Find the largest angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The angles in a triangle sum to \(180^\circ\), so add the three expressions and set them equal to \(180\). $$2x + 3x + 4x = 180$$ That gives \(9x = 180\), so \(x = 20\). The largest angle is \(4x\), which is \(4\times 20 = \boxed{80}\).
Review 5.
The diagram above shows a heptagon (a polygon with 7 sides). Find the sum of its interior angles. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
By the interior angle sum rule, the angles of an \(n\)-sided polygon add to \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\). A heptagon has \(n=7\) sides, so $$(7-2)\times 180^\circ = 5\times 180^\circ = \boxed{900}^\circ.$$ The sum of the interior angles is \(900\) degrees.
Review 6.
In the diagram above, five of the six interior angles of a hexagon are \(110^\circ\), \(130^\circ\), \(115^\circ\), \(125^\circ\), and \(120^\circ\). Find the sixth angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
A hexagon has \(n=6\) sides, so its interior angles add to \((6-2)\times 180^\circ = 720^\circ\). The five marked angles add to $$110^\circ + 130^\circ + 115^\circ + 125^\circ + 120^\circ = 600^\circ.$$ The sixth angle is what is left over, so \(720^\circ - 600^\circ = \boxed{120}\) degrees.
Review 7. The interior angles of a polygon add to \(1440^\circ\). How many sides does it have? Give the number of sides.
Show solution
By the polygon angle sum, the interior angles of an \(n\)-sided polygon add to \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\). Set this equal to the total. $$(n-2)\times 180^\circ = 1440^\circ$$ Divide both sides by \(180^\circ\) to get \(n-2 = 8\), so \(n = \boxed{10}\).
Review 8.
The diagram above shows a regular dodecagon, a polygon with \(12\) equal sides and \(12\) equal angles. Find the measure of one interior angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
A regular polygon spreads its interior angle total evenly across all \(n\) corners, so each angle is \(\frac{(n-2)\times 180^\circ}{n}\). Here \(n = 12\). $$\frac{(12-2)\times 180^\circ}{12} = \frac{1800^\circ}{12} = \boxed{150^\circ}$$
Review 9.
In the diagram above, a regular polygon has 15 sides, with one interior angle marked. Find the size of that angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The interior angles of any polygon add to \((n-2)\times 180^\circ\), and a regular polygon splits that total evenly across all \(n\) angles. With \(n=15\), $$\frac{(15-2)\times 180^\circ}{15} = \frac{2340^\circ}{15} = \boxed{156^\circ}.$$
Review 10.
In the diagram above, a regular decagon has one exterior angle marked. Find that exterior angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
A decagon has \(n=10\) sides. In a regular polygon the exterior angles are all equal and together make one full turn, so each one is the exterior angle rule \(\frac{360^\circ}{n}\). $$\frac{360^\circ}{10} = \boxed{36}$$ Each exterior angle of the regular decagon is \(36^\circ\).
Review 11.
In the diagram above, one corner of a regular polygon has an exterior angle of \(45^\circ\), where a side is extended past the vertex. How many sides does the polygon have? Give the number of sides.
Show solution
Walk all the way around the polygon and you turn through one full circle, so the exterior angles add to \(360^\circ\). In a regular polygon they are all equal, which makes each one \(\frac{360^\circ}{n}\). The diagram marks that angle as \(45^\circ\), so $$\frac{360^\circ}{n}=45^\circ \;\Rightarrow\; n=\frac{360}{45}=\boxed{8}.$$ The polygon has \(8\) sides.
Review 12.
The diagram above shows one corner of a regular polygon, where each interior angle is \(140^\circ\). How many sides does the polygon have? Give the number of sides.
Show solution
Since the interior angle and the exterior angle sit on a straight line, the exterior angle is \(180^\circ - 140^\circ = 40^\circ\). For a regular polygon the exterior angle is \(\frac{360^\circ}{n}\), so $$n = \frac{360^\circ}{40^\circ} = \boxed{9}.$$ The polygon has 9 sides.
Review 13.
In the diagram above, one corner of a regular polygon is marked, and its interior angle is \(5\) times its exterior angle. How many sides does the polygon have? Give the number of sides.
Show solution
Interior and exterior angles at a corner form a linear pair, so they add to \(180^\circ\). The interior angle is \(5\) times the exterior angle, so with exterior angle \(e\), $$5e + e = 180^\circ,\quad 6e = 180^\circ,\quad e = 30^\circ.$$ For a regular polygon the exterior angle is \(\frac{360^\circ}{n}\), so $$\frac{360^\circ}{n} = 30^\circ \;\Rightarrow\; n = \frac{360^\circ}{30^\circ} = \boxed{12}.$$
