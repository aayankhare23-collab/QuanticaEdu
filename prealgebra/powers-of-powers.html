Prealgebra · Lesson 2.5

Powers of Powers

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Back in section 2.2 you found that raising a power to a power multiplies the two exponents. That one move is about to grow up. It still works when three or more exponents are stacked, when the inside is a fraction, when an exponent turns negative or zero, and even when a number rides out in front of the variable. This last section gathers every law in the chapter into one toolkit and turns it loose at full strength.

A glassblower mixes a colour by the ratio \(\dfrac{7}{2}\), and a deeper batch needs that ratio cubed, \(\left(\dfrac{7}{2}\right)^3\). Find it two ways. First multiply the fraction by itself three times. Then cube the top and the bottom on their own, \(\dfrac{7^3}{2^3}\). Give the value as a fraction.
Show solution
Multiplying the fraction by itself three times piles three 7s upstairs and three 2s downstairs. $$\left(\dfrac{7}{2}\right)^3 = \dfrac{7\times 7\times 7}{2\times 2\times 2} = \dfrac{7^3}{2^3} = \dfrac{343}{8}$$ Cubing the top and the bottom separately lands on the very same place. The answer is \(\boxed{\dfrac{343}{8}}\).
Test the same idea on a fourth power so it is not just a quirk of cubing. A kite design scales the ratio \(\dfrac{3}{5}\) to the fourth power. Send the exponent onto the top and the bottom separately, \(\dfrac{3^4}{5^4}\), and give the value as a fraction.
Show solution
Test the rule on a fourth power to confirm it holds beyond the cube. Send the exponent onto the top and the bottom separately. $$\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^4 = \frac{3^4}{5^4} = \frac{81}{625}$$ Four copies of \(\frac{3}{5}\) multiplied together gather four 3s on top and four 5s on the bottom, so both routes arrive at \(\boxed{\dfrac{81}{625}}\). The power of a quotient is not a quirk of cubing; it holds for every exponent.
Key idea
Power of a quotient. An outer exponent lands on the top and the bottom alike. $$\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^n = \dfrac{a^n}{b^n} \quad (b \neq 0)$$ It is the power of a product wearing a fraction. A quotient to the \(n\) means \(n\) copies of the whole fraction multiplied, so \(n\) tops gather over \(n\) bottoms.

Two stacked exponents multiply. So what should three do? Nothing new really, just the same rule fired twice. Peel the outermost exponent off first and watch the rest wait their turn.

A folding screen carries a motif repeated \(2^2\) times across one panel. A room divider holds \((2^2)^3\) such panels in a grid, and an exhibition lines up \(\big((2^2)^3\big)^2\) of those dividers. Simplify the whole tower \(\big((2^2)^3\big)^2\) to a single power of 2 and give the exponent.
Show solution
Work from the outside in, using the power rule each time. $$\big((2^2)^3\big)^2 = (2^2)^{3\times 2} = (2^2)^6 = 2^{2\times 6} = 2^{12}$$ Every exponent in the tower multiplied together, \(2\times 3\times 2 = 12\). The exponent is \(\boxed{12}\).
Key idea
Stacked exponents multiply. A tower of powers collapses by multiplying every exponent in it. $$\big((a^m)^n\big)^p = a^{m\times n\times p}$$ Each outer exponent is just the power rule firing again, so the multiplying never stops no matter how tall the tower grows.

Watch your step here, because this is the single most common slip in the whole chapter. Stacking exponents multiplies them, while multiplying powers of the same base adds them. They look alike on the page and do opposite things to the exponents.

A cargo line runs two belts. The upper belt batches packages as \((5^3)^2\), and the lower belt batches them as \(5^3 \times 5^2\). They are not the same count. Rewrite each as a single power of 5, then give the difference of the two exponents, the upper belt exponent minus the lower belt exponent.
Show solution
Each belt follows its own rule. The upper stacks a power on a power, so the exponents multiply, \((5^3)^2 = 5^{3\times 2} = 5^6\). The lower multiplies two powers, so the exponents add, \(5^3\times 5^2 = 5^{3+2} = 5^5\). The exponents are \(6\) and \(5\), and \(6-5 = \boxed{1}\). Stacking multiplied, multiplying added.

Nothing about the power rule ever asked the inner exponent to be positive. So point it downward and see whether multiplying the exponents still behaves when one of them is negative.

A sound desk stores the faint gain \(2^{-3}\), then a sharpening step squares whatever is stored, computing \((2^{-3})^2\). Multiply the exponents just as always and write the result as a single power of 2 with one exponent. Give that exponent.
Show solution
Multiply the exponents, sign and all. $$(2^{-3})^2 = 2^{(-3)\times 2} = 2^{-6}$$ The exponent is \(\boxed{-6}\). Written without a negative that is \(\dfrac{1}{2^6} = \dfrac{1}{64}\), a very small number, which fits a power that points down twice.
An elevator panel shows the reading \((9^4)^{0}\). Without a calculator, give its exact value.
Show solution
Two routes, one answer. Multiplying the exponents gives \((9^4)^0 = 9^{4\times 0} = 9^0 = 1\). Or recall \(9^0 = 1\) first. Either way the panel reads \(\boxed{1}\).
Key idea
The power rule ignores signs. Raising a power to a power multiplies the exponents whether they are positive, negative, or zero. $$(a^m)^n = a^{m\times n}$$ A negative inner exponent stays negative under a positive outer one, and a zero anywhere in the product collapses the whole thing to \(a^0 = 1\).

Now let a number ride in front of the variable. When an outer exponent meets something like two times a power, it is tempting to leave the number out front alone. It does not get to stay alone. The exponent is a power of a product, and the coefficient is one of the factors inside.

A bakery stamps trays of cookies, each tray holding \(2x^3\) rows where \(x\) is the dough setting. A holiday order raises that whole tray to the fourth power, \((2x^3)^4\). One apprentice writes \(2x^{12}\) and another writes \(16x^{12}\). Exactly one is right. Give the correct simplified form. Type it in the form such as 16x^12.
Show solution
The outer exponent lands on every factor inside, the coefficient included. $$(2x^3)^4 = 2^4 \times (x^3)^4 = 16 \times x^{12} = 16x^{12}$$ The apprentice who wrote \(2x^{12}\) forgot to raise the \(2\). The correct form is \(\boxed{16x^{12}}\).
Key idea
Power of a product with a coefficient. An outer exponent lands on the number out front just as surely as on the variable. $$(c\,x^{m})^{n} = c^{n}\,x^{m \times n}$$ The coefficient is just another factor inside the parentheses, so the exponent grips it the same way it grips \(x\). Forgetting it is the classic capstone trap.

One last twist on the outer exponent ties the negative powers of the previous section to the quotient rule of this one. Point the outer exponent downward while it sits on a fraction, and watch what the fraction does.

A recipe scales to \(\dfrac{2}{3}\) strength, but a reset raises that whole setting to the power \(-3\). Evaluate \(\left(\dfrac{2}{3}\right)^{-3}\). Treat the negative exponent as a reciprocal first, flipping \(\dfrac{2}{3}\) over, then cube. Give the value as a fraction.
Show solution
The negative exponent flips the fraction upright and turns positive. $$\left(\dfrac{2}{3}\right)^{-3} = \left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^{3} = \dfrac{3^3}{2^3} = \dfrac{27}{8}$$ Flip first, then send the exponent onto the top and the bottom. The answer is \(\boxed{\dfrac{27}{8}}\).
Key idea
Negative outer exponent flips a fraction. A fraction to a negative power turns over, then the exponent acts as usual. $$\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^{-n} = \left(\dfrac{b}{a}\right)^{n} = \dfrac{b^{n}}{a^{n}} \quad (a \neq 0,\ b \neq 0)$$ The negative sign is just a reciprocal asking the fraction to flip, after which the exponent lands on the new top and bottom.
Fun fact
Stacked exponents grow at a pace the mind cannot picture. The tidy looking tower \(\big((10^3)^3\big)^3\) multiplies out to \(10^{27}\), a number with twenty seven zeros, already more than the estimated count of every grain of sand on every beach on Earth. And that is the gentle kind of tower. A true tower where each exponent is itself a full power, like \(2^{2^{2^{2}}}\), reaches 65536 in just four 2s, and one floor higher the number is too long to write on this page. Mathematicians invented a whole separate notation, called up-arrows, just to tame them.

Time to let the laws meet. The power of a quotient, a raised coefficient, and the quotient rule can all live in one expression, kept in line by order of operations. Take it one grouped piece at a time.

A lighting rig simplifies \(\dfrac{(3x^4)^2}{x^5}\) to a single coefficient and power of \(x\). Find it. Type it in the form such as 16x^12.
Show solution
Open the numerator, letting the outer exponent hit both factors. $$(3x^4)^2 = 3^2 \times x^{4\times 2} = 9x^8$$ Now divide by \(x^5\). The coefficient is untouched by the division, and the quotient rule subtracts the variable exponents. $$\dfrac{9x^8}{x^5} = 9x^{8-5} = 9x^3$$ The answer is \(\boxed{9x^3}\).
A power grid balances two terms. Simplify \(\left(\dfrac{x^3}{2}\right)^{2} \times \left(\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right)^{-3}\) to a single number with no variable left. Find that number.
Show solution
Handle each term by its own rule. The first sends the exponent onto top and bottom. $$\left(\dfrac{x^3}{2}\right)^{2} = \dfrac{(x^3)^2}{2^2} = \dfrac{x^6}{4}$$ The second has a negative outer exponent, so flip the fraction first, then raise, coefficient and all. $$\left(\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right)^{-3} = \left(\dfrac{2}{x^2}\right)^{3} = \dfrac{2^3}{(x^2)^3} = \dfrac{8}{x^6}$$ Multiply the two results. $$\dfrac{x^6}{4} \times \dfrac{8}{x^6} = \dfrac{8x^6}{4x^6} = 2x^{6-6} = 2$$ The variable cancelled completely. The answer is \(\boxed{2}\).

Here is the capstone. One line, every law in the chapter. It hides a coefficient inside a flip, a quotient under an exponent, a tower of three powers, and the quotient rule, all refereed by order of operations. Take it one grouped piece at a time and watch the whole chapter cooperate.

Evaluate $$\left(\dfrac{(2x^3)^2}{4x^4}\right)^{3} \times \left(\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right)^{-3}.$$ It is built so the variable cancels completely and a single number remains. Find that number.
Show solution
Work the first factor from the inside out. Raise the inner top, coefficient included, \((2x^3)^2 = 2^2 x^6 = 4x^6\). Divide by \(4x^4\) with the quotient rule. $$\dfrac{4x^6}{4x^4} = \dfrac{4}{4}\,x^{6-4} = x^2$$ Now apply the outer exponent, a tower that multiplies. $$\left(x^2\right)^{3} = x^{6}$$ Turn to the second factor. A negative outer exponent flips the fraction upright, then lands on top and bottom. $$\left(\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right)^{-3} = \left(\dfrac{2}{x^2}\right)^{3} = \dfrac{2^3}{(x^2)^3} = \dfrac{8}{x^6}$$ Finally multiply the two simplified pieces. $$x^6 \times \dfrac{8}{x^6} = 8\,x^{6-6} = 8x^0 = 8$$ Every law in the chapter took a turn and the variable cancelled to nothing. The number is \(\boxed{8}\).

Review problems

Review 1. You already know that stacking a power on a power multiplies the two exponents. This section pushes that idea everywhere it can go, onto fractions, onto coefficients, up nested towers, and across negative exponents, until every law in the chapter can show up in one expression at once.
Review 2. A canyon guide tests an echo by stacking it on itself. One shout returns as \(\left(4^2\right)^3\) faint repeats. Rewrite that as a single power of \(4\) and give the exponent.
Show solution
Stacking a power on a power multiplies the two exponents rather than adding them. $$\left(4^2\right)^3 = 4^{2\times 3} = 4^6$$ The outer \(3\) asks for three copies of \(4^2\), and each copy carries two factors of \(4\), so there are \(2\times 3=6\) factors in all. The exponent is \(\boxed{6}\).
Review 3. A quilter sews a block by raising a small square to a power. The block holds \(\left(\dfrac{2}{3}\right)^4\) of one unit of fabric. Write that as a single fraction.
Show solution
An outer exponent on a quotient lands on both the top and the bottom. $$\left(\dfrac{2}{3}\right)^4 = \dfrac{2^4}{3^4} = \dfrac{16}{81}$$ Four copies of \(\dfrac{2}{3}\) multiplied together gather four \(2\)s on top and four \(3\)s on the bottom. The block is \(\boxed{\dfrac{16}{81}}\) of a unit.
Review 4. An outer exponent on a fraction lands on the top and the bottom alike, so \(\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^n=\dfrac{a^n}{b^n}\) for any nonzero \(b\). It works because multiplying \(n\) copies of \(\dfrac{a}{b}\) gathers \(n\) factors of \(a\) on top and \(n\) factors of \(b\) on the bottom.
Review 5. On a beadwork loom a single tile pattern is enlarged by raising it to the fourth power. The tile is described by \(2b^3\), so the enlarged pattern is \(\left(2b^3\right)^4\). Simplify it to a coefficient times a single power of \(b\).
Show solution
The outer exponent reaches every factor inside, including the coefficient. The \(2\) gets raised too, which is the trap. $$\left(2b^3\right)^4 = 2^4 \times \left(b^3\right)^4 = 16 \times b^{3\times 4} = 16b^{12}$$ It is not \(2b^{12}\). Forgetting to raise the \(2\) is the most common slip. The pattern simplifies to \(\boxed{16b^{12}}\).
Review 6. When a product inside parentheses carries a number out front, the outer exponent lands on that number too, so \(\left(k\,x^m\right)^n=k^n\,x^{mn}\). For example \(\left(2x^3\right)^4=16x^{12}\), never \(2x^{12}\). The coefficient is a factor like any other, so it cannot be left behind.
Review 7. Two coral reef readings look alike but mean different things. The first colony grows as \(\left(5^2\right)^3\) polyps, a power stacked on a power. The second grows as \(5^2 \times 5^3\) polyps, two powers multiplied together. Both fold to a single power of \(5\). By how much does the first reading's exponent beat the second reading's exponent?
Show solution
These are the two ideas people mix up. Stacking a power on a power multiplies the exponents, while multiplying two powers of the same base adds them. $$\left(5^2\right)^3 = 5^{2\times 3} = 5^6, \qquad 5^2 \times 5^3 = 5^{2+3} = 5^5$$ The first reading reaches exponent \(6\), the second reaches exponent \(5\). Stacking pulls ahead by \(6-5=\boxed{1}\).
Review 8. A lantern festival dims a light by raising a faint setting to a power. The setting is \(2^{-3}\) of full brightness, and squaring it gives \(\left(2^{-3}\right)^2\). Write the result as a single power of \(2\) and give the exponent.
Show solution
The power of a power rule does not care about the sign of the exponent. It just multiplies the two. $$\left(2^{-3}\right)^2 = 2^{(-3)\times 2} = 2^{-6}$$ Two copies of \(2^{-3}\) give a total of six factors of \(2\) in the denominator. The exponent is \(\boxed{-6}\), which is \(\dfrac{1}{64}\).
Review 9. Stacking really does outrun adding. Starting from \(10^4\), multiplying by another \(10^3\) only reaches \(10^7\), but raising it to the \(3\)rd power leaps to \(10^{12}\). That gap of five zeros is the difference between joining copies and making copies of copies.
Review 10. A model railway sets its scale as a fraction, then a reversing switch raises that scale to the power \(-2\). The scale is \(\dfrac{3}{5}\), so the switch produces \(\left(\dfrac{3}{5}\right)^{-2}\). Write the result as a single fraction.
Show solution
A negative outer exponent flips the fraction, then the exponent goes positive. $$\left(\dfrac{3}{5}\right)^{-2} = \left(\dfrac{5}{3}\right)^{2} = \dfrac{5^2}{3^2} = \dfrac{25}{9}$$ The flip handles the minus sign, and the squaring lands on both the new top and the new bottom. The switch produces \(\boxed{\dfrac{25}{9}}\).
Review 11. A negative outer exponent on a fraction turns it upside down and makes the exponent positive, so \(\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^{-n}=\left(\dfrac{b}{a}\right)^{n}\) for nonzero \(a\) and \(b\). The minus sign is really just the reciprocal rule wearing fraction clothes.
Review 12. A fractal antenna is etched in stages. Each stage splits every arm into \(2^2\) smaller arms, and the design runs through three stages, so the tip count builds up as the nested tower \(\left(\left(2^2\right)^2\right)^2\). When three exponents are stacked, all of them multiply together. Rewrite the tower as a single power of \(2\) and give its exponent.
Show solution
Every stacked exponent multiplies into the next. $$\left(\left(2^2\right)^2\right)^2=2^{\,2\times2\times2}=2^{8}$$ The exponent is \(\boxed{8}\), so the tip count is \(256\).
Review 13. A salt cavern records its volume as \(\left(2c^2\right)^3\) and the brine it holds as \(4c^5\). The packing density is the volume divided by the brine, \(\dfrac{\left(2c^2\right)^3}{4c^5}\). Simplify it to a coefficient times a single power of \(c\).
Show solution
Open the top with the power of a product rule, raising the coefficient too. $$\left(2c^2\right)^3 = 2^3 \times c^{2\times 3} = 8c^6$$ Now divide, splitting the coefficients from the powers of \(c\). $$\dfrac{8c^6}{4c^5} = \dfrac{8}{4} \times c^{6-5} = 2c^1 = 2c$$ The packing density simplifies to \(\boxed{2c}\).
Review 14. A tuning fork's overtone strength is modeled by \(\left(a^2\right)^3\times\left(a^{-3}\right)^2\) for a nonzero \(a\). Simplify each tower first, then combine. The messy expression collapses to something tiny. What is its value?
Show solution
Multiply within each tower, then add when the powers meet. $$\left(a^2\right)^3=a^{6},\qquad \left(a^{-3}\right)^2=a^{-6}$$ $$a^{6}\times a^{-6}=a^{6+(-6)}=a^{0}=1$$ Any nonzero base to the zero power is \(1\), so the whole thing folds to \(\boxed{1}\).
Review 15. A tower of exponents grows almost too fast to picture. Stacking just \(\left(\left(\left(2^2\right)^2\right)^2\right)^2\) multiplies four twos to \(2^{16}\), already past sixty thousand. A few more floors and the number would dwarf the count of grains of sand on Earth.
Review 16. A wind chime nests three tunings. The deepest tube rings as \(\left(\left(2^2\right)^3\right)^2\) and a damper divides that ring by \(\left(2^4\right)^2\). The audible tone is \(\dfrac{\left(\left(2^2\right)^3\right)^2}{\left(2^4\right)^2}\). Write it as a single power of \(2\) and give its value.
Show solution
Collapse each tower by multiplying all of its stacked exponents. On top, three exponents multiply. $$\left(\left(2^2\right)^3\right)^2 = 2^{2\times 3\times 2} = 2^{12}$$ On the bottom, two multiply. $$\left(2^4\right)^2 = 2^{4\times 2} = 2^{8}$$ Now divide a common base by subtracting. $$\dfrac{2^{12}}{2^{8}} = 2^{12-8} = 2^{4} = 16$$ The audible tone is \(\boxed{16}\).
Review 17. A foundry mixes powers of \(2\) and \(5\) into one casting reading, $$\left(\dfrac{2^3 \times 5}{10^2}\right)^{-2}.$$ Simplify the whole thing to a single fraction.
Show solution
Settle the inside first. The top is \(2^3 \times 5 = 8\times 5 = 40\) and the bottom is \(10^2 = 100\). $$\dfrac{2^3 \times 5}{10^2} = \dfrac{40}{100} = \dfrac{2}{5}$$ Now the negative outer exponent flips the fraction, then squares it on both top and bottom. $$\left(\dfrac{2}{5}\right)^{-2} = \left(\dfrac{5}{2}\right)^{2} = \dfrac{25}{4}$$ The casting reading is \(\boxed{\dfrac{25}{4}}\).
Review 18. A beehive arranges its active brood cells by a single formula, $$\dfrac{\left(2^3\right)^4\times\left(2^{-2}\right)^3}{\left(2^4\right)^2}.$$ Every law in this chapter shows up once. Collapse it all into a single power of \(2\) and give that power's exponent.
Show solution
Flatten every stacked power first, multiplying the exponents and carrying the signs. $$\left(2^3\right)^4=2^{12},\qquad \left(2^{-2}\right)^3=2^{-6},\qquad \left(2^4\right)^2=2^{8}$$ Multiply the two powers on top by adding their exponents. $$2^{12}\times2^{-6}=2^{12+(-6)}=2^{6}$$ Now divide by the bottom power by subtracting its exponent. $$\dfrac{2^{6}}{2^{8}}=2^{6-8}=2^{-2}=\boxed{2^{-2}}$$ The exponent is \(-2\), and the whole formula is just \(\dfrac{1}{4}\). The power of a power, the product rule, the quotient rule, and a negative exponent each took one turn, and the tower folded to almost nothing.
