Prealgebra · Lesson 10.5

An Introduction to Probability

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Counting tells you not just how many outcomes exist, but how likely each one is. When all outcomes are equally likely, flip a coin a million times and heads shows up about half the time, and that steady fraction is probability.

Key idea
Probability formula. When every outcome of an experiment is equally likely, the probability of an event is the number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of outcomes, $$P = \frac{\text{favorable}}{\text{total}}.$$ So probability is really counting wearing a new coat. You count the outcomes you want on top, you count all the outcomes on the bottom, and both of those counts come straight from the tools you built this chapter.
A fair 6-sided die is rolled once. What is the probability of rolling a \(3\)? Give your answer as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by listing what can happen on one roll. The die can land on \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\), and because it is fair, every one of those is equally likely. So the total count is \(6\). Next count the rolls that win. You are hoping for a \(3\), and exactly one face shows a \(3\), so the favorable count is \(1\). When every outcome is equally likely the probability is the favorable count over the total count, which gives $$\frac{1}{6}.$$ That fraction is already in lowest terms, so the probability of rolling a \(3\) is \(\frac{1}{6}\).
A fair 6-sided die is rolled once. What is the probability of rolling an odd number? Give your answer as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
A fair die has \(6\) equally likely faces, so the total count is \(6\). The odd faces are \(1, 3, 5\), so \(3\) of the \(6\) equally likely faces are favorable. Putting the favorable count over the total count and you get $$\frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}.$$ So the probability of rolling an odd number is \(\frac{1}{2}\).
A fair 6-sided die is rolled once. What is the probability of rolling a \(7\)? Answer with \(0\) or \(1\) if certain or impossible.
Show solution
Probability is just careful counting wearing a new coat, so let us start by counting what is equally likely. A fair die has faces numbered \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\), and each face is equally likely to land up, which gives a total count of \(6\) equally likely outcomes. \nNow count the favorable outcomes, the faces that show a \(7\). Looking through \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\), none of the \(6\) faces is a \(7\), so the favorable count is \(0\). \nPut the favorable count over the total count and you get $$\frac{0}{6} = 0.$$ \nThere is simply no way for a die numbered \(1\) through \(6\) to show a \(7\), so this is an impossible event, and an impossible event sits at the very start of the number line with probability \(\boxed{0}\).
Key idea
Probability scale. A probability is a part of the whole, so it never dips below \(0\) or climbs above \(1\), and always lands somewhere from \(0\) to \(1\). Something impossible sits right at \(0\), something certain sits right at \(1\), and a probability of \(\frac{1}{2}\) means an even chance. The closer a probability sits to \(1\), the more likely the event, and the closer to \(0\), the less likely.
Key idea
Equally-likely warning. Favorable over total only tells the truth when the outcomes you count are all equally likely, so before you count anything, ask what the truly equal outcomes are. On a die with colored sides the equal outcomes are the \(6\) sides, not the colors, because the colors might be split unevenly. So count the sides: if \(2\) of the \(6\) sides are red, the chance of red is \(\frac{2}{6}\). Whenever something feels uneven, dig down to the layer where every outcome really has the same chance, and count there.
An 8-sided die has \(2\) red, \(5\) yellow, and \(1\) blue side, all equally likely. What is the probability of rolling yellow?
Show solution
Count the sides, not the colors. The die has \(8\) sides, and since every side is equally likely to land up, those \(8\) sides are the equally likely outcomes, so the total count is \(8\). Now count the favorable outcomes, the ones where a yellow side comes up. There are \(5\) yellow sides, so the favorable count is \(5\). The probability is the favorable count over the total count, which gives $$\frac{5}{8}.$$ This fraction is already in lowest terms, since \(5\) and \(8\) share no common factor bigger than \(1\). So the probability of rolling yellow is \(\frac{5}{8}\).
A shuffled 52-card deck is drawn from the top. What is the probability the top card is a spade? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by deciding what is equally likely. The deck was shuffled well, so the top card has the same chance of being any one of the \(52\) cards, and that gives you \(52\) equally likely outcomes for the total. Next count the favorable outcomes, the ones that make you happy. You want a spade, and a full suit has \(13\) cards, so there are \(13\) spades. The probability is the favorable count over the total count, which gives \(\frac{13}{52}\). Now simplify, and since \(13\) divides into \(52\) exactly four times you get $$\frac{13}{52} = \frac{1}{4}.$$ That matches what you would expect, because the spades are one of the four equal suits, so they take up exactly one quarter of the deck. $$\boxed{\dfrac{1}{4}}$$
A shuffled 52-card deck is drawn from the top. What is the probability the top card is a face card (Jack, Queen, or King)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Since the deck is shuffled, every one of the \(52\) cards is equally likely to end up on top, so the total count is \(52\). \nNow we count the favorable cards. A face card is a Jack, a Queen, or a King, which is \(3\) face ranks, and each of those ranks appears once in every suit. There are \(4\) suits, so the number of face cards is $$3 \times 4 = 12.$$ \nThat gives \(12\) favorable cards out of \(52\) total, so the probability is \(\frac{12}{52}\). Both numbers share a factor of \(4\), and dividing top and bottom by \(4\) gives $$\frac{12}{52} = \frac{3}{13}.$$ \nSo the probability that the top card is a face card is \(\boxed{\frac{3}{13}}\).
Key idea
Two stages multiply. When an experiment happens in stages, the total number of equally likely outcomes does not add, it multiplies, which is just the Multiplication Principle from 10.2 again. Two dice are not \(12\) outcomes, they are \(6 \times 6 = 36\), one for every cell of a \(6\) by \(6\) grid. So lay out the grid, count the favorable cells, and put that over \(36\). Multiply to find the total, then count what you want.
Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability the sum is \(6\)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
First count the total number of outcomes. Each die has \(6\) equally likely faces, and the dice are separate, so the multiplication principle gives \(6 \times 6 = 36\) equally likely pairs in all. Now count the favorable ones, the pairs that add up to \(6\). Walk through the first die in order and find its partner each time, which gives \((1,5), (2,4), (3,3), (4,2), (5,1)\). That is \(5\) favorable outcomes. So the probability is the favorable count over the total count, and you get $$\frac{5}{36}.$$ This fraction is already in lowest terms.
Key idea
Complement rule. Sometimes the favorable cases are a tangled mess but the one opposite case is simple and quick to count. When that happens, count the chance of the thing you do not want and subtract from \(1\), giving \(P(\text{not }A) = 1 - P(A)\). This is the clean way to handle any at-least-one question, since instead of chasing every winning case you find the single way it all fails and take it away from the whole. It is the same subtracting-cases move from 10.1, now measuring how likely instead of how many.
Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled and their values multiplied. What is the probability the product is a multiple of \(5\)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
First count the total. The red die has \(6\) equally likely faces and so does the blue die, so the multiplication principle gives \(6 \times 6 = 36\) equally likely pairs, and \(36\) is our total count. Now think about when the product is a multiple of \(5\). The only multiple of \(5\) sitting on a single die is the number \(5\) itself, so the product is a multiple of \(5\) exactly when at least one die shows a \(5\). Whenever you see at least one, the kind move is to count the opposite case and subtract. The opposite is no five at all. No five on one die has probability \(\frac{5}{6}\), so no five on either die is $$\frac{5}{6} \times \frac{5}{6} = \frac{25}{36}.$$ That is the unfavorable count of \(25\) over \(36\). Subtracting from \(1\) gives the at least one five chance, which is $$1 - \frac{25}{36} = \frac{11}{36}.$$ So the favorable count is \(11\) and the total count is \(36\), and the probability that the product is a multiple of \(5\) is \(\boxed{\frac{11}{36}}\).
Four fair coins are flipped. What is the probability all four come up heads? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by counting all the equally likely outcomes. Each coin can land heads or tails, so it has \(2\) results, and the four flips do not affect each other. The total number of equally likely outcomes is \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 2^4 = 16\). Now count the favorable ones. Only \(1\) of these has every coin showing heads, the outcome where the penny, nickel, dime, and quarter are all heads. So with \(1\) favorable outcome over \(16\) total, the probability is \(\frac{1}{16}\). You can also get this by multiplying the chances, since each coin shows heads with probability \(\frac{1}{2}\), which gives $$\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{16}.$$ Either way the probability that all four come up heads is \(\frac{1}{16}\).
Key idea
Drawing without replacement. When you draw cards right off the top, the deck does not reset between picks, so the first stage has \(52\) cards but the second has only \(51\) left. To find the chance of both happening, walk the stages in order and multiply, the probability of the first event times the chance of the second given the first already happened. So handle one pick, then the smaller deck, then multiply across, the same sequence thinking you used to count pairs in 10.4, now measuring how likely instead of how many.
A shuffled 52-card deck has 26 red cards. Two cards are drawn without replacement. What is the probability both are red? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
This is a two-stage draw, the first card and then the second, so we walk the stages in order and multiply their chances. \nStart with the first card. Every one of the \(52\) cards is equally likely to land on top, and \(26\) of them are red. The favorable count is \(26\) and the total count is \(52\), so the first card is red with probability $$\frac{26}{52} = \frac{1}{2}.$$ \nNow the second card, and here is the key. We did not put the first card back, so once a red card is already on top there are only \(51\) cards left and only \(25\) of them are still red. The favorable count is \(25\) and the total count is \(51\), so the second card is red with probability \(\frac{25}{51}\). \nTo make both stages happen we multiply the two chances, which gives $$\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{25}{51} = \frac{25}{102}.$$ \nThat fraction is already in lowest terms, so the probability that the top two cards are both red is \(\boxed{\frac{25}{102}}\).
A shuffled 52-card deck has 26 black and 26 red cards. Two cards are drawn without replacement. What is the probability the first is black and the second is red? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
We are drawing without putting cards back, so we walk through the deck one card at a time and multiply the chance at each stage, just like the card problems from Lesson 10.4 but now measuring how likely instead of how many. \nStart with the top card. Every one of the \(52\) cards is equally likely to land on top, and \(26\) of them are black, so the favorable count is \(26\) out of a total of \(52\). That gives a first-stage probability of \(\frac{26}{52} = \frac{1}{2}\). \nNow the second card. One card is already gone, so only \(51\) cards remain. All \(26\) red cards are still sitting in the deck, so the favorable count is \(26\) out of a total of \(51\), which gives \(\frac{26}{51}\). \nTo find the chance that both things happen we multiply the two stages, and you get $$\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{26}{51} = \frac{26}{102} = \frac{13}{51}.$$ \nSo the probability that the top card is black and the second card is red is \(\boxed{\frac{13}{51}}\).
Key idea
Probability is counting. When every outcome is equally likely, the probability of an event is the favorable count over the total count, \(\frac{\text{favorable}}{\text{total}}\), so every tool you built this chapter feeds straight into it. Adding and subtracting cases from 10.1 keeps your counts honest, the Multiplication Principle from 10.2 hands you the total number of outcomes, casework from 10.3 gathers up the favorable ones, and counting pairs from 10.4 handles the times order does not matter. Count the favorable, count the total, and the probability falls right out.
Two fair 8-sided dice are rolled and multiplied. What is the probability the product is greater than \(36\)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
First let us figure out how many equally likely outcomes there are in all. The first die can land on any of its \(8\) faces, and the second die can land on any of its \(8\) faces, and the multiplication principle ties them together, so the total number of equally likely outcomes is $$8 \times 8 = 64.$$\nThink of these as the cells of a \(8\) by \(8\) grid, one cell for each pair of rolls. \nNow we count the favorable cells, the ones where the product is greater than \(36\). Only the larger numbers can climb that high, so working through the possibilities gives the ordered pairs \((5,8),(6,7),(6,8),(7,6),(7,7),(7,8),(8,5),(8,6),(8,7),(8,8)\). Counting them up, that is \(10\) favorable outcomes. \nSo we have a favorable count of \(10\) over a total count of \(64\), and reducing that fraction by dividing top and bottom by \(2\) gives $$\frac{10}{64} = \frac{5}{32}.$$\nThe probability that the product is greater than \(36\) is \(\boxed{\frac{5}{32}}\).
Fun fact
In 1654, Pascal and Fermat exchanged letters about splitting a prize in an unfinished dice game. Those letters became the first real foundation of probability theory.
Key idea
Full summary. When every outcome is equally likely, the probability of an event is the favorable count over the total count, and that answer always lands from \(0\) for impossible to \(1\) for certain, with closer to \(1\) meaning more likely. Two dice are \(6 \times 6 = 36\) equally likely cells, not \(12\). For an at-least-one question, flip to the complement and use \(P(\text{not }A) = 1 - P(A)\), since counting the single opposite case is far kinder. For picks made in stages, like drawing cards without putting them back, walk the stages and multiply their chances, \(52\) then \(51\) and so on. It is all the counting from this chapter wearing a new coat.

Review problems

Review 1. An 8-sided die has \(2\) red, \(5\) yellow, and \(1\) blue side, all equally likely. What is the probability of rolling blue?
Show solution
The equally likely outcomes are the \(8\) sides of the die, so the total count is \(8\). A roll counts as a win only when blue lands on top, and there is \(1\) blue side, so the favorable count is \(1\). Putting the favorable count over the total count gives $$\frac{1}{8}.$$ That fraction is already in lowest terms, so the probability of rolling blue is \(\frac{1}{8}\).
Review 2. A fair 6-sided die is rolled. What is the probability of rolling \(4\) or less? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
A fair die has \(6\) equally likely faces, \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\), so the total count is \(6\). The faces that are \(4\) or less are \(1, 2, 3, 4\), which is a favorable count of \(4\). Probability is the favorable count over the total count, and the faces \(1,2,3,4\) are \(4\) of \(6\), so $$\frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3}.$$ The probability is \(\frac{2}{3}\).
Review 3. A fair 6-sided die is rolled. What is the probability of NOT rolling a \(1\)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by counting what is equally likely. A fair die has \(6\) faces, the numbers \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\), and each is just as likely as any other, so the total count is \(6\). Now count the favorable faces, the ones that are not a \(1\). Those are \(2, 3, 4, 5, 6\), and that is \(5\) faces. The favorable count is \(5\) and the total count is \(6\), so the probability is the favorable over the total, which gives \(\frac{5}{6}\). You can also see it as the complement, since only one face is a \(1\), so the chance of not rolling a \(1\) is \(1 - \frac{1}{6} = \frac{5}{6}\), the same answer. $$\boxed{\frac{5}{6}}$$
Review 4. A shuffled 52-card deck is drawn from the top. What is the probability the top card is a \(10\) or a Jack? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by asking what is equally likely. The deck is shuffled, so any of the \(52\) cards is just as likely as any other to be on top, which means the total count is \(52\). Next count the favorable cards, the ones you are hoping for. There are \(4\) tens and \(4\) jacks, and a card cannot be both at once, so the favorable count is \(4 + 4 = 8\). Now put the favorable count over the total count and you get \(\frac{8}{52}\). The last step is to write it in lowest terms, and since \(8\) and \(52\) share a factor of \(4\), dividing both by \(4\) gives \(4\) tens and \(4\) jacks are \(8\) cards of \(52\), \(\frac{8}{52} = \frac{2}{13}\). So the probability is $$\frac{8}{52} = \frac{2}{13}.$$
Review 5. Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability the sum is \(4\)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Rolling two dice is just the counting from this chapter wearing a new coat, so we count the favorable outcomes and divide by the total. The equally likely outcomes are the ordered pairs, the first roll together with the second roll. The first die has \(6\) sides and the second die has \(6\) sides, so the multiplication principle gives \(6 \times 6 = 36\) equally likely outcomes, and that is our total count. Now we hunt for the outcomes where the sum is \(4\). Going through them in order, the pairs that work are \((1,3),(2,2),(3,1)\), which is \(3\) of the \(36\). That gives a favorable count of \(3\). So the probability is the favorable count over the total count, which is \(\frac{3}{36}\). Reducing to lowest terms, $$\frac{3}{36} = \frac{1}{12}.$$ So the probability that the sum is \(4\) is \(\boxed{\frac{1}{12}}\).
Review 6. Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability the sum is \(11\)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by counting every equally likely outcome. The first die has \(6\) faces and the second die has \(6\) faces, so the multiplication principle gives \(6 \times 6 = 36\) equally likely pairs, and that is our total count. Now we count the favorable pairs, the ones whose two faces add up to \(11\). The only ways to reach \(11\) are \((5,6)\) and \((6,5)\), so that is a favorable count of \(2\). Putting the favorable count over the total count, \((5,6),(6,5)\) is \(2\) of \(36\), which gives \(\frac{2}{36}\), and reducing gives \(\frac{2}{36} = \frac{1}{18}\). So the probability is \(\boxed{\frac{1}{18}}.\)
Review 7. Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability the sum is \(2\)? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by counting all the equally likely outcomes. The red die has \(6\) faces and the blue die has \(6\) faces, so by the multiplication principle there are \(6 \times 6 = 36\) equally likely pairs, which is our total count. Now count the favorable pairs, the ones whose sum is \(2\). Each die shows at least a \(1\), so the only way to reach a sum of \(2\) is for both dice to land on \(1\). Only \((1,1)\) works, which gives a favorable count of \(1\). The probability is the favorable count over the total count, so $$\boxed{\frac{1}{36}}.$$
Review 8. A shuffled 52-card deck has 13 spades. Two cards are drawn without replacement. What is the probability both are spades? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
This is a two stage draw with no putting cards back, so we walk the picks one at a time and multiply their chances. For the top card, the favorable count is the \(13\) spades and the total count is the \(52\) cards, which gives \(\frac{13}{52}\). Now one spade is gone, so for the second card the favorable count is the \(12\) spades that are left and the total count is the \(51\) cards still in the deck, which gives \(\frac{12}{51}\). Both stages have to happen, so we multiply them and you get \(\frac{13}{52} \times \frac{12}{51} = \frac{1}{4} \times \frac{12}{51} = \frac{12}{204} = \frac{1}{17}\). So the probability that the top two cards are both spades is \(\frac{1}{17}\). $$\boxed{\dfrac{1}{17}}$$
Review 9. A shuffled 52-card deck has 4 aces. Two cards are drawn without replacement. What is the probability both are aces? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Walk the two cards in order, since one is dealt before the other. For the top card, all \(52\) cards are equally likely and \(4\) of them are favorable, the aces, so the chance the first card is an ace is \(\frac{4}{52}\). Now suppose that first card really was an ace. It is no longer in the deck, so there are only \(51\) cards left and only \(3\) of them are still aces. The favorable count is \(3\) and the total count is \(51\), which gives a chance of \(\frac{3}{51}\) for the second card. To land both aces in a row you multiply the two stage chances, and you get \(\frac{4}{52} \times \frac{3}{51} = \frac{1}{13} \times \frac{3}{51} = \frac{3}{663} = \frac{1}{221}\). So the probability that the top two cards are both aces is \(\boxed{\frac{1}{221}}\).
Review 10. A shuffled 52-card deck has 12 face cards. Two cards are drawn without replacement. What is the probability both are face cards? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
We want both of the top two cards to be face cards, so we measure the chance one stage at a time and multiply, just like counting a pair in sequence. For the first card, all \(52\) cards are equally likely and \(12\) of them are face cards, so the favorable count is \(12\) out of a total of \(52\), which gives \(\frac{12}{52}\). After that face card is set aside, the deck has one fewer card, so now there are \(51\) cards left and only \(11\) of them are still face cards, which gives \(\frac{11}{51}\) for the second pick. We multiply the two stages, and you get $$\frac{12}{52} \times \frac{11}{51} = \frac{3}{13} \times \frac{11}{51} = \frac{33}{663} = \frac{11}{221}.$$ So the probability that the top two cards are both face cards is \(\boxed{\frac{11}{221}}\).
Review 11. Four fair coins are flipped. What is the probability at least one comes up heads? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Each coin lands heads or tails, so a full result is a string of four letters like HTTH. Each of the four coins has \(2\) choices, and the multiplication principle gives \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 = 16\) equally likely outcomes, which is our total count. Now we want at least one head, and going after that head on directly means counting one head, two heads, three heads, and four heads, which is a pile of cases. The complement saves us. The only way to get no heads is to have all tails, and that is just the single outcome TTTT, so the favorable count for no heads is \(1\) out of \(16\), which gives \(\frac{1}{16}\). Flipping it around with \(P(\text{at least one head}) = 1 - P(\text{no heads})\), we get \(1 - \frac{1}{16} = \frac{15}{16}\). So the probability of at least one head is $$\boxed{\frac{15}{16}}.$$
Review 12. Four fair coins are flipped. What is the probability exactly two come up heads? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
First find the total number of equally likely outcomes. Each of the \(4\) coins lands heads or tails, so it has \(2\) results, and the multiplication principle gives \(2^4 = 16\) outcomes in all. That is our total count. \nNow count the favorable outcomes, the ones with exactly two heads. Picking which coins come up heads is the same as choosing which \(2\) of the \(4\) coins are the heads, so this is a pair count from one group of \(4\). Each of the \(4\) coins can pair with the other \(3\), which gives \(4 \times 3\), but that counts every pair twice, once from each side, so we divide by \(2\) and you get \(\frac{4 \times 3}{2} = 6\). So there are \(6\) favorable outcomes. \nPut the favorable count over the total count, which gives $$\frac{6}{16} = \frac{3}{8}.$$ So the probability of exactly two heads is \(\boxed{\frac{3}{8}}\).
Review 13. Two fair 6-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability the product of the two numbers is even? Give your answer in lowest terms.
Show solution
Trying to list every way the product comes out even is a long road, so let us walk the kinder one and count the opposite first. A product of two whole numbers is odd only when both numbers are odd, because as soon as even one of them is even, the product turns even too. \nSo when is the product odd? Only when the first die is odd and the second die is odd. On one die the odd faces are \(1\), \(3\), and \(5\), which is \(3\) favorable faces out of \(6\), giving \(\frac{3}{6}\). The two dice are independent, so we multiply the two stages and you get $$\frac{3}{6} \times \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{4}.$$ \nThat \(\frac{1}{4}\) is the chance the product is odd, which is the one case we did not want. The complement does the rest, since even is everything that is left over, so we subtract from \(1\) and you get \(1 - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}\). So the probability that the product is even is \(\boxed{\frac{3}{4}}\).
Review 14. A bag holds \(4\) red, \(3\) blue, and \(5\) green marbles. One is drawn at random. What is the probability it is green?
Show solution
There are \(5\) green of \(4+3+5 = 12\) marbles, \(\frac{5}{12}\).
