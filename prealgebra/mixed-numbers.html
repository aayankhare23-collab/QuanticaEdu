Prealgebra · Lesson 4.7

Mixed Numbers

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Seven quarter-hops carry you across a creek from \(0\). After \(4\) hops your boot lands on \(1\), and three more put you past \(1\) but short of \(2\). You could call this spot seven quarters, \(\tfrac{7}{4}\), or just as fairly one whole plank and three quarters more, \(1\tfrac{3}{4}\). Same boot, same point, two names that are quietly equal. So how do you write a number that has slipped past a whole without landing on the next one, and how do you flip between the two names?

Your boot is at \(\tfrac{7}{4}\), past \(1\) but short of \(2\). How many whole planks have you completely crossed? Give that count.
Show solution
Each whole plank is split into four quarter-hops, so the marks fall at \(0\), \(\tfrac{4}{4}=1\), and \(\tfrac{8}{4}=2\). Your boot sits on the seventh hop, at \(\tfrac{7}{4}\). To find the finished planks, ask which whole-number mark your boot has fully cleared. The first four hops carry you exactly to the mark at \(1\), finishing one full plank. The next three hops, the fifth, sixth, and seventh, push you out onto the second plank but stop at \(\tfrac{7}{4}\), which is short of the mark at \(2\). So the second plank is only partly crossed and does not count yet. The largest whole-number mark your boot has passed is \(1\), so exactly one plank is finished. That whole \(1\) sitting behind your boot is the whole part of the mixed number. The three leftover quarter-hops are the fraction \(\tfrac{3}{4}\), and together they name the very same point as \(\tfrac{7}{4}\), written as the mixed number \(1\tfrac{3}{4}\). The question asks only for the count of finished planks. \(\boxed{1}\)
Key idea
Mixed number. A mixed number like \(1\tfrac{3}{4}\) is a quiet way of writing \(1+\tfrac{3}{4}\), so you scoop up the whole pieces first and then name only the leftover. It is not a new amount. It is the same point as the improper fraction \(\tfrac{7}{4}\), only dressed differently, since bundling pieces into wholes never adds or loses a single hop. And because the whole part sits right out front, a mixed number tells you at a glance that it lives between that whole number and the next one up.
A stretch is \(23\) beads; every \(5\) beads make one full band (\(\tfrac{23}{5}\)). Divide \(23\) by \(5\): whole bands and leftover. Write the length as a mixed number.
Show solution
The fraction \(\tfrac{23}{5}\) means 23 beads cut into groups of 5, so do that division. \(23 \div 5\) gives \(4\) with a remainder, because \(5 \times 4 = 20\) and that leaves \(23 - 20 = 3\) beads still hanging. So 23 beads make 4 complete bands with 3 loose beads dangling past the last one. Those 3 beads are 3 parts of a band that needs 5, so they read as \(\tfrac{3}{5}\) of a band. Stack the whole bands next to that leftover fraction and the length is \(4\tfrac{3}{5}\) bands. A quick check, four full bands is \(4 \times 5 = 20\) beads, plus the 3 loose beads makes \(23\), exactly the strand you started with. $$\frac{23}{5} = 4\tfrac{3}{5}$$ \(\boxed{4\tfrac{3}{5}}\)
Key idea
Improper fraction to mixed number. A fraction bar has always meant divide, so to read \(\tfrac{23}{5}\) as a mixed number, divide \(23\) by \(5\). The quotient counts the whole groups you finished, and that number stands out front, while the remainder counts the pieces that could not fill another group and stays over the same bottom, because no piece ever changed size. You only sorted the pieces into full bundles and a leftover, so \(\tfrac{23}{5}\) and \(4\tfrac{3}{5}\) are one place on the line. When the remainder lands on zero, the bundles came out even and you were holding a whole number all along.
Order slip: \(3\tfrac{2}{7}\) bands. Three whole bands = \(21\) beads; add \(2\) loose. Write \(3\tfrac{2}{7}\) as an improper fraction in sevenths.
Show solution
The trick is to stop seeing the wholes as wholes and start counting the actual beads inside them. The machine measures everything in single beads, and a single bead is one seventh of a band, so I want one total bead count sitting over 7. Each whole band is 7 beads, and there are 3 of them, so the wholes carry \(3\times 7=21\) beads. On top of that sit the 2 loose beads. Altogether that is \(21+2=23\) beads. Every one of those beads is a seventh of a band, so the length is \(\dfrac{23}{7}\) of a band. As a quick sanity check, \(23\div 7\) is 3 with a remainder of 2, which lands right back on \(3\tfrac{2}{7}\), exactly where we started. $$3\tfrac{2}{7}=\frac{3\times 7+2}{7}=\frac{21+2}{7}=\boxed{\dfrac{23}{7}}$$
Key idea
Converting both ways. Both moves are just bundling run forward or in reverse, so neither one moves the point. To read an improper fraction as a mixed number you divide the top by the bottom, and the quotient is how many whole groups you bundled while the leftover stays as a fraction over the same bottom. To go the other way you smash each whole back into pieces, so a whole is worth bottom-many pieces, and \(\text{whole}\times\text{bottom}+\text{top}\) counts every piece from \(0\) over that same bottom. Same spot on the line, two ways of saying it, one that gathers pieces into wholes and one that counts every piece.
A lookout sits at \(\tfrac{47}{6}\) miles. Convert to a mixed number. Give the nearest post behind the hiker, the largest whole number below \(\tfrac{47}{6}\).
Show solution
Start by asking how many full miles are buried inside \(\frac{47}{6}\). Each whole mile is \(\frac{6}{6}\), so the real question is how many \(6\)'s fit into \(47\). Since \(6\times 7 = 42\) and \(6\times 8 = 48\) would overshoot, exactly \(7\) whole miles fit, and \(47 - 42 = 5\) sixths are left over. That gives \(\frac{47}{6} = 7\tfrac{5}{6}\). The whole part \(7\) tells you she has fully passed post \(7\), and the leftover \(\frac{5}{6}\) of a mile means she has not yet reached post \(8\). So the lookout sits between posts \(7\) and \(8\), and the largest whole number still less than \(\frac{47}{6}\) is the post right behind her. $$\frac{47}{6} = 7\tfrac{5}{6}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \boxed{7}$$
Fun fact
In \(7\tfrac{5}{6}\) the space means "and" (\(+\)), a surveyors' shorthand. Once algebra arrives, juxtaposition means multiply (\(2x\)), so mixed-number notation is quietly retired.
Sensor at \(-7\tfrac{1}{3}\): the minus covers the whole quantity, so the sensor is a third below \(-7\). Give \(-7\tfrac{1}{3}\) as an improper fraction.
Show solution
The trap is treating the minus as if it only touches the \(7\) and leaves \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) positive. It does not. The minus sign belongs to the whole amount, so \(-7\tfrac{1}{3}\) is shorthand for \(-\left(7\tfrac{1}{3}\right) = -\left(7 + \tfrac{1}{3}\right) = -7 - \tfrac{1}{3}\). Notice that second sign flipped too. So the sensor sits a third below \(-7\), not above it. The attendant marked it a third toward \(-6\), which is the wrong direction. On the line, \(-7\tfrac{1}{3}\) lands between \(-8\) and \(-7\), a little past \(-7\) on the way down. A clean way to see it is by mirroring. The point \(7\tfrac{1}{3}\) sits a third past \(7\) heading up toward \(8\). Reflect that across \(0\) and up becomes down, so it must sit a third past \(-7\) heading down toward \(-8\). For the exact value, first build the improper fraction from the positive part. \(7\tfrac{1}{3} = \dfrac{7 \times 3 + 1}{3} = \dfrac{22}{3}\). The mirror image across \(0\) just attaches the minus, giving \(-\dfrac{22}{3}\). As a check, \(-\dfrac{22}{3} = -7.333\ldots\), which is indeed a third below \(-7\). $$-7\tfrac{1}{3} = -\left(7 + \tfrac{1}{3}\right) = -\frac{22}{3}$$ $$\boxed{-\dfrac{22}{3}}$$
Key idea
Negative mixed numbers. A minus sign in front of a mixed number grabs the whole quantity, not just the whole part, so \(-7\tfrac{1}{3}\) means \(-7-\tfrac{1}{3}\), a third below \(-7\) and resting between \(-8\) and \(-7\). The safe move is to find where the positive version \(7\tfrac{1}{3}\) sits, then mirror that whole distance across \(0\), or just hang the minus on the single improper fraction. That keeps \(-7\tfrac{1}{3}\) from getting confused with \(-7+\tfrac{1}{3}\), a completely different number living between \(-7\) and \(-6\).
Potter uses \(2\tfrac{5}{6}+1\tfrac{5}{6}\) scoops. The fraction parts sum past \(1\), so carry a whole. Total scoops as a mixed number?
Show solution
Split each mixed number into its whole part and its fraction part and add the matching kinds together. The whole scoops give \(2+1=3\). The fraction parts give \(\tfrac{5}{6}+\tfrac{5}{6}=\tfrac{10}{6}\). So far the total looks like \(3\) wholes and \(\tfrac{10}{6}\), but \(\tfrac{10}{6}\) is more than a whole scoop, so it is not finished. Pull the whole scoop out of \(\tfrac{10}{6}\). Since \(\tfrac{6}{6}\) is exactly one scoop, \(\tfrac{10}{6}=\tfrac{6}{6}+\tfrac{4}{6}=1\tfrac{4}{6}\), and \(\tfrac{4}{6}\) simplifies to \(\tfrac{2}{3}\), so \(\tfrac{10}{6}=1\tfrac{2}{3}\). That hidden whole scoop has to leave the fraction column and join the whole count. The \(3\) wholes become \(3+1=4\) wholes, and \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of a scoop stays behind. The total is \(4\tfrac{2}{3}\) scoops, which you can check as the improper fraction \(\tfrac{17}{6}+\tfrac{11}{6}=\tfrac{28}{6}=\tfrac{14}{3}=4\tfrac{2}{3}\). What you just did has a name, you carried, exactly like carrying a ten in column addition, except here a full group of \(\tfrac{6}{6}\) carries instead of a full group of ten. \(\boxed{4\tfrac{2}{3}\text{ scoops}}\)
Rail is \(5\tfrac{1}{4}\) spans; cut \(2\tfrac{3}{4}\). \(\tfrac{1}{4}<\tfrac{3}{4}\): trade one whole for \(4\) quarters. Finish and give what remains as a mixed number.
Show solution
The plan is sound, the only trouble is that the quarters column will not subtract as written, since \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) is less than \(\tfrac{3}{4}\). So we rewrite the rail without changing its length. One whole span is exactly \(\tfrac{4}{4}\), so take a single whole out of the \(5\) and turn it into quarters. That leaves \(4\) wholes, and the quarters become \(\tfrac{4}{4}+\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{5}{4}\). In other words \(5\tfrac{1}{4}\) and \(4\tfrac{5}{4}\) are two names for the same amount of rail. Now the columns cooperate. Quarters give \(\tfrac{5}{4}-\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{2}{4}\), and wholes give \(4-2=2\), so what remains is \(2\tfrac{2}{4}\). Halving top and bottom of \(\tfrac{2}{4}\) gives \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), so the leftover rail is $$2\tfrac{2}{4}=\boxed{2\tfrac{1}{2}}$$ hand-spans.
Key idea
Mixed number arithmetic. Adding or subtracting mixed numbers is two small jobs side by side, the wholes with the wholes and the fraction parts with the fraction parts, once the fractions share a bottom. If the fraction parts add up past one whole, that spare whole climbs into the whole count and only the leftover fraction stays put, so \(2\tfrac{5}{6}+3\tfrac{5}{6}\) becomes \(5\) and \(\tfrac{5}{3}\), which is \(6\tfrac{2}{3}\). If you have to take away more fraction than you own, you break one whole back into pieces and hand them down, turning a whole into something like \(\tfrac{4}{4}\) so the subtraction can finish. Carrying and borrowing are the same bundling trade, one read forward and the other backward.
Legs: \(1\tfrac{1}{2}+2\tfrac{1}{3}\) laps. Halves and thirds differ; find lcd, rename fractions, then add. Check for carry. Total as a mixed number?
Show solution
Pull each mixed number apart into a whole part and a fraction part. The wholes are \(1\) and \(2\), and \(1+2=3\) laps. The fraction parts are \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{3}\), and these are pieces of different sizes, so they cannot be combined until they share a bottom number. The smallest number that both \(2\) and \(3\) divide into is \(6\), so rename each one over sixths. A half is three of those sixths, \(\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{3}{6}\), and a third is two of them, \(\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{2}{6}\). Now the pieces match, so \(\tfrac{3}{6}+\tfrac{2}{6}=\tfrac{5}{6}\). Check whether this spills past a whole lap. Since \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) is still under \(1\), there is no extra whole to carry over, so the \(3\) whole laps stay as \(3\). Putting the parts back together gives \(3\) whole laps and \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) of a lap, which is \(3\tfrac{5}{6}\) laps. As a single improper fraction that is \(\tfrac{23}{6}\), a handy way to confirm the work. \(\boxed{3\tfrac{5}{6}}\)
\(4\) planters each need \(2\tfrac{5}{8}\) bags. Convert to an improper fraction, multiply by \(4\), convert back. Total bags as a mixed number?
Show solution
First fold the mixed number into a single fraction. The whole part \(2\) is \(\tfrac{16}{8}\), and adding the \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) gives \(2\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{21}{8}\). Now one box is just \(\tfrac{21}{8}\) bags, with no separate pieces to keep track of. Multiply by the \(4\) boxes, $$4\times\frac{21}{8}=\frac{84}{8}.$$ That fraction simplifies. Dividing top and bottom by \(4\) gives \(\tfrac{84}{8}=\tfrac{21}{2}\), and \(\tfrac{21}{2}\) is \(10\) with \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) left over, so the total is \(10\tfrac{1}{2}\) bags. Here is why the separate-pieces road is dangerous. Multiplying gives \(4\times 2=8\) whole bags and \(4\times\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{20}{8}=2\tfrac{1}{2}\) more bags. The fraction part did not stay a fraction, it ballooned into another \(2\tfrac{1}{2}\) bags, and writing the answer as "eight and two and a half" tangled together is begging for a slip. Converting to an improper fraction first never lets that surprise sneak up on you. The four boxes hold \(\boxed{10\tfrac{1}{2}}\) bags of soil.
Full day: \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\) km; half-staffed day gets \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of that. Convert, multiply by \(\tfrac{2}{3}\), convert back. How many km, as a mixed number?
Show solution
First clear the mixed number out of the way, because the multiply rule wants a plain fraction. The three whole kilometres are \(\tfrac{12}{4}\), and adding the extra \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) gives \(3\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{15}{4}\). Now take \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of that by multiplying straight across, \(\tfrac{2}{3}\times\tfrac{15}{4}=\tfrac{2\times 15}{3\times 4}=\tfrac{30}{12}\). That fraction is honest but bulky, so reduce it. The greatest common factor of \(30\) and \(12\) is \(6\), and \(\tfrac{30}{12}=\tfrac{5}{2}\). Finally turn \(\tfrac{5}{2}\) back into a mixed number, since the question asks for kilometres in that shape. Two halves make one whole and there are five halves in all, so that is two wholes with one half left over, giving \(2\tfrac{1}{2}\). A quick sanity check fits the story too, half a staff should clear a bit more than half of \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\), and \(2\tfrac{1}{2}\) is comfortably in that range. The half-staffed day clears $$\boxed{2\tfrac{1}{2}}$$ kilometres.
Key idea
Multiplying and dividing mixed numbers. When you multiply two mixed numbers, the whole parts and the fraction parts do not stay neatly separated the way they did with adding. Every piece of one number has to meet every piece of the other, so you cannot just multiply the wholes, multiply the fractions, and stick the answers back together. The reliable move is to drop the mixed form first, rewrite each number as a single improper fraction, and then multiply straight across, or flip the second one and multiply to divide. After that you can fold the result back into a mixed number if the question wants it that way.
Ribbon \(4\tfrac{1}{2}\) m; bows are \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) m each. Convert, divide using flip-and-multiply. How many whole bows?
Show solution
Start by turning the mixed number into a single fraction so the division rule can act on it. Four and a half metres is \(4\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{9}{2}\), because each whole metre is two halves and four and a half metres hold nine halves. The question \(4\tfrac{1}{2}\div\tfrac{3}{4}\) is really asking how many groups of \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) fit inside \(\tfrac{9}{2}\). Dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal, so flip \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) to get \(\tfrac{4}{3}\) and multiply. $$\frac{9}{2}\div\frac{3}{4}=\frac{9}{2}\times\frac{4}{3}=\frac{9\times 4}{2\times 3}=\frac{36}{6}=6.$$ The answer comes out to a clean whole number, which tells you something nice. The ribbon splits into exactly six \(\tfrac{3}{4}\)-metre pieces with nothing left over. You can check it by going the other way: six pieces of \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) metre give \(6\times\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{18}{4}=\tfrac{9}{2}=4\tfrac{1}{2}\) metres, which matches the ribbon you started with. So the count is the number of pieces, and here that count is whole and exact. \(\boxed{6}\)
A sign maker uses \(1\tfrac{5}{8}\) m of ribbon per letter for a \(4\)-letter word, starting from a \(7\) m roll. Convert, multiply, subtract. How many metres of ribbon are left on the roll?
Show solution
Start by rewriting the amount one letter uses. A whole is \(\tfrac{8}{8}\), so \(1\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{8}{8}+\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{13}{8}\) metres per letter. Working in improper form keeps the multiplying tidy. Since all four letters are identical, you do not add four separate pieces. You multiply once. \(4\times\tfrac{13}{8}=\tfrac{4\times 13}{8}=\tfrac{52}{8}\). Now simplify, because \(52\) and \(8\) share a factor of \(4\). \(\tfrac{52}{8}=\tfrac{13}{2}=6\tfrac{1}{2}\) metres of ribbon used by the whole word. The last step is the subtraction, and this is where it pays to match denominators rather than guess. Write the full roll as \(7=\tfrac{14}{2}\), then \(\tfrac{14}{2}-\tfrac{13}{2}=\tfrac{1}{2}\) metre. So half a metre is left on the roll. And that answers the maker's real question. Another letter needs \(1\tfrac{5}{8}\) metres, and \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) is far short of that, so no extra letter fits. $$7-4\times 1\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{14}{2}-\tfrac{13}{2}=\boxed{\tfrac{1}{2}}\text{ metre}$$

Review problems

Review 1. An anchor leg is logged as \(\tfrac{29}{6}\) of a segment. Rewrite \(\tfrac{29}{6}\) as a mixed number in simplest form.
Show solution
An improper fraction packs more than a whole into a single stack over the bar, so converting to a mixed number just means pulling out the whole groups. Each whole unit is \(\tfrac{6}{6}\), so I ask how many sixes fit into \(29\). Dividing, \(29 \div 6 = 4\) with a remainder of \(5\), because \(6 \times 4 = 24\) and \(29 - 24 = 5\). The quotient \(4\) is the whole-number part, and the remainder \(5\) stays over the original denominator as \(\tfrac{5}{6}\). The fraction \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) is already in simplest form since \(\gcd(5,6)=1\). Putting the pieces together gives four wholes and five sixths. As a quick check, \(4 + \tfrac{5}{6} = \tfrac{24}{6} + \tfrac{5}{6} = \tfrac{29}{6}\), which matches what we started with. \(\boxed{4\tfrac{5}{6}}\)
Review 2. A staircase rises \(6\tfrac{2}{5}\) full turns. Rewrite \(6\tfrac{2}{5}\) as a single improper fraction in fifths.
Show solution
A mixed number is just a whole part plus a fraction part, so I want to turn the whole part into fifths and combine. Each full turn is \(\tfrac{5}{5}\), and there are 6 of them, which gives \(6\times 5 = 30\) fifths. The fraction already carries another 2 fifths, so altogether I have \(30 + 2 = 32\) fifths. That is the shortcut you see written as \(6\times 5 + 2 = 32\) on top, with the denominator 5 left unchanged underneath. So \(6\tfrac{2}{5} = \dfrac{32}{5}\), and a quick check confirms it since \(32\div 5 = 6.4 = 6\tfrac{2}{5}\). \(\boxed{\tfrac{32}{5}}\)
Review 3. A runner covers \(\tfrac{38}{7}\) laps. \(\tfrac{38}{7}\) falls between two consecutive whole numbers. What is the smaller of those two whole numbers?
Show solution
Turn the improper fraction into a mixed number by seeing how many whole sevens fit inside \(38\). Since \(7\times 5=35\) and \(7\times 6=42\), five sevens fit with \(3\) left over, so \(\tfrac{38}{7}=5\tfrac{3}{7}\). That whole part of \(5\) tells you the value has passed \(5\) but has not reached \(6\), because the extra \(\tfrac{3}{7}\) is more than nothing yet less than a full \(1\). So \(\tfrac{38}{7}\) sits between the consecutive whole numbers \(5\) and \(6\). The runner has finished \(5\) complete laps and is partway through her sixth. The smaller of the two is \(\boxed{5}\).
Review 4. A diver's tag reads \(-3\tfrac{2}{5}\) m: the minus grabs the whole amount, so this is \(-3-\tfrac{2}{5}\). Rewrite \(-3\tfrac{2}{5}\) as a single improper fraction.
Show solution
The tricky part is the sign. The reading \(-3\tfrac{2}{5}\) is not three and two fifths with a stray minus on the 3. It is the negative of the whole amount, \(-\left(3\tfrac{2}{5}\right)\), which is why we can read it as \(-3 - \tfrac{2}{5}\). So I convert the positive piece first, then carry the minus along. To turn \(3\tfrac{2}{5}\) into an improper fraction, I count how many fifths I have. Each whole is \(5\) fifths, so \(3\) wholes are \(15\) fifths, and the extra \(\tfrac{2}{5}\) brings it to \(17\) fifths. $$3\tfrac{2}{5} = \frac{3\times 5 + 2}{5} = \frac{17}{5}$$ Now I put the negative sign back on the whole thing, giving \(-\tfrac{17}{5}\). A quick sanity check: \(\tfrac{17}{5}\) is between \(3\) and \(4\), so \(-\tfrac{17}{5}\) sits between \(-4\) and \(-3\), exactly where a depth of about \(-3.4\) metres should land. \(\boxed{-\tfrac{17}{5}}\)
Review 5. A flag is planted at \(\frac{23}{4}\) on a number line. What is the largest whole number to the left of the flag?
Show solution
Turn the improper fraction into a mixed number by dividing. Since \(23 \div 4 = 5\) with a remainder of \(3\), we get \(\frac{23}{4}=5\tfrac{3}{4}\). So the flag lands three quarters of the way past \(5\), somewhere between \(5\) and \(6\). The whole numbers smaller than \(5\tfrac{3}{4}\) are \(5, 4, 3,\) and so on, and the largest of those is the one right below the flag. That whole number is \(\boxed{5}\).
Review 6. A runner starts at a trail marker \(3\tfrac{4}{5}\) km from start, then jogs \(2\tfrac{3}{5}\) km more. How far from start is the runner? Give as a mixed number in simplest form.
Show solution
Split each mixed number into its whole part and its fraction part, then add like with like. The wholes give \(3+2=5\). The fifths give \(\tfrac{4}{5}+\tfrac{3}{5}=\tfrac{7}{5}\). Here is the moment to watch. Seven fifths is already bigger than one whole, so it does not belong sitting next to the fives as a leftover fraction. Trade it in. Since \(\tfrac{7}{5}=\tfrac{5}{5}+\tfrac{2}{5}=1\tfrac{2}{5}\), you carry one whole across. Now the wholes become \(5+1=6\) and the honest leftover is \(\tfrac{2}{5}\). Putting them back together, the runner is \(6\tfrac{2}{5}\) kilometers from the start. As a check, \(3\tfrac{4}{5}=\tfrac{19}{5}\) and \(2\tfrac{3}{5}=\tfrac{13}{5}\), and \(\tfrac{19}{5}+\tfrac{13}{5}=\tfrac{32}{5}=6\tfrac{2}{5}\). \(\boxed{6\tfrac{2}{5}}\)
Review 7. Evaluate \(6\tfrac{1}{3}-2\tfrac{2}{3}\) and write the result as a mixed number. The thirds will not subtract cleanly, so you will need to borrow one whole from the \(6\). What is the value?
Show solution
The thirds are the trouble. We want \(\tfrac{1}{3}-\tfrac{2}{3}\), but \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) is smaller than \(\tfrac{2}{3}\), so there is nothing clean to take away. Instead of stopping, we borrow. Take one whole out of the \(6\), which leaves \(5\), and turn that whole into \(\tfrac{3}{3}\). Stacking it onto the \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) gives \(\tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{3}{3}=\tfrac{4}{3}\). So \(6\tfrac{1}{3}\) is the very same amount as \(5\tfrac{4}{3}\). Now everything subtracts in order. The wholes give \(5-2=3\), and the thirds give \(\tfrac{4}{3}-\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{2}{3}\). Putting the pieces together leaves \(3\tfrac{2}{3}\). A quick sanity check with improper fractions agrees, since \(\tfrac{19}{3}-\tfrac{8}{3}=\tfrac{11}{3}=3\tfrac{2}{3}\). \(\boxed{3\tfrac{2}{3}}\)
Review 8. Two legs: \(1\tfrac{1}{2}\) miles and \(2\tfrac{1}{4}\) miles. Line up fraction parts over the lcd before adding. What is the total distance as a mixed number?
Show solution
First give both fraction parts the same bottom. Since \(\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{2}{4}\), the sum becomes \(1\tfrac{2}{4}+2\tfrac{1}{4}\). Now add the whole parts and the fraction parts on their own. The wholes give \(1+2=3\), and the fourths give \(\tfrac{2}{4}+\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{3}{4}\). That fraction part is less than one whole, so there is no extra whole to carry over. Putting the pieces back together gives \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\) miles, which is \(\tfrac{15}{4}\) as an improper fraction. \(\boxed{3\tfrac{3}{4}}\)
Review 9. Six planters each need \(1\tfrac{2}{3}\) bags of mulch. Convert to an improper fraction, then multiply by \(6\). How many bags total?
Show solution
Start by turning the mixed number into one fraction so the multiplication is clean. One whole is \(\tfrac{3}{3}\), so \(1\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{3}{3}+\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{5}{3}\). Now multiply by the six planters. Treat \(6\) as \(\tfrac{6}{1}\) and multiply straight across, \(6\times\tfrac{5}{3}=\tfrac{6\times 5}{1\times 3}=\tfrac{30}{3}\). Finally simplify, and since \(30\div 3=10\) the fraction collapses to a whole number. The improper fraction was the shortcut here, it let the six and the three cancel into a tidy count instead of adding \(1\tfrac{2}{3}\) six separate times. The row of planters needs \(\boxed{10}\) bags of mulch.
Review 10. One press pass uses \(2\tfrac{2}{3}\) oz of ink; a proof run needs \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) of a pass. Convert, then multiply. How many ounces does the proof run use?
Show solution
Start by turning the mixed number into a single fraction. The whole number 2 is \(\tfrac{6}{3}\), and adding the \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) gives \(2\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{8}{3}\). Now the problem is a clean product of two fractions, \(\tfrac{3}{4}\times\tfrac{8}{3}\). Multiplying across the tops and across the bottoms gives \(\tfrac{3\times 8}{4\times 3}=\tfrac{24}{12}\). That fraction is really a whole number in disguise, since \(24\div 12=2\). So the proof run uses \(\boxed{2}\) ounces of ink.
Review 11. A ribbon is \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\) m. Cut into \(1\tfrac{1}{2}\) m pieces. Convert both to improper fractions, divide, write the result as a mixed number. How many pieces fit?
Show solution
First change both mixed numbers into improper fractions. For \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\), multiply the whole part by the denominator and add the top, giving \(3\times 4+3=15\), so it is \(\tfrac{15}{4}\). For \(1\tfrac{1}{2}\), the same move gives \(1\times 2+1=3\), so it is \(\tfrac{3}{2}\). Now the problem is \(\tfrac{15}{4}\div\tfrac{3}{2}\). Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal, so flip the second fraction and multiply. $$\frac{15}{4}\div\frac{3}{2}=\frac{15}{4}\times\frac{2}{3}=\frac{30}{12}=\frac{5}{2}$$ Finally, write \(\tfrac{5}{2}\) as a mixed number. Two goes into five twice with one left over, so it is \(2\tfrac{1}{2}\). That matches the picture too, since two full pieces use \(3\) meters and the leftover \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) meter is exactly half of a \(1\tfrac{1}{2}\) meter piece. \(\boxed{2\tfrac{1}{2}}\)
Review 12. One loop lap is \(4\tfrac{2}{3}\) km; two runners split it equally. How long is each leg? Give as a mixed number in km.
Show solution
Start by turning the mixed number into an improper fraction. Three thirds make one whole, so four wholes hold twelve thirds, and adding the extra two thirds gives \(4\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{14}{3}\).

Now split that lap into two equal legs by dividing by 2. Dividing by 2 is the same as multiplying by \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), so \(\tfrac{14}{3}\div 2=\tfrac{14}{3}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{14}{6}\).

Both 14 and 6 share a factor of 2, so \(\tfrac{14}{6}=\tfrac{7}{3}\). Since three goes into seven twice with one left over, each leg is \(2\tfrac{1}{3}\) kilometers long. As a quick check, two legs of \(2\tfrac{1}{3}\) add to \(4\tfrac{2}{3}\), the whole lap, so the split is right.

\(\boxed{2\tfrac{1}{3}\text{ km}}\)
Review 13. A full batch needs \(2\tfrac{1}{4}\) cups of flour. You make \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of a batch. How many cups do you need? Give as a fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Making \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of a batch means taking \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of the flour, and the word "of" here is a signal to multiply. So you need \(\tfrac{1}{3}\times 2\tfrac{1}{4}\). Mixed numbers are awkward to multiply directly, so convert first. The whole number \(2\) is the same as \(\tfrac{8}{4}\), and adding the \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) gives \(2\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{9}{4}\). Now multiply straight across, top times top and bottom times bottom. $$\frac{1}{3}\times\frac{9}{4}=\frac{1\times 9}{3\times 4}=\frac{9}{12}.$$ Both \(9\) and \(12\) share a factor of \(3\), so \(\tfrac{9}{12}=\tfrac{3}{4}\). You need three quarters of a cup of flour, which makes sense since a third of a batch should use a good deal less than the full \(2\tfrac{1}{4}\) cups. \(\boxed{\tfrac{3}{4}}\)
Review 14. A board is \(5\tfrac{1}{2}\) ft. Two side brackets, each \(1\tfrac{3}{4}\) ft, are cut off. How many feet of board remain?
Show solution
Start with the two pieces the builder removes. Each is \(1\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{7}{4}\) feet, and there are two of them, so together they use \(2\times\tfrac{7}{4}=\tfrac{14}{4}=\tfrac{7}{2}=3\tfrac{1}{2}\) feet. Now subtract that from the original board. The board is \(5\tfrac{1}{2}\) feet, so what remains is \(5\tfrac{1}{2}-3\tfrac{1}{2}\). The two halves match and cancel, leaving \(5-3=2\) feet. So \(2\) feet of board are left over. \(\boxed{2}\)
