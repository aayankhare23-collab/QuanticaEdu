Prealgebra · Lesson 3.5

GCD and LCM

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Two numbers side by side reveal what they share and what they jointly build. The GCD answers the sharing question; the LCM answers the building question. The prime fingerprints from 3.3 unlock both at once.

A maker has ribbons of 56 cm and 70 cm. She wants to cut both into equal whole-centimeter pieces with no scrap. List the divisors of each and find the longest piece length that works for both.
Show solution
A piece length can be used on a ribbon only when it divides that ribbon evenly, so the lengths that work for both ribbons are exactly the numbers that divide \(56\) and \(70\) at the same time. List each set of divisors and watch for the overlap. $$56: \quad 1,\ 2,\ 4,\ 7,\ 8,\ 14,\ 28,\ 56$$ $$70: \quad 1,\ 2,\ 5,\ 7,\ 10,\ 14,\ 35,\ 70$$ The numbers appearing on both lists are \(1, 2, 7,\) and \(14\). These are the common divisors, the only piece lengths that clear both ribbons with no scrap, and the largest of them is \(\boxed{14}\) centimeters. Quick check, \(56 = 14 \times 4\) and \(70 = 14 \times 5\), so the first ribbon gives four pieces and the second gives five, all of them \(14\) long and nothing left over. The general idea is that two numbers always share a small handful of common divisors, and the biggest one is the most a single piece can stretch while still fitting both.
Key idea
The greatest common divisor \(\gcd(a,b)\), also called the greatest common factor, is the largest number that divides both \(a\) and \(b\) with nothing left over. Every pair has \(1\) on both divisor lists, so a common divisor always exists. And since lesson 3.1 showed a number's divisors never climb past the number itself, each list is finite, the overlap is finite, and there is always a single greatest one. It is the most that two numbers can be cut down by at the same time.
Use the fingerprints \(168 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 7\) and \(180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5\) to find \(\gcd(168, 180)\). Take each shared prime to its lower power.
Show solution
Set the two fingerprints side by side and read them prime by prime. $$168 = 2^3 \times 3^1 \times 7, \qquad 180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5$$ A common divisor is built only from primes both numbers own, using no more copies than the smaller supply allows, since you cannot borrow a factor a number does not have. For the prime \(2\), the smaller power is \(2^2\). For the prime \(3\), the smaller power is \(3^1\). The prime \(5\) lives only in \(180\) and the prime \(7\) lives only in \(168\), so neither can divide both and neither joins in. Multiplying the survivors, $$\gcd(168,180) = 2^2 \times 3 = \boxed{12}.$$ Quick check, \(168 = 12 \times 14\) and \(180 = 12 \times 15\), so \(12\) really does divide both, and nothing larger could, since any bigger common divisor would need a prime power neither fingerprint can spare. The general idea is that the gcd of two numbers is found by taking each shared prime to the lower of its two powers, which is far faster than listing every divisor.
Key idea
Lay two fingerprints side by side. The gcd takes each shared prime to the lower of its two powers, because a common divisor can only use primes both numbers carry, and only as many copies as the stingier number has. A prime in just one fingerprint contributes nothing, since its lower power across the two is \(0\). When the two numbers share no prime at all, the only common divisor is \(1\) and we call them coprime. This is the same coprime idea lesson 3.2 needed for combining divisibility tests, now seen through the fingerprints.
Two gears have 24 and 35 teeth, with \(24 = 2^3 \times 3\) and \(35 = 5 \times 7\). They share no prime factor. How many teeth must pass the meshing point before the red teeth touch again?
Show solution
The red teeth meet again at the first tooth count that is a whole number of turns for both gears, which means the smallest number divisible by \(24\) and by \(35\) at once. Look at the fingerprints, \(24 = 2^3 \times 3\) and \(35 = 5 \times 7\). They share nothing, so they are coprime. A number divisible by \(24\) must carry \(2^3\) and \(3\), and a number divisible by \(35\) must carry \(5\) and \(7\), and since none of these primes overlap, the smallest number holding all of them is simply their product. $$24 \times 35 = \boxed{840}$$ So \(840\) teeth pass the meshing point before the red teeth touch again. Quick check, \(840 = 24 \times 35\) gives the large gear \(24\) full turns and the small gear \(35\) full turns, both whole, so both red teeth are back at the meeting point together. The general idea is that when two numbers are coprime, nothing can be shared or saved, so the smallest number they both divide is just the two numbers multiplied together.

That last problem opened the second door: the smallest count that both numbers fit evenly into, the first moment two rhythms align. When the numbers share no factors, the answer is just their product. When they do share factors, it is smaller.

Two beacons flash every 9 seconds and every 12 seconds. List the flash times and find the first second after the start when both flash together.
Show solution
The first beacon flashes at the multiples of \(9\) and the second at the multiples of \(12\), so they flash together exactly at the numbers that are multiples of both. Walk the two lists until they first meet. $$9: \quad 9,\ 18,\ 27,\ 36,\ 45,\ 54,\ \ldots$$ $$12: \quad 12,\ 24,\ 36,\ 48,\ \ldots$$ The first number on both lists is \(36\), so the beacons next flash together at second \(\boxed{36}\). Quick check, \(36 = 9 \times 4\) and \(36 = 12 \times 3\), so the first beacon has flashed four times and the second three times, both landing on second \(36\). Notice this is smaller than \(9 \times 12 = 108\), because \(9\) and \(12\) share a factor of \(3\) that does not need to be counted twice. The general idea is that two repeating events coincide at the common multiples of their periods, and the first coincidence is the smallest of those.
Key idea
The least common multiple \(\operatorname{lcm}(a,b)\) is the smallest positive number that both \(a\) and \(b\) divide evenly, the smallest number that is a multiple of each. Two events repeating every \(a\) and every \(b\) steps line up exactly at the common multiples of \(a\) and \(b\), and the first alignment is their lcm. There is no greatest common multiple to speak of, because lesson 3.1 showed multiples run on forever, so the least one is the only interesting extreme.
Use \(168 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 7\) and \(180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5\) to find \(\operatorname{lcm}(168, 180)\). Take each prime to its higher power, including primes in only one fingerprint.
Show solution
A number that both \(168\) and \(180\) divide must contain enough of every prime to satisfy each of them, so for each prime it must meet the larger of the two demands. $$168 = 2^3 \times 3^1 \times 7, \qquad 180 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5$$ For \(2\), the larger power is \(2^3\), demanded by \(168\). For \(3\), the larger power is \(3^2\), demanded by \(180\). The prime \(5\) is demanded by \(180\) and the prime \(7\) by \(168\), so both must appear. Multiplying these highest demands together, $$\operatorname{lcm}(168,180) = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5 \times 7 = 8 \times 9 \times 5 \times 7 = \boxed{2520}.$$ Quick check, \(2520 = 168 \times 15\) and \(2520 = 180 \times 14\), both whole, so \(2520\) really is a multiple of each, and nothing smaller could be, since dropping any prime power would starve one of the two numbers. The general idea is that the lcm of two numbers takes each prime to the higher of its two powers, the mirror image of how the gcd takes the lower.
Key idea
Lay two fingerprints side by side. The lcm takes every prime to the higher of its two powers, because a common multiple must contain enough of every prime demanded by either number. Every prime in either fingerprint appears; every prime in neither contributes nothing.
A locksmith knows two numbers have \(\gcd = 8\) and \(\operatorname{lcm} = 240\), and one number is 48. Use the relationship \(\gcd \times \operatorname{lcm} = a \times b\) to find the other number.
Show solution
The shortcut the locksmith leans on comes straight from the fingerprints. For each prime, the gcd took the lower power and the lcm took the higher power, so between them they used the lower plus the higher, which is exactly the two powers the original numbers had. Multiply across all primes and the gcd times the lcm rebuilds the product of the two numbers themselves. $$\gcd(a,b) \times \operatorname{lcm}(a,b) = a \times b$$ Here that reads \(8 \times 240 = 48 \times (\text{other})\), so the product of the two numbers is \(8 \times 240 = 1920\). Dividing out the known number, $$\text{other} = \frac{1920}{48} = \boxed{40}.$$ Quick check, \(\gcd(48,40) = 8\) since \(48 = 2^4 \times 3\) and \(40 = 2^3 \times 5\) share \(2^3\), and \(\operatorname{lcm}(48,40) = 2^4 \times 3 \times 5 = 240\), matching both given facts. The general idea is that the gcd and the lcm of a pair always multiply back to the pair's own product, so knowing three of the four numbers hands you the fourth.
Key idea
For any two whole numbers, \(\gcd(a,b) \times \operatorname{lcm}(a,b) = a \times b\). The gcd took the lower power and the lcm the higher power of each prime, and together they restore the full pair. Most useful rearrangement: \(\operatorname{lcm}(a,b) = \dfrac{a \times b}{\gcd(a,b)}\).
Three subway lines depart every 12, 18, and 30 minutes. Build \(\operatorname{lcm}(12,18,30)\) from the fingerprints and report how many minutes until all three lines next leave together.
Show solution
All three lines leave together exactly at the numbers that are multiples of \(12\), \(18\), and \(30\) at once, so the next shared departure is the least common multiple of the three. The lcm idea extends from two numbers to three with no change, you still take each prime to the highest power any of them demands. Read the fingerprints, $$12 = 2^2 \times 3, \qquad 18 = 2 \times 3^2, \qquad 30 = 2 \times 3 \times 5.$$ For \(2\), the highest demand is \(2^2\), from the red line. For \(3\), the highest demand is \(3^2\), from the green line. For \(5\), the only demand is \(5\), from the gold line. Multiplying the highest demands, $$\operatorname{lcm}(12,18,30) = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5 = 4 \times 9 \times 5 = \boxed{180}.$$ So the lines next leave together \(180\) minutes after \(6\!:\!00\), which is \(3\) hours later at \(9\!:\!00\). Quick check, \(180 = 12 \times 15 = 18 \times 10 = 30 \times 6\), all whole, so every line really does have a departure at minute \(180\). The general idea is that the least common multiple stretches to any number of values, always taking each prime to the highest power demanded by any of them, and it tells you when several repeating rhythms all align.
A bakery wants the smallest four-digit batch number divisible by 6, 8, and 9 at once. Find \(\operatorname{lcm}(6,8,9)\), then find its smallest four-digit multiple.
Show solution
A batch number divisible by \(6\), \(8\), and \(9\) at once is a common multiple of all three, so the candidates are precisely the multiples of their least common multiple. Build it from the fingerprints, $$6 = 2 \times 3, \qquad 8 = 2^3, \qquad 9 = 3^2.$$ Taking each prime to its highest demand gives \(\operatorname{lcm}(6,8,9) = 2^3 \times 3^2 = 72\). Now find the smallest four-digit multiple of \(72\). Four-digit numbers start at \(1000\), and dividing, \(1000 \div 72 \approx 13.9\), so \(13\) full \(72\)s land at \(936\), still three digits, and the next one steps over the line. $$72 \times 14 = \boxed{1008}$$ Quick check, \(1008 = 6 \times 168 = 8 \times 126 = 9 \times 112\), all whole, and \(1008\) is the first four-digit number for which that is true, since \(72 \times 13 = 936\) is only three digits. The general idea is that to be divisible by several numbers at once is to be a multiple of their lcm, so questions about hitting all of them collapse into a single search through the multiples of one number.
Dividing marbles into groups of 4, 6, or 9 always leaves exactly 1 over. What is the smallest number of marbles greater than 1 that behaves this way?
Show solution
Every grouping leaves exactly one marble stranded, so if you lift that one marble away, what remains divides cleanly into groups of \(4\), of \(6\), and of \(9\) at the same time. That means the count minus \(1\) is a common multiple of all three, and the smallest such common multiple is their least common multiple. Building it, $$4 = 2^2, \qquad 6 = 2 \times 3, \qquad 9 = 3^2, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(4,6,9) = 2^2 \times 3^2 = 36.$$ So the count without its leftover is \(36\), and adding the single stranded marble back, $$36 + 1 = \boxed{37}.$$ Quick check, \(37 = 4 \times 9 + 1 = 6 \times 6 + 1 = 9 \times 4 + 1\), one left over every time, and it is the smallest count above \(1\) that does this, since the next common multiple \(72\) would give \(73\). The general idea is that a number leaving the same remainder under several divisors sits a fixed step above a common multiple, so its smallest value is the lcm plus that shared remainder.
Key idea
Same remainder \(r\) under several divisors: subtract \(r\) to get a common multiple, so the smallest answer is \(\operatorname{lcm}+r\). Remainder one less than the divisor each time: the number is one below a common multiple, so smallest is \(\operatorname{lcm}-1\).
A volunteer has 90 soap bars and 126 seed packets. Every kit holds the same counts with no leftovers. With \(\gcd(90,126)\), find the greatest number of identical kits she can build.
Show solution
The number of kits has to divide both \(90\) and \(126\) evenly, since the soap and the seeds each split across the kits with nothing left over. So the kit count is a common divisor of the two, and making as many kits as possible means taking the greatest common divisor. Read the shared fingerprint, $$90 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 5, \qquad 126 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 7.$$ Both carry one \(2\) and both carry \(3^2\), while \(5\) sits only in \(90\) and \(7\) only in \(126\), so those two cannot be shared. Multiplying the overlap, $$\gcd(90,126) = 2 \times 3^2 = \boxed{18}.$$ So she can build \(18\) kits, each holding \(90 \div 18 = 5\) bars of soap and \(126 \div 18 = 7\) packets of seeds. Quick check, \(18 \times 5 = 90\) and \(18 \times 7 = 126\), everything used up, and no larger number divides both, since the leftover primes \(5\) and \(7\) cannot join the shared part. The general idea is that splitting two quantities into the largest possible number of identical, complete shares is exactly a greatest common divisor in disguise.
Two pendulums swing every 21 and 28 seconds (\(21=3\times7\), \(28=2^2\times7\)). Find their lcm, then count how many times both start a swing together in the first 600 seconds.
Show solution
Each pendulum begins a swing at the multiples of its own period, so they begin together exactly at the numbers that are multiples of both \(21\) and \(28\). Those are precisely the multiples of the least common multiple, so first build it. $$21 = 3 \times 7, \qquad 28 = 2^2 \times 7, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(21,28) = 2^2 \times 3 \times 7 = 84.$$ The two pendulums therefore begin together every \(84\) seconds, at \(84, 168, 252,\) and so on. Now count how many of these land in the first \(600\) seconds, after the start. Stepping by \(84\), $$84,\ 168,\ 252,\ 336,\ 420,\ 504,\ 588,$$ and the next would be \(84 \times 8 = 672\), which overshoots \(600\). That is \(\boxed{7}\) shared swings. Quick check, \(84 \times 7 = 588 \le 600\) while \(84 \times 8 = 672 > 600\), so exactly seven multiples of \(84\) fit, matching the count from the previous lesson's method of dividing \(600\) by \(84\) and keeping the whole part. The general idea is that two repeating events coincide at the multiples of the lcm of their periods, so counting coincidences in a window becomes a count of multiples of a single number, exactly the multiple-counting from lesson 3.1.
Fun fact
Euclid computed gcd without factoring: divide the larger by the smaller, keep the remainder, repeat until the remainder is zero. The last nonzero remainder is the gcd. Computers still use this today.

The lesson rode on two fingerprints: overlap for gcd, union for lcm, tied by \(\gcd\times\operatorname{lcm}=a\times b\). Common multiples of a pair are the multiples of their lcm, folding back to 3.1.

Review problems

Review 1. Find \(\gcd(120, 144)\) using \(120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5\) and \(144 = 2^4 \times 3^2\). Take each shared prime to its lower power.
Show solution
The gcd keeps each shared prime at its lower power. $$120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5, \qquad 144 = 2^4 \times 3^2$$ For \(2\), the lower power is \(2^3\). For \(3\), the lower power is \(3^1\). The prime \(5\) appears only in \(120\), so it contributes nothing. Multiplying, $$\gcd(120,144) = 2^3 \times 3 = \boxed{24}.$$ Quick check, \(120 = 24 \times 5\) and \(144 = 24 \times 6\), both whole. The general idea is that the greatest common divisor is the product of the shared primes, each taken to the smaller of its two powers.
Review 2. Using \(120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5\) and \(144 = 2^4 \times 3^2\), find \(\operatorname{lcm}(120, 144)\) by taking each prime to its higher power.
Show solution
The lcm takes each prime to its higher power, including primes carried by just one number. $$120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5, \qquad 144 = 2^4 \times 3^2$$ For \(2\) the higher power is \(2^4\), for \(3\) it is \(3^2\), and the lone \(5\) from \(120\) must appear too. Multiplying, $$\operatorname{lcm}(120,144) = 2^4 \times 3^2 \times 5 = 16 \times 9 \times 5 = \boxed{720}.$$ Quick check, the gcd was \(24\), and \(24 \times 720 = 17280 = 120 \times 144\), so the gcd times the lcm rebuilds the product, just as it should. The general idea is that the lcm gathers every prime at its highest power, the mirror of the gcd taking the lowest.
Review 3. With \(49 = 7^2\) and \(60 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5\), do these numbers share any prime factor? Report their greatest common divisor.
Show solution
Set the fingerprints side by side, \(49 = 7^2\) and \(60 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5\). They share no prime at all, since \(49\) is made only of \(7\) and \(60\) carries none. With nothing in the overlap, the only common divisor is \(\boxed{1}\), so the numbers are coprime. Quick check, the divisors of \(49\) are \(1, 7, 49\), and none of \(7\) or \(49\) divides \(60\), leaving only \(1\). The general idea is that two numbers with no shared prime are coprime, and their greatest common divisor is always \(1\).
Review 4. Two numbers have \(\gcd = 9\) and \(\operatorname{lcm} = 270\). One number is 45. Find the other.
Show solution
For any pair, \(\gcd \times \operatorname{lcm} = a \times b\), so the product of the two numbers is \(9 \times 270 = 2430\). Dividing out the known number, $$\text{other} = \frac{2430}{45} = \boxed{54}.$$ Quick check, \(45 = 3^2 \times 5\) and \(54 = 2 \times 3^3\) share \(3^2 = 9\) as their gcd, and their lcm is \(2 \times 3^3 \times 5 = 270\), matching both facts. The general idea is that the gcd and lcm of a pair always multiply back to the pair's product, so any one of the four numbers can be recovered from the other three.
Review 5. Two ferries leave together and return every 16 and 24 minutes. How many minutes until both depart together again?
Show solution
The ferries leave together at the common multiples of \(16\) and \(24\), and the first is their least common multiple. $$16 = 2^4, \qquad 24 = 2^3 \times 3, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(16,24) = 2^4 \times 3 = \boxed{48}.$$ So they next depart together \(48\) minutes after noon. Quick check, \(48 = 16 \times 3 = 24 \times 2\), both whole. The general idea is that two repeating departures first coincide at the least common multiple of their periods.
Review 6. Stacking badges leaves 1 over for groups of 5, 6, or 8. What is the smallest badge count greater than 1 with this property?
Show solution
Each stacking leaves exactly one badge over, so the count minus \(1\) divides evenly by \(5\), \(6\), and \(8\) at once, making it a common multiple of the three. The smallest such common multiple is their lcm. $$5 = 5, \qquad 6 = 2 \times 3, \qquad 8 = 2^3, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(5,6,8) = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5 = 120.$$ Adding back the single leftover badge, \(120 + 1 = \boxed{121}\). Quick check, \(121 = 5 \times 24 + 1 = 6 \times 20 + 1 = 8 \times 15 + 1\), one over every time. The general idea is that a fixed remainder under several divisors places the number a fixed step above a common multiple, so the smallest is the lcm plus that remainder.
Review 7. Two readings of two fingerprints: gcd takes each shared prime to its lower power; lcm takes every prime to its higher power. They satisfy \(\gcd\times\operatorname{lcm}=a\times b\). Common multiples are multiples of the lcm.
Review 8. Two rhythms of 9 and 12 beats loop together. Find the largest common multiple of 9 and 12 below 300.
Show solution
A loop length that is a whole number of both rhythms is a common multiple of \(9\) and \(12\), and every common multiple is a multiple of their least common multiple. $$9 = 3^2, \qquad 12 = 2^2 \times 3, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(9,12) = 2^2 \times 3^2 = 36.$$ So the allowed lengths are the multiples of \(36\). Dividing \(300 \div 36 \approx 8.3\), the largest whole number of steps that stays under \(300\) is \(8\), giving \(36 \times 8 = \boxed{288}\). Quick check, \(288 = 9 \times 32 = 12 \times 24\), both whole, and the next multiple \(36 \times 9 = 324\) sails past \(300\). The general idea is that the common multiples of two numbers are exactly the multiples of their lcm, so finding the largest one under a cap is the multiple-hunting of lesson 3.1 applied to that single lcm.
Review 9. A poster shop prints only in runs divisible by 4, 5, and 6 at once. Find \(\operatorname{lcm}(4,5,6)\) and its smallest three-digit multiple.
Show solution
A run divisible by all three is a common multiple of \(4\), \(5\), and \(6\), hence a multiple of their lcm. $$4 = 2^2, \qquad 5 = 5, \qquad 6 = 2 \times 3, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(4,5,6) = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5 = 60.$$ The multiples of \(60\) are \(60, 120, 180, \ldots\), and the smallest with three digits is \(\boxed{120}\), since \(60\) itself has only two. Quick check, \(120 = 4 \times 30 = 5 \times 24 = 6 \times 20\), all whole. The general idea is that being divisible by several numbers at once means being a multiple of their lcm, turning the question into a single search through that lcm's multiples.
Review 10. Two lights flash every 10 and 15 seconds. In 300 seconds after the start, how many times do both flash together?
Show solution
The lights coincide at the common multiples of \(10\) and \(15\), which are the multiples of their least common multiple. $$10 = 2 \times 5, \qquad 15 = 3 \times 5, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(10,15) = 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 30.$$ So they flash together every \(30\) seconds. In the \(300\) seconds after the start, the count of multiples of \(30\) is \(300 \div 30 = \boxed{10}\), landing at \(30, 60, 90,\) and on up to \(300\). Quick check, \(30 \times 10 = 300\) sits right at the end of the span, so all ten coincidences fit. The general idea is that the coincidences of two rhythms are the multiples of their lcm, so counting them in a window is a plain count of multiples.
Review 11. A crowd leaves 1 person over for teams of 2, 3, 4, or 5. What is the smallest crowd greater than 1 with this property?
Show solution
Every grouping strands exactly one person, so the crowd minus \(1\) divides evenly by \(2\), \(3\), \(4\), and \(5\) at once, making it a common multiple of all four. The smallest is their lcm. $$2,\ 3,\ 4 = 2^2,\ 5, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(2,3,4,5) = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5 = 60.$$ Adding back the leftover person, \(60 + 1 = \boxed{61}\). Quick check, \(61\) leaves a remainder of \(1\) when divided by \(2\), \(3\), \(4\), and \(5\) alike. The general idea is that a single shared remainder sets the number one step above the lcm, so the smallest such number is the lcm plus that remainder.
Review 12. A wall is 132 cm by 198 cm. Using \(132 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 11\) and \(198 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 11\), find the side length of the largest square tile that fits exactly.
Show solution
A square tile of side \(s\) tiles the wall exactly only when \(s\) divides both \(132\) and \(198\) evenly, so the side must be a common divisor, and the largest tile uses the greatest common divisor. $$132 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 11, \qquad 198 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 11$$ Taking each shared prime to its lower power, \(2\) appears as \(2^1\), \(3\) as \(3^1\), and \(11\) as \(11^1\). Multiplying, $$\gcd(132,198) = 2 \times 3 \times 11 = \boxed{66}.$$ So the largest square tile is \(66\) centimeters on a side, fitting \(132 \div 66 = 2\) across and \(198 \div 66 = 3\) up. Quick check, \(66 \times 2 = 132\) and \(66 \times 3 = 198\), exact in both directions. The general idea is that the largest square that tiles a rectangle with whole sides is the greatest common divisor of the two side lengths.
Review 13. A number leaves remainder 3 when divided by 4, remainder 5 when divided by 6, and remainder 7 when divided by 8. Notice each remainder is one less than its divisor. What is the smallest such number?
Show solution
Look at how each remainder sits against its divisor. The remainder \(3\) is one less than \(4\), the remainder \(5\) is one less than \(6\), and the remainder \(7\) is one less than \(8\). So in every case the number falls one short of dividing cleanly, which means adding \(1\) would make it divide evenly by \(4\), \(6\), and \(8\) all at once. That makes the number one less than a common multiple, and the smallest comes from the lcm. $$4 = 2^2, \qquad 6 = 2 \times 3, \qquad 8 = 2^3, \qquad \operatorname{lcm}(4,6,8) = 2^3 \times 3 = 24.$$ So the number is \(24 - 1 = \boxed{23}\). Quick check, \(23 = 4 \times 5 + 3 = 6 \times 3 + 5 = 8 \times 2 + 7\), the three remainders exactly as asked. The general idea is that when every remainder is one short of its divisor, the number sits one below a common multiple, so the smallest is the lcm minus one.
Review 14. GCD and LCM are the engines of fractions: reducing divides by the gcd; adding different-denominator fractions uses the lcm as common denominator. Master these now and fraction work becomes routine.
