Prealgebra · Lesson 10.6

Mean, Median, and Mode

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Probability gave one number for how likely something is. A whole list of numbers deserves the same treatment, one number that stands in for the crowd. There are three natural ways to name the center of a data set, and each answers a slightly different question. The mean shares everything out fairly, the median finds the value sitting in the middle, and the mode picks the one that shows up most.

Five aquarium tanks along a wall hold \(12\), \(7\), \(15\), \(9\), and \(12\) guppies. A keeper scoops guppies from tank to tank until every tank holds the same number, with none added and none removed overall. How many guppies end up in each tank? Give the number.
Show solution
The keeper only moves guppies around, so the total never changes. Add the five tanks to find how many guppies there are in all, giving \(12 + 7 + 15 + 9 + 12 = 55\). Now share those \(55\) guppies equally among the \(5\) tanks, so each tank gets $$\frac{55}{5} = \boxed{11}.$$ Every tank ends up holding \(11\) guppies. That equal share is exactly the mean of the original counts, the number you would get if you leveled them all out.
Key idea
The mean. The mean (or average) of a list is the total of the values divided by how many there are, $$\text{mean}=\frac{\text{sum of the values}}{\text{how many values}}.$$ Think of it as the fair share. Pool everything together, then split it evenly among the values, and the mean is the amount each one gets. It is the single level everything settles to, so a list where nobody has more or less than anyone else already sits at its mean.
A kite flyer records how many minutes her kite stayed up on four windy afternoons: \(18\), \(25\), \(22\), and \(30\) minutes. What is the mean number of minutes the kite stayed up per afternoon? Give your answer as a decimal.
Show solution
The mean is the sum of the values divided by how many there are. Add the four recorded times, \(18 + 25 + 22 + 30 = 95\), then divide by the \(4\) afternoons, giving $$\text{mean} = \frac{95}{4} = \boxed{23.75}.$$ Notice that \(23.75\) is not one of the recorded times. The mean is a balancing point for the whole set, so it can land between the values rather than on any of them. The kite stayed up a mean of \(23.75\) minutes per afternoon.
Key idea
Recovering a total. The mean does not have to be one of your actual values. The \(23.75\) minutes was never a single reading on any day, it is just where the numbers balance. And the formula runs backwards. Since \(\text{mean} = \text{sum} \div \text{count}\), you can rearrange to get $$\text{sum} = \text{mean} \times \text{count}.$$ So knowing the mean and how many values there are tells you the total, even without seeing the values. That reverse move is exactly what lets you recover one missing value when you know everything else, which is the next problem.
A bakery records how many loaves it sells each day from Monday through Friday. On four of the days it sold \(40\), \(52\), \(38\), and \(61\) loaves, and the five-day average came out to exactly \(49\) loaves. How many loaves were sold on the fifth day? Give the number.
Show solution
Work backward from the mean. The average of the five days is the total divided by \(5\), so the total number of loaves must be $$49 \times 5 = 245.$$ Now add the four days you already know, giving \(40 + 52 + 38 + 61 = 191\). The missing day is whatever brings the running sum up to \(245\), so subtract, $$245 - 191 = \boxed{54}.$$ The bakery sold \(54\) loaves on the fifth day.
A coding club has \(12\) beginners who averaged \(30\) points on a puzzle, and \(8\) advanced members who averaged \(55\) points. What is the average score of all \(20\) members together? It is tempting to just average \(30\) and \(55\), but that ignores how many people are in each group, so think about the total points first. Give the number.
Show solution
Use the weighted mean, which means you rebuild the total points and divide by the total number of people instead of averaging the two group averages. Each group's total is its average times its size, so the beginners scored \(12 \times 30 = 360\) points and the advanced members scored \(8 \times 55 = 440\) points. Together all \(20\) members scored \(360 + 440 = 800\) points, and the average is that total divided by \(20\), $$\frac{12 \times 30 + 8 \times 55}{20} = \frac{800}{20} = \boxed{40}.$$ Notice this is not \(42.5\), the plain average of \(30\) and \(55\). The groups have different sizes, and since there are more beginners than advanced members, their lower score carries more weight and pulls the combined average below the midpoint. The average score of all \(20\) members is \(40\).
Key idea
Weight by size. To combine two groups into one mean, you cannot just average the two averages, unless the groups are the same size. Instead, rebuild the totals. Each group's mean times its own count gives that group's total, and adding those totals and dividing by everybody counted gives the combined mean, $$\text{mean}=\frac{\text{sum of every group total}}{\text{everybody counted}}.$$ The bigger group has more values pulling on the total, so the combined mean drifts toward that group's own average. A class of \(30\) outvotes a class of \(5\).
Seven kids skip stones across a pond and count the skips: \(4, 9, 6, 3, 8, 7, 11\). The median is the middle score once the list is sorted from smallest to largest. What is the median number of skips? Give the number.
Show solution
The median is the middle value, but middle only makes sense once the numbers are in order, so sort first. Sorting \(4, 9, 6, 3, 8, 7, 11\) from smallest to largest gives $$3,\ 4,\ 6,\ \underline{7},\ 8,\ 9,\ 11.$$ There are \(7\) values, and the middle one is the \(4\)th, with three values below it \((3, 4, 6)\) and three above it \((8, 9, 11)\). That balance is what makes it the center. So the median number of skips is \(\boxed{7}\).
Key idea
The median. The median is the middle value once the list is written in order, so order the numbers first, always. When the count is odd there is a single value sitting dead center, and that lone value is the median. When the count is even there are two values in the middle, and the median is their mean, $$\text{median} = \frac{\text{lower middle} + \text{upper middle}}{2}.$$ So just like the mean, the median need not be one of the numbers in the list.
Six friends compare how many books they read over the summer: \(5, 12, 8, 3, 9, 14\). What is the median number of books? Give your answer as a decimal.
Show solution
The median is the middle of the ordered data, so first sort the counts from smallest to largest, giving \(3, 5, 8, 9, 12, 14\). There are six values, an even count, so no single number sits in the middle. The two middle values are the third and fourth, \(8\) and \(9\), and the median is their mean, $$\frac{8 + 9}{2} = \frac{17}{2} = \boxed{8.5}.$$ So the median number of books read is \(8.5\).
A five-person startup pays its four employees \(42\), \(48\), \(45\), and \(51\) thousand dollars a year, while the founder earns \(314\) thousand. By how many thousand dollars does the mean salary exceed the median salary? Give the number (of thousand dollars).
Show solution
Find the mean and the median, then take the difference. The mean adds all five salaries and divides by \(5\), $$\text{mean} = \frac{42 + 48 + 45 + 51 + 314}{5} = \frac{500}{5} = 100.$$ For the median, order the salaries as \(42, 45, 48, 51, 314\) and take the middle one, giving \(48\). The gap between them is $$100 - 48 = \boxed{52}.$$ So the mean salary sits \(52\) thousand dollars above the median. The four ordinary salaries pin the median near the middle of the pack, but the founder's \(314\) thousand yanks the mean far upward, which is exactly how one lone large value distorts an average.
Key idea
Outliers and robustness. An outlier is one value sitting far from the rest, and the two measures react to it very differently. The median only cares about position, the middle slot, so an outlier barely nudges it. The mean feels every value's size, so a single giant value drags it toward itself. That is why for lopsided data like incomes, home prices, or a handful of enormous entries, the median is the more honest "typical" number. When the data is fairly even, no value pulls hard on the mean, and the mean and median land almost on top of each other.
A jacket shop records the sizes it sold today: \(6, 8, 8, 10, 8, 12, 8, 10, 6, 8\). They want to reorder the most of whichever size sells best, so they need the size that appears most often in the list. Which size should they reorder the most of? Give the size.
Show solution
The size to reorder most is the mode, the value that appears most often. Tally each size in \(6, 8, 8, 10, 8, 12, 8, 10, 6, 8\). Size \(6\) shows up twice, size \(10\) shows up twice, and size \(12\) shows up once. Size \(8\) shows up five times, which is more than any other size, giving $$\text{mode} = \boxed{8}.$$ So the shop should reorder the most of size \(8\), since that is the size that sells most often.
Key idea
The mode. The mode is the value that appears most often. The mean and median care about size and position, but the mode only cares about frequency, which is what makes it the natural "typical" for choices and categories. A shoe store stocks the most common size, not the average one, and a survey reports the most frequent answer. Frequency is the whole story, so a list where every value is different has no mode at all, and a list where two values tie for most frequent has two modes.
A list can have more than one mode. A survey records these numbers: \(2, 5, 2, 7, 5, 9, 5, 2\). Both \(2\) and \(5\) appear three times, which is more often than any other value. A value counts as a mode when it ties for the most frequent. How many values are modes?
Show solution
Count how often each value appears. The value \(2\) shows up three times, the value \(5\) shows up three times, the value \(7\) once, and the value \(9\) once. The most frequent count is three, and two different values reach it, so both \(2\) and \(5\) are modes. That is what "multimodal" means, a list with more than one mode. Counting the values that tie for most frequent gives $$\boxed{2}.$$ There are two modes.
A gym sets your membership tier by the median of your nine monthly visit counts. Seven months in, your counts are \(20, 8, 16, 5, 22, 12, 16\). You have two months left, and you can visit at most \(30\) times in a month. What is the highest median you can reach across all nine months? Give the number.
Show solution
The median of nine values is the \(5\)th value once they are sorted, so the goal is to make that \(5\)th slot as large as possible. Sort the seven known counts, giving $$5,\ 8,\ 12,\ 16,\ 16,\ 20,\ 22.$$ Now add the two remaining months. Each can be at most \(30\), and \(30\) is bigger than every number already on the list, so both new values land at the top no matter what you pick. Adding two numbers only at the high end shifts nothing below the middle, so the \(5\)th of the nine sorted values stays put at the \(5\)th of the original seven, which is \(16\). Pushing the new months higher just makes the top of the list bigger, never the middle, so the best you can do is $$\boxed{16}.$$ The highest median you can reach is \(16\).
A gymnast's five routine scores average \(9.2\). She has one routine left, and she wants her average across all six scores to stay exactly \(9.2\). What score must she earn on the sixth routine? Give your answer as a decimal.
Show solution
Use the fact that the mean is the total divided by the count. The five scores average \(9.2\), so their total is \(5 \times 9.2 = 46\). Call the sixth score \(x\). For the six-score average to stay \(9.2\), the new total \(46 + x\) divided by \(6\) must equal \(9.2\), giving $$\frac{46 + x}{6} = 9.2.$$ Multiply both sides by \(6\), so \(46 + x = 55.2\), and subtract \(46\) to get \(x = 9.2\). This matches the shortcut worth remembering. Adding one more value that equals the current mean leaves the mean exactly where it was, since you are adding one more average-sized share to an average-sized pile. She must score $$\boxed{9.2}.$$
Key idea
The balancing point. The mean is the balance point of the data. Measure how far each value sits above or below the mean, and those gaps cancel out exactly, so they always sum to zero. That one fact explains a lot. Adding a new value equal to the mean changes nothing, since it adds no gap to either side. A value above the mean tips the balance up, and one below tips it down, $$\text{sum of gaps above} = \text{sum of gaps below}.$$ So a single far-off outlier, with a huge gap on one side, can drag the mean a surprising distance to keep the two sides even.
A staircase has an LED on every step. The steps show \(7, 11, 15, 19,\) and so on, going up by \(4\) each step, with the top step showing \(43\). What is the mean of all the numbers shown? Give the number.
Show solution
The numbers are evenly spaced, each one \(4\) above the one before, so a shortcut works. Pair them from the ends inward, giving \(7\) with \(43\), then \(11\) with \(39\), then \(15\) with \(35\), and each pair sums to \(50\). Because the pairs are all equal, the values are perfectly balanced around one center, so the mean equals the median equals the average of the first and last term. That average is $$\frac{7 + 43}{2} = \frac{50}{2} = \boxed{25}.$$ No full sum needed. For any evenly spaced list, the mean is just the average of the two ends, which here is \(25\).
Key idea
Evenly spaced lists. When the values step up by a constant amount, the list is symmetric about its center. Each value below the middle is matched by one an equal distance above it, so those pairs balance out and the mean lands exactly at the median. Both equal the average of the first and last value, $$\text{mean}=\frac{\text{first}+\text{last}}{2}.$$ You get the center from just the two ends, with no need to add everything up. This is why a run of consecutive whole numbers, or any list with a fixed gap, gives up its mean at a glance.
A set of five positive whole numbers has a mean of \(16\), a median of \(15\), and \(15\) is its only mode. What is the greatest value the largest number in the set could be? Give the number.
Show solution
The mean tells you the sum. Five numbers averaging \(16\) add up to \(5 \times 16 = 80\). Sort the set from smallest to largest and fill the five slots to push the top number as high as possible. The median is the 3rd value, so slot 3 is \(15\). For \(15\) to be the only mode it must appear at least twice and more often than anything else, so put another \(15\) in slot 4. To make the largest number big, shrink the first two slots as much as the rules allow. They must be positive whole numbers, and they cannot both be \(15\) or a repeated value that ties \(15\) for mode, so the smallest safe choice is \(1\) and \(2\). That leaves the largest number as the rest of the total, $$80 - (1 + 2 + 15 + 15) = \boxed{47}.$$ Checking, the set \(\{1, 2, 15, 15, 47\}\) has sum \(80\) so mean \(16\), middle value \(15\) so median \(15\), and \(15\) is the only repeat so the only mode. The greatest the largest number can be is \(47\).
Fun fact
The word "average" traces back through Old French and Italian to the Arabic "awariya", meaning damaged goods. When a storm ruined part of a ship's cargo, the loss was shared out evenly among all the merchants, and that fair split of a total is exactly what a mean does today.
Key idea
Three summaries, one question each. The mean is the fair share, the total split evenly across the count, so it feels every value and gets tugged hard by an outlier. The median is the middle of the ordered list, and because it only cares about position it stays honest when the data is lopsided. The mode is the most common value, the right tool for categories and for deciding what is typical to stock or expect. Pick the summary that answers the question you actually asked. And when the mean and median disagree by a lot, do not just average them, that gap is the data telling you it is skewed, with a long tail dragging the mean away from the middle.

Review problems

Review 1. Find the mean of the four numbers \(14\), \(9\), \(22\), and \(15\). Give the number.
Show solution
The mean is the total of the values divided by how many values there are. First add them, \(14 + 9 + 22 + 15 = 60\). There are \(4\) numbers, so divide the total by \(4\), $$\text{mean} = \frac{60}{4} = \boxed{15}.$$ The mean of the four numbers is \(15\).
Review 2. Find the median of the data set \(7, 2, 9, 4, 6\). Give the number.
Show solution
The median is the middle value once the data is in order, so start by sorting the five numbers from smallest to largest, giving \(2, 4, 6, 7, 9\). With five values there is a single middle position, the third one, with two numbers below it and two above it. $$2,\ 4,\ \boxed{6},\ 7,\ 9$$ The value in that center spot is \(6\), so the median is \(6\).
Review 3. Find the mode of this data set: \(3, 7, 3, 9, 3, 7, 5\). Give the number.
Show solution
The mode is the value that occurs most frequently, so count how often each number appears. Tallying the seven values gives \(3\) three times, \(7\) twice, and \(9\) and \(5\) once each. The winner is clear, since $$3 \text{ appears } 3 \text{ times},$$ more than any other value. The mode is \(\boxed{3}\).
Review 4. Find the mean of the four numbers \(10\), \(13\), \(14\), and \(10\). Give your answer as a decimal.
Show solution
The mean is the total of the values divided by how many there are. Adding the four numbers gives \(10 + 13 + 14 + 10 = 47\), and there are \(4\) of them, so divide the total by \(4\), $$\text{mean} = \frac{47}{4} = \boxed{11.75}.$$ The mean of the four numbers is \(11.75\).
Review 5. A weather station takes five daily temperature readings, and their mean is \(20\) degrees. Four of the readings are \(15\), \(22\), \(18\), and \(25\). What is the fifth reading? Give the number.
Show solution
Work backward from the mean. Since the mean of five readings is \(20\), the five readings together must add to \(5 \times 20 = 100\). The four known readings sum to \(15 + 22 + 18 + 25 = 80\), so the fifth reading is whatever fills the gap up to \(100\), giving $$100 - 80 = \boxed{20}.$$ The fifth reading is \(20\) degrees.
Review 6. Find the median of these six numbers: \(12, 4, 8, 20, 6, 10\). Give the number.
Review 7. On a test, a group of \(10\) students averaged \(80\) points and a separate group of \(15\) students averaged \(90\) points. What is the average score of all \(25\) students combined? Give the number.
Show solution
This is a weighted mean, so you cannot just average \(80\) and \(90\), because the two groups are different sizes. Instead, rebuild the total score of each group from its average. The \(10\) students who averaged \(80\) scored \(10 \times 80 = 800\) points all together, and the \(15\) students who averaged \(90\) scored \(15 \times 90 = 1350\) points. Add those to get the total for everyone, then divide by all \(25\) students, giving $$\frac{800 + 1350}{25} = \frac{2150}{25} = \boxed{86}.$$ The combined average is \(86\), which sits closer to \(90\) than to \(80\) because the larger group pulls the mean toward its score.
Review 8. A data set has the values \(8\), \(10\), \(9\), \(11\), and \(62\). The mean of these five numbers is \(20\), and the median is \(10\). By how much does the mean exceed the median? Give the number.
Show solution
Subtract the median from the mean to measure how far apart the two centers sit. The mean of the five values is \(\frac{8 + 10 + 9 + 11 + 62}{5} = \frac{100}{5} = 20\), and sorting the list to \(8, 9, 10, 11, 62\) puts \(10\) in the middle, so the median is \(10\). The single value \(62\) is an outlier that pulls the mean far above the typical value while leaving the median untouched, which is exactly why the two differ. Taking the difference gives $$20 - 10 = \boxed{10}.$$ The mean exceeds the median by \(10\).
Review 9. The ten multiples of \(3\) from \(3\) up to \(30\) are \(3, 6, 9, \dots, 30\), and they add up to \(165\). Remove one number so that the nine numbers left have a mean of \(16\). Which number is removed? Give the number.
Show solution
The mean of the nine remaining numbers is their sum divided by \(9\), so if that mean is \(16\), the nine numbers must add to \(9 \times 16 = 144\). All ten numbers together add to \(165\), so the removed number is the gap between those two totals, giving $$165 - 144 = \boxed{21}.$$ Since \(21\) is one of the multiples of \(3\) in the list, removing it drops the sum from \(165\) to \(144\) and leaves the nine numbers averaging exactly \(16\). The number removed is \(21\).
Review 10. Six different positive whole numbers have a mean of \(10\), so they add up to \(60\). What is the largest that any single one of them can be? Give the number.
Show solution
The six numbers are fixed to sum to \(60\), since a mean of \(10\) across six values means \(6 \times 10 = 60\). To push one value as high as possible, make the other five as small as they can be. They have to be different positive whole numbers, so the smallest five distinct choices are \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5\), which add to \(15\). Whatever is left over goes to the sixth number, giving $$60 - 15 = \boxed{45}.$$ The largest any single one of them can be is \(45\).
Review 11. The mean of five numbers is \(12\). A sixth number is added, and the mean of all six numbers rises to \(14\). What is the sixth number? Give the number.
Show solution
Turn each mean back into a total by multiplying the mean by how many numbers it covers. The first five numbers have mean \(12\), so their total is \(5 \times 12 = 60\). After the sixth number is added, all six have mean \(14\), so their total is \(6 \times 14 = 84\). The sixth number is the difference between the new total and the old one, giving $$84 - 60 = \boxed{24}.$$ So the sixth number is \(24\).
Review 12. A shoe shop sold these sizes over one afternoon: \(7, 8, 8, 9, 8, 10, 7, 8\). The owner wants to restock the most common size, since that is what customers buy the most. Which size should the shop restock the most of? Give the size.
Show solution
The most common value in a data set is the mode, so restocking the size that sold most means finding the mode. Tally each size: \(7\) appears twice, \(9\) appears once, \(10\) appears once, and \(8\) appears in four of the eight sales. No other size comes close, so the mode is $$\boxed{8}.$$ The shop should restock size \(8\), because it was the size customers bought most often.
Review 13. Find the average of all the whole numbers from \(41\) to \(59\), including both ends. Give the number.
Show solution
The numbers \(41, 42, 43, \dots, 59\) are evenly spaced, one apart, so they are perfectly balanced around their center. For any evenly spaced list the mean equals the average of the first and last value, which saves you from adding all nineteen numbers. Averaging the two ends gives $$\frac{41+59}{2}=\frac{100}{2}=\boxed{50}.$$ The average of the whole numbers from \(41\) to \(59\) is \(50\).
Review 14. A set of five positive whole numbers has a mean of \(6\), a median of \(6\), and \(8\) as its only mode. What is the difference between the largest and smallest values in the set? Give the number.
Show solution
Work from the three clues one at a time. A mean of \(6\) across five numbers means they add to \(5 \times 6 = 30\). A median of \(6\) means the middle value, the third when the numbers are sorted, equals \(6\). For \(8\) to be the only mode it must appear more than any other value, so the two largest slots are both \(8\), giving \(6, 8, 8\) for the top three and a running total of \(22\). That leaves the two smallest numbers to add to \(30 - 22 = 8\), and they must be different from each other and from \(8\) so no value ties \(8\) for most frequent. The pair \(3\) and \(5\) works, so the set is \(3, 5, 6, 8, 8\). The largest value is \(8\) and the smallest is \(3\), giving $$8 - 3 = \boxed{5}.$$ The difference between the largest and smallest values is \(5\).
