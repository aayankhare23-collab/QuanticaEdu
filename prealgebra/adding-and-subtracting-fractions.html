Prealgebra · Lesson 4.6

Adding and Subtracting Fractions

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A robot at \(0\) steps a third of the way to \(1\), then half of the way, and rolls to a stop past the middle. It is tempting to stack the two fractions, top on top and bottom on bottom, and read off the landing spot, but the two steps are not the same length and that matters. Adding fractions is just stepping right along the line and subtracting is stepping left, so the whole lesson is one question. When do two steps stack cleanly, and what do you do when they do not?

A counter on a strip notched into ninths slides right \(2\) notches, then \(5\) more. Every notch is the same width. What is \(\tfrac{2}{9}+\tfrac{5}{9}\), written as a fraction?
Show solution
Because the strip is cut into 9 equal notches, each notch is exactly \(\tfrac{1}{9}\) of the whole strip. That number, the 9 on the bottom, is the name of the step size, and it does not change as the counter moves. The first slide of 2 notches covers \(\tfrac{2}{9}\), which is just 2 copies of that one ninth-sized step. The second slide of 5 notches covers \(\tfrac{5}{9}\), another 5 copies of the exact same step. Since every step is the same size, adding the two slides is nothing more than counting all the notches the counter crossed, \(2+5=7\) of them. Seven equal ninth-sized notches from 0 lands the counter on \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) of the strip. The step size 9 stays put on the bottom, and only the count of steps, 7, lands on top. So \(\tfrac{2}{9}+\tfrac{5}{9}=\tfrac{2+5}{9}=\tfrac{7}{9}\), which is already in simplest form since 7 and 9 share no common factor. \(\boxed{7/9}\)
A marker at \(\tfrac{7}{8}\) steps left by \(\tfrac{3}{8}\). Count remaining eighth-notches, then reduce. What is \(\tfrac{7}{8}-\tfrac{3}{8}\) in simplest form?
Show solution
Because both fractions are measured in the same eighth-sized notches, the bottom number stays put and only the count of notches changes. You begin at 7 notches and step left by 3, so \(7-3=4\) notches are left. That gives \(\tfrac{7}{8}-\tfrac{3}{8}=\tfrac{4}{8}\). Now comes the habit worth building. The number \(\tfrac{4}{8}\) is a real answer, but its top and bottom still share a factor, since 4 divides into both. Dividing the top and bottom by 4 gives \(\tfrac{4}{8}=\tfrac{4\div 4}{8\div 4}=\tfrac{1}{2}\). If you glance back at the bench, landing on the fourth notch out of eight is exactly the halfway point, which is a nice check that \(\tfrac{4}{8}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) name the same spot. In simplest form the marker rests at \(\boxed{1/2}\).
Key idea
Adding and subtracting with equal denominators. When two fractions are made of the very same size piece, the bottom number is just the name of that piece, telling you how big one piece is and nothing more. That name does not change when you gather more pieces or hand some back, so you add or subtract only the top numbers, the count of pieces, and copy the bottom down exactly as it was. You never add the bottoms, because you are totaling pieces, not names. After you land, check whether the count and the size still share a common factor, since a sum or difference is often not in simplest form yet. That is how \(\tfrac{7}{8}-\tfrac{3}{8}\) quietly becomes \(\tfrac{1}{2}\).
A friend adds \(\tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{2}{5}\) (tops and bottoms). But \(\tfrac{2}{5}<\tfrac{1}{2}\), less than one step. Find the correct sum \(\tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{1}{2}\) over the lcd.
Show solution
The friend's shortcut fails because \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) are pieces of different sizes, and you can only add counts of the same size piece. So the first job is to make the two steps match. The lcd of \(2\) and \(3\) is \(6\), which means both moves can be measured in sixths. Rewrite each one over \(6\). For the third, multiply top and bottom by \(2\): \(\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{2}{6}\). For the half, multiply top and bottom by \(3\): \(\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{3}{6}\). Now the robot's two moves are \(2\) sixths and then \(3\) sixths, all the same size step. Counting them together gives \(2+3=5\) of those sixths, so the total is $$\tfrac{2}{6}+\tfrac{3}{6}=\tfrac{5}{6}.$$ Check it against the picture. Since \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) is more than \(\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{3}{6}\), the robot does land to the right of halfway, just like the half-step move promised. And \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) is already in simplest form, because \(\gcd(5,6)=1\). \(\boxed{5/6}\)
Key idea
Never add the denominators. Adding the tops and adding the bottoms is a trap, because it pretends a half and a third are the same size when they are not, so the count it spits out means nothing. The bottom number is a size name, not a thing you count, so you never add it. To actually add two fractions, first rebuild both out of pieces that match, which is all a common denominator does. Once \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) are both rewritten as a number of equal sixths, you are back to plain counting.
A glider burns \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) then \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) of a charge. Find \(\operatorname{lcm}(4,6)\), rewrite, add. How much burned total? Write in simplest form.
Show solution
The two fractions have different bottoms, so before adding anything you need pieces of the same size. That is the whole reason for the lcd. The lcd of \(4\) and \(6\) is the smallest number both divide into, which is \(12\). Now do the build-up move from 4.4 on each fraction. For \(\tfrac{1}{4}\), since \(4\times 3=12\), multiply top and bottom by \(3\) to get \(\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{3}{12}\). For \(\tfrac{1}{6}\), since \(6\times 2=12\), multiply top and bottom by \(2\) to get \(\tfrac{1}{6}=\tfrac{2}{12}\). Both flights are now measured in twelfths, so the numerators are countable. The first hop is \(3\) twelfths and the second is \(2\) twelfths, which is \(3+2=5\) twelfths in total. That gives \(\tfrac{3}{12}+\tfrac{2}{12}=\tfrac{5}{12}\). Finally check for reducing. Since \(\gcd(5,12)=1\), the fraction is already in simplest form. So the two hops burned \(\boxed{5/12}\) of a charge.

Nothing about subtraction is new. If adding is a step right along the line, subtracting is a step left, and the rule about sizes does not care which way you walk. You still cannot count pieces until they match, so the same three moves carry you through a difference just as they did a sum. Match the sizes, count the pieces, simplify. The next problem asks for \(\tfrac{5}{6}-\tfrac{3}{8}\), so watch how little really changes once the denominators agree.

Timer is \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) used; shortcut saves \(\tfrac{3}{8}\). Find \(\operatorname{lcm}(6,8)\), rename, subtract. Find \(\tfrac{5}{6}-\tfrac{3}{8}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
The trouble is that a sixth of the ring and an eighth of the ring are different lengths, so the \(5\) and the \(3\) are counting pieces of different sizes. Subtraction only works once every piece is the same size, so first rebuild both fractions over a common denominator. The least common denominator of \(6\) and \(8\) is the smallest number both divide into. Walk the multiples of \(8\), which are \(8, 16, 24\), and \(24\) is the first one that \(6\) also reaches, so the lcd is \(24\). Now re-cut each fraction into twenty-fourths. Since \(6\times 4=24\), multiply top and bottom of \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) by \(4\) to get \(\tfrac{20}{24}\). Since \(8\times 3=24\), multiply top and bottom of \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) by \(3\) to get \(\tfrac{9}{24}\). With matching bottoms the subtraction is easy. You have \(20\) twenty-fourths used and you claw back \(9\) twenty-fourths, leaving \(20-9=11\) twenty-fourths over the same denominator, so \(\tfrac{20}{24}-\tfrac{9}{24}=\tfrac{11}{24}\). Last, check for simplifying. The number \(11\) is prime and does not divide \(24\), so \(\gcd(11,24)=1\) and the fraction is already in lowest terms. After the shortcut, the round is still \(\tfrac{11}{24}\) used. $$\boxed{11/24}$$
Key idea
Three-step rule. Adding and subtracting fractions is three moves in the same order every time. First make the pieces the same size by finding the lcd and renaming both fractions, because counting only works when every piece counts as the same amount. Once the bottoms match, add or subtract the top numbers and keep that shared bottom, since the size of a piece never changes when you count more or fewer of them. Then check whether the answer still shares a factor top and bottom, and reduce it if it does. Match the sizes, count, simplify, and the only genuinely new part is the easy counting step in the middle.
Mixer pours \(\tfrac{5}{12}\) then \(\tfrac{1}{4}\). The lcd is \(12\). Add the tops, then reduce, since the sum shares a factor. What fraction was poured in simplest form?
Show solution
Start by finding a common bottom. The denominators are \(12\) and \(4\), and \(12\) is already a multiple of \(4\), so the lcd is just \(12\). That means \(\tfrac{5}{12}\) needs no change at all. Only \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) gets renamed. Multiply its top and bottom by \(3\) to get \(\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{3}{12}\), the same amount measured in twelfths. Now both portions are in twelfths, so add the counts on top and keep the bottom. \(\tfrac{5}{12}+\tfrac{3}{12}=\tfrac{8}{12}\). This is a true total, but pause here. The top \(8\) and the bottom \(12\) are both even, and in fact both are divisible by \(4\). Their \(\gcd\) is \(4\), so the fraction is not in simplest form yet. Divide top and bottom by \(4\). \(\tfrac{8}{12}=\tfrac{8\div 4}{12\div 4}=\tfrac{2}{3}\). Now the top \(2\) and the bottom \(3\) share no common factor besides \(1\), so it is fully reduced. The mixer poured out \(\boxed{2/3}\) of the can.
Fun fact
The \(+\) sign appeared in European arithmetic in the late 1400s, likely from writing the Latin et ("and"). The fraction bar is older, from Arabic c. 1100s.
Dial reads \(\tfrac{3}{8}\); step left by \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) crosses \(0\). Common denominator, subtract in order, keep the sign. What is \(\tfrac{3}{8}-\tfrac{5}{6}\) in simplest form?
Show solution
Start by getting both fractions over one common bottom. The denominators are \(8\) and \(6\), and the least common denominator is \(24\), because \(24\) is the smallest number that both \(8\) and \(6\) divide into evenly. Rename each fraction. For \(\tfrac{3}{8}\), multiply top and bottom by \(3\) to get \(\tfrac{9}{24}\). For \(\tfrac{5}{6}\), multiply top and bottom by \(4\) to get \(\tfrac{20}{24}\). The subtraction becomes $$\frac{9}{24}-\frac{20}{24}.$$ Now both pieces are the same size, so you just subtract the top counts and keep the bottom. The order matters here. You are taking \(20\) twenty-fourths away from only \(9\) twenty-fourths, and you cannot, so the count drops below zero. \(9-20=-11\), which gives \(\tfrac{-11}{24}\). This is the dial walking left past \(0\) and stopping on the negative side, just like the points you placed left of \(0\) in 4.1. Last, check whether it simplifies. The number \(11\) is prime and \(24\) is not a multiple of \(11\), so \(\gcd(11,24)=1\) and the fraction is already in lowest terms. The reading lands at $$\boxed{-11/24}.$$
Key idea
Negative results. A difference between fractions does not have to land above zero. If you step left by more than you started with, you sail past \(0\) and the answer comes out negative, which is the same number line from 4.1 and changes nothing about how you work. You still match the sizes, subtract the counts, and keep the minus sign, so order matters and \(\tfrac{3}{8}-\tfrac{5}{6}\) is the opposite of \(\tfrac{5}{6}-\tfrac{3}{8}\). And whether you add or subtract, the combining step never checks whether its answer is tidy, so the last move is always to hunt for a shared factor and divide it out.
Spool holds \(4\) m; cut \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) m off. Rewrite \(4=\tfrac{12}{3}\), subtract. How much cord remains? Find \(4-\tfrac{2}{3}\) as a single fraction.
Show solution
The snippet is measured in thirds, but the spool is measured in whole meters, so the two amounts are not yet speaking the same language. The fix is to notice that \(4\) is already a fraction wearing a disguise, \(4=\tfrac{4}{1}\). To give it thirds, multiply the top and bottom by the same number \(3\), which does not change its value, only its appearance. $$4=\frac{4}{1}=\frac{4\times 3}{1\times 3}=\frac{12}{3}.$$ So four whole meters is exactly twelve thirds of a meter. That makes sense, each meter splits into 3 thirds, and \(4\times 3=12\). Now both amounts wear the same denominator, and the subtraction is just counting backward in thirds. $$\frac{12}{3}-\frac{2}{3}=\frac{12-2}{3}=\frac{10}{3}.$$ You start with 12 thirds, step back 2 thirds, and 10 thirds remain. Check whether it simplifies. The top is \(10\) and the bottom is \(3\), and since \(\gcd(10,3)=1\), the fraction is already in simplest form. The spool has \(\tfrac{10}{3}\) of a meter of cord left. $$\boxed{10/3}$$
Spring: \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) planted; path takes \(\tfrac{2}{3}\); \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) replanted. Find lcd for \(4,3,6\), combine in one sweep. What fraction ends up in use?
Show solution
The trap here is doing it in pairs, where it is easy to lose a sign halfway through. The cleaner move is to put everything over one common denominator first, then handle all three numerators together. Since \(4\), \(3\), and \(6\) all divide \(12\), use \(12\). Rewrite each fraction by scaling top and bottom by the same factor, so the value never changes. \(\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{9}{12}\), \(\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{8}{12}\), and \(\tfrac{1}{6}=\tfrac{2}{12}\). Now the whole expression sits over a single denominator and the signs travel with their numbers. $$\frac{3}{4}-\frac{2}{3}+\frac{1}{6}=\frac{9}{12}-\frac{8}{12}+\frac{2}{12}=\frac{9-8+2}{12}.$$ Sweep across the top in order. \(9-8=1\), then \(1+2=3\), giving \(\tfrac{3}{12}\). Both \(3\) and \(12\) share a factor of \(3\), so divide each by \(3\) to land on \(\tfrac{1}{4}\). So a quarter of the plot ends up in use, which makes sense, the path removed almost everything the spring planting added, and the summer bit nudged it back up a little. $$\boxed{1/4}$$
Rider: \(+\tfrac{7}{10}-\tfrac{1}{5}+\tfrac{1}{2}\). lcd for \(10,5,2\) is \(10\). Combine in one pass. What fraction of the loop is her total progress?
Show solution
The trap here is reaching for a fresh common denominator without checking whether you already have one. Look at \(10\), \(5\), and \(2\). Both \(5\) and \(2\) divide evenly into \(10\), so \(10\) is itself the least common denominator. No bigger number is needed. Now rewrite all three fractions over \(10\) in one move. The first term stays as \(\tfrac{7}{10}\). For the second, \(\tfrac{1}{5}=\tfrac{1\cdot 2}{5\cdot 2}=\tfrac{2}{10}\). For the third, \(\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{1\cdot 5}{2\cdot 5}=\tfrac{5}{10}\). The signed sum becomes $$\frac{7}{10}-\frac{2}{10}+\frac{5}{10}.$$ With a shared bottom, you combine the top numbers in a single pass, carrying each sign exactly as written, so \(7-2+5=10\). That gives \(\tfrac{10}{10}\), which is one whole. As a sanity check, the backtrack of \(\tfrac{2}{10}\) cancels part of the first stretch and the final \(\tfrac{5}{10}\) carries her the rest of the way, landing her right at the start point of a fresh loop. Her total progress is exactly one full loop, so the answer is \(\boxed{1}\).
Key idea
Summary. Every move in this lesson came from one sentence. You can only add or subtract pieces when they are the same size, and the denominator is just the name of that size. So if the sizes already match, you count, adding or subtracting the tops and keeping the bottom as it is. If they do not match, you make them match first by renaming both fractions over a common denominator, the same lcd you already know how to find, and then you count. A whole number joins in wearing the disguise \(\tfrac{n}{1}\), and three or more fractions all go over one shared bottom at once. Whatever you land on, keep the sign honest, even when a difference falls left of zero, and reduce so the answer wears its simplest name.

Review problems

Review 1. A counter on a sevenths number line steps right \(\tfrac{1}{7}\) then \(\tfrac{3}{7}\). Count the notches. Write \(\tfrac{1}{7}+\tfrac{3}{7}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
Both fractions are already measured in the same unit, sevenths, so no new common denominator is needed. The first step moves the counter \(1\) notch and the second step moves it \(3\) more, which is \(1+3=4\) notches from \(0\). Stack that over the shared bottom and you get \(\tfrac{1}{7}+\tfrac{3}{7}=\tfrac{1+3}{7}=\tfrac{4}{7}\). Now check for simplifying. The top is \(4\) and the bottom is \(7\), and \(\gcd(4,7)=1\), so nothing cancels and the fraction is already as simple as it gets. The counter lands at \(\boxed{4/7}\).
Review 2. A counter at \(\tfrac{9}{10}\) slides left by \(\tfrac{3}{10}\). Subtract and reduce. What is \(\tfrac{9}{10}-\tfrac{3}{10}\) in simplest form?
Show solution
The two fractions are already same size pieces, both counted in tenths, so the subtraction happens only on top. Nine tenths take away three tenths leaves six tenths, \(\tfrac{9}{10}-\tfrac{3}{10}=\tfrac{6}{10}\). Now reduce. The greatest common divisor of \(6\) and \(10\) is \(2\), so divide both by \(2\) to get \(\tfrac{6\div 2}{10\div 2}=\tfrac{3}{5}\). Since \(3\) and \(5\) share no factor bigger than \(1\), this is as low as it goes. \(\boxed{3/5}\)
Review 3. A classmate wrote \(\tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{2}{7}\) by stacking tops and bottoms. But \(\tfrac{2}{7}<\tfrac{1}{3}\), so a sum came out smaller than a part, which is impossible. Find the correct sum over the lcd, in simplest form.
Show solution
The classmate's mistake is adding tops and bottoms straight across, which treats thirds and fourths as if they were the same size. They are not, so the first job is to give both fractions a common denominator. The lcd of \(3\) and \(4\) is \(12\), since that is the smallest number both divide into. Rename each fraction over \(12\). For \(\tfrac{1}{3}\), multiply top and bottom by \(4\) to get \(\tfrac{4}{12}\). For \(\tfrac{1}{4}\), multiply top and bottom by \(3\) to get \(\tfrac{3}{12}\). Now both are counted in twelfths, so the pieces finally match. Add the numerators and keep the denominator, \(\tfrac{4}{12}+\tfrac{3}{12}=\tfrac{7}{12}\). Check whether it reduces. Since \(\gcd(7,12)=1\), the fraction is already in simplest form. And notice it passes the sanity test that fooled your classmate, \(\tfrac{7}{12}\) is larger than \(\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{4}{12}\), exactly as a sum should be. \(\boxed{7/12}\)
Review 4. "Sunset" blend: \(\tfrac{2}{5}\) mango, \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) pineapple. Find \(\operatorname{lcm}(5,3)\), rename, add. Total fruit share \(\tfrac{2}{5}+\tfrac{1}{3}\) in simplest form?
Show solution
The two fractions count different-sized pieces, so they cannot be added until they share a denominator. The least common denominator of \(5\) and \(3\) is \(15\). Rename each fraction over \(15\). For the mango share, \(\tfrac{2}{5}=\tfrac{2\times 3}{5\times 3}=\tfrac{6}{15}\). For the pineapple share, \(\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{1\times 5}{3\times 5}=\tfrac{5}{15}\). Now both count fifteenths, so add the numerators and keep the common denominator. \(\tfrac{6}{15}+\tfrac{5}{15}=\tfrac{11}{15}\). Since \(11\) is prime and does not divide \(15\), the fraction is already in lowest terms. So the Sunset blend is \(\boxed{11/15}\) fruit juice by volume.
Review 5. A candle is \(\tfrac{7}{8}\) of its original height; it burns \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) more. Find the lcd of \(8\) and \(3\), rename, subtract. What is \(\tfrac{7}{8}-\tfrac{1}{3}\) in simplest form?
Show solution
The two parts are measured against the same whole, the original height, so we can subtract them once they share a denominator. The denominators \(8\) and \(3\) have no common factor, so the lcd is just their product, \(8\times 3=24\). Rename each fraction over \(24\). \(\tfrac{7}{8}=\tfrac{7\times 3}{8\times 3}=\tfrac{21}{24}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{1\times 8}{3\times 8}=\tfrac{8}{24}\). Now subtract the numerators over the common denominator. \(\tfrac{21}{24}-\tfrac{8}{24}=\tfrac{13}{24}\). Since \(13\) is prime and does not divide \(24\), the fraction is already in lowest terms. So the candle keeps \(\boxed{13/24}\) of its original height.
Review 6. A bottle is \(\tfrac{7}{10}\) full; the hiker drinks \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) of a bottle. Find the lcd, rename, subtract. What fraction of a bottle is left, in simplest form?
Show solution
The two amounts are measured in different sized pieces, so first give them a common denominator. The smallest bottom that both \(10\) and \(2\) divide into is \(10\), and \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) renamed over \(10\) is \(\tfrac{5}{10}\), since multiplying top and bottom by \(5\) does not move the point. Now both are tenths, so subtract the tops and keep the bottom, $$\frac{7}{10} - \frac{5}{10} = \frac{7-5}{10} = \frac{2}{10}.$$ That is a correct answer but not yet the simplest one. The top \(2\) and the bottom \(10\) share a factor of \(2\), so divide both by it, \(\tfrac{2}{10} = \tfrac{1}{5}\). The bottle is \(\boxed{1/5}\) full.
Review 7. A probe is at \(+\tfrac{1}{6}\) then sinks by \(\tfrac{3}{4}\), crossing \(0\). Compute \(\tfrac{1}{6}-\tfrac{3}{4}\) over a common denominator. What is the result, with sign, in simplest form?
Show solution
The two denominators are \(6\) and \(4\). Their least common denominator is \(12\), since \(12\) is the smallest number both \(6\) and \(4\) divide into. Rename each fraction over \(12\). For the first, \(\tfrac{1}{6}=\tfrac{1\times 2}{6\times 2}=\tfrac{2}{12}\). For the second, \(\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{3\times 3}{4\times 3}=\tfrac{9}{12}\). Now subtract with a common denominator. \(\tfrac{2}{12}-\tfrac{9}{12}=\tfrac{2-9}{12}=\tfrac{-7}{12}\). The probe started only \(\tfrac{2}{12}\) above zero and dropped \(\tfrac{9}{12}\), so it overshoots the surface and the reading is negative, which matches our sign. Check that \(\tfrac{-7}{12}\) is already in simplest form. Since \(7\) is prime and does not divide \(12\), the numerator and denominator share no common factor, so nothing cancels. The final reading is \(\boxed{-7/12}\).
Review 8. A pitcher holds \(5\) cups; \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of a cup is added. Rewrite \(5=\tfrac{5}{1}\) over denominator \(3\), then combine. Find \(5+\tfrac{2}{3}\) as a single fraction.
Show solution
The two amounts have different forms, so first give them a shared denominator. Start by writing the whole number as a fraction, \(5=\tfrac{5}{1}\). To turn the denominator into 3, multiply top and bottom by 3, which keeps the value the same. That gives \(\tfrac{5}{1}\times\tfrac{3}{3}=\tfrac{15}{3}\). Now both pieces are measured in thirds, so you can simply add the numerators. \(\tfrac{15}{3}+\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{15+2}{3}=\tfrac{17}{3}\). Check whether it reduces. Since 17 is prime and shares no factor with 3, the fraction is already as simple as it gets. So the pitcher holds \(\boxed{17/3}\) cups.
Review 9. A ribbon is \(2\) m; \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) m is cut off. Rewrite \(2=\tfrac{2}{1}\) over eighths, then subtract. What is \(2-\tfrac{3}{8}\) as a single fraction?
Show solution
The trouble with \(2-\tfrac{3}{8}\) is that \(2\) and \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) are not in matching pieces yet. So turn the whole number into eighths first. Write \(2\) as \(\tfrac{2}{1}\), then multiply top and bottom by \(8\) so the denominator becomes \(8\). That gives \(2=\tfrac{16}{8}\), because \(16\) eighths really is two whole meters. Now both amounts are measured in eighths, so the subtraction is clean. Take \(\tfrac{16}{8}-\tfrac{3}{8}=\tfrac{16-3}{8}=\tfrac{13}{8}\). Check whether it can be reduced. The top is \(13\), which is prime, and \(13\) does not divide \(8\), so \(\gcd(13,8)=1\) and the fraction is already in lowest terms. The tailor is left with \(\tfrac{13}{8}\) of a meter of ribbon, which is a little more than one and a half meters, and that fits the picture since they only snipped off a small piece. So \(2-\tfrac{3}{8}=\boxed{13/8}\).
Review 10. Three bursts: \(\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{4}+\tfrac{1}{8}\) of a fuel cell. lcd for \(2,4,8\) is \(8\). Total fraction used, in simplest form?
Show solution
When you have three fractions to add, you do not have to combine them in pairs. Look for one denominator that works for all of them at once. The denominators here are 2, 4, and 8, and 8 is a multiple of both 2 and 4, so 8 is the common denominator for the whole group. Rewrite each fraction over 8. Since \(\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{4}{8}\), \(\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{2}{8}\), and \(\tfrac{1}{8}\) is already there, the sum becomes \(\tfrac{4}{8}+\tfrac{2}{8}+\tfrac{1}{8}\). Now add the numerators in one sweep, \(4+2+1=7\), keeping the denominator 8, which gives \(\tfrac{7}{8}\). Check whether it simplifies. The numerator 7 is prime and does not divide 8, so the fraction is already in lowest terms. The machine uses \(\boxed{7/8}\) of a fuel cell over the shift.
Review 11. Kite: \(+\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{3}-\tfrac{1}{5}\). lcd for \(2,3,5\) is \(30\). How far above ground, in simplest form?
Show solution
Each move is measured in a different sized piece, so the first job is one shared bottom. The denominators \(2\), \(3\), and \(5\) are all primes with nothing in common, so the smallest number all three divide into is their product, \(2\times 3\times 5 = 30\). This is a case where the least common denominator really is the product, not just the largest denominator. Rewrite each move over \(30\), $$\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{15}{30}, \qquad \tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{10}{30}, \qquad \tfrac{1}{5} = \tfrac{6}{30}.$$ Two of the moves go up and one goes down, so combine the tops with those signs over the shared bottom, $$\frac{15}{30} + \frac{10}{30} - \frac{6}{30} = \frac{15 + 10 - 6}{30} = \frac{19}{30}.$$ Since \(19\) is prime and does not divide \(30\), the top and bottom share no factor, so this is already simplest. The kite sits \(\boxed{19/30}\) of the way to the marker.
Review 12. A tank starts \(\tfrac{11}{12}\) full; pipes draw \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) then \(\tfrac{1}{4}\). Find \(\tfrac{11}{12}-\tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{1}{4}\) over the lcd. What fraction remains, in simplest form?
Show solution
The three fractions live over denominators 12, 2, and 4. Twelve is a multiple of all three, so use it as the single common denominator and convert each fraction once. The \(\tfrac{11}{12}\) is already there. For \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), multiply top and bottom by 6 to get \(\tfrac{6}{12}\). For \(\tfrac{1}{4}\), multiply top and bottom by 3 to get \(\tfrac{3}{12}\). Now the whole chain reads \(\tfrac{11}{12}-\tfrac{6}{12}-\tfrac{3}{12}\). Because the bottoms match, combine the top numbers in one go, \(11-6-3=2\), which gives \(\tfrac{2}{12}\). That is not in lowest terms. Both 2 and 12 share a factor of 2, so divide each by 2 to land on \(\tfrac{1}{6}\). The tank ends the day one sixth full. \(\boxed{1/6}\)
