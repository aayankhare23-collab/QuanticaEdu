Prealgebra · Lesson 11.6

Area

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Perimeter measured the boundary, the walk around the edge. Area measures the surface inside, the flat space a figure actually covers. We count that space in little squares. Start with a rectangle and see why its squares line up so neatly, then slice that rectangle into a triangle, and finally build up figures made of several shapes stitched together.

Key idea
Area and square units. Area is the amount of surface a flat figure covers, measured by how many unit squares fit inside it. A unit square is one unit on each side and has an area of one square unit. Because we are counting squares, area is always given in square units, like square centimeters or square feet, written with a small \(2\), as in \(\text{cm}^2\).
Key idea
Area of a rectangle and a square. Fill a rectangle with unit squares and they line up in neat rows and columns, one row for each unit of height and one column for each unit of width. So the count is just length times width, \(A = l \times w\). A square has equal sides, so its area is a side times itself, \(A = s^2\). The figure above shows a rectangle broken into its unit squares.
In the diagram above is a rectangle 8 units wide and 5 units tall. Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
The area of a rectangle is its length times its width. Reading the two sides off the diagram above, $$A = l \times w = 8 \times 5 = \boxed{40}.$$ The area is 40 square units.
In the diagram above is a square with side 7. Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
A square's area is the side squared, \(A = s^2\). Here the side is 7, so $$A = 7^2 = \boxed{49}.$$ The area is 49 square units.
Key idea
Working backward from the area. The area formula runs in reverse. If you know a rectangle's area and one of its sides, you can find the missing side by dividing. Since \(l \times w = A\), the width has to be \(w = \frac{A}{l}\). A square is even quicker. Its area is a side squared, so the side is the number that multiplies by itself to give the area, which is its square root.
In the diagram above is a rectangle with length \(9\) and an unknown width. Its area is \(72\). Find the width. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The area of a rectangle is \(l \times w\), so \(9 \times w = 72\). To find the missing width, divide the area by the length. $$w = \frac{72}{9} = 8$$ The width is \(\boxed{8}\).
The diagram above is a square whose area is \(81\). Find the length of one side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A square's area is the side times itself, so to undo that we take the square root of the area. $$s = \sqrt{81} = 9$$ The side is \(\boxed{9}\).
Key idea
A right triangle is half a rectangle. Take any rectangle and slice it along a diagonal. The two pieces are identical right triangles, so each one covers exactly half the rectangle. The rectangle's area is the product of its sides, and those two sides are the triangle's legs, so a right triangle's area is half that product, \(A = \frac{1}{2}\times \text{leg}\times \text{leg}\). The figure above shows the diagonal split.
The diagram above shows a right triangle. The small square marks the right angle, and its two legs measure \(8\) and \(6\). Find the area of the triangle. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
A triangle's area is \(\frac{1}{2}\times \text{base}\times \text{height}\). In a right triangle the two legs meet at the right angle, so they act as the base and the height. Here the legs are \(8\) and \(6\), so $$A = \frac{1}{2}\times 8 \times 6 = \frac{1}{2}\times 48 = \boxed{24}.$$ The area is \(24\) square units.
Key idea
Area of a triangle. Every triangle, not just a right one, has area \(A = \frac{1}{2}\times \text{base}\times \text{height}\). Pick any side to be the base. The height is the straight-across distance from that base up to the opposite corner, meeting the base at a right angle. The triangle fills exactly half of the rectangle that is base wide and height tall, which the figure above shows with a dashed box.
In the diagram above is a triangle with base \(10\) and height \(6\) (the dashed line is the height). Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
The area of a triangle is \(\frac{1}{2}\times \text{base}\times \text{height}\). The base is \(10\) and the dashed height is \(6\), so $$A = \frac{1}{2}\times 10 \times 6 = \boxed{30}.$$
In the diagram above is a triangle with base \(14\) and height \(9\). Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
A triangle covers half the rectangle you could box around it, so its area is \(\frac{1}{2}\times \text{base}\times \text{height}\). Reading the base \(14\) and the height \(9\) off the figure above, $$A = \frac{1}{2}\times 14 \times 9 = \boxed{63}.$$ The area is \(63\) square units.
Key idea
Areas that come in pieces. A figure made of several shapes stitched together can be handled two ways. You can split it into rectangles and triangles, find each area, and ADD them up. Or you can start from a whole rectangle that surrounds the figure and SUBTRACT the piece that was cut away. Both give the same answer, so pick whichever is less work. The L-shape above splits into two rectangles, and their areas add.
The diagram above is an L-shaped figure with the sides marked \(9\), \(3\), \(5\), \(3\), \(4\), \(6\). Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Split the L into two rectangles and add their areas. The bottom rectangle is \(9\) wide and \(3\) tall, so it holds \(9 \times 3 = 27\). The top rectangle is \(4\) wide and \(3\) tall, so it holds \(4 \times 3 = 12\). Adding them gives $$27 + 12 = \boxed{39}.$$ You could also take the full \(9 \times 6\) box and subtract the \(5 \times 3\) notch, \(54 - 15 = 39\), which lands on the same answer.
The figure above is a \(10\) by \(8\) rectangle with a right-triangular corner sliced off, the cut having legs \(6\) and \(4\) (shown dashed). Find the area of the shape that remains. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Take the full rectangle and remove the triangle that was cut away. The rectangle covers \(10\times 8 = 80\) square units. The sliced corner is a right triangle whose legs \(6\) and \(4\) act as base and height, so it covers \(\frac{1}{2}\times 6\times 4 = 12\) square units. What remains is the rectangle minus that corner. $$10\times 8 - \frac{1}{2}\times 6\times 4 = 80 - 12 = \boxed{68}$$ The area of the remaining shape is \(68\) square units.
In the diagram above, a \(12\) by \(6\) region is to be covered by tiles that are \(2\) by \(3\), like the shaded one. How many tiles are needed? Give the number of tiles.
Show solution
Tiling a region means the number of tiles is the region's area divided by one tile's area. The region covers \(12\times 6 = 72\) square units and each tile covers \(2\times 3 = 6\). Divide to get the count. $$\frac{12\times 6}{2\times 3} = \frac{72}{6} = \boxed{12}$$ So \(12\) tiles are needed.
The diagram above shows a rectangular frame: a \(10\) by \(8\) outer rectangle with a \(6\) by \(4\) rectangular hole cut out of the middle. The shaded part is what remains. Find the shaded area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Take the big rectangle and subtract the cut-out piece. The outer rectangle has area \(10\times 8 = 80\), and the hole has area \(6\times 4 = 24\). Subtract to get what remains, $$10\times 8 - 6\times 4 = 80 - 24 = \boxed{56}.$$ The shaded area is \(56\) square units.
Fun fact
An acre started as a day's work, not a measurement. It was roughly the strip of land a farmer with one ox team could plough in a single day, so its size grew out of how much ground the animals could cover before sundown rather than any tidy formula. A long field took less turning of the team than a wide one, so acres came out long and narrow. Only much later was the acre pinned down to exactly \(43{,}560\) square feet, which is why such an ordinary patch of ground hides such an odd-looking number.
In the diagram above is an L-shaped room floor with the marked sides \(12\), \(6\), \(4\), \(3\), \(8\), and \(9\). Find its area so you know how much flooring to buy. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Fill in the notch and treat the shape as one big rectangle, then subtract the corner that was cut away. The full rectangle is \(12\) by \(9\), so its area is \(12\times 9 = 108\). The missing corner is \(4\) by \(3\), an area of \(4\times 3 = 12\). Subtract the cut-out from the whole. $$108 - 12 = \boxed{96}$$ The floor covers \(96\) square units.
The figure above is a house shape: a rectangle \(10\) wide and \(6\) tall, with a triangular roof of height \(4\) sitting on top. The dashed line is where the roof meets the wall. Find the total area of the whole house. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Split the house at the dashed line and add the two pieces. The rectangle below has area \(10\times 6 = 60\). The roof is a triangle with base \(10\) and height \(4\), so its area is \(\frac{1}{2}\times 10 \times 4 = 20\). Add them for the total, $$60 + 20 = \boxed{80}.$$
Key idea
The area toolkit. Area counts the unit squares that fit inside a figure, so it is always measured in square units. A rectangle holds \(l \times w\) of them, and a square holds \(s^2\). Both rules run backward too, so when you know the area and one side you can divide to recover the missing side. A triangle, whether it has a right angle or not, covers \(\frac{1}{2}\times \text{base}\times \text{height}\), exactly half of the base-by-height rectangle it sits inside, where the height is measured straight across from the base. When a figure comes in pieces, you have two moves. Split it into rectangles and triangles and add those areas up, or start from a surrounding rectangle and subtract the piece that was cut away. And to tile a region, divide the region's area by the area of a single tile to get the number of tiles you need. Every one of these comes back to the same idea, counting the squares inside.

Review problems

Review 1.
In the diagram above is a rectangle 11 wide and 6 tall. Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
The area of a rectangle is its width times its height. Multiply the two lengths marked on the diagram, \(11\times 6 = 66\). So the area is \(\boxed{66}\) square units.
Review 2.
The diagram above shows a square with side 8. Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
A square's area is \(s^2\), the side multiplied by itself. The side here is 8, so $$8^2 = 8 \times 8 = \boxed{64}.$$ The area is 64 square units.
Review 3.
The rectangle in the diagram above has length \(15\) and an unknown width, and its area is \(90\) square units. Find the missing width. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The area of a rectangle is length times width, so the width is what the length must be multiplied by to reach the area. Divide the area by the length.$$\frac{90}{15} = \boxed{6}$$So the missing width is \(6\).
Review 4.
The diagram above shows a square with an area of \(100\) square units. Find the length of one side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A square with side \(s\) has area \(s^2\). Here \(s^2 = 100\), so \(s = \sqrt{100}\). Since \(10 \times 10 = 100\), the side is $$s = \sqrt{100} = \boxed{10}.$$
Review 5.
In the diagram above is a right triangle. Its two legs, the sides that meet at the square corner, are 12 and 5. Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
The two legs meet at the right angle, so one serves as the base and the other as the height measured straight across from it. Area is half the base times the height. $$\frac{1}{2}\times 12 \times 5$$ Multiply the legs first, \(12 \times 5 = 60\), then take half. $$\frac{1}{2}\times 60 = 30$$ So the area is \(\boxed{30}\) square units.
Review 6.
The diagram above shows a triangle with base 16 and height 5. Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
A triangle's area is half its base times its height. The diagram gives a base of 16 and a height of 5, so $$\frac{1}{2}\times 16 \times 5 = 40.$$ The area is \(\boxed{40}\) square units.
Review 7.
The diagram above shows a triangle with base 9 and height 8. Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
A triangle covers half of the rectangle that boxes it in, so its area is \(\frac{1}{2}\times\text{base}\times\text{height}\). Here the base is 9 and the height is 8, so $$\frac{1}{2}\times 9 \times 8 = \frac{72}{2} = 36.$$ The area is \(\boxed{36}\) square units.
Review 8.
The diagram above shows an L-shaped figure with sides \(8\), \(4\), \(5\), \(3\), \(3\), \(7\). Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Split the L into two rectangles. The wide part is \(8\times 4 = 32\), and the narrow part below it is \(3\times 3 = 9\). Add them. $$32 + 9 = \boxed{41}$$ You can check it the other way. The whole \(8\times 7\) box is \(56\), and the missing notch is \(5\times 3 = 15\), so \(56 - 15 = 41\). The area is \(41\) square units.
Review 9.
The diagram above shows a rectangle 9 by 8 with a right-triangular corner sliced off, the two legs of the cut measuring 6 and 4. Find the area of the shape that remains. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Take the full rectangle and subtract the triangle that was cut away. The rectangle is \(9\times 8 = 72\). The removed corner is a right triangle with legs 6 and 4, so its area is \(\frac{1}{2}\times 6\times 4 = 12\). What is left is $$72 - 12 = \boxed{60}$$
Review 10.
The diagram above shows a \(10\) by \(8\) region covered by \(2\) by \(2\) square tiles, with no gaps or overlaps. How many tiles cover the whole region? Give the number of tiles.
Show solution
The number of tiles is the region's area divided by the area of one tile. The region is \(10\times 8 = 80\) square units, and each tile is \(2\times 2 = 4\) square units. Divide to get the count. $$\frac{80}{4} = \boxed{20}$$ So it takes \(20\) tiles.
Review 11.
The diagram above shows a rectangular frame: a \(12\) by \(9\) rectangle with an \(8\) by \(5\) rectangular hole cut out of it. Find the shaded area of the frame. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
The shaded frame is the big rectangle with the hole taken out, so take the big area and subtract the hole. The outer rectangle is \(12\times 9 = 108\), and the hole is \(8\times 5 = 40\). Subtracting gives $$108 - 40 = \boxed{68}.$$
Review 12.
The diagram above shows an L-shaped patio with sides \(11\), \(6\), \(3\), \(2\), \(8\), \(8\). Find its area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Fill in the notch to make a full rectangle, then subtract the piece you added. The whole rectangle is \(11\times 8 = 88\), and the missing corner is \(3\times 2 = 6\). So the patio is $$88 - 6 = \boxed{82}.$$ The area is 82 square units.
Review 13.
The figure above is a house shape, a rectangle \(8\) wide and \(5\) tall with a triangular roof of height \(3\) on top. Find the total area. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
Split the house into the rectangle and the triangular roof, then add the two areas. The rectangle is \(8\times 5 = 40\). The roof has base \(8\) and height \(3\), so its area is \(\frac{1}{2}\times 8\times 3 = 12\). Adding them, $$40 + 12 = \boxed{52}$$ The total area is \(52\) square units.
