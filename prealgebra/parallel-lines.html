Prealgebra · Lesson 11.2

Parallel Lines

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Two lines in a plane that run side by side forever, always the same distance apart, never touching, are parallel. Now draw a third line straight across both of them. That crossing line is a transversal, and where it slices through, it opens up eight angles, four at each meeting point. You might expect eight different measurements. The surprise is that they are not all different. They lock together into a few small families, and the moment you know one angle, every other one falls into place.

Key idea
Parallel lines and a transversal. Two lines in the same plane that never meet, staying exactly the same distance apart forever, are parallel, and the matching arrowheads you see on the lines are the mark that says so. A line that crosses both of them is a transversal, and at each crossing it makes four angles, eight angles in all. Because the two parallel lines run in exactly the same direction, those eight angles are not all different. They fall into a few families of equal and supplementary angles, and the next ideas name those families one at a time.
Key idea
Corresponding angles. Corresponding angles sit in the same position at the two crossings, for instance the upper-right angle at each one. When the two lines are parallel, corresponding angles are equal. Imagine sliding one crossing straight along the transversal until it lands on the other. Since the lines point the same way, the angle lands exactly on top of its partner, which is why the two measures match.
In the diagram above, a transversal cuts two parallel lines (the matching arrowheads mark them parallel). The \(65^\circ\) angle and the marked angle sit in the same position at their two crossings, so they are corresponding angles. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The \(65^\circ\) angle and the marked angle are corresponding angles, matching positions at the two crossings. Across parallel lines, corresponding angles are equal, so the marked angle is \(\boxed{65}\).
Key idea
Alternate interior angles. The interior angles are the ones that live between the two parallel lines. Alternate interior angles are the pair between the lines but on opposite sides of the transversal. When the lines are parallel, alternate interior angles are equal. They trace out a Z shape along the transversal, so you may see them called Z angles.
In the diagram above, the \(72^\circ\) angle and the marked angle are alternate interior angles, sitting between the parallel lines on opposite sides of the transversal. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The two angles are alternate interior angles, so they are equal. That means the marked angle is the same as the given one. $$\angle = 72^\circ = \boxed{72}$$
Key idea
Alternate exterior angles. The exterior angles lie outside the two parallel lines, above the top line and below the bottom one. Alternate exterior angles are the pair outside the lines on opposite sides of the transversal, and just like their interior cousins they are equal when the lines are parallel.
In the diagram above, the \(48^\circ\) angle and the marked angle are alternate exterior angles. They lie outside the parallel lines, on opposite sides of the transversal. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The two marked angles are alternate exterior angles, and across parallel lines those are always equal. So the marked angle equals the given one. \(\boxed{48}\).
In the diagram above, the two marked angles sit side by side at the same crossing on the top line. One is \(65^\circ\) and the other is marked with a question mark. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The \(65^\circ\) angle and the marked angle form a linear pair on a straight line, so they are supplementary and add to \(180^\circ\). $$180 - 65 = \boxed{115}$$ The marked angle is \(115^\circ\).
Key idea
Co-interior angles. Co-interior angles, also called same-side interior angles, are the two interior angles on the same side of the transversal. Unlike the others these are not equal. They are supplementary and add to \(180^\circ\). Together with the two parallel lines they close off a C shape, and a C holds a straight angle's worth of turning, which is the \(180^\circ\).
In the diagram above, the \(110^\circ\) angle and the marked angle are co-interior angles, sitting between the parallel lines on the same side of the transversal. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The \(110^\circ\) angle and the marked angle are co-interior, so they are supplementary and add to \(180^\circ\). That gives $$180 - 110 = \boxed{70}.$$ The marked angle is \(70^\circ\).
In the diagram above, a transversal cuts two parallel lines. The \(130^\circ\) angle is at the top crossing and the marked angle is at the bottom crossing. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The \(130^\circ\) angle and the angle in the matching spot at the bottom crossing are corresponding angles, so that angle is also \(130^\circ\). The marked angle forms a linear pair with it, so the two add to \(180^\circ\). $$180 - 130 = \boxed{50}$$

Now put these pairs to work as equations. When the two angles are given as expressions in \(x\), the same two moves are all you need. Set the expressions equal for corresponding or alternate angles, since those pairs are equal. Set their sum to \(180\) for co-interior angles, since those add to a straight angle. Either way you get one equation, and solving it for \(x\) unlocks every angle in the figure.

In the diagram above, a transversal cuts two parallel lines, and the two marked angles are corresponding angles labeled \(2x^\circ\) and \((x+40)^\circ\). Find \(x\). Give the number of degrees (the value of \(x\)).
Show solution
The two marked angles are corresponding angles, so they are equal. That gives $$2x = x + 40.$$ Subtracting \(x\) from both sides leaves \(x = 40\), so \(\boxed{40}\).
In the diagram above, a transversal cuts two parallel lines. The two co-interior angles are labeled \(3x^\circ\) and \(2x^\circ\). Find \(x\). Give the number of degrees (the value of \(x\)).
Show solution
The two marked angles are co-interior, so they are supplementary and add to \(180^\circ\). That gives $$3x + 2x = 180$$ so \(5x = 180\), and dividing by 5 gives \(x = \boxed{36}\).
Key idea
Angles in a parallelogram. A parallelogram has two pairs of parallel sides, so each side acts as a transversal of the other pair. That forces two facts at once. Opposite angles are equal, since they are alternate angles between parallel sides, and any two angles next to each other, the consecutive angles, are co-interior and so supplementary. A single known angle of a parallelogram therefore hands you all four.
In the parallelogram \(ABCD\) above, \(\angle A = 73^\circ\). Find \(\angle C\), the angle opposite it. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
\(\angle A\) and \(\angle C\) are opposite angles of the parallelogram, and opposite angles of a parallelogram are equal. So $$\angle C = \angle A = \boxed{73}$$
Key idea
Running the rules backward. Every rule you just learned also works in reverse. So far you have started with two parallel lines and found equal or supplementary angles. Now flip it around. If a transversal makes a pair of corresponding angles equal, or a pair of alternate angles equal, or a pair of co-interior angles supplementary, then the two lines it crosses HAVE to be parallel. There is no other way for those angles to come out that way. This reversed version is called the converse, and it is powerful. It is how you prove that two lines are parallel in the first place, and it lets you solve for the value that forces them to be parallel. When a problem tells you the lines are parallel, you reach for the rules going forward. When a problem asks you to make the lines parallel, you set the matching angles equal (or the co-interior ones supplementary) and solve.
In the diagram above, a transversal crosses two lines. The angles \(3x^\circ\) and \(75^\circ\) sit in matching positions, so they are corresponding angles. For the two lines to be parallel, these corresponding angles must be equal. Find \(x\). Give the number of degrees (the value of \(x\)).
Show solution
The two marked angles are corresponding angles, and parallel lines force corresponding angles to be equal. So $$3x = 75.$$ Divide both sides by 3 to get \(x = \boxed{25}\).
In the diagram above, a transversal crosses two parallel lines and meets the top line at a right angle, shown by the small square. Find the marked angle where the transversal crosses the bottom line. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The transversal meets the top line at a right angle, so it is perpendicular to that line. A line perpendicular to one of two parallel lines is perpendicular to the other one too. So the transversal meets the bottom line at a right angle as well, and the marked angle is \(\boxed{90}\).
In the diagram above, two parallel guide rails are crossed by a straight brace. The brace makes a \(62^\circ\) angle with the top rail, and the marked angle sits in the same position where the brace meets the bottom rail. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
The two rails are parallel and the brace is the transversal. The \(62^\circ\) angle and the marked angle sit in the same position at each rail, so they are corresponding angles, which are equal. That gives $$\angle = 62^\circ = \boxed{62}$$
Fun fact
The idea that parallel lines never meet is one of five postulates Euclid wrote down in Greece more than two thousand years ago. Four of them felt obvious, but this one always felt like something you should be able to prove from the rest. For centuries mathematicians tried, and every one of them failed. Eventually a few of them tried the opposite, letting parallel lines behave differently, and instead of nonsense they discovered entirely new curved geometries. Those strange geometries are exactly the ones Einstein reached for when he described how gravity bends space.
In the diagram above, a transversal cuts two parallel lines. One angle at the top crossing measures \(116^\circ\), and the acute angle marked at the bottom crossing is unknown. Find the marked angle. Give the number of degrees.
Show solution
This takes two steps. First use corresponding angles. The \(116^\circ\) angle at the top crossing matches the angle in the same position at the bottom crossing, so that bottom angle is also \(116^\circ\). That angle and the marked angle form a linear pair along the straight line, so they add to \(180^\circ\). $$180 - 116 = \boxed{64}$$ The marked angle is \(64^\circ\).
Key idea
The parallel-line toolkit. Matching arrowheads mark two lines as parallel, and a transversal slices across them to make eight angles. Four rules read straight off that picture. Corresponding angles are equal, sitting in the same spot at each crossing. Alternate interior angles are equal, tucked between the lines on opposite sides of the transversal. Alternate exterior angles are equal, doing the same thing outside the lines. And co-interior angles, the pair between the lines on the same side, are supplementary, so they add to \(180^\circ\). Nothing you learned about a single crossing goes away. At each of the two intersections a linear pair still adds to \(180^\circ\) and vertical angles are still equal. Stack those old facts on the four new ones and every angle in the figure is reachable from just one. Give me a single measure and all eight are yours, either equal to it or supplementary to it. A parallelogram is the same idea folded shut, since each pair of opposite sides is parallel. So opposite angles are equal and any two consecutive angles are supplementary, adding to \(180^\circ\). Best of all, every rule runs backward. Finding equal corresponding angles, or equal alternate angles, or co-interior angles that sum to \(180^\circ\), does not just follow from parallel lines, it proves the lines are parallel in the first place.
