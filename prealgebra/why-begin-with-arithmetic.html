Why Begin with Arithmetic?

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

You already know how to add, subtract, multiply, and divide. Honestly, you could probably solve a fair number of the problems in this course on sight. So why would we open an entire course with a chapter on arithmetic?

Start with the name on the door: Prealgebra. People don't fully agree on what the word means, but here's how we think of it: prealgebra is the bridge between arithmetic and algebra.

Arithmetic is the world of adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing, plus a few bolder moves like squaring a number or taking a square root. You've met most of it already. The hardest part is usually a word problem: “If Maya has 6 stickers and Theo has 9, how many do they have together?” As you get older the numbers grow larger, but problems like that never really get harder.

Arithmetic is perfect for counting stickers. But the moment a question gets richer (forecasting a satellite's orbit, modeling how a rumor spreads through a school, or counting the routes a single message can take across the internet), we need a bigger toolbox.

That toolbox is algebra. Algebra is the language of all higher mathematics. It takes the ideas you already trust from arithmetic and makes them general, so they work far beyond the one problem where you first met them.

Here's a small taste. With arithmetic, you can check a single case.

3 × (4 + 6) = (3 × 4) + (3 × 6)both sides come out to 30

Algebra hands you the far more powerful statement.

a × (b + c) = (a × b) + (a × c)true for every a, b, and c, not just one example

And in more advanced mathematics, a, b, and c might not even be ordinary numbers, and “+” and “×” might not be the addition and multiplication you know today. But we're getting a little ahead of ourselves.

So our first goal is simple to state: lay down the rules of arithmetic carefully, and show you why each one is true.

That word, why, is the heart of this course. You're ready now to ask not just how a calculation works, but why it works. That's the difference that lets you bend a familiar technique to crack an unfamiliar problem. So throughout, we'll rarely just tell you how something works; we'll show you why.

By the end of this opening chapter

You'll be able to explain (really explain, not just compute) each of these.

  • 1
    (−4) × (−6) = 24, and why the answer comes out positive, not −24.
  • 2
    (2025 × 2030) − (2020 × 2025) = 20250, and how to get it in your head in seconds.
  • 3
    (3 × 8 × 25) ÷ (6 × 5 × 4) = 5 (again, entirely in your head).

You'll know these not because you memorized a rule or tapped buttons on a calculator, but because you understand the mathematics underneath them.
