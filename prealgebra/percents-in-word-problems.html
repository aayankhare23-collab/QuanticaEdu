Prealgebra · Lesson 9.4

Percents in Word Problems

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

This lesson applies percent-as-fraction, percent of a number, and percent change to word problems. Four moves: read (each percent has its own whole), translate, solve, check that size and direction make sense.

A hoodie costs \(\$48\) and the local sales tax is \(7.5\%\). How many dollars is the sales tax alone (not the total)?
Show solution
Walk the wording one step at a time. The price, the whole, is 48 dollars, and the rate is \(7.5\%\). The tax is a straight percent of a number, so the part equals the rate times the whole. First rewrite the percent as a decimal, \(7.5\% = 0.075\). Then the tax is $$0.075 \times 48 = 3.6.$$ So the tax adds 3.6 dollars. Now sanity check the size. A sales tax should be a small slice of the price, well under a tenth of it here, and definitely never bigger than the price itself. A tenth of 48 would be 4.8 dollars, and 3.6 dollars is a bit less than that, which fits \(7.5\%\) being a little under \(10\%\). The tax alone is \(\boxed{3.6}\) dollars.
Key idea
Tax or tip \(=\) part; total \(=\) price \(\times (1 + \text{rate})\). A tax or tip is a percent of the price stacked on top, so it is the increase multiplier from \(9.3\) in new clothes. For the added piece alone use \(\text{part} = \text{rate} \times \text{price}\), but for the total keep all \(100\%\) of the price and pile the rate on in one move, \(\text{total} = \text{price} \times (1 + \text{rate})\), so a \(9\%\) tax is a single \(\times 1.09\). Reread which one the question wants, and a total with tax has to come out larger than the price.
Four friends have an \(\$80\) dinner bill and agree on an \(18\%\) tip. What is the total amount they pay, food plus tip, in dollars?
Show solution
They pay for the full food bill and then add a tip on top, so the total is the whole food plus a bit more. Paying all \(100\%\) of the food and another \(18\%\) is \(118\%\) of the food, which as a multiplier is \(1.18\). So the total is the food times that one number. $$80 \times 1.18 = 94.40$$ You can confirm it the slow way by building the tip separately. The tip is \(0.18 \times 80 = 14.40\), and adding it back gives \(80 + 14.40 = 94.40\), the same total. As a make-sense check, a tip always pushes the bill a little above the food, never below, and 94.40 dollars sits just above 80 dollars by about 14 dollars, exactly what an \(18\%\) tip should feel like. The total they pay is \(\boxed{94.40}\) dollars.
Devon earns a \(12\%\) commission and sold \(\$1{,}450\) in glassware on Sunday. How many dollars does Devon earn in commission that day?
Show solution
A commission is a percent of the sale, which is exactly a "percent of a number." Here the whole is the \(1{,}450\) dollar sale and the rate is \(12\%\), so I write \(12\%\) as the decimal \(0.12\) and multiply, $$0.12 \times 1{,}450 = 174.$$ A handy split confirms it. Ten percent of \(1{,}450\) is \(145\), and \(2\%\) is \(29\), and \(145 + 29 = 174\). Now the sanity check. A \(12\%\) commission should be a modest slice of the sale, a touch more than a tenth, and \(174\) sits just above \(145\), which is the \(10\%\) mark, so it is the small share we expected and nowhere near the whole \(1{,}450\). Devon earns a commission of \(\boxed{174}\) dollars.
Key idea
Commission \(=\) rate \(\times\) sale; sale \(=\) commission \(\div\) rate. A commission is the rate times the sale, so when you know the commission and want the sale it came from, undo the multiply by dividing. This is the find-the-base question from \(9.2\), with the commission as the part, the rate as the percent, and the sale as the whole. Do not take the rate of the commission. Dividing by a small rate makes the answer grow, so if your sale comes out smaller than the commission you divided the wrong way.
Naomi earns \(8.5\%\) commission and received \(\$510\) on one telescope sale. What was the sale price of that telescope in dollars?
Show solution
Translate the words one piece at a time. The commission is \(8.5\%\) of the sale price, and that commission is \(510\) dollars, so $$510 = 0.085 \times \text{sale}.$$ This is a find-the-whole-from-a-part problem, so divide both sides by the rate \(0.085\), $$\text{sale} = \frac{510}{0.085} = 6000.$$ Now the sanity check, which really matters here. The sale price of \(6000\) dollars is far larger than the \(510\) dollar commission, which is exactly what we expect since the commission is only a small slice of the full price. And working forward, \(8.5\%\) of \(6000\) is \(0.085 \times 6000 = 510\), the very commission she was paid. The telescope sold for \(\boxed{6000}\) dollars.
Mira bought a kettle for \(\$180\) and marks it up \(35\%\) of cost. What is the selling price of the kettle in dollars?
Show solution
The cost is the whole, 180 dollars, and the markup adds \(35\%\) of that cost on top. Keeping the full cost and adding another \(35\%\) means the selling price is \(100\% + 35\% = 135\%\) of the cost. As a multiplier that is \(1.35\), so $$\text{selling price} = 180 \times 1.35 = 243.$$ Sanity check. A markup only adds to the cost, so the price should land above 180 dollars, and 243 dollars is comfortably above it. You can also see the pieces fit. The markup alone is \(0.35 \times 180 = 63\) dollars, and \(180 + 63 = 243\) dollars, matching the multiplier method. The selling price is \(\boxed{243}\) dollars.
A longboard is tagged at \(\$240\). The shop takes \(25\%\) off, then the register adds \(6\%\) tax on the reduced price. What is the final price paid in dollars?
Show solution
Translate each move into a multiplier. A \(25\%\) discount removes a quarter and leaves the surviving \(100\% - 25\% = 75\%\), so that step is \(\times 0.75\). A \(6\%\) tax keeps the full price and adds \(6\%\) on top, which is \(100\% + 6\% = 106\%\), so that step is \(\times 1.06\). Stack them in order. First the discount, $$240 \times 0.75 = 180,$$ which is the price on the shelf after the cut. Then the tax rides on that reduced \(180\), $$180 \times 1.06 = 190.80.$$ Done in one chained product that is \(240 \times 0.75 \times 1.06 = 190.80\). Here is a tidy surprise. If the register had taxed first and discounted second, \(240 \times 1.06 \times 0.75\), the answer would be the exact same \(190.80\), because multiplication does not care about order. The two wholes look different along the way, yet the chained multipliers settle it either road. Now the make-sense check. The \(25\%\) cut should pull the price well below \(240\), and it does, down to \(180\). The small \(6\%\) tax nudges it back up by \(0.06 \times 180 = 10.80\), landing at \(190.80\), still comfortably under \(240\) because the bigger discount outweighs the smaller tax. The final price paid is \(\boxed{190.80}\) dollars.
Key idea
Stacked percent moves chain as multipliers. A discount then a tax is two multipliers in a row, \(\text{price} \times (1 - d) \times (1 + t)\), the cut for what is left behind and the tax for what is added on. Like the two changes in \(9.3\), these moves multiply, they do not add. Because multiplication can be done in any order, taxing first or discounting first lands on the exact same final price, so you can even fold the two multipliers into one net number ahead of time.
Maple Ridge Middle School cast \(320\) ballots on a schedule plan. Of those, \(208\) voted in favor. What percent of the votes were in favor of the plan?
Show solution
The question asks what percent of the votes were in favor, so the part is the 208 votes in favor and the whole is the 320 votes cast. Finding what percent one number is of another means writing the part over the whole and scaling to a percent, so the setup is $$\frac{208}{320}\times 100.$$ First divide to get \(\frac{208}{320}=0.65\), then multiply by 100 to get \(65\), so \(65\%\) of the votes were in favor. As a make-sense check, 208 is a little under two-thirds of 320, and two-thirds is about \(67\%\), so a result in the mid-sixties is right where it should be. There is a second free check too. The against votes are \(320-208=112\), and \(\frac{112}{320}\times 100 = 35\), so the against share is \(35\%\). The two shares add to \(65\% + 35\% = 100\%\), exactly as they must when every ballot is either for or against. The percent who voted in favor is \(\boxed{65}\).
Key idea
Two-way splits sum to \(100\%\). In any clean two-way split the two shares finish each other off, so the passers and the failers add to \(100\%\), and knowing one gives the other by subtracting. The reverse is just as handy. If you know the count in one group and the percent that group is of the whole, recover the whole by dividing, \(\text{whole} = \text{count} \div \text{rate}\), the same find-the-base move. Pair each count with its matching rate, and remember the whole is bigger than any one slice.
At a beekeeping challenge, \(15\%\) of members did not find the queen in time, and that came to exactly \(9\) members. How many people took the challenge in all?
Show solution
Start by reading carefully what the \(15\%\) describes. It describes the members who tried and did not find the queen, and there were 9 of those. So the 9 are the part, and \(15\%\) is the rate that part makes of the whole club. The whole club is the unknown base. Write that as \(0.15 \times (\text{group size}) = 9\). To find a whole from a part you divide the part by its rate, so the group size is \(9 \div 0.15\). Dividing, \(9 \div 0.15 = 60\). Now check that this makes sense. Only \(15\%\) of the group failed, a small slice, so the whole group should be much bigger than 9, and 60 is comfortably larger. Confirm it by going forward, \(0.15 \times 60 = 9\), exactly the 9 who missed the queen, which leaves \(60 - 9 = 51\) members who found her. Everything lines up, so the number of people who took the challenge is \(\boxed{60}\).
Key idea
Percent of remaining means multiply leftover fractions. When each step takes a percent of what is LEFT, it acts on the smaller pile, never the original, so what survives a step is its leftover fraction. Selling \(40\%\) leaves \(60\%\), then giving away \(25\%\) of that leaves \(75\%\) of it, and the final amount is the start times every leftover fraction multiplied together, like \(\text{start} \times 0.60 \times 0.75\). Adding up the percents removed and subtracting once over-counts, because each later step chips at a pile that already shrank.
A warehouse has \(720\) boxes. Monday ships \(25\%\). Tuesday ships \(40\%\) of what remains. Wednesday ships \(50\%\) of what is left then. How many boxes remain after Wednesday's shipment?
Show solution
The trick is that every percent is taken of the current pile, so it is cleaner to follow what stays than what leaves. Monday ships \(25\%\), so \(100\% - 25\% = 75\%\) stays, a multiplier of \(0.75\). Tuesday ships \(40\%\) of the remaining boxes, so \(60\%\) of them stay, a multiplier of \(0.60\). Wednesday ships \(50\%\) of what is left, so \(50\%\) stays, a multiplier of \(0.50\). Chain the leftover fractions onto the starting 720. $$720 \times 0.75 \times 0.60 \times 0.50$$ Work it left to right. After Monday \(720 \times 0.75 = 540\) boxes remain. After Tuesday \(540 \times 0.60 = 324\) boxes remain. After Wednesday \(324 \times 0.50 = 162\) boxes remain. Sanity check, the pile shrinks every single day, from 720 down to 540 down to 324 down to 162, which is exactly what should happen when boxes only leave. And 162 is a clean whole number of boxes, no fractions of a box, so it makes sense. The question asks how many remain after Wednesday, not how many were shipped, so the answer is the final leftover. \(\boxed{162}\)
Fun fact
The per mille sign ‰ means "per thousand", an extra circle on the percent sign. \(25‰ = 2.5\%\). Sea water runs near \(35‰\) salt. Some countries express blood alcohol limits in per mille.
A \(40\)L barrel of mango cooler is \(25\%\) juice. The owner stirs in \(10\) extra liters of plain water, adding no juice. What percent of the new barrel is real mango juice?
Show solution
First pin down the juice, since that is the part that stays fixed. The juice is \(25\%\) of 40 liters, so \(0.25\times 40 = 10\) liters of mango juice. Adding water does not change that, the barrel still holds 10 liters of juice afterward. Next find the new total. Pouring in 10 liters of water gives \(40 + 10 = 50\) liters in all. Now the new concentration is the juice over the new total, written as a percent. That is \(\frac{10}{50}\times 100 = 20\), so the barrel is \(20\%\) juice. Sanity check the direction. You added only water and no juice, so the drink must get weaker, and \(20\%\) is indeed lower than the original \(25\%\). The dilution makes sense, so the percent of the barrel that is real mango juice is \(\boxed{20}\).
The Maple Hollow Otters play a \(25\)-game season. After \(13\) games they have \(7\) wins. The coach targets a \(60\%\) win rate for the whole season. How many of the remaining games must they win?
Show solution
Start by translating the goal. The Otters want to win \(60\%\) of all 25 games, and "60 percent of 25" means multiply, \(0.60 \times 25 = 15\). So the season goal is 15 wins in total. They have already won 7, so the number of wins still needed is \(15 - 7 = 8\). Those wins must come from the games that are left, and the games left are \(25 - 13 = 12\). So the Otters need 8 wins out of their final 12 games. Now run a make-sense check. The answer should be a whole number that is at least 0 and no more than the 12 games remaining, and 8 sits comfortably in that range. To be sure, suppose they win exactly 8 of the last 12. Their season total becomes \(7 + 8 = 15\) wins out of 25, and \(\tfrac{15}{25} = \tfrac{3}{5} = 60\%\), exactly the target. The Otters must win \(\boxed{8}\) of their remaining games.
A keyboard is \(25\%\) off, then \(8\%\) tax is added to the reduced price. Devansh pays \(\$162\). What was the original listed price in dollars?
Show solution
Translate the story into multipliers, one step at a time. Marking the keyboard 25 percent off means Devansh pays \(100\% - 25\% = 75\%\) of the listed price, which is the multiplier \(0.75\). Then 8 percent tax is added on the reduced price, and adding 8 percent means multiplying by \(1 + 0.08 = 1.08\). Read forward, the listed price runs through both steps in a row, so $$\text{listed} \times 0.75 \times 1.08 = 162.$$ Collapse the two multipliers into one, \(0.75 \times 1.08 = 0.81\), which leaves $$\text{listed} \times 0.81 = 162.$$ Now run the chain backward. We know the ending total and want the starting price, so undo the multiplication by dividing, $$\text{listed} = \frac{162}{0.81} = 200.$$ Make sense check, run it forward again. The keyboard was listed at 200 dollars, a quarter off brings it to \(200 \times 0.75 = 150\) dollars, and 8 percent tax on 150 is \(150 \times 0.08 = 12\) dollars, for a total of \(150 + 12 = 162\) dollars, exactly what he paid. And the answer should feel right, the original 200 is larger than the 162 he paid, which makes sense because a 25 percent cut followed by only a small 8 percent tax nets out below the full price. The original listed price was \(\boxed{200}\) dollars.

Review problems

Review 1. A kettle sells for \(\$60\) and a \(5\%\) sales tax is added. How many dollars is the sales tax alone?
Show solution
The sales tax is \(5\%\) of the price, so I take a percent of a number. Rewrite \(5\%\) as a decimal, \(5\% = 0.05\), and multiply by the 60 dollar price. $$0.05 \times 60 = 3$$ The tax is 3 dollars. The question asks for the tax alone, so I stop here and do not add it to the price. Sanity check. \(5\%\) is one twentieth, and one twentieth of 60 is \(60 \div 20 = 3\), which matches. A 3 dollar tax on a 60 dollar kettle is small, just as a \(5\%\) rate should be. The total with tax would be 63 dollars, but that is not what was asked. So the sales tax alone is \(\boxed{3}\) dollars.
Review 2. A ukulele is listed at \(\$150\) before \(6\%\) sales tax. What is the total amount you pay including the tax?
Show solution
The price is 150 dollars and the tax adds \(6\%\) of that price on top. Adding a percent means the multiplier is \(1 + 0.06 = 1.06\), so the total paid is $$150 \times 1.06 = 159.$$ As a sanity check, find the tax on its own. \(6\%\) of 150 is \(0.06 \times 150 = 9\) dollars, and \(150 + 9 = 159\), which matches. The total sits just above the sticker price, exactly as a small tax should make it. So you pay \(\boxed{159}\) dollars.
Review 3. Naomi's grooming bill is \(\$40\) and she adds a \(15\%\) tip. How many dollars does Naomi pay in all?
Show solution
A tip is a percent added on, so Naomi pays \(100\%\) of the bill plus another \(15\%\). That turns the bill into the multiplier \(1 + \tfrac{15}{100} = 1.15\). Apply it to the \(40\) dollar bill. $$40 \times 1.15 = 46$$ You can check this the long way. The tip alone is \(15\%\) of \(40\), which is \(0.15 \times 40 = 6\) dollars, and \(40 + 6 = 46\), which matches. Since a tip only adds a little to the bill, a total of \(46\) sitting just above \(40\) makes sense. Naomi pays \(\boxed{46}\) dollars in all.
Review 4. Mara earns \(5\%\) commission and closed a \(\$2400\) solar job. How much does she earn in commission, in dollars?
Show solution
Commission means a percent of the sale, here \(5\%\) of the \(\$2400\) job. Writing the percent as a decimal, \(5\% = \tfrac{5}{100} = 0.05\), so the commission is $$0.05 \times 2400 = 120.$$ A quick sanity check makes this feel right. Ten percent of \(2400\) is \(240\), and \(5\%\) is half of that, which is \(120\), matching. Since \(5\%\) is a small slice of the sale, a commission well under the full \(\$2400\) is exactly what we expect. Mara earns \(\boxed{120}\) dollars in commission.
Review 5. Priya earns \(9\%\) commission and received \(\$630\) on one kayak sale. What was the sale price of that kayak in dollars?
Show solution
The commission is a percent of the sale price, so \(9\%\) of the price equals 630 dollars. Turn the percent into a multiplier and write the relationship as a sentence in math, \(0.09 \times \text{price} = 630\). The price is the whole I am missing, so I divide the part by the rate, $$\text{price} = \frac{630}{0.09} = 7000.$$ Sanity check, take \(9\%\) of 7000 to make sure it lands back on the commission, \(0.09 \times 7000 = 630\), which matches. A 630 dollar commission also feels right for a kayak that costs several thousand dollars, since the commission should be a small slice of a much larger price. The sale price was \(\boxed{7000}\) dollars.
Review 6. A garden center pays \(\$90\) for a planter and marks it up \(40\%\). What is the shelf price in dollars?
Show solution
A markup is a percent increase laid on top of what the store paid, so the shelf price is \(100\%\) of the cost plus another \(40\%\). That multiplier is \(1 + \tfrac{40}{100} = 1.40\), so the shelf price is $$90 \times 1.40 = 126.$$ You can check this the slow way. \(40\%\) of \(90\) is \(0.40 \times 90 = 36\), the markup in dollars, and \(90 + 36 = 126\), which matches. A quick sanity check fits too. The price went up but not even doubled, and \(126\) sits a comfortable \(36\) dollars above the \(90\) the store paid, exactly the kind of bump a \(40\%\) markup should give. The shelf price is \(\boxed{126}\) dollars.
Review 7. A peacoat is priced at \(\$220\) and marked \(15\%\) off. What is the discounted price Devang pays in dollars?
Show solution
A discount takes a percent off, so the price drops by \(15\%\) and Devang pays the rest. The part he still pays is \(100\% - 15\% = 85\%\) of the listed price, which is the multiplier \(0.85\). Multiply the price by that multiplier. $$220 \times 0.85 = 187$$ Sanity check. The discount itself is \(220 \times 0.15 = 33\) dollars, and \(220 - 33 = 187\), which agrees. The sale price should land below 220 but not too far below for a small \(15\%\) cut, and 187 fits comfortably. So the discounted price he pays is \(\boxed{187}\) dollars.
Review 8. A desk lamp is priced at \(\$250\). The shop takes \(10\%\) off, then rings up \(8\%\) tax on the reduced price. What is the final price paid in dollars?
Show solution
Take the two changes one at a time. A \(10\%\) discount means the lamp keeps \(100\% - 10\% = 90\%\) of its price, so the discount multiplier is \(1 - 0.10 = 0.90\). Applying it gives the reduced price. $$250 \times 0.90 = 225$$ Now the \(8\%\) tax is added on that reduced \(225\), which is multiplying by \(1 + 0.08 = 1.08\). $$225 \times 1.08 = 243$$ You can also chain both multipliers in one shot, \(250 \times 0.90 \times 1.08 = 243\). Quick sanity check, the discount knocks off \(25\) dollars to reach \(225\), and \(8\%\) of \(225\) is \(18\) dollars of tax, so \(225 + 18 = 243\), which matches. The final price paid is \(\boxed{243}\) dollars.
Review 9. A lending library has \(84\) books, and \(21\) were checked out on the weekend. What percent of the library's books were checked out?
Show solution
The question asks what percent of the whole collection was checked out, so the whole is all 84 books and the part is the 21 that left the shelves. To find what percent one number is of another, divide the part by the whole and multiply by 100. That gives $$\frac{21}{84}\times 100.$$ The fraction \(\frac{21}{84}\) simplifies, since \(21\) divides into \(84\) exactly 4 times, so \(\frac{21}{84}=\frac{1}{4}\). Then \(\frac{1}{4}\times 100 = 25\), so \(25\%\) of the books were checked out. As a sanity check, a quarter of 84 is \(21\), which matches the number checked out, and \(25\%\) feels right because 21 is well under half of 84. The answer is \(\boxed{25}\).
Review 10. A robotics club of \(200\) members voted on practice time. Exactly \(140\) voted for mornings. What percent voted for evenings? Give the number only.
Show solution
There are only two choices, so the evening voters are the members left over after the \(140\) morning voters. That count is \(200 - 140 = 60\). To find what percent this is of the whole club, divide by the total membership, not by the other side. $$\frac{60}{200} = 0.30$$ As a percent that is \(0.30 \times 100 = 30\%\). You can sanity-check it against the morning side, which is \(\tfrac{140}{200} = 70\%\), and \(70\% + 30\% = 100\%\) covers everyone exactly. So the percent who voted for evenings is \(\boxed{30}\).
Review 11. Exactly \(20\%\) of people who sat a certification exam did not pass, and that group numbered \(12\). How many people took the exam in all?
Show solution
The \(12\) people who did not pass are the \(20\%\) of the whole group that fell short, so they are a part, not the total. Writing \(20\%\) as the decimal \(0.20\), this says $$0.20 \times \text{total} = 12.$$ To find the total, divide the part by the fraction it represents rather than multiplying. $$\text{total} = \frac{12}{0.20} = 60.$$ As a check, take \(20\%\) of \(60\) and see if you land back on \(12\). Since \(0.20 \times 60 = 12\), the count holds, and the other \(80\%\) who passed comes out to \(48\), which fits because \(48 + 12 = 60\). So \(\boxed{60}\) people took the exam.
Review 12. A crew prints \(600\) flyers. They hand out \(20\%\) to passersby. Then rain ruins \(25\%\) of the flyers still in the box. How many flyers does the crew have left?
Show solution
This is a percent-of-the-remaining chain, where each step takes a percent of whatever is left at that point, so I multiply the leftover fractions instead of subtracting from the original 600. Start with 600 flyers. Handing out \(20\%\) means \(20\%\) leave and \(80\%\) stay, so the box still holds $$600 \times 0.80 = 480$$ flyers after the morning. The rain then ruins \(25\%\) of those 480, so \(75\%\) survive. The key is that this \(25\%\) is taken from the 480 in the box, not from the original 600. $$480 \times 0.75 = 360$$ I can do both steps at once by multiplying the leftover fractions, \(600 \times 0.80 \times 0.75 = 360\), which matches. Sanity check. The crew handed out \(600 - 480 = 120\) flyers in the morning, and the rain ruined \(480 - 360 = 120\). That accounts for \(120 + 120 = 240\) flyers gone, leaving \(600 - 240 = 360\) in the box, so the pieces add back to the original 600. The answer is also smaller than the 480 from after the first step, which makes sense since more were lost. The number of flyers remaining at the end of the day is \(\boxed{360}\).
Review 13. An \(80\)L barrel of polishing fluid is \(25\%\) solvent. An apprentice stirs in \(20\) more liters of plain water. What percent of the new mixture is solvent? Give the number only.
Show solution
Start with what is actually in the barrel. The solvent is \(25\%\) of \(80\) liters, so \(0.25 \times 80 = 20\) liters of solvent, and the other \(60\) liters are water. Pouring in \(20\) more liters of plain water adds no solvent, so the solvent stays at \(20\) liters while the total grows to \(80 + 20 = 100\) liters. The new concentration compares the solvent to the WHOLE new mixture, so divide \(20\) by \(100\). That gives \(\tfrac{20}{100} = 0.20\), and as a percent \(0.20 \times 100 = 20\%\). Sanity check, the mixture got more watery, so its solvent percent should drop below the original \(25\%\), and \(20\%\) is indeed lower. So the new mixture is \(\boxed{20}\) percent solvent.
Review 14. The Marlowe Middle quiz bowl team plays a \(28\)-game season. After \(16\) games they have \(9\) wins. They want a \(50\%\) win rate for the whole season. How many of their remaining games must they win?
Show solution
First find how many total wins a \(50\%\) season needs. Half of all \(28\) games is $$\tfrac{1}{2} \times 28 = 14$$ so the team must finish with \(14\) wins. They have already won \(9\), so the wins they still need are \(14 - 9 = 5\). That is the answer, since the question asks for wins from the remaining games, not the total. Sanity check the count. The team has \(28 - 16 = 12\) games left, so winning \(5\) of them is possible. If they do, their wins come to \(9 + 5 = 14\), and \(\tfrac{14}{28} = \tfrac{1}{2} = 50\%\), exactly the target. The team must win \(\boxed{5}\) of its remaining games.
