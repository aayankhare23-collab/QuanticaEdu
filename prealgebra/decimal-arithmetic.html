Decimal Arithmetic

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Decimal arithmetic is not new arithmetic. Every digit still sits in a place worth ten times the place to its right, and a decimal is just a fraction over a power of ten. The only genuinely new job is bookkeeping, knowing exactly where the point belongs in the answer. We will test that one idea on adding first, then multiplying, then dividing, and see whether it quietly covers all three.

A runner covers \(1.7\) km then \(0.45\) km. Stack the numbers with decimal points aligned, pad \(1.7\) to \(1.70\), and add column by column. What is \(1.7 + 0.45\) in km?
Show solution
Stack the numbers with the decimal points aligned, and pad the top one with a trailing zero so both have a hundredths digit. $$\begin{array}{r} 1.70 \\ +\,0.45 \\ \hline \end{array}$$ Now add right to left. Hundredths give \(0 + 5 = 5\). Tenths give \(7 + 4 = 11\), so write \(1\) and carry \(1\) into the ones. Ones give \(1 + 0 + 1 = 2\). Drop the decimal point straight down between the ones and the tenths. $$\begin{array}{r} 1.70 \\ +\,0.45 \\ \hline 2.15 \end{array}$$ The two stretches total \(\boxed{2.15}\) km.
A bottle holds \(5.2\) litres. After pouring, \(3.65\) litres remain. Line up the decimal points and subtract. How many litres were poured out?
Show solution
Stack the numbers with their points aligned and pad the top so both show two decimal places. \(5.2\) becomes \(5.20\), which is the same amount, now matching \(3.65\) digit for digit. Work right to left. In the hundredths column \(0 - 5\) cannot go, so borrow \(1\) tenth, turning the \(0\) into \(10\) and giving \(10 - 5 = 5\). That borrow drops the tenths digit from \(2\) to \(1\), and \(1 - 6\) still cannot go, so borrow \(1\) one, making it \(11 - 6 = 5\). The borrow drops the ones digit from \(5\) to \(4\), so the ones column gives \(4 - 3 = 1\). Bring the decimal point straight down and read off \(1.55\). A quick check seals it, since \(3.65 + 1.55 = 5.20\), right back to the full bottle. So \(\boxed{1.55}\) litres were poured out.
A café lists a sandwich at \(12.5\) dollars and a cookie at \(0.45\) dollars. Align the decimal points, pad \(12.5\) to \(12.50\), and add. What is the correct total?
Show solution
The trap is treating the rightmost digits as a column. They are not. A column only holds digits of the same place value, so what must line up is the decimal points, not the edges. Pad \(12.5\) to \(12.50\) so both numbers show two decimal places, then stack them with the points in one vertical line. $$\begin{array}{r}12.50\\ +\;0.45\\ \hline 12.95\end{array}$$ Now add place by place. The hundredths give \(0 + 5 = 5\). The tenths give \(5 + 4 = 9\). The ones and tens carry straight down as \(12\), and the point drops into place beneath the others. The total is \(12.95\) dollars, a price that actually makes sense for a sandwich and a cookie. The cashier's near-\(17\) answer came from letting the tenths of \(12.5\) share a column with the hundredths of \(0.45\), adding digits of different sizes as though they matched and inflating a few cents into a few dollars. So the correct total is \(\boxed{12.95}\) dollars.
Key idea
Adding and subtracting decimals. This is not new addition. Line up the decimal points so tenths sit over tenths and hundredths over hundredths, since the point marks where the ones place stops. Pad a shorter number with trailing zeros, which is fair because a zero on the end is worth nothing. Then add or subtract column by column the way you always have, and the answer's point drops straight down under the points above. Line up the right edges instead and you stack tenths on hundredths, which is nonsense.
A herb bed measures \(0.6\) m by \(0.7\) m. Write \(0.6=\tfrac{6}{10}\), \(0.7=\tfrac{7}{10}\), multiply as fractions, and read off the decimal area. What is \(0.6 \times 0.7\) in square metres?
Show solution
Trade the decimals for their fraction meanings, \(0.6 = \tfrac{6}{10}\) and \(0.7 = \tfrac{7}{10}\). Multiply across the top and across the bottom, \(\tfrac{6}{10}\times\tfrac{7}{10} = \tfrac{6\times 7}{10\times 10} = \tfrac{42}{100}\). That bottom of \(100\) is the hundredths place, so \(\tfrac{42}{100}\) reads as forty-two hundredths, which is \(0.42\). Notice the shortcut hiding here. You multiplied \(6\times 7 = 42\) as whole numbers, and the two factors had one decimal place each, so the answer carries two decimal places. Count the places, place the point, done. The bed is \(0.42\) square metres. \(\boxed{0.42}\)
Your gut might say \(0.3 \times 0.2 = 0.6\). Resist it and compute \(\tfrac{3}{10} \times \tfrac{2}{10}\) instead. What is \(0.3 \times 0.2\)?
Show solution
Switch to fractions so nothing can hide. \(0.3 = \tfrac{3}{10}\) and \(0.2 = \tfrac{2}{10}\), and multiplying fractions means multiply across the top and across the bottom. So \(\tfrac{3}{10} \times \tfrac{2}{10} = \tfrac{3 \times 2}{10 \times 10} = \tfrac{6}{100}\). Now read that fraction as a decimal. A denominator of \(100\) puts the digits two places past the point, so \(\tfrac{6}{100}\) is not \(0.6\), it is \(0.06\). The \(6\) sits in the hundredths place, and a leading zero holds the empty tenths place so the value is not overstated. The product came out smaller than both \(0.3\) and \(0.2\), which is exactly what taking a fraction of a fraction does. So \(0.3 \times 0.2 = \boxed{0.06}\).
Key idea
Multiplying decimals. Ignore the points and multiply the digits like plain whole numbers, then place the point by counting. Add up how many decimal places the two factors have between them, and the product gets exactly that many. It works because each factor is a whole number over a power of ten, and multiplying the bottoms means one place times one place lands on \(10 \times 10 = 100\), giving two places. So \(0.3 \times 0.2\): digits give \(6\), two places total, so the answer is \(0.06\), not \(0.6\).
Key idea
Multiplying by a number below one shrinks the result. You are taking only a part of what you started with, so \(0.3 \times 0.2\) has to land below both \(0.3\) and \(0.2\), and it does, at \(0.06\). The length of a decimal is a trick of the eye: \(0.06\) is longer to read than \(0.6\) yet ten times smaller, because size is set by the leftmost place, not by how many digits trail behind.
Almonds cost \(0.25\) dollars per scoop. You take \(7\) scoops. Multiply \(7 \times 25 = 175\), then place the decimal point. What do you owe in dollars?
Show solution
Strip the point and multiply the digits like ordinary whole numbers, \(7 \times 25 = 175\). Now total the decimal places in the two factors. The whole number \(7\) carries zero places and \(0.25\) carries two, so the product keeps \(0 + 2 = 2\) places. Placing the point two digits from the right turns \(175\) into \(1.75\). A quick gut check, \(0.25\) is a quarter of a dollar, so seven quarters is \(1.75\) dollars, exactly as expected. So \(7 \times 0.25 = \boxed{1.75}\) dollars.
A recipe needs \(0.2\) of a \(0.04\) gram pinch of saffron. Multiply: \(4 \times 2 = 8\), count three total decimal places, and place the point. How many grams is that?
Show solution
Ignore the decimal points and multiply the digits, \(4 \times 2 = 8\). Next, count how many decimal places the answer must have by adding the places in each factor. \(0.04\) has two decimal places and \(0.2\) has one, so the product needs \(2 + 1 = 3\) places. The single digit \(8\) only reaches one place on its own, so you pack two zeros in front to push it out to the third place, the thousandths, giving \(0.008\). To check, rewrite the factors as fractions, \(\tfrac{4}{100} \times \tfrac{2}{10} = \tfrac{8}{1000}\), and a denominator of \(1{,}000\) is exactly the thousandths place where the zeros parked the \(8\). The recipe uses \(\boxed{0.008}\) grams of saffron.
Key idea
Padding zeros when the digit count is short. The places-add count does not say how long the answer is, it says how many digits must end up after the point. So when the whole-number product is too short to reach that far, write zeros in front of it until the last digit lands where the count demands, the same way eight thousandths must be \(0.008\) and not \(0.8\). Each of those front zeros is holding an empty place, not just sitting there for looks.
Fun fact
The US chose base-ten money in 1792, dividing the dollar into 100 cents. Britain held out until 1971, when it replaced 240 pence per pound with 100 pence, an event called Decimal Day.
A \(4.5\) m rope is cut into \(0.5\) m pieces. Write \(4.5 \div 0.5\) as \(\tfrac{4.5}{0.5}\), multiply top and bottom by \(10\) to get \(\tfrac{45}{5}\), then divide. How many pieces?
Show solution
Start by writing the division as a fraction, \(\tfrac{4.5}{0.5}\). To make the divisor whole, multiply the top and bottom by \(10\), which is the same as multiplying by \(\tfrac{10}{10} = 1\), so the value cannot change. Multiplying by \(10\) slides each point one place right, giving \(\tfrac{45}{5}\). Now it is a clean whole-number division, and \(5 \times 9 = 45\), so \(45 \div 5 = 9\). Notice the slide moved both numbers by the same amount, which is exactly why the answer matches the original. A \(4.5\) m rope cut into \(0.5\) m pieces gives \(\boxed{9}\) pieces.
Find \(1.44 \div 1.2\). Write it as \(\tfrac{1.44}{1.2}\), multiply top and bottom by \(10\) to clear the divisor, then divide. What is the quotient?
Show solution
Start from \(\tfrac{1.44}{1.2}\). The divisor has one decimal place, so multiply the top and the bottom by \(10\). The bottom becomes \(12\) and the top becomes \(14.4\), so the problem is now \(14.4 \div 12\) with the same value as before. Divide a decimal by a whole number by keeping the point in place and carrying it straight up. Twelve fits into \(14\) once, leaving \(2.4\), and twelve fits into \(2.4\) exactly \(0.2\) times, so the quotient is \(1.2\). You can check it the easy way, since \(1.2 \times 1.2 = 1.44\). So \(1.44 \div 1.2 = \boxed{1.2}\).
Key idea
Dividing by a decimal. Slide both numbers the same number of places right, just enough to make the divisor whole, then divide the plain way you already know. Sliding both by the same amount is multiplying the top and bottom of the fraction by the same power of ten, so it stays an equivalent fraction and the quotient never changes. Now all three moves rhyme: line up the points to add and subtract, count the places to multiply, slide the point to divide.
A maker has \(3\) spools of \(1.2\) m wire each and uses \(2.75\) m. Find the total wire on hand, then subtract what is used. How many metres are left?
Show solution
Step one, find the wire on hand. Three spools of \(1.2\) m each is a whole number times a decimal, so multiply the digits and then place the point. \(3 \times 12 = 36\), and \(1.2\) carries one decimal place, so the answer carries one place too. That gives \(3 \times 1.2 = 3.6\) m on hand. Step two, take away what the build uses. Line up the decimal points and pad \(3.6\) to \(3.60\) so both numbers show hundredths. $$3.60 - 2.75 = 0.85$$ So \(0.85\) m of copper wire is left coiled up. \(\boxed{0.85}\)

Review problems

Review 1. Two reef stretches measure \(4.3\) m and \(5.6\) m. Line up the decimal points and add. What is \(4.3 + 5.6\)?
Show solution
Line up the decimal points so each place value sits in its own column. Both numbers already show one decimal place, so nothing needs padding. Add the tenths, \(3 + 6 = 9\) tenths. Add the ones, \(4 + 5 = 9\). Bring the decimal point straight down into the answer. That gives \(4.3 + 5.6 = 9.9\). The total length of healthy reef is \(\boxed{9.9}\) meters.
Review 2. A stack holds \(7.2\) liters. You pour out \(3.85\) liters. Pad \(7.2\) to \(7.20\) and subtract. How many liters remain?
Show solution
Pad the top number so both have two decimal places, giving \(7.20 - 3.85\). Line up the decimal points and subtract column by column from the right. In the hundredths place \(0\) is less than \(5\), so borrow from the tenths, making it \(10 - 5 = 5\). The tenths place is now \(1\), and \(1\) is less than \(8\), so borrow from the ones, making it \(11 - 8 = 3\). The ones place is now \(6\), and \(6 - 3 = 3\). Bring the decimal point straight down to get \(3.35\). So the stack has \(\boxed{3.35}\) liters left.
Review 3. A drone starts with \(6\) units of battery and the trip uses \(2.35\) units. Write \(6\) as \(6.00\) and subtract. How many units remain?
Show solution
The only trick is giving the whole number a decimal part to subtract from. Write \(6\) as \(6.00\), the same value with two decimal places, and stack it over \(2.35\) with the points aligned. $$\begin{array}{r} 6.00 \\ -\,2.35 \\ \hline \end{array}$$ Work right to left. The hundredths column needs \(0 - 5\), so borrow to get \(10 - 5 = 5\). The tenths column is now \(9 - 3 = 6\) after the borrow cascades, and the ones column is \(5 - 2 = 3\). Bring the point straight down. $$\begin{array}{r} 6.00 \\ -\,2.35 \\ \hline 3.65 \end{array}$$ A quick check, \(2.35 + 3.65 = 6.00\), back to a full battery. So \(\boxed{3.65}\) units of charge are left.
Review 4. A square sticker measures \(0.8\) cm by \(0.4\) cm. Multiply the digits, count two total decimal places, and place the point. What is the area?
Show solution
First ignore the decimal points and multiply the digits like whole numbers, so \(8 \times 4 = 32\). Now count how many decimal places the factors had together. The number \(0.8\) has one place and \(0.4\) has one place, which makes two places in all. Give the product two decimal places by writing \(32\) as \(0.32\). So \(0.8 \times 0.4 = \boxed{0.32}\).
Review 5. Fog covers \(0.6\) of a window. By noon it blocks only \(0.5\) of that area. What fraction of the whole window is still fogged? Give your answer as a decimal.
Show solution
The fog used to cover \(0.6\) of the window. Now it blocks half of that, so multiply. \(0.5 \times 0.6\). First multiply the digits, \(5 \times 6 = 30\). Each factor has one decimal place, so the product needs two decimal places, giving \(0.30\). Notice it landed below both \(0.5\) and \(0.6\), since taking a part of a part shrinks the amount. \(\boxed{0.30}\)
Review 6. A vial holds \(2.5\) grams of saffron. A recipe uses \(0.4\) of the vial. What is \(2.5 \times 0.4\) in grams?
Show solution
Set the points aside and multiply like whole numbers, \(25 \times 4 = 100\). Now count the decimal places in the factors. There is one in \(2.5\) and one in \(0.4\), two together, so the product needs two decimal places. Counting two spots from the right of \(100\) gives \(1.00\). The trailing zeros after the point are just empty padding, so \(1.00\) is simply \(1\). Here is the small surprise worth keeping. Two numbers that both carry decimals can multiply to a clean whole number, because the decimal places happened to fill in exactly. So \(2.5 \times 0.4 = \boxed{1}\) gram.
Review 7. A reading is \(0.03\) grams. Another mass is \(0.3\) of that first amount. What is \(0.03 \times 0.3\)?
Show solution
Start with the digits and forget the decimals. Multiplying \(3 \times 3\) gives \(9\). Now count how many decimal places the factors have. The number \(0.03\) has two places and \(0.3\) has one place, for a total of three. The product must therefore have three decimal places. Writing \(9\) with three places means filling in zeros, so \(9\) sits in the thousandths spot as \(0.009\). \(\boxed{0.009}\)
Review 8. Slide both numbers in \(7.2 \div 0.9\) one place right to make the divisor whole. What is \(7.2 \div 0.9\)?
Show solution
Sliding both numbers one place to the right means multiplying each by \(10\), and doing the same thing to both keeps the quotient the same. So \(7.2 \div 0.9\) becomes \(72 \div 9\). Now the divisor is a whole number and the work is easy. Since \(9 \times 8 = 72\), we get \(72 \div 9 = 8\). Therefore \(7.2 \div 0.9 = \boxed{8}\).
Review 9. A ribbon is \(2.56\) m long. A pattern piece needs \(1.6\) m. How many times as long as one strip is the whole ribbon? Find \(2.56 \div 1.6\).
Show solution
Start by making the divisor whole. Slide both points one place to the right, so \(2.56\) becomes \(25.6\) and \(1.6\) becomes \(16\). The quotient does not change, so \(2.56 \div 1.6 = 25.6 \div 16\). Now divide. \(16\) goes into \(25\) once, leaving \(9\). Bring down the \(6\) to make \(96\), and \(16 \times 6 = 96\), so the next digit is \(6\). Keeping the decimal point in place gives \(1.6\). Check by multiplying back, \(1.6 \times 1.6 = 2.56\), which matches the ribbon. So the ribbon is \(\boxed{1.6}\) times as long as one strip.
Review 10. Add \(0.08\) mm and \(0.9\) mm. Pad the shorter number so both show two decimal places, then add. What is \(0.08 + 0.9\)?
Show solution
Line up the decimal points and pad \(0.9\) so it has two places, giving \(0.90\). The inner zero in \(0.08\) keeps the \(8\) sitting in the hundredths place, so nothing drifts.
Add column by column. Hundredths: \(8 + 0 = 8\). Tenths: \(0 + 9 = 9\). The decimal point stays put.
$$0.08 + 0.90 = 0.98$$ So the total length is \(\boxed{0.98}\) of a millimeter.
Review 11. A kit needs half of a \(1.4\) m wire length. Multiply \(1.4 \times 0.5\). Count decimal places and place the point. What is the result?
Show solution
Ignore the points for a moment and multiply the digits. \(14 \times 5 = 70\). Now count the decimal places in the factors. There is one place in \(1.4\) and one place in \(0.5\), which is two places in total. Place the point so the answer has two decimal places, giving \(0.70\). That matches the idea of taking half of \(1.4\), which is \(0.7\). \(1.4 \times 0.5 = \boxed{0.70}\)
Review 12. You buy \(4\) jars of jam at \(0.85\) dollars each and pay with a \(5\) dollar bill. Find the total cost, then subtract from \(5\). How much change do you get?
Show solution
Start with the cost of the jam. Multiply \(4 \times 0.85\). Ignoring the point for a moment, \(4 \times 85 = 340\). The factor \(0.85\) has two digits after the point, so the product does too, giving \(4 \times 0.85 = 3.40\). Now find the change. Write the \(5\) dollar bill as \(5.00\) so the points line up, then subtract. \(5.00 - 3.40 = 1.60\). So your change is \(\boxed{1.60}\) dollars.
