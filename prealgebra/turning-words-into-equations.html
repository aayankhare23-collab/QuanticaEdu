Prealgebra · Lesson 6.4

Turning Words into Equations

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Every word problem is one fact written twice, once in plain English and once in algebra, and the two versions say the identical thing. A sentence like a number tripled and then dropped by four lands on eleven is just \(3x - 4 = 11\) wearing a coat of words. Use the same five steps every time: read it all the way through, name the unknown with a letter, translate each phrase by its meaning, solve, then answer the exact question asked and check it against the original story.

An arcade prize machine doubles the bronze tokens you feed it and drops in \(7\) bonus tokens, and after Priya's turn the screen reads \(31\). In other words, seven more than twice her tokens is thirty-one. How many bronze tokens did Priya feed the machine?
Show solution
Start by naming the unknown. Let \(x\) be the number of bronze tokens Priya fed the machine. Now build the equation by translating the words one piece at a time. "Twice her tokens" is \(2x\). "Seven more than" that means you add seven, giving \(2x + 7\). The phrase "is thirty-one" supplies the equals sign, so the equation is \(2x + 7 = 31\). Subtract \(7\) from both sides to get \(2x = 24\), then divide both sides by \(2\) to find \(x = 12\). The question asks how many tokens Priya fed the machine, which is exactly \(x\), so she fed in \(12\) tokens. Read it back against the words to check. Double \(12\) is \(24\), and seven more is \(31\), matching the screen. \(\boxed{12}\)
A beekeeper scoops out exactly a third of whatever a jar holds, pours it into a bottle, then stirs in \(9\) more ounces. The bottle then weighs \(17\) ounces, since here the word of means multiply. How many ounces of honey did the jar hold at the very start?
Show solution
Let \(x\) be the number of ounces the jar held at the very start. Now translate the words piece by piece. A third of that amount is \(\tfrac{1}{3}x\), increasing it by nine ounces adds \(9\), and the words tell you this is seventeen, so the equation is \(\tfrac{1}{3}x + 9 = 17\). Subtract \(9\) from both sides to get \(\tfrac{1}{3}x = 8\). Multiply both sides by \(3\) to undo the one third, which gives \(x = 24\). Read it back against the words to check. A third of \(24\) is \(8\) ounces, and nine more ounces brings it to \(17\), which matches the scale. The question asks how many ounces the jar held at the start, so the answer is \(\boxed{24}\) ounces.
Maya tapes streamers to a wall, and the number on the wall is nineteen fewer than the number in her box, which counts out to \(40\). Watch the order: fewer than subtracts from the number, so this is \(x - 19\), not \(19 - x\). How many streamers were in Maya's box to start with?
Show solution
Let \(x\) be the number of streamers in Maya's box to start with. The tricky phrase is "nineteen fewer than the number." Fewer than subtracts the nineteen from the number, and it reverses the order you hear, so it translates to \(x - 19\), not \(19 - x\). Since that equals forty, the equation is \(x - 19 = 40\). Add 19 to both sides to get \(x = 59\). Now check against the words. Nineteen fewer than 59 is \(59 - 19 = 40\), which matches the count on the wall. The wrong reading \(19 - x\) would have given \(19 - 59 = -40\), a negative number of streamers, which makes no sense, so the order really does flip. The question asks how many streamers were in the box to start, which is \(x\), so the box held \(\boxed{59}\) streamers.
Key idea
The five steps. First name what the letter stands for and write that line down, so you do not solve for the wrong thing. Then translate the sentence into an equation one phrase at a time, by meaning rather than left to right, since a phrase like seven less than the number is \(x - 7\), not \(7 - x\). Solve with the tools you already have, report the exact quantity the question asked for, and finally read your answer back into the original sentence to be sure it fits.
A workshop has two number machines. The first triples a secret number and subtracts \(5\), and its readout shows \(40\). The second doubles that same number and adds \(2\). First solve for the secret number, then feed it into the second machine. What does the second machine's readout show?
Show solution
Let \(n\) be the secret number. The first machine triples \(n\) and subtracts five, and the readout shows forty, so the words become \(3n - 5 = 40\). Add five to both sides to get \(3n = 45\), then divide by three to get \(n = 15\). Here is the trap. It is tempting to type \(15\) and call it done, but \(15\) is the secret number, not the thing the question asked for. The question asks what the second machine reads. The second machine doubles the number and adds two, so its reading is \(2n + 2\). Substitute \(n = 15\) to get \(2 \cdot 15 + 2 = 30 + 2 = 32\). Read it back against the words. The first machine triples \(15\) to \(45\) and subtracts \(5\) to show \(40\), which matches. Feed \(15\) into the second machine, double it to \(30\), add \(2\), and the readout shows \(32\). The variable \(n\) was just a helper to find the secret number. The answer to the question asked is \(\boxed{32}\).
Key idea
Two traps. First, words do not always run in the same order as the math. Phrases like less than, fewer than, and subtracted from flip the two pieces, so seven less than a number is \(x - 7\) while a number subtracted from seven is \(7 - x\). Read for meaning, not left to right. Second, the letter you pick is just a helper, and the question often asks for something else, so underline the actual question and translate your answer back at the end.
A rooftop garden has \(96\) plants, all marigolds and lavender, with three times as many lavender as marigolds. Let \(m\) be the number of marigolds, so the lavender count is \(3m\) and the two add to \(96\). The question asks for lavender, not \(m\). How many lavender plants are there?
Show solution
Start by naming the quantity that everything else leans on. Let \(m\) be the number of marigolds. The garden has three times as many lavender plants as marigolds, so the lavender count is \(3m\). The two kinds are the whole garden, and the whole garden is \(96\) plants, so the marigolds plus the lavender give \(m + 3m = 96\). Combine the like terms to get \(4m = 96\), and divide both sides by \(4\) to find \(m = 24\). So there are \(24\) marigolds. Now reread the question. It does not ask for marigolds, it asks for lavender, which is \(3m\). That is \(3 \cdot 24 = 72\). The \(24\) was just the variable you chose along the way, not the thing being asked. Check it against the words. There are \(24\) marigolds and \(72\) lavender, and \(24 + 72 = 96\) plants in all, and \(72\) is indeed three times \(24\). The number of lavender plants is \(\boxed{72}\).
Key idea
The variable is a tool, not always the prize. In the garden problem all the algebra was right and \(m\) came out to \(24\), but \(24\) was the marigold count, while the question asked for lavender, so the real answer was \(72\). Before you start, find the actual question and underline it. Once you have your number, ask whether it is what the question wanted, and if not, take the one more step to turn the variable into the quantity asked for.
A \(150\) cm ribbon is cut into two pieces, with the longer piece \(30\) cm more than the shorter. Let \(x\) be the shorter piece, so the longer is \(x + 30\) and the two add to \(150\). The question asks for the longer piece, not \(x\). How long is the longer piece, in centimeters?
Show solution
There are two lengths to find, but tying them together lets one letter do all the work. Let \(x\) be the length of the shorter piece in centimeters. The longer piece is \(30\) centimeters longer than the shorter one, so it is \(x + 30\). Notice that the second unknown is now written in terms of the first, so the whole problem rides on a single variable. The two pieces came from one ribbon, so their lengths add to \(150\). That gives the equation $$x + (x + 30) = 150.$$ Combine the like terms to get \(2x + 30 = 150\). Subtract \(30\) from both sides to reach \(2x = 120\), then divide both sides by \(2\) to find \(x = 60\). So the shorter piece is \(60\) centimeters. The question did not ask for \(x\), it asked for the longer piece, which is \(x + 30 = 60 + 30 = 90\). Read it back against the words to check. The shorter piece is \(60\) and the longer is \(90\), they differ by \(30\), and \(60 + 90 = 150\), matching the full ribbon. The longer piece is \(\boxed{90}\) centimeters.
Key idea
One variable for two unknowns. When two unknown amounts are locked together by a total you already know, you never need two letters. Call one of them \(x\), and since the pieces must add to the total, the other is just the total minus \(x\). Now everything is written with the one variable you know how to solve for, and the two-thing puzzle quietly becomes an ordinary one-variable equation.

That dictionary is the whole vocabulary. Every word problem is built from those same few phrases, so nothing genuinely new is coming. What changes is the richness of the situation, how many phrases stack into one story and which quantity the question finally asks for. The next problems run on the same five steps in fuller settings: counts of two different kinds of thing, a choice between two pricing plans, ages a few years apart, and runs of consecutive whole numbers. Same toolkit, bigger rooms.

A puzzle box holds \(30\) tokens, each a square with \(4\) corners or a triangle with \(3\) corners, and all the corners together count \(104\). Let \(s\) be the number of squares, so the triangles are \(30 - s\), the key move being that the second category is the total minus the first. The question asks for triangles. How many triangle tokens are in the box?
Show solution
Start by naming what you do not yet know. Let \(s\) be the number of square tokens. The box holds \(30\) tokens in all, and every token that is not a square is a triangle, so the number of triangle tokens is \(30 - s\). That is the key move, the second category is the total minus the first.

Now translate the corner count. Each square contributes \(4\) corners and each triangle contributes \(3\) corners, and the grand total is \(104\). That gives the equation $$4s + 3(30 - s) = 104.$$ Distribute the \(3\) to get \(4s + 90 - 3s = 104\). Combine the like terms \(4s\) and \(-3s\) into \(s\), so \(s + 90 = 104\). Subtract \(90\) from both sides to find \(s = 14\), which means there are \(14\) square tokens.

The question did not ask for squares though, it asked for triangles. The triangles are \(30 - s = 30 - 14 = 16\). Read it back against the words to be sure. With \(14\) squares and \(16\) triangles you have \(14 + 16 = 30\) tokens, and the corners come to \(4 \times 14 + 3 \times 16 = 56 + 48 = 104\), exactly as stated. So the box holds \(\boxed{16}\) triangle tokens.
A street magician's riddle gives two descriptions of the same spot. Half her secret number with four shaved off lands exactly where a third of her number with three piled on lands, so \(\tfrac{1}{2}x - 4 = \tfrac{1}{3}x + 3\). Clear the fractions, gather the variable, and solve. What is her secret number?
Show solution
Let \(x\) be the magician's secret number. The riddle gives you two descriptions of the same spot, so they must be equal. The first half, "half of my number and shave off four", is \(\tfrac{1}{2}x - 4\). The second half, "a third of my number and pile on three", is \(\tfrac{1}{3}x + 3\). Setting them equal gives the equation \(\tfrac{1}{2}x - 4 = \tfrac{1}{3}x + 3\). The variable sits on both sides and the fractions are awkward, so clear the denominators first by multiplying every term by \(6\). That gives \(3x - 24 = 2x + 18\). Subtract \(2x\) from both sides to get \(x - 24 = 18\), then add \(24\) to both sides to find \(x = 42\). Read it back against the words to be sure. Half of \(42\) is \(21\), and shaving off four leaves \(17\). A third of \(42\) is \(14\), and piling on three gives \(17\). Both land on the same spot, so the secret number is \(\boxed{42}\).
Key idea
No new tricks, just familiar equations. When a sentence drops part of the number on each side of the word equals, the equation that falls out simply has the variable on both sides, the kind you beat in 6.3, so gather the variable terms and finish. When fractions of the number show up, do not panic: multiply every term by a common multiple to wipe the denominators first, then gather. Translating is just reading carefully enough to write an equation you already know how to solve.
A climbing gym offers two plans. The Steady plan charges a flat \(\$12\) per month plus \(\$0.20\) per session, and the Casual plan charges a flat \(\$4\) per month plus \(\$0.30\) per session. For few sessions Casual is cheaper, for many Steady wins, so somewhere they cost the same. How many sessions in one month make the two plans cost the same amount?
Show solution
Start by naming the unknown. Let \(s\) be the number of sessions you book in the month. To stay exact, work in cents, so the flat \(\$12\) is \(1200\) cents and \(\$0.20\) per session is \(20s\) cents. That makes the Steady plan cost \(1200 + 20s\) cents. The Casual plan has a \(\$4\) flat fee, which is \(400\) cents, plus \(\$0.30\) per session, so it costs \(400 + 30s\) cents. The words "cost the same" turn straight into an equals sign, which gives \(1200 + 20s = 400 + 30s\). Subtract \(20s\) from both sides to gather the variable on the right, leaving \(1200 = 400 + 10s\). Subtract \(400\) from both sides to get \(800 = 10s\), and divide both sides by \(10\) to find \(s = 80\). Now read it back against the words. At \(80\) sessions the Steady plan costs \(1200 + 20 \cdot 80 = 2800\) cents and the Casual plan costs \(400 + 30 \cdot 80 = 2800\) cents, and both come out to \(\$28\), so the two plans really do match. The question asked for the number of sessions that make the plans cost the same, which is \(\boxed{80}\).
Marisol notices that six years from now her tortoise Pebble will be exactly seven sixths of its current age. Let \(t\) be Pebble's age now, so its future age is \(t + 6\), and that equals \(\tfrac{7}{6}t\). Clear the fraction and solve. How old is Pebble right now, in years?
Show solution
Let \(t\) be Pebble's age right now, in years. That is the number the question is really after. Six years from now Pebble will be six years older, so the future age is \(t + 6\). The words tell us that future age is seven sixths of the current age, which means \(t + 6 = \tfrac{7}{6}t\). To get rid of the fraction, multiply every term by \(6\), giving \(6t + 36 = 7t\). Subtract \(6t\) from both sides and you are left with \(36 = t\), so \(t = 36\). The question asked for Pebble's age now, and that is exactly what \(t\) stands for. Check it against the words. In six years Pebble would be \(36 + 6 = 42\), and seven sixths of \(36\) is \(\tfrac{7}{6}\cdot 36 = 42\) as well, so the future age really does match. Pebble is \(\boxed{36}\) years old now.
Fun fact
About 4,000 years ago Egyptian scribes posed "aha" puzzles: find the heap such that the heap plus a fraction of itself equals a total. All in words, no symbols, yet the logic is exactly this.
Three steel rings have weights that are consecutive even numbers of kilograms and total \(96\) kg. Let the lightest be \(n\), so the three weights are \(n\), \(n + 2\), and \(n + 4\). Set their sum equal to \(96\) and solve for \(n\). The question asks for the heaviest ring, not \(n\). What does the heaviest ring weigh, in kilograms?
Show solution
Start by naming the unknown. Let \(n\) be the weight of the lightest ring in kilograms. Since the rings are consecutive even numbers, each one is \(2\) kilograms heavier than the last, so the middle ring weighs \(n + 2\) and the heaviest weighs \(n + 4\). The three weights together read \(96\) kilograms, which turns the words into an equation. $$n + (n + 2) + (n + 4) = 96$$ Combine the like terms on the left. The three copies of \(n\) make \(3n\), and the loose numbers \(2\) and \(4\) make \(6\), so the equation becomes $$3n + 6 = 96.$$ Subtract \(6\) from both sides to get \(3n = 90\), then divide both sides by \(3\) to find \(n = 30\). So the lightest ring is \(30\) kilograms, the middle ring is \(32\), and the heaviest is \(34\). Here is the trap worth naming. The variable \(n\) came out to \(30\), but the question never asked for the lightest ring. It asked for the heaviest, which is \(n + 4 = 34\). Check it against the words. The weights \(30\), \(32\), and \(34\) step up by \(2\) each time and add to \(30 + 32 + 34 = 96\), and \(34\) is indeed the largest of the three. The heaviest ring weighs \(\boxed{34}\) kilograms.
Key idea
Consecutive integers. When integers run one after another, anchor the first to \(n\) and write the rest as \(n+1\), \(n+2\), and so on, stepping by \(2\) instead for consecutive even or odd integers, so \(n\), \(n+2\), \(n+4\). Add them, set the sum equal to the given total, solve for \(n\), then add the right step to read off the exact member asked for. Always check by reading your answer back into the original sentence, since a misread equation is still satisfied by its own wrong answer.
A baker fills exactly \(40\) boxes, each a small box holding \(6\) cupcakes or a large box holding \(10\), for a total of \(312\) cupcakes. Let \(s\) be the small boxes, so the large boxes are \(40 - s\). Count the cupcakes, solve, then answer for the large boxes. How many large boxes did she fill?
Show solution
Let \(s\) be the number of small boxes the baker fills. Every box is either small or large and there are \(40\) boxes altogether, so the two counts add to \(40\) and the number of large boxes is \(40 - s\). That single relationship is what lets one variable carry the whole problem. Now count cupcakes. The small boxes hold \(6\) each, so they account for \(6s\) cupcakes, and the large boxes hold \(10\) each, so they account for \(10(40 - s)\) cupcakes. The total is \(312\), which gives the equation $$6s + 10(40 - s) = 312.$$ Distribute the \(10\) to get \(6s + 400 - 10s = 312\), then combine the like terms \(6s\) and \(-10s\) to get \(-4s + 400 = 312\). Subtract \(400\) from both sides, so \(-4s = -88\), and divide by \(-4\) to find \(s = 22\). So there are \(22\) small boxes. The question did not ask for \(s\), it asked for the large boxes, which is \(40 - s = 40 - 22 = 18\). Check it against the words. With \(22\) small boxes and \(18\) large boxes the total is \(22 + 18 = 40\) boxes, and the cupcakes come to \(6 \cdot 22 + 10 \cdot 18 = 132 + 180 = 312\), both exactly right. The baker filled \(\boxed{18}\) large boxes.

Review problems

Review 1. A magician guessing the number on your card says, four more than triple your number lands exactly on thirty-one. What number is on the card?
Show solution
Let \(x\) be the number written on the card. Triple the number is \(3x\), and four more than that is \(3x + 4\). The clue says this lands on thirty-one, so the equation is $$3x + 4 = 31.$$ Subtract 4 from both sides to get \(3x = 27\), then divide both sides by 3 to get \(x = 9\). The question asks for the number on the card, which is \(\boxed{9}\). Check against the words. Triple of 9 is 27, and four more than 27 is 31, exactly as the magician said.
Review 2. A lighthouse keeper logs ships each night. Wednesday's count was eight less than Tuesday's and came to \(23\), and eight less than Tuesday's number is \(x - 8\). How many ships passed on Tuesday?
Show solution
Let \(x\) be the number of ships that passed on Tuesday. The phrase "eight less than Tuesday's number" tells you to begin with Tuesday's count and remove 8, which is \(x - 8\). Watch the order, because "less than" flips it. It is \(x - 8\), never \(8 - x\). That Wednesday count equals 23, so the equation is $$x - 8 = 23.$$ Add 8 to both sides to get \(x = 23 + 8\), so \(x = 31\). The question asks for Tuesday's count, and that is exactly the \(x\) we solved for, so the answer is \(\boxed{31}\). Check it against the words. Eight less than 31 is \(31 - 8 = 23\), which matches the Wednesday count, so 31 is correct.
Review 3. A museum desk starts the morning with \(50\) maps and \(12\) remain by lunch. The number handed out subtracted from \(50\) leaves \(12\). How many maps were handed out?
Show solution
Let \(x\) stand for the number of maps handed out during the morning. The phrase that builds the equation is that number subtracted from the fifty, which means the fifty is the starting pile and \(x\) is taken away from it, written as \(50 - x\). What remains is twelve, so the equation is $$50 - x = 12.$$ Solve for \(x\). Add \(x\) to both sides to get \(50 = 12 + x\), then subtract \(12\) from both sides, which gives \(50 - 12 = x\), so \(x = 38\). The question asks how many maps were handed out, and that is exactly the number \(x\), so the answer is \(38\). Check it against the words. Thirty-eight maps subtracted from the original fifty leaves \(50 - 38 = 12\) maps still on the desk, which matches the twelve the guide found. So the number of maps handed out is \(\boxed{38}\).
Review 4. Doubling the count of new birdhouses and adding \(7\) gives \(33\). Solve for the count, then find how many would remain if five were taken down. How many birdhouses would be left?
Show solution
Let \(x\) be the number of birdhouses the crew hung. Doubling the count gives \(2x\), and adding seven gives \(2x + 7\). The words say this equals thirty-three, so the equation is \(2x + 7 = 33\). Subtract \(7\) from both sides to get \(2x = 26\), then divide by \(2\) to get \(x = 13\). The crew hung \(13\) birdhouses. But the question asks how many would be left if five were taken down, which is \(13 - 5 = 8\). So the answer is \(\boxed{8}\). Check against the words. Double \(13\) to get \(26\), then add \(7\) to get \(33\), which matches "is thirty-three", and \(13 - 5 = 8\) leftover.
Review 5. Two bakery trays hold \(45\) croissants together, and the window tray holds \(9\) fewer than the kitchen tray. How many croissants are on the kitchen tray?
Show solution
Let \(k\) be the number of croissants on the kitchen tray. The window tray holds 9 fewer than the kitchen tray, so it holds \(k - 9\). The two trays together hold 45, which gives the equation $$k + (k - 9) = 45.$$ Combine the like terms to get \(2k - 9 = 45\). Add 9 to both sides to get \(2k = 54\), then divide by 2 to get \(k = 27\). The question asks for the kitchen tray, which is exactly the variable, so the answer is \(\boxed{27}\). Check it against the words. The kitchen tray has 27, the window tray has \(27 - 9 = 18\), and together they hold \(27 + 18 = 45\), which matches.
Review 6. A llama drinks four times as much water as a goat, and together they drink \(40\) liters. How many liters did the llama drink?
Show solution
Let \(g\) be the number of liters the goat drank. The llama drank four times as much, so the llama drank \(4g\). Together they drank \(40\) liters, which gives the equation $$g + 4g = 40.$$ Combine the like terms to get \(5g = 40\), then divide both sides by \(5\) to find \(g = 8\). That is the goat's amount, but the question asks how much the llama drank, so report \(4g = 4 \times 8 = 32\) liters. Check it against the words. The goat drank \(8\) liters and the llama drank \(32\) liters, the llama's \(32\) is four times the goat's \(8\), and \(8 + 32 = 40\), which matches the total. The llama drank \(\boxed{32}\) liters.
Review 7. A robotics club builds \(26\) machines, each a roller with \(4\) wheels or a hopper with \(2\) wheels, totaling \(84\) wheels. How many hoppers did they build?
Show solution
Let \(c\) be the number of rollers. Since every machine is a roller or a hopper and there are 26 in all, the number of hoppers is \(26 - c\). Each roller has 4 wheels and each hopper has 2 wheels, so the wheel count is \(4c + 2(26 - c) = 84\). Expand the left side to get \(4c + 52 - 2c = 84\), which simplifies to \(2c + 52 = 84\). Subtract 52 from both sides to get \(2c = 32\), so \(c = 16\). That is the number of rollers, but the question asks for hoppers, and the hoppers are \(26 - c = 26 - 16 = 10\). So the club built \(\boxed{10}\) hoppers. Check it against the words. With 16 rollers and 10 hoppers that is \(16 + 10 = 26\) machines, and the wheels come to \(4 \times 16 + 2 \times 10 = 64 + 20 = 84\), which matches both counts.
Review 8. A satellite finds that a quarter of the ice crystal count plus \(5\) equals a sixth of the count plus \(9\). How many ice crystals are in the cloud?
Show solution
Let \(x\) be the number of ice crystals in the cloud. A quarter of the count increased by five is \(\frac{1}{4}x + 5\), and a sixth of the count increased by nine is \(\frac{1}{6}x + 9\). The words say these are equal, so \(\frac{1}{4}x + 5 = \frac{1}{6}x + 9\). Multiply every term by \(12\) to clear the fractions, which gives \(3x + 60 = 2x + 108\). Subtract \(2x\) from both sides to get \(x + 60 = 108\), then subtract \(60\) to get \(x = 48\). The question asks for the number of ice crystals, so the answer is \(\boxed{48}\). Check against the words. A quarter of \(48\) is \(12\), and \(12 + 5 = 17\). A sixth of \(48\) is \(8\), and \(8 + 9 = 17\). Both sides give \(17\), so it fits.
Review 9. A club buys \(12\) glow items, each a stick at \(\$3\) or a lantern at \(\$5\), spending \(\$44\) in all. How many glow lanterns did they buy?
Show solution
Let \(p\) be the number of glow sticks the club buys. There are \(12\) items in all, so the number of lanterns is \(12 - p\). Each glow stick costs \(\$3\) and each lantern costs \(\$5\), and the total spent is \(\$44\), which gives the equation $$3p + 5(12 - p) = 44.$$ Distribute the \(5\) across the group, \(5(12 - p) = 60 - 5p\), so the equation becomes \(3p + 60 - 5p = 44\). Combine the \(p\) terms, \(3p - 5p = -2p\), leaving \(-2p + 60 = 44\). Subtract \(60\) from both sides to get \(-2p = -16\), then divide both sides by \(-2\) to find \(p = 8\). So the club bought \(8\) glow sticks. The question asks for lanterns, not glow sticks, and the number of lanterns is \(12 - p = 12 - 8 = 4\). Check it against the words. With \(8\) glow sticks and \(4\) lanterns the count is \(8 + 4 = 12\) items, and the cost is \(3 \cdot 8 + 5 \cdot 4 = 24 + 20 = 44\) dollars, both of which match. So the club bought \(\boxed{4}\) glow lanterns.
Review 10. Four years ago a boat was two thirds of its current age, so with \(t\) its age now, \(t - 4 = \tfrac{2}{3}t\). How old is the boat now, in years?
Show solution
Let \(t\) be the boat's age in years today. Four years ago its age was \(t - 4\), and the plaque says that equals two thirds of its current age, which gives \(t - 4 = \tfrac{2}{3}t\). Clear the fraction by multiplying every term by \(3\), so \(3t - 12 = 2t\). Subtract \(2t\) from both sides to get \(t - 12 = 0\), and then \(t = 12\). The question asks how old the boat is now, and the variable \(t\) is exactly that age, so the answer is \(\boxed{12}\) years. Check it against the words. Four years ago the boat was \(12 - 4 = 8\) years old, and two thirds of its current age is \(\tfrac{2}{3}\times 12 = 8\). Those match, so \(12\) is right.
Review 11. Priya and Dev's ages add to \(44\) years, and Priya is exactly three times as old as Dev. How old is Priya, in years?
Show solution
Let \(y\) be Dev's age, the younger cousin. Priya is three times as old, so her age is \(3y\). Their ages add to \(44\), which gives the equation $$y + 3y = 44.$$ Combine the like terms to get \(4y = 44\), and divide both sides by \(4\) to find \(y = 11\). So Dev is \(11\). The question asks for Priya, whose age is \(3y = 3 \cdot 11 = 33\). Check it against the words. The ages add to \(11 + 33 = 44\), and \(33\) is three times \(11\), so both conditions hold. Priya is \(\boxed{33}\) years old.
Review 12. Three hallway panels have consecutive integer widths in tiles that sum to \(72\). How many tiles wide is the widest panel?
Show solution
Let \(n\) be the width of the narrowest panel in tiles. Each panel is one tile wider than the one before it, so the three widths are \(n\), \(n+1\), and \(n+2\).
