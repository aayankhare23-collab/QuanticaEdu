Prealgebra · Lesson 5.4

Rounding and Estimation

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A step counter reads \(4{,}732\). You round to \(4{,}700\). Somewhere between \(4{,}700\) and \(4{,}800\) is a halfway mark. Whichever side you land on determines the rounded answer. What exactly does your brain check in that instant?

Round \(4{,}732\) to the nearest hundred. The midpoint between \(4{,}700\) and \(4{,}800\) is \(4{,}750\). Which neighbor is \(4{,}732\) closer to?
Show solution
The two multiples of one hundred hugging \(4{,}732\) are \(4{,}700\) below and \(4{,}800\) above. The midpoint between them is \(4{,}750\), the halfway mark of that stretch. Now compare. Since \(4{,}732\) is less than \(4{,}750\), it sits on the left half of the stretch, nearer to \(4{,}700\). You can check the nearness directly. The gap down to \(4{,}700\) is \(4{,}732 - 4{,}700 = 32\), while the gap up to \(4{,}800\) is \(4{,}800 - 4{,}732 = 68\). A jump of \(32\) is much shorter than a jump of \(68\), so \(4{,}700\) wins. If the step count had landed exactly on \(4{,}750\), the tie would go to the larger neighbor \(4{,}800\), but here there is no tie. The nearest hundred is \(\boxed{4700}\).
A festival shows \(68{,}431\) attendees. Round to the nearest thousand. The deciding digit is the hundreds digit. What is \(68{,}431\) rounded to the nearest thousand?
Show solution
First find the place you are aiming for. The nearest thousand lives in the thousands digit, which is the \(8\) in \(68{,}431\). So the answer will be either \(68{,}000\) or the next thousand up, \(69{,}000\). The whole job is deciding which one is closer. The deciding digit is always the one immediately to the right of the place you are rounding to. Here that is the hundreds digit. Reading \(68{,}431\) from the left, the hundreds digit is \(4\). Now the only question that matters, is \(4\) in the "5 or more" group or the "4 or less" group? It is \(4\), so the thousands place stays put and does not climb to \(9\). The last step is the satisfying part. Every digit to the right of the thousands place gets swept down to zero, all at once, no matter what they were. The \(4\), the \(3\), and the \(1\) all become \(0\), giving \(68{,}000\). A quick sanity check confirms it. The two candidates are \(68{,}000\) and \(69{,}000\), and the halfway mark between them is \(68{,}500\). Our count \(68{,}431\) sits below \(68{,}500\), so it really is nearer to \(68{,}000\). $$68{,}431 \approx \boxed{68{,}000}$$
Round \(2{,}950\) to the nearest hundred. The value lands exactly on the midpoint \(2{,}950\), so the tie rule sends it up. The hundreds digit is \(9\), so a carry rolls forward. What is the result?
Show solution
Mark the two hundreds on either side of \(2{,}950\). The one just below is \(2{,}900\) and the one just above is \(3{,}000\). The midpoint sits halfway between them at \(2{,}950\), which is exactly the number we have. Since we are right on the tie, the larger-value rule sends us to the upper neighbor, \(3{,}000\). Now look at the carry. Rounding up usually means nudging the hundreds digit one step higher, but here that digit is already a 9 and there is no single digit above 9. So the 9 hundreds plus one more hundred makes ten hundreds, which is a whole new thousand. The 2 in the thousands place ticks up to a 3 and the hundreds settle back to 0. That is why \(2{,}950\) does not become \(2{,}1000\), it becomes \(3{,}000\). So \(2{,}950\) rounded to the nearest hundred is \(\boxed{3000}\).
Key idea
Rounding rule. To round to a place, you really find the two nearest multiples of that place and the midpoint between them and pick the side you are closer to. But you never have to measure the gap, because the single digit just after the rounding place already tells you which half you are in. If that digit is \(5\) or more you are in the top half, so the place ticks up; if \(4\) or less you are in the bottom half, so it stays. Every digit after the rounding place drops to zero. A tie, deciding digit exactly \(5\), goes to the larger value.
A 3D printer reads \(7.83\) m of filament remaining. Round to the nearest tenth. The hundredths digit \(3\) decides. What is \(7.83\) rounded to the nearest tenth?
Show solution
Rounding to the nearest tenth means choosing whichever multiple of one tenth is closest to \(7.83\). The two candidates are the tenths just below and just above, namely \(7.8\) and \(7.9\), and the fence between them sits exactly halfway, at \(7.85\). So the whole decision comes down to one question. Is \(7.83\) below the fence or above it? The hundredths digit answers that for us, because it measures how far past \(7.8\) we have traveled. Here that digit is \(3\), so we are at \(7.8\) plus three hundredths, which is short of the five hundredths needed to reach \(7.85\). That puts \(7.83\) on the lower side of the midpoint, closer to \(7.8\) than to \(7.9\). We keep the tenths digit exactly as it is and drop everything after it. The half-way rule never even comes up, since \(3\) is not \(5\). \(\boxed{7.8}\)
A tide gauge reads \(3.617\) m. The harbor chart records to the nearest hundredth. The thousandths digit \(7\) decides. Round \(3.617\) to the nearest hundredth.
Show solution
Rounding to the nearest hundredth means choosing between the two-place values closest to the reading. Those neighbors are \(3.61\) and \(3.62\), and the fence post halfway between them is \(3.615\). The whole decision comes down to which side of that fence the reading lands on. The fast way to see it is to look at the very next digit after the hundredths place, the thousandths digit. In \(3.617\) that digit is \(7\). A thousandths digit of \(5\) or more means the reading is at or above the midpoint, so we move up to the larger neighbor. You can feel it in the distances too. From \(3.617\) down to \(3.61\) is \(0.007\), but up to \(3.62\) is only \(0.003\), so \(3.62\) is genuinely closer. Both ways agree. The harbor chart records the height as \(\boxed{3.62}\) meters.
Key idea
Tie-breaking convention. Usually the next digit clearly drops you into the top or bottom half and there is nothing to argue about. But every so often a value lands exactly on the midpoint, like \(12.5\) halfway between \(12\) and \(13\), and then "nearest" gives no answer because both ends are equally far. So this course picks one rule and uses it everywhere: an exact tie goes to the larger, more positive value. That is why a deciding digit of \(5\) with only zeros after it still rounds up, and the same larger-wins rule carries through even when the numbers turn negative.
A lab scale reads \(1.995\) grams. Round to the nearest hundredth. The thousandths digit \(5\) is a tie, so round up. The hundredths digit is \(9\), triggering a carry. What is the result?
Show solution
Start by naming the place we want and the digit that decides it. Rounding to the nearest hundredth means we keep two places after the decimal point, the \(1.99\) part of \(1.995\). The digit just past that, the thousandths digit, is the one that tells us whether to round up or stay put. In \(1.99\underline{5}\) that deciding digit is \(5\). A \(5\) sitting there with nothing but the end behind it is an exact halfway point, and our rule says a tie rounds to the larger value, so we round the hundredths place up. Now the careful part. Rounding up usually just adds \(1\) to the hundredths digit, but that digit is already \(9\). You cannot write a \(10\) in a single slot, so the \(9\) becomes \(0\) and passes a carry of \(1\) to the left, into the tenths place. The tenths digit is also a \(9\), so the same thing happens again. It becomes \(0\) and hands a carry into the ones place, which lifts the \(1\) up to a \(2\). Lining up the carry from right to left, \(1.99\) climbs to \(2.00\). The notebook still records two decimal places, so we keep both zeros and write \(2.00\) rather than just \(2\). Notice what the carry did. It quietly turned a number that started with a \(1\) into one that starts with a \(2\), so the answer actually gained a whole place when the nines toppled like a row of dominoes. $$1.995 \approx \boxed{2.00}$$
Key idea
Rounding through a 9. When the rounding place holds a \(9\) and needs to go up, it cannot, so it rolls over to \(0\) and shoves a carry into the next place left, exactly the way carrying works in addition. If that place is a \(9\) too the carry keeps marching, so every \(9\) in the carry path rolls over. That is how a number can tip up to a clean new value, like \(1.995\) climbing to \(2.00\) as both \(9\)s roll over, and if the carry runs off the front the number even gains a digit, the way \(9.96\) rounds to \(10.0\). Rounding is still just picking the nearer multiple; the carry is what "up" looks like with a \(9\) in the way.
A freezer reads \(-83\) degrees. Round to the nearest ten. The neighbors are \(-90\) and \(-80\). Measure each gap and pick the closer one. What is the result?
Show solution
Start by naming the two multiples of ten that hug \(-83\). To its left sits \(-90\), and to its right sits \(-80\). Rounding to the nearest ten just asks which of these two the number is closer to. Now measure each gap. From \(-83\) over to \(-90\) is a jump of \(7\), since \(-83-(-90)=7\). From \(-83\) over to \(-80\) is a jump of only \(3\), since \(-80-(-83)=3\). The trip to the right is much shorter, so \(-83\) is nearer to \(-80\). This is the same midpoint reasoning you use with positive numbers. The halfway point here is \(-85\), and \(-83\) sits to the right of that midpoint, on the side closer to \(-80\). Notice that \(-80\) is the larger of the two values, because it lies further right on the number line, and we landed there simply by being closer, not by any special rule. So the technician records \(\boxed{-80}\) degrees.
A thermostat reads \(-2.35\) degrees. Round to the nearest tenth. \(-2.35\) is the exact midpoint between \(-2.4\) and \(-2.3\). The tie rule rounds to the larger value. What does the display show?
Show solution
Start by finding the two tenths that \(-2.35\) sits between. Going down to the next tenth gives \(-2.4\), and going up to the next tenth gives \(-2.3\). On the number line \(-2.4\) is on the left and \(-2.3\) is on the right, since numbers grow as you move right. Now check the distances. From \(-2.35\) to \(-2.4\) is \(0.05\), and from \(-2.35\) to \(-2.3\) is also \(0.05\). The gaps match, so \(-2.35\) is the exact midpoint, a true tie. Ties do not get to pick "closer," so the one rule takes over, round to the larger value. Here is the careful part. With negatives, "larger" still means farther to the right on the number line, and the value farther right is \(-2.3\). It can feel backwards because \(2.3\) looks smaller than \(2.4\), but once you attach the minus signs, \(-2.3\) is the bigger number. So we round up toward zero, not away from it. The rounded reading is \(\boxed{-2.3}\).
Key idea
Rounding negatives. A negative number is not a special case with its own rulebook. It rounds to the nearer multiple just like everything else, and the only thing to keep straight is what larger means down here. Larger is always farther right on the number line, so between two negatives the larger one is closer to zero. When a negative value sits exactly on the midpoint, the same round-to-the-larger tie rule sends you toward zero, which is why \(-2.35\) rounds to \(-2.3\) and not \(-2.4\). Same midpoint, same tie rule, just keep your left and right straight.
A field-trip invoice lists \(29\) students at \(6.10\) dollars each, printing \(1{,}769\) dollars. Round each to a friendly value and multiply. What is your quick estimate of \(29 \times 6.10\)?
Show solution
Estimating works by swapping the messy parts for the nearest easy numbers, multiplying those, and trusting the result to land in the right neighborhood. Start with the count of students. Rounding \(29\) to the nearest ten means asking whether it is closer to \(20\) or to \(30\). It sits one step below \(30\) and nine steps above \(20\), so it rounds up to \(30\). Next round the price. Rounding \(6.10\) to the nearest whole dollar means asking whether it is closer to \(6\) or to \(7\). The ten cents barely lifts it off \(6\), nowhere near the halfway mark of \(6.50\), so it rounds down to \(6\). Now multiply the friendly parts, \(30 \times 6 = 180\). That is your ballpark. Here is the payoff. The printed total claims \(1{,}769\), but your honest estimate is only \(180\), and \(1{,}769\) is about ten times bigger. A gap that size is the unmistakable signature of a whole-digit slip, almost certainly a decimal point that drifted one spot to the right. (The exact total is actually \(29 \times 6.10 = 176.90\), comfortably close to your \(180\), which confirms the printed figure is the wrong one.) The number you would compare against \(1{,}769\) is \(\boxed{180}\).
Key idea
Estimation as an error-check. Round each piece before you combine and you get a quick ballpark close enough to catch a real mistake. The estimate is not the exact answer, so write it with \(\approx\), like \(29 \times 6.10 \approx 180\), not an equals sign. The whole point is that when a printed total like \(1{,}769\) lands ten times away from your estimate of about \(180\), you know something is off before doing any careful work. Rounding trades a little accuracy for a lot of speed, which is exactly what makes it good for checking answers and for mental math.
Three crates weigh \(487\), \(213\), and \(356\) kg. Round each to the nearest hundred, then add the rounded values. What is your estimated total?
Show solution
Round each crate to the nearest hundred first. For \(487\), the part after the hundreds is \(87\), which is past \(50\), so it rounds up to \(500\). For \(213\), the leftover is \(13\), which is below \(50\), so it stays at \(200\). For \(356\), the leftover is \(56\), which is past \(50\), so it rounds up to \(400\). Now the sum is easy because every number ends in two zeros. Add the hundreds, \(5 + 2 + 4 = 11\), so the total is \(1100\). That is \(500 + 200 + 400 = 1100\). Notice how much friendlier these numbers are than the originals. The exact total happens to be \(1056\), so the estimate of \(1100\) is close enough to tell you instantly whether you are nudging the van's limit, and you never had to line up a single column. \(\boxed{1100}\)
Fun fact
In 1856 surveyors measured Everest and got exactly \(29{,}000\) feet. Worried a round number would look like a guess, they published \(29{,}002\) feet, inventing two feet to appear more precise.
A gauge is rounded to the nearest tenth and reports \(4.3\) mm. The original reading was stored to two decimal places. What is the largest two-decimal value that still rounds to \(4.3\)?
Show solution
A reported \(4.3\) is shorthand for an interval. Every true reading that rounds to \(4.3\) at the tenths place lives between the midpoint below it and the midpoint above it, and those midpoints are \(4.25\) and \(4.35\). The lower end \(4.25\) is exactly halfway between \(4.2\) and \(4.3\), so by the larger-value rule it rounds up into \(4.3\) and is included. The upper end \(4.35\) is exactly halfway between \(4.3\) and \(4.4\), so by that same rule it jumps up to \(4.4\) and is shut out. So the readings that report as \(4.3\) are exactly those with \(4.25 \le \text{value} < 4.35\). We want the largest one that has only two decimal places. The top of the interval, \(4.35\), is not allowed because it rounds to \(4.4\). Step down one hundredth to \(4.34\). Its hundredths digit is \(4\), which is below \(5\), so it rounds down to \(4.3\). It works, and nothing between \(4.34\) and \(4.35\) has just two decimal places, so this is the ceiling. The largest two-decimal reading that still reports as \(4.3\) is \(\boxed{4.34}\).
Key idea
Rounding is one-way. When you round, you swap one exact number for a tidier one, and the moment you do, the original is gone for good. The rounded \(4.3\) no longer stands for a single number, it stands for a whole stretch, everything from the lower midpoint \(4.25\) up to just under the upper midpoint \(4.35\), since any original in that band rounds back to \(4.3\). That is why rounding is a one-way street. Many different originals funnel down to the same rounded value, so you can never run it backward; the most you can ever say in reverse is name the two boundaries the original had to live between.
A price rounds to \(7\) at the nearest dollar, to \(6.8\) at the nearest tenth, and its hundredths digit is \(0\). Exactly one price to the cent satisfies all three. What is it?
Show solution
The trick is to read each clue as a range and then hunt for the single point that lives in all of them. Start with clue one. Rounding to the nearest whole dollar gives \(7\), and with the half-way-up rule the prices that round to \(7\) are \(6.5\) up to just under \(7.5\). So far the price is somewhere in \(6.5 \le p < 7.5\). Now clue two. Rounding to the nearest tenth gives \(6.8\), so the prices that round to \(6.8\) are \(6.75\) up to just under \(6.85\), that is \(6.75 \le p < 6.85\). Notice this second range sits entirely inside the first one, so the two clues together pin the price to \(6.75 \le p < 6.85\). Now bring in clue three. The price is written to the cent, and its hundredths digit is \(0\). The prices to the cent inside the band \(6.75\) up to just under \(6.85\) are \(6.75, 6.76, 6.77, \dots, 6.84\). The only one of those whose last digit is \(0\) is \(6.80\). Quick double check. Rounded to the nearest whole dollar \(6.80\) gives \(7\), since \(6.80\) is past the \(6.5\) tipping point. Rounded to the nearest tenth it gives \(6.8\), since the next digit is \(0\) and we keep the tenth. And its hundredths digit is indeed \(0\). All three clues hold, and no other price to the cent does the same. The price per scoop is \(\boxed{6.80}\) dollars.

Review problems

Review 1. A scoreboard reads \(7{,}284\) fans. The press box reports to the nearest hundred. What is \(7{,}284\) rounded to the nearest hundred?
Show solution
Start by trapping \(7{,}284\) between the nearest multiples of one hundred. Counting up in hundreds, the multiple just below is \(7{,}200\) and the one just above is \(7{,}300\). So the true count lives somewhere on the stretch between them. The midpoint of that stretch is the average of the two endpoints, which is \(\frac{7{,}200 + 7{,}300}{2} = 7{,}250\). That midpoint is the fence. A count below \(7{,}250\) falls back to \(7{,}200\), and a count at or above \(7{,}250\) climbs to \(7{,}300\) (half-way rounds to the larger value). Now place the fans. Since \(7{,}284\) is greater than \(7{,}250\), the count sits on the upper side of the fence, so it rounds up. The press box reports \(\boxed{7300}\) fans.
Review 2. A fundraiser total of \(3{,}500\) dollars is to be printed rounded to the nearest thousand. \(3{,}500\) is the exact midpoint. Using the half-way-up rule, what goes on the banner?
Show solution
To round \(3{,}500\) to the nearest thousand, first find the two thousands it lives between. Those are \(3{,}000\) below and \(4{,}000\) above. Now measure the distance to each one. From \(3{,}500\) down to \(3{,}000\) is \(500\), and from \(3{,}500\) up to \(4{,}000\) is also \(500\). The gaps are equal, so \(3{,}500\) is not closer to either thousand. It sits at the exact midpoint. Because the distances tie, the usual "closer one wins" idea cannot decide for us. That is exactly when our convention steps in. For a halfway value, we round to the larger thousand, which is \(4{,}000\). So the banner should read \(\boxed{4000}\).
Review 3. A scale reads \(6.47\) kg. A recipe records to the nearest tenth. Round \(6.47\) to the nearest tenth.
Show solution
You want the value to the nearest tenth, so picture \(6.47\) sitting between the two nearest tenths, \(6.4\) and \(6.5\). The deciding digit is the one just past the tenths place, the hundredths digit, which is \(7\). The halfway mark between \(6.4\) and \(6.5\) is \(6.45\), and \(6.47\) is past it, so it sits closer to \(6.5\). By the rule that a deciding digit of \(5\) or more sends the tenths digit up, the \(4\) becomes a \(5\). So \(6.47\) rounds to \(\boxed{6.5}\).
Review 4. A lap is timed at \(0.382\) seconds. The results sheet keeps two decimal places. Round \(0.382\) to the nearest hundredth.
Show solution
Keeping two decimal places means the hundredths place is the last one we get to write, so the deciding digit is the next one to the right. In \(0.382\) the hundredths digit is \(8\) and the thousandths digit is \(2\). Picture \(0.382\) sitting on a number line between the two hundredth marks \(0.38\) and \(0.39\). The exact halfway point is \(0.385\), and \(0.382\) sits below that, much closer to \(0.38\). You can also count the gaps. The distance down to \(0.38\) is only \(0.002\), while the distance up to \(0.39\) is \(0.008\). Since the thousandths digit \(2\) is less than \(5\), we leave the hundredths digit alone and cut off the rest. The lap time recorded on the sheet is \(\boxed{0.38}\) seconds.
Review 5. A bus ride takes exactly \(9.5\) minutes. The schedule lists whole minutes only. \(9.5\) is exactly halfway between \(9\) and \(10\). What whole number does the schedule show?
Show solution
The value \(9.5\) falls between the whole numbers \(9\) and \(10\). Check the distance to each. From \(9\) it is \(9.5 - 9 = 0.5\), and from \(10\) it is \(10 - 9.5 = 0.5\). The distances are equal, so this is a true tie, sitting exactly on the midpoint. When a number lands precisely half-way, our convention is to round to the larger value. The larger of \(9\) and \(10\) is \(10\), so the schedule lists this trip as \(\boxed{10}\) minutes.
Review 6. A fitness band reads \(0.65\) battery and rounds to one decimal place. \(0.65\) is exactly halfway between \(0.6\) and \(0.7\). What does the band display?
Show solution
Rounding to the nearest tenth means choosing between the two tenths that surround the number. Here those are \(0.6\) and \(0.7\). The distance from \(0.65\) down to \(0.6\) is \(0.05\), and the distance from \(0.65\) up to \(0.7\) is also \(0.05\). So \(0.65\) is a perfect tie, sitting exactly halfway between them. When a value lands right on the midpoint, our rule is to round to the larger value, which means we go up to \(0.7\). The fitness band shows \(\boxed{0.7}\).
Review 7. A caliper reads \(0.396\) cm and the spec sheet keeps two decimal places. The thousandths digit \(6\) rounds the hundredths \(9\) up, triggering a carry. Round \(0.396\) to the nearest hundredth.
Show solution
Find the hundredths place first. In \(0.396\) the digits after the point are \(3\) tenths, \(9\) hundredths, and \(6\) thousandths, so the hundredths place holds the \(9\). To round it, peek at the digit directly to its right, the thousandths digit, which is \(6\). Since \(6\) is at least \(5\), the hundredths place rounds up. Here is the interesting part. Rounding the \(9\) up does not make a single digit \(10\). Instead the \(9\) turns into \(0\) and carries a \(1\) into the tenths place, so the \(3\) tenths becomes \(4\) tenths. The thousandths digit is dropped. That gives \(0.40\). We keep the trailing zero so the answer still shows the hundredths place the spec sheet asked for. \(\boxed{0.40}\)
Review 8. A fuel gauge reads \(8.97\) liters and the trip computer shows one decimal place. The tenths digit is \(9\), so rounding it up triggers a carry into the ones. Round \(8.97\) to the nearest tenth.
Show solution
Rounding to the nearest tenth means the tenths place is the last digit we keep, so the hundredths digit is the one that makes the decision. In \(8.97\) the hundredths digit is \(7\). Since \(7 \ge 5\), we round the tenths place up. The tenths digit here is already \(9\), and bumping it up gives \(10\) tenths, which is the same as one whole. So the \(9\) in the tenths place rolls over to \(0\) and carries a \(1\) into the ones place. The \(8\) ones become \(9\) ones. Writing the result with one decimal place to match the nearest tenth, we get \(9.0\). A quick gut check helps too. \(8.97\) sits much closer to \(9.0\) than to \(8.9\), so rounding up is exactly what we expect. \(\boxed{9.0}\)
Review 9. A wind-chill of \(-46\) degrees is reported to the nearest ten. The two neighboring tens are \(-50\) and \(-40\), with midpoint \(-45\). Which side is \(-46\) on? Round \(-46\) to the nearest ten.
Show solution
Start by pinning \(-46\) between the two multiples of ten on either side of it. Those are \(-50\) and \(-40\), and the midpoint between them is \(-45\). Now measure how far \(-46\) is from each neighbor. The distance to \(-50\) is \(\left|-46-(-50)\right| = 4\), and the distance to \(-40\) is \(\left|-46-(-40)\right| = 6\). Since \(4\) is smaller than \(6\), the value \(-46\) is nearer to \(-50\). Another way to see it is that \(-46\) sits to the left of the halfway mark \(-45\), and everything left of the midpoint rounds toward the more leftward multiple. The halfway rule that sends a tie to the larger value never comes into play here, because \(-46\) is not a tie. So \(-46\) rounded to the nearest ten is \(\boxed{-50}\).
Review 10. An elevation app shows \(-3.5\) m. The value is exactly halfway between \(-4\) and \(-3\). The larger of those two is closer to zero. Round \(-3.5\) to the nearest whole number.
Show solution
The whole meters on either side of \(-3.5\) are \(-4\) and \(-3\). Since \(-3.5\) sits exactly half-way between them, the usual "closer one wins" test gives no winner, so the half-way rule decides. Our convention is to round a half-way value to the larger of the two choices. With negatives it helps to picture the number line. Moving right means getting larger, and \(-3\) sits to the right of \(-4\), closer to zero, so \(-3\) is the larger value. That makes the rounded elevation \(\boxed{-3}\) meters.
Review 11. A freezer probe reads \(-1.25\) degrees. The app rounds to the nearest tenth. \(-1.25\) is an exact tie between \(-1.3\) and \(-1.2\). Round to the larger value. What does the app display?
Show solution
Rounding to the nearest tenth means choosing between the two tenths on either side of \(-1.25\). Those are \(-1.3\) and \(-1.2\). Check the distance to each. From \(-1.25\) to \(-1.3\) is \(0.05\), and from \(-1.25\) to \(-1.2\) is also \(0.05\). The reading is the same distance from both, so it is an exact tie. The tie-breaking rule sends a halfway value to the larger, more positive number. On the number line \(-1.2\) lies to the right of \(-1.3\), which makes \(-1.2\) the larger of the two. So the app shows \(\boxed{-1.2}\).
Review 12. A concert order lists \(41\) tickets at \(19.80\) dollars each, printing a total of \(81.18\) dollars. Round each to the nearest friendly value and multiply. What is your quick estimate?
Show solution
Estimation works by trading the messy numbers for friendly ones, then multiplying. Take the parts separately. The number 41 has a ones digit of 1. Since 1 is below 5, the value leans toward the smaller ten, so \(41\) rounds to \(40\). The price \(19.80\) sits between \(19\) and \(20\) whole dollars. The cents are \(80\), which is more than the halfway point of \(50\), so the price leans toward the larger whole dollar and \(19.80\) rounds to \(20\). Now multiply the two rounded parts. \(40 \times 20 = 800\). So the ballpark total is about \(800\) dollars. That number towers over the printed \(81.18\) dollars, which tells you the receipt is almost certainly wrong, probably missing a digit. The estimate is \(\boxed{800}\).
Review 13. Three boxes weigh \(596\), \(207\), and \(398\) grams. Round each to the nearest hundred, then add. What is the estimated total?
Show solution
Round each weight to the nearest hundred one at a time. For \(596\), the tens digit is \(9\), which is well past halfway, so it rounds up to \(600\). For \(207\), the tens digit is \(0\), so it rounds down to \(200\). For \(398\), the tens digit is \(9\), so it rounds up to \(400\). Now add the friendly numbers, \(600 + 200 + 400 = 1200\). The estimate clears the \(1000\) gram mark with room to spare. \(\boxed{1200}\)
Review 14. A rain gauge stores readings to the nearest hundredth but its display shows the nearest tenth, reading \(5.3\) mm. What is the smallest two-decimal stored value that rounds to \(5.3\)?
Show solution
The display rounds the stored value to the nearest tenth and shows \(5.3\). For a value to round to \(5.3\) rather than to the neighbor below, \(5.2\), it has to be at least as large as the point exactly halfway between them. That halfway point is \(\frac{5.2 + 5.3}{2} = 5.25\). By the rule that an exact halfway value rounds up to the larger tenth, \(5.25\) itself rounds to \(5.3\). Going one hundredth lower, \(5.24\) is closer to \(5.2\), so it would round down and the display would read \(5.2\) instead. That makes \(5.25\) the smallest two-decimal stored value the gauge could hold while still showing \(5.3\). The smallest qualifying value is \(\boxed{5.25}\).
