Prealgebra · Lesson 6.1

Building Expressions

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A letter is allowed to stand for a number you do not know yet, or one that is free to change, and that single move lets one short line of math describe a whole family of situations at once instead of a single frozen case. Numbers and letters stitched together with the usual operations form an expression, like \(3x + 5\). Nothing you learned gets thrown out, since a letter is just a number in disguise. Two conventions to keep for good: \(3x\) means \(3 \times x\), and \(x^2\) means \(x \times x\).

A bead bracelet holds \(3\) colored beads per charm plus \(4\) fixed clasp beads, so a bracelet with \(n\) charms has \(3n + 4\) beads. A customer orders \(6\) charms. Evaluate \(3n + 4\) at \(n = 6\) by replacing \(n\) with \(6\). How many beads are on that bracelet? Type the single number.
Show solution
The total number of beads on a bracelet with \(n\) charms is the expression $$3n + 4.$$ To handle the order for \(6\) charms, evaluate at \(n = 6\) by replacing every \(n\) with \(6\). $$3(6) + 4 = 18 + 4 = 22.$$ The bracelet has \(\boxed{22}\) beads.
Key idea
Evaluating an expression. Replace every letter with the given value and finish the arithmetic. The expression on its own answers the general question, describing every case at once. The moment you want one specific case back, plugging in a number collapses the whole thing into ordinary arithmetic. Build the expression once, then evaluate it as many times as you like.
A diver's height in meters is \(11 - 4t\), where \(t\) counts minutes of descent. An expression splits into terms separated by signs, here \(11\) and \(-4t\). The number multiplying \(t\), carried with its sign, is the coefficient of \(t\). Read off the coefficient of \(t\) and type that single value, sign included.
Show solution
Start by splitting the expression into its terms. Reading \(11 - 4t\) as a sum makes the signs honest, \(11 + (-4t)\). Now the two terms are \(11\) and \(-4t\). The coefficient of \(t\) is the number multiplying \(t\), carried along with its sign, so it is \(-4\). The lone \(11\) is the constant term, the diver's starting height, and it is not attached to \(t\) at all. The coefficient of \(t\) is \(\boxed{-4}\).
Key idea
Terms, coefficients, like terms. A term is a number stuck to a variable, like \(4x\); that front number with its sign is the coefficient, so a lone \(x\) means \(1x\) and a lone \(-x\) means \(-1x\). A plain number alone is a constant. Two terms with the exact same variable part are like terms, and combining them is just counting, since \(x + x = 2x\). The plus and minus signs are the seams between terms, so carry each sign with its term when you tally the coefficients.
Sort by shape before combining. In \(x^2 + 5x + 2x^2 + 3\), the squares belong with squares and the strips with strips. A thing squared and the thing itself are different kinds of object, so \(x^2\) and \(5x\) refuse to merge, which is exactly why counting terms is a real question. Write \(x^2 + 5x + 2x^2 + 3\) in simplest form. How many terms does it have once fully simplified? Type the single count.
Show solution
Group the like terms by shape. The square tiles combine, \(x^2 + 2x^2 = 3x^2\). The strip \(5x\) is alone in its shape, and the number \(3\) is a constant by itself, so neither one combines with anything. Putting the survivors in order from the highest power down gives $$x^2 + 5x + 2x^2 + 3 = 3x^2 + 5x + 3.$$ Now read off the separate terms joined by plus signs. They are \(3x^2\), then \(5x\), then \(3\). The \(x^2\) part and the \(x\) part stay apart because squaring \(x\) makes a different kind of object than \(x\) itself, so they cannot fold together. That leaves three distinct terms, so the count is \(\boxed{3}\).
Key idea
Only like terms combine. Fold two terms together only when their variable parts match exactly; then add the coefficients and the variable rides along unchanged. If the variable parts differ even slightly, like \(x^2\) next to \(x\), or \(x\) next to \(y\), they are different kinds of thing and no move smushes them into one. That is why the count of terms in simplest form means something: it tells you how many genuinely different quantities are still standing.
Different letters stand for different kinds, so \(x\) and \(y\) never combine. In \(3x + 5y + 2x + y\), gather the \(x\) terms with each other and the \(y\) terms with each other, remembering the lone \(y\) means \(1y\). After you simplify, what is the coefficient of \(x\)? Type that single value.
Show solution
Keep the two letters in separate piles, since \(x\) and \(y\) are different kinds and never combine. Gather the \(x\) terms, \(3x + 2x = 5x\). Gather the \(y\) terms, remembering the lone \(y\) means \(1y\), so \(5y + y = 6y\). Putting the piles side by side, the expression in simplest form is $$3x + 5y + 2x + y = 5x + 6y.$$ The coefficient of \(x\) is the number multiplying it, which is \(5\). So the answer is \(\boxed{5}\).
Fun fact
Nobody used a letter for the unknown until the 1500s. Viète introduced vowels; Descartes shifted to \(x, y, z\), possibly because his printer had plenty of spare \(x\) type blocks.
A school orders supply kits in two batches, \(3(x+5) + 4(x+8)\), where each kit holds \(x\) notebooks plus some pens. Distribute each group on its own, then combine the like terms so the order collapses to (notebooks)\(\,x\,\) plus (pens). How many pens does the order come to? Type that single number.
Show solution
Distribute across each group on its own. The first group gives \(3(x+5) = 3x + 15\), and the second group gives \(4(x+8) = 4x + 32\). Putting them together, the full order is \(3x + 15 + 4x + 32\). Now combine the like terms. The notebook terms join as \(3x + 4x = 7x\), and the pen counts join as \(15 + 32 = 47\). So the order simplifies to $$7x + 47.$$ The plain number with no \(x\) on it is the pen count, the constant term \(47\). The order comes to \(\boxed{47}\) pens.
This one leans on signs, so go slowly. Expand \(5(z - 3) + 3(7 - 2z)\), letting each subtraction ride along with its term and remembering \(3 \times (-2z) = -6z\). Combine the like terms all the way to one cleanest form, then read off the coefficient of \(z\). Type that single value, sign included.
Show solution
Distribute each group, letting the subtractions travel with the terms. \(5(z - 3) = 5z - 15\), and \(3(7 - 2z) = 21 - 6z\) since \(3 \times (-2z) = -6z\). That gives \(5z - 15 + 21 - 6z\). Now combine like terms. The z terms are \(5z\) and \(-6z\), and \(5z - 6z = -z\), one short of zero rather than five or eleven. The constants are \(-15\) and \(21\), and \(-15 + 21 = 6\). So the simplest form is \(-z + 6\). Since \(-z\) means \(-1z\), the coefficient of \(z\) is \(\boxed{-1}\).
A subtraction in front of a group flips the sign of everything inside, since the multiplier carries its minus into every piece. Take \(8 - 2(x - 5)\). The \(-2\) lands on both the \(x\) and the \(-5\), so the \(-5\) becomes \(+10\), not \(-10\). Distribute, combine the constants, and the expression collapses to (something)\(\,x\,\) plus a constant. What is the constant term once fully simplified? Type that single value.
Show solution
The \(-2\) in front of the group multiplies both pieces inside. Distributing it, \(-2 \times x = -2x\), and \(-2 \times (-5) = +10\) because a negative times a negative is positive. So $$8 - 2(x - 5) = 8 - 2x + 10.$$ The trap is that the \(-5\) became \(+10\), not \(-10\). Now combine the two constants, \(8 + 10 = 18\), which leaves $$-2x + 18.$$ The constant term is the part with no variable, so the value is \(\boxed{18}\).
Key idea
Distribute to every piece. When a number sits in front of a group, hand it to every piece inside, not just the first, so \(3(x+4) = 3x + 12\) with the \(3\) reaching all the way to the back. A subtraction or minus in front of a group is really multiplying by \(-1\), so it flips the sign of every piece: \(-(x-5) = -x + 5\). Almost every distribution slip is one term the multiplier was supposed to touch and got left behind.
Two expressions are equivalent when they agree for every value of the variable. Staring at them is not proof, so here is the grading trick: build the difference \(A - B\) and plug in a value. If they truly are equivalent, that difference must come out \(0\). Let \(A = 2(3x+4) + x\) and \(B = 7x + 8\). Compute \(A - B\) at \(x = 5\) and type the single number you get.
Show solution
First simplify \(A\) so you can see the twins clearly. Distributing the \(2\) gives \(2(3x + 4) = 6x + 8\), and adding the outside \(x\) gives \(6x + 8 + x = 7x + 8\). So \(A = 7x + 8\), which is identical to \(B = 7x + 8\). The difference simplifies all the way down, since \(A - B = (7x + 8) - (7x + 8) = 0\). That \(0\) is not an accident of one lucky number. The \(x\) terms cancel and the constants cancel, so the difference is \(0\) for every value of \(x\). Now plug in the requested value. At \(x = 5\), \(A = 2(3 \cdot 5 + 4) + 5 = 2 \cdot 19 + 5 = 43\) and \(B = 7 \cdot 5 + 8 = 43\), so the difference is \(43 - 43 = 0\). The two expressions truly are equivalent. \(\boxed{0}\)
Key idea
Equivalence. Testing values is lopsided. If two expressions disagree even once, you are done, they cannot be equivalent. But matching once, or even a few times, proves nothing, since two different expressions can line up at a lucky value. The only sure way is to rewrite both to the same simplest form. Reordering a sum keeps the meaning, so \(x + 7\) and \(7 + x\) are the same, but \(x - 7\) and \(7 - x\) are not, because subtraction is not symmetric.
The target is \(6n + 10\), and four suspects line up beside it: \(2(3n+5)\), \(3(2n+4)\), \(5 + 6n + 5\), and \(10 + 6n\). Simplify each one until you can compare it cleanly with \(6n + 10\). How many of the four are equivalent to \(6n + 10\)? Type the single count.
Show solution
Rewrite each suspect in the form "number times \(n\), plus a constant" and compare it with the target \(6n + 10\). Suspect one distributes as \(2(3n + 5) = 6n + 10\), which matches the target exactly. Suspect two distributes as \(3(2n + 4) = 6n + 12\), and since \(12 \ne 10\) the constant is wrong, so this one is an impostor. Suspect three combines its loose numbers, \(5 + 6n + 5 = 6n + 10\), which matches. Suspect four is \(10 + 6n\), and reordering a sum never changes its value, so \(10 + 6n = 6n + 10\), another match. Three of the four suspects simplify to \(6n + 10\) and one does not, so the count of equivalent expressions is \(\boxed{3}\).
Coefficients do not have to be whole numbers. A fraction can sit in front of a variable, and combining such terms is the same common-denominator move from Chapter 4 with an \(x\) riding along. The terms \(\tfrac{2x}{3}\) and \(\tfrac{5x}{7}\) are like terms, since both carry a single \(x\). Combine \(\tfrac{2x}{3} + \tfrac{5x}{7}\) into one term, then state the coefficient of \(x\) as a fraction in lowest terms, typed as a over b.
Show solution
A common denominator of \(3\) and \(7\) is \(21\). Rewrite each term with denominator \(21\), so \(\tfrac{2x}{3} = \tfrac{14x}{21}\) and \(\tfrac{5x}{7} = \tfrac{15x}{21}\). Now add the numerators over the shared denominator. $$\frac{2x}{3} + \frac{5x}{7} = \frac{14x}{21} + \frac{15x}{21} = \frac{29x}{21}$$ That single term is \(\tfrac{29x}{21}\), and its coefficient of \(x\) is \(\tfrac{29}{21}\). Since \(29\) is prime and does not divide \(21\), the fraction is already in lowest terms. The coefficient of \(x\) is \(\boxed{29/21}\).
Key idea
Fractional coefficients. A fraction in front of a letter never starts a new kind of math: \(\tfrac{2x}{3}\) is just \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of an \(x\). Adding two such terms is the same common-denominator work you already know, with the \(x\) riding along untouched. Find the shared bottom, rebuild each coefficient over it, add the numerators, and set the \(x\) back down beside the answer. Every fraction skill from earlier still works with the letter in the seat.
Sometimes the pieces you are adding live over different denominators, and one piece can be an expression. Combine \(\tfrac{a}{2} + \tfrac{6a - 5}{4}\) onto a common bottom, then write the result as a coefficient of \(a\) plus a constant, like \(2a - \tfrac{5}{4}\). In that clean form, type the constant term as a fraction in lowest terms, with its sign.
Show solution
The denominators are \(2\) and \(4\). Since \(4\) is a multiple of \(2\), the common denominator is \(4\). Rewrite the first piece by multiplying its top and bottom by \(2\), which turns \(\tfrac{a}{2}\) into \(\tfrac{2a}{4}\). The second piece already sits over \(4\), so leave it alone. Now both pieces share a bottom, so add the numerators over that single \(4\). $$\tfrac{a}{2} + \tfrac{6a - 5}{4} = \tfrac{2a}{4} + \tfrac{6a - 5}{4} = \tfrac{2a + 6a - 5}{4} = \tfrac{8a - 5}{4}$$ To read off the parts, split the single fraction back into a term with \(a\) and a term without. $$\tfrac{8a - 5}{4} = \tfrac{8a}{4} - \tfrac{5}{4} = 2a - \tfrac{5}{4}$$ The coefficient of \(a\) is \(2\), and the constant term is the piece carrying no \(a\), which is \(-\tfrac{5}{4}\). The fraction \(\tfrac{5}{4}\) is already in lowest terms, so the constant term is \(\boxed{-5/4}\).
Here is the whole lesson in one expression: a piece scaled by a fraction, a piece scaled by a whole number, and a piece over a denominator. Simplify \(\frac{1}{2}(4x + 6) + 3(x - 1) + \frac{x - 5}{3}\) all the way down to the form (something)\(\,x\,\) plus (something), then report the coefficient of \(x\) as a fraction in lowest terms, typed as a over b.
Show solution
Take the three groups one at a time. The first group is \(\frac{1}{2}(4x + 6) = 2x + 3\). The second is \(3(x - 1) = 3x - 3\), keeping the sign on the \(-3\). The third is \(\frac{x - 5}{3} = \frac{x}{3} - \frac{5}{3}\). Lining them up gives \(2x + 3 + 3x - 3 + \frac{x}{3} - \frac{5}{3}\). Gather the \(x\) terms first. Their coefficients add as \(2 + 3 + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{15}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{16}{3}\). The constants come out to \(3 - 3 - \frac{5}{3} = -\frac{5}{3}\). So the fully simplified expression is \(\frac{16}{3}x - \frac{5}{3}\), and the coefficient of \(x\) is \(\frac{16}{3}\), already in lowest terms since \(16\) and \(3\) share no factor. The answer is \(\boxed{16/3}\).

Review problems

Review 1. A weather balloon's altitude in meters is \(13 - 7p\), where \(p\) is the number of minutes a valve has been venting. The number multiplying a variable, carried with its sign, is its coefficient. State the coefficient of \(p\) and type that single value, sign included.
Show solution
The expression is already in simplest form, \(13 - 7p\). It has two terms, the constant \(13\) and the variable term \(-7p\). Reading the subtraction as adding a negative, the term built from \(p\) is \(-7p\), which means \(-7\) times \(p\). The coefficient of \(p\) is the number multiplying it, sign and all, so the coefficient is \(\boxed{-7}\).
Review 2. A print run is modeled by \(19 - 5q\), where \(q\) counts paper jams. The piece \(5q\) shifts as jams shift, but one piece does not change at all. That unchanging number is the constant term. State the constant term of \(19 - 5q\) and type that single number.
Show solution
The expression is already written in simplest form as \(19 - 5q\). It is built from two terms. The term \(5q\) depends on the variable \(q\), since \(5q\) means \(5\) times \(q\), so its value shifts whenever the number of jams shifts. The other term is just \(19\), a plain number with no variable attached. The constant term is the term with no variable, which is the part that stays fixed no matter what \(q\) equals. You can see this fixed value by imagining zero jams, where \(q = 0\) gives \(19 - 5(0) = 19\). So the constant term is \(\boxed{19}\).
Review 3. A lone variable carries a hidden \(1\) in front, so \(m\) means \(1m\). Combine the like terms in \(3m + m + 5m\) into a single term, then type the coefficient of \(m\).
Show solution
Every term here is a number of copies of \(m\), so they are like terms and can be merged. Reading the lone \(m\) as \(1m\), the expression becomes \(3m + 1m + 5m\). Add the numbers in front, \(3 + 1 + 5 = 9\), and keep the \(m\). The simplified expression is \(9m\). The coefficient of \(m\) is \(\boxed{9}\).
Review 4. Tally each kind of term on its own, the plain numbers in one pile and the \(x\) terms in another. Simplify \(8 + 2x + 5 + 4x\), then read off the constant term and type that single value.
Show solution
Group the like terms first. The plain numbers are \(8\) and \(5\), and the \(x\) terms are \(2x\) and \(4x\). Combine each group on its own. The numbers give \(8 + 5 = 13\), and the \(x\) terms give \(2x + 4x = 6x\). Putting both groups back together, the expression in simplest form is \(6x + 13\). The constant term is the piece with no \(x\) attached to it, which is \(13\). So the value you type is \(\boxed{13}\).
Review 5. Square tiles each cover \(x^2\) and thin tiles each cover \(x\), and the two kinds never combine because \(x^2\) and \(x\) are different objects. Gather each kind in \(5x^2 + 2x + 3x^2 + x\), write it in simplest form, then count how many terms remain.
Show solution
Keep the two kinds of tiles separate while you combine. The square tiles give \(5x^2 + 3x^2 = 8x^2\), and the thin x-tiles give \(2x + x = 3x\). Putting the piles back together, the simplest form is \(8x^2 + 3x\). A term is one chunk separated by a plus or minus sign, so \(8x^2\) is one term and \(3x\) is another. They cannot merge into a single piece because a square tile is not the same kind of tile as a thin one. That leaves two terms. \(\boxed{2}\)
Review 6. Since \(a\) and \(b\) stand for different things, an \(a\) term and a \(b\) term can never merge. Combine only the matching kinds in \(6a + 2b + a + 3b\), write the result in simplest form, then type how many terms are left.
Show solution
Start by collecting the pieces that are the same kind. The \(a\) terms are \(6a\) and \(a\), which together make \(6a + a = 7a\). The \(b\) terms are \(2b\) and \(3b\), which together make \(2b + 3b = 5b\). An \(a\) bundle and a \(b\) bundle measure different objects, so they stay apart. The simplest form is $$6a + 2b + a + 3b = 7a + 5b.$$ Now read off what the question wants. The simplified expression \(7a + 5b\) is made of two separate pieces joined by addition, the \(7a\) and the \(5b\). That is two terms. So the count is \(\boxed{2}\).
Review 7. Expand \(8(3x + 2)\) by handing the \(8\) to every piece inside, so it lands on both the \(3x\) and the \(2\). In the result, the number multiplying \(x\) is its coefficient. What is the coefficient of \(x\)? Type that single value.
Show solution
Hand the \(8\) to every piece inside the group. \(8 \times 3x = 24x\) and \(8 \times 2 = 16\). Putting those together, the expanded expression is \(24x + 16\). No parentheses are left, so it is in simplest form. The term holding the \(x\) is \(24x\), and the number multiplying \(x\) is \(24\). That number is the coefficient of \(x\). So the coefficient is \(\boxed{24}\).
Review 8. Distribute across each group in \(2(x+6) + 5(x+1)\), then combine the like terms into simplest form. Read off the constant term, the lone number with no \(x\) attached, and type that single value.
Show solution
Distribute each group on its own. The first group gives \(2(x+6) = 2x + 12\), and the second gives \(5(x+1) = 5x + 5\). Putting those together, the expression is \(2x + 12 + 5x + 5\). Now combine like terms. The \(x\) terms are \(2x\) and \(5x\), which add to \(7x\). The plain numbers are \(12\) and \(5\), which add to \(17\). So in simplest form the expression is \(7x + 17\). The constant term is the number standing alone with no \(x\), which is \(17\). So the answer is \(\boxed{17}\).
Review 9. Distribute each number across its own parentheses in \(6(n-2) + 4(5-3n)\), letting every sign ride along, then collect the like terms. In simplest form, read off the coefficient of \(n\) and type that single value, sign included.
Show solution
Distribute each factor across its parentheses, keeping every sign attached to its term. The first chunk gives \(6(n - 2) = 6n - 12\). The second chunk gives \(4(5 - 3n) = 20 - 12n\), where the \(4\) multiplies both the \(5\) and the \(-3n\). So far the expression reads \(6n - 12 + 20 - 12n\). Now collect like terms. The \(n\) terms are \(6n\) and \(-12n\), which combine to \(-6n\). The plain numbers are \(-12\) and \(20\), which combine to \(8\). The simplest form is \(-6n + 8\). The coefficient of \(n\) is the number multiplying \(n\), which is \(-6\). \(\boxed{-6}\)
Review 10. In \(20 - 3(2x - 4)\), the \(-3\) distributes across the group and flips the sign of every piece inside because it is negative. Simplify into a number plus a multiple of \(x\), then find the constant term, the plain number with no \(x\), and type that single value.
Show solution
Start by distributing the \(-3\) into the group \(2x - 4\). Multiply it by each term inside. You get \(-3 \times 2x = -6x\) and \(-3 \times (-4) = +12\). The negative in front flips the sign of the \(-4\), turning it into \(+12\). So the expression becomes \(20 - 6x + 12\). Now combine the like terms. The two plain numbers \(20\) and \(12\) join together into \(32\), while the \(-6x\) has no partner and stays as it is. The simplest form is \(32 - 6x\). The constant term is the number standing alone with no \(x\) attached, which is \(32\). So the value you read off is \(\boxed{32}\).
Review 11. Two expressions are equivalent when their difference \(A - B\) is \(0\) for every value of \(x\). Let \(A = 3(2x + 5)\) and \(B = 6x + 15\). Evaluate \(A - B\) at \(x = 4\) by replacing every \(x\) with \(4\), and type the single number you get.
Show solution
Start by simplifying \(A\). The \(3\) in front of \(3(2x + 5)\) gets shared with both terms inside, so \(3 \cdot 2x = 6x\) and \(3 \cdot 5 = 15\), which gives \(A = 6x + 15\). That is exactly the same expression as \(B = 6x + 15\). Now form the difference and combine like terms, \(A - B = (6x + 15) - (6x + 15) = 0\). The variable has already vanished, so the simplified difference is just \(0\). Plugging in \(x = 4\) cannot change a plain \(0\), and you can check it directly, since \(A = 3(2 \cdot 4 + 5) = 3 \cdot 13 = 39\) and \(B = 6 \cdot 4 + 15 = 24 + 15 = 39\), so \(A - B = 39 - 39 = 0\). The two builds agree, which is the whole point. The answer is \(\boxed{0}\).
Review 12. The target is \(8x + 12\). Simplify each of \(4(2x + 3)\), \(2(4x + 6)\), \(8x + 6\), and \(12 + 8x\), then compare with the target. How many of the four are equivalent to \(8x + 12\)? Type the single count.
Show solution
Simplify each expression and compare it to the target \(8x + 12\). For the first, \(4(2x + 3) = 8x + 12\), which matches. For the second, \(2(4x + 6) = 8x + 12\), which also matches. For the third, \(8x + 6\) is already simplified, and its constant is \(6\) instead of \(12\), so it does not match. For the fourth, \(12 + 8x\) is the same two terms as \(8x + 12\) written in the other order, and order does not change the value, so it matches. Three of the four are equivalent to the target. \(\boxed{3}\)
Review 13. Two dials add \(\tfrac{5x}{6}\) and \(\tfrac{x}{4}\) to a score, where \(x\) is the number of presses. Combine \(\tfrac{5x}{6} + \tfrac{x}{4}\) into one term using a common denominator of \(12\), keeping the \(x\). State the coefficient of \(x\) as a fraction in lowest terms, typed as a over b.
Show solution
Rewrite each fraction with denominator \(12\). Since \(\tfrac{5x}{6} = \tfrac{10x}{12}\) and \(\tfrac{x}{4} = \tfrac{3x}{12}\), the sum becomes $$\frac{10x}{12} + \frac{3x}{12} = \frac{13x}{12}.$$ The combined term is \(\tfrac{13x}{12}\). The coefficient of \(x\) is the number multiplying it, which is \(\tfrac{13}{12}\). Since \(13\) is prime and does not divide \(12\), this fraction is already in lowest terms. The coefficient is \(\boxed{13/12}\).
Review 14. A tiled border uses \(3(2s + 3)\) tiles in one row, \(2(s - 1)\) in another, plus \(5\) corner tiles, where \(s\) is a size setting. Build one expression for the total, simplify it, then evaluate at \(s = 4\) by replacing every \(s\) with \(4\). Type the single number you get.
Show solution
Start by expanding each grouped piece. The first row is \(3(2s + 3) = 6s + 9\), and the second row is \(2(s - 1) = 2s - 2\). Adding everything, including the \(5\) corner tiles, gives \(6s + 9 + 2s - 2 + 5\). Combine the like terms. The \(s\) terms give \(6s + 2s = 8s\), and the plain numbers give \(9 - 2 + 5 = 12\), so the simplified total is \(8s + 12\). Now evaluate at \(s = 4\) by replacing \(s\) with \(4\), which gives \(8 \cdot 4 + 12 = 32 + 12 = 44\). \(\boxed{44}\)
