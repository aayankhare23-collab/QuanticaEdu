Multiplying and Dividing Fractions

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A baker gives her apprentice \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of a dough strip; he uses half of that for a braid. The braid is a part of a part, visibly smaller than either. What single fraction of the whole strip names it?

A garden bed has \(12\) equal squares; \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) are covered. Split \(12\) into \(3\) groups, keep \(2\). How many squares end up covered?
Show solution
The word of here is a quiet instruction. Two thirds of 12 means we want \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of the 12 squares, and the way to find a fraction of an amount is to split, then keep. The bottom number, the 3, says split the 12 squares into three equal groups. \(12 \div 3 = 4\), so each group is 4 squares. The top number, the 2, says keep two of those groups. Two groups of 4 squares is \(2 \times 4 = 8\) squares under the cloth. Notice what just happened. Finding \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of 12 came out exactly the same as \(\tfrac{2}{3} \times 12 = \tfrac{2 \times 12}{3} = \tfrac{24}{3} = 8\). The little word of behaved precisely like a multiply sign, and it will keep doing that even when the amount you take a part of is itself a fraction. $$\boxed{8}$$
A worker uses \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) of a netting roll, then patches \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of that panel. The patch is \(1\) cell in the grid of equal cells. Write it as \(a/b\) of the whole roll.
Show solution
Start with the panel she actually unrolled. It is one of 4 equal panels, so it is \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) of the whole roll. She does not use the whole panel though. She cuts that panel into 3 equal slivers and keeps 1, so she uses \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of that quarter. The question is really asking for \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) of the roll. Here is the picture that nails it. When you carry the 3-way cut across all 4 panels, the second cut tripled the number of pieces. You went from 4 equal panels to \(4 \times 3 = 12\) equal slivers, and every sliver is identical. Her patch is exactly one of those 12 equal slivers, so it is \(\tfrac{1}{12}\) of the whole roll. That matches the rule for a unit fraction of a unit fraction. Taking \(\tfrac{1}{a}\) of \(\tfrac{1}{b}\) means cutting the whole into \(a\) parts and then each part into \(b\) parts, which leaves \(a \times b\) equal pieces, so the result is \(\tfrac{1}{ab}\). With \(a = 4\) and \(b = 3\) that gives \(\tfrac{1}{4 \times 3} = \tfrac{1}{12}\). $$\boxed{1/12}$$
Key idea
Of means multiply. Taking a part of a part is cutting, then cutting again inside one of the pieces. The first cut splits the whole into \(a\) pieces, and cutting one of those into \(b\) pieces chops the whole into \(a\times b\) tiny equal pieces, of which you keep one. That is why \(\tfrac{1}{a}\) of \(\tfrac{1}{b}\) lands on \(\tfrac{1}{ab}\), and it is the secret the word of has held all along. Of means multiply, the same as when you took two thirds of \(12\). A fraction of a fraction always comes out smaller, since each cut leaves a thinner sliver.
A \(3\times 5\) mosaic: pale glass fills \(2\) of \(3\) columns; gold fills \(3\) of \(5\) rows. What fraction of the panel does the overlap cover? Reduce fully, write as \(a/b\).
Show solution
Read the picture as a count. The panel is sliced into \(3\) columns and \(5\) rows, so there are \(3 \times 5 = 15\) equal cells, and each cell is \(\tfrac{1}{15}\) of the whole. The pale glass fills the left \(2\) columns, and the gold leaf fills the bottom \(3\) rows. The overlap is the rectangle that is \(2\) columns wide and \(3\) rows tall, so it holds \(2 \times 3 = 6\) cells. That is why multiplying fractions multiplies the tops and multiplies the bottoms, the tops count how wide and tall the overlap block is, and the bottoms count how the whole was cut up. So \(\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{5} = \frac{2 \times 3}{3 \times 5} = \frac{6}{15}\). Now reduce. The gcd of \(6\) and \(15\) is \(3\), and dividing both by \(3\) gives \(\frac{6 \div 3}{15 \div 3} = \frac{2}{5}\). The overlap covers \(\frac{2}{5}\) of the panel, which makes sense, two thirds of three fifths should land a little under half. $$\boxed{2/5}$$
Key idea
Fraction multiplication rule. To multiply two fractions, multiply the tops to get the new top and the bottoms to get the new bottom, $$\frac{a}{b}\times\frac{c}{d}=\frac{a\times c}{b\times d}.$$ Read off a grid, the new top counts the overlap, so many cells across times so many up, and the new bottom counts the whole square, columns times rows. This one rule swallows everything before it. A whole number is just itself over \(1\), and taking a fraction of an amount is this same multiply.
One batch needs \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) cup of cherries; \(6\) batches are made. Compute \(6\times\tfrac{5}{8}\). Leave your answer as an improper fraction.
Show solution
Six batches is just \(\frac{5}{8}\) of a cup repeated 6 times. The size of each scoop never changes, so every piece is still one eighth of a cup. What grows is how many eighths you have collected. Each batch brings 5 eighths. After 6 batches you have \(6 \times 5 = 30\) eighths of a cup. The bottom of the fraction stays 8 the whole time, because an eighth is still an eighth no matter how many you pile up. You can see this as the whole number 6 written as \(\frac{6}{1}\), then multiplied across. $$\frac{6}{1} \times \frac{5}{8} = \frac{6 \times 5}{1 \times 8} = \frac{30}{8}$$ Now simplify. Both 30 and 8 share a factor of 2, so \(\frac{30}{8} = \frac{15}{4}\). The troop needs $$\boxed{15/4}$$ cups of dried cherries in all.
Key idea
Cancel before multiplying. When you multiply fractions, every top piles into one big top and every bottom into one big bottom, so it does not matter which fraction a number started in. If some number up top and some number down low share a factor, you can divide both by it right now, before multiplying anything, even when one sits in the left fraction and the other in the right. A matched factor like \(4\) over \(4\) would only cancel to \(1\) anyway, so pulling it out early spares you from wrestling with huge numbers. It is the same self-cancelling from 4.2, except the answer lands already reduced.
Compute \(\tfrac{14}{15}\times\tfrac{25}{21}\): prime-factor each number, cancel any prime appearing in a top and a bottom, multiply what remains. Fully reduced result as \(a/b\)?
Show solution
Multiplying tops and bottoms straight away gives \(\frac{14\times 25}{15\times 21}=\frac{350}{315}\), and now you are stuck reducing a big fraction. The trick is to cancel before you multiply, because in a product of fractions every top factor is paired against every bottom factor. \nBreak each number into primes. \(14=2\cdot 7\), \(25=5\cdot 5\), \(15=3\cdot 5\), and \(21=3\cdot 7\). So the product is $$\frac{2\cdot 7}{3\cdot 5}\times\frac{5\cdot 5}{3\cdot 7}=\frac{2\cdot 7\cdot 5\cdot 5}{3\cdot 5\cdot 3\cdot 7}.$$\nNow strike out a factor on top against the same factor on the bottom. The \(7\) (from \(14\)) cancels the \(7\) (from \(21\)), and one \(5\) (from \(25\)) cancels the \(5\) (from \(15\)). What remains on top is \(2\cdot 5=10\), and what remains on the bottom is \(3\cdot 3=9\). \nThat leaves \(\frac{10}{9}\), already in lowest terms since \(\gcd(10,9)=1\). The same answer you would get from \(\frac{350}{315}\) after a lot more work, with none of the mess. \(\boxed{10/9}\)
\(\tfrac{3}{4}\times\tfrac{4}{5}\times\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{6}{7}\): each bottom is the next top. Most cancel. What single reduced fraction remains?
Show solution
The slow way is to multiply everything out, but the chain is begging us not to. Stack all four numerators over all four denominators in one big fraction, $$\frac{3}{4}\times\frac{4}{5}\times\frac{5}{6}\times\frac{6}{7}=\frac{3\times 4\times 5\times 6}{4\times 5\times 6\times 7}.$$\nNow the staircase pays off. The \(4\) sits on top and also on the bottom, so that pair cancels to \(1\). The \(5\) does the same. The \(6\) does the same. Three matching pairs quietly delete themselves, $$\frac{3\times \cancel{4}\times \cancel{5}\times \cancel{6}}{\cancel{4}\times \cancel{5}\times \cancel{6}\times 7}=\frac{3}{7}.$$\nWhat survives is exactly the first numerator and the last denominator, the two ends of the chain that had no partner to cancel against. Everything in the middle was both a top and a bottom, so it washed out. And \(\tfrac{3}{7}\) is already in lowest terms, since \(3\) and \(7\) share no factor bigger than \(1\). The colony passes along \(\;\boxed{\dfrac{3}{7}}\;\) of the spores it started with.
Fun fact
A telescoping product collapses like a folding spyglass: each bottom matched the next top, cancelled, leaving only the first top and last bottom standing.
Key idea
The word "of" means multiply. Every time you hear the word of you can swap in a times sign, so a long string of "two thirds of three fifths of..." is one big multiplication. To do it, multiply across the tops and across the bottoms, and make life easy by cancelling any factor a top shares with a bottom before you ever multiply. A whole number is welcome too, showing up as itself over \(1\). And here is the quiet shock that keeps returning. Multiplying two proper fractions gives an answer smaller than both, because taking a part of an already partial amount can only leave less.

Every "of" shrinks. Division asks the reverse: how many small things fit in one big one? That is multiplication running backward. The tool that flips division back into multiplication is the reciprocal.

A machine scales by \(\tfrac{7}{12}\); a second machine must undo it so the combined product is \(1\). What fraction times \(\tfrac{7}{12}\) gives \(1\)? Write as \(a/b\).
Show solution
You are hunting for a fraction \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) so that \(\tfrac{7}{12}\times\tfrac{a}{b}=1\). Multiplying fractions just multiplies tops and bottoms, which gives \(\tfrac{7\times a}{12\times b}\). A fraction equals \(1\) exactly when its top and bottom are the same number, so you want \(7\times a\) to match \(12\times b\) in the cleanest possible way. The simplest move is to make the top a \(12\) and the bottom a \(7\), because then the new top is \(7\times 12\) and the new bottom is \(12\times 7\), and those are the same value. So flipping \(\tfrac{7}{12}\) upside down hands you \(\tfrac{12}{7}\). Checking, \(\tfrac{7}{12}\times\tfrac{12}{7}=\tfrac{84}{84}=1\), which is the unchanged stick you wanted. The partner that reverses a fraction's scaling is always that fraction turned over, and it is called the reciprocal. \(\boxed{12/7}\)
Key idea
Reciprocal. The partner that multiplies with a fraction to land on exactly \(1\) is that same fraction turned upside down, so the reciprocal of \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) is \(\tfrac{b}{a}\). The flip always works, because multiplying gives \(a\times b\) sitting over the very same \(a\times b\), and any number over itself is \(1\). Three small things fall out. The number \(1\) flips to itself and so does \(-1\), and \(0\) has no reciprocal at all, since nothing you multiply by \(0\) can ever climb up to \(1\), the same reason you can never divide by \(0\).
Five builders share a \(3\) m plank: \(3\div 5 = 3\times\tfrac{1}{5}\). How long is each share in meters? Write as \(a/b\).
Show solution
Each builder gets the plank shared equally among five people, which is \(3 \div 5\). The cleanest way to see what that number is comes from the meaning of division itself. Splitting the plank into \(5\) equal pieces and keeping one piece is the same as taking one fifth of the plank, and taking \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) of a length means multiplying that length by \(\tfrac{1}{5}\). So each share is $$3 \times \tfrac{1}{5}.$$ Picture three separate one meter rods laid end to end. If you cut every rod into five equal slivers, each sliver is \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) of a meter, and one builder collects one sliver from each of the three rods, which is three of those fifth meter slivers. Three copies of \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) is \(\tfrac{3}{5}\). This is the quiet truth hiding behind division by a whole number. Dividing \(3\) by \(5\) is multiplying \(3\) by the reciprocal \(\tfrac{1}{5}\), and the answer is simply the fraction \(\tfrac{3}{5}\). Each builder's share is \(\dfrac{3}{5}\) of a meter, so $$\boxed{3/5}.$$
Key idea
Division by a fraction. Dividing always asks the same thing, how many of the divisor fit. Splitting something into \(b\) equal shares and keeping one is just taking \(\tfrac{1}{b}\) of it, so dividing by \(b\) and multiplying by \(\tfrac{1}{b}\) are the same move. And asking how many quarter cups fit in \(3\) cups gives \(12\), since each cup holds \(4\) of them, and that \(4\) is exactly the reciprocal of \(\tfrac{1}{4}\). So to divide by any fraction, flip it to its reciprocal and multiply, $$a\div\frac{p}{q}=a\times\frac{q}{p}.$$ Every division hands itself straight back to the multiplying you already know.
A trim piece is \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) m. You want pieces of \(\tfrac{2}{9}\) m each. Keep the first fraction, flip the divisor, and multiply. What is \(\tfrac{5}{6}\div\tfrac{2}{9}\) in lowest terms?
Show solution
Asking how many \(\tfrac{2}{9}\)-meter pieces fit inside \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) of a meter is a division question, \(\tfrac{5}{6} \div \tfrac{2}{9}\). To divide by a fraction, keep the first one and flip the second, then multiply. The \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) stays put and \(\tfrac{2}{9}\) flips to \(\tfrac{9}{2}\), so $$\frac{5}{6} \div \frac{2}{9} = \frac{5}{6} \times \frac{9}{2}.$$ Now cancel before multiplying. The \(9\) on top and the \(6\) on the bottom share a factor of \(3\), so the \(9\) shrinks to \(3\) and the \(6\) shrinks to \(2\). That leaves $$\frac{5}{6} \times \frac{9}{2} = \frac{5}{2} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{5 \times 3}{2 \times 2} = \frac{15}{4}.$$ So \(\tfrac{15}{4}\) of those pieces fit, which is \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\) pieces. You get three full pieces and three quarters of another one left over, which matches the "how many fit" feeling. The fully reduced answer is \(\boxed{15/4}\).
A \(5\) L carboy is poured into \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) L jars. One liter fills \(3\) jars; the carboy holds \(5\) liters. How many \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) L jars does the full carboy fill?
Show solution
The question hiding inside this is \(5\div\tfrac{1}{3}\), which asks how many third liter jars fit into \(5\) whole liters. Start with a single liter. It splits cleanly into \(3\) jars, because \(\tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{1}{3}=1\). Now line up all \(5\) liters from the carboy. Each one gives \(3\) jars, so the total is \(5\) groups of \(3\), which is \(5\times 3=15\). Here is the part worth sitting with. We divided by \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) and the answer jumped up to \(15\), far bigger than the \(5\) we started with. That feels backward until you remember what the jars are doing. They are tiny, smaller than a whole liter, so a great many of them fit into the same amount of water. Dividing by a number below \(1\) always grows the answer for exactly this reason. The full carboy fills \(\boxed{15}\) jars.
A fundraiser raised \(32\) (thousands). That is \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) of the goal. Multiply \(32\) by \(\tfrac{3}{2}\) to recover the whole. What is the full goal, in thousands?
Show solution
The forward story is goal \(\times\,\tfrac{2}{3} = 32\). To find the goal you reverse that step, so you divide both sides by \(\tfrac{2}{3}\). Dividing by a fraction is multiplying by its reciprocal, which gives goal \(= 32 \times \tfrac{3}{2}\). Before multiplying, cancel the 32 against the 2 in the bottom, since \(32 = 16 \times 2\). That leaves \(16 \times 3 = 48\). Check it forward, two thirds of 48 is \(48 \div 3 = 16\), then \(16 \times 2 = 32\), exactly the amount in the jar. The full goal is \(\boxed{48}\) thousand dollars.
Key idea
Finding the whole from a part. The word of multiplies a number by a fraction and shrinks it into a part. So when a problem hands you the part and hides the whole, all that happened was that multiplication run forward. To climb back to the whole, multiply the known part by the reciprocal, the flipped fraction, because that is exactly what undoes the original times. If \(\tfrac{p}{q}\) of a mystery number is \(k\), the number is \(k\times\tfrac{q}{p}\). For instance, if two thirds of a number is \(32\), the number is \(32\times\tfrac{3}{2}=48\), and this same flip works every time.
Feed \(12\) into a machine: first multiply by \(\tfrac{4}{9}\), then divide by \(\tfrac{8}{15}\). What is the result?
Show solution
Start by turning the divide step into a multiply step, since that is the move that makes everything line up. Dividing by \(\tfrac{8}{15}\) is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal \(\tfrac{15}{8}\). Now both settings are multiplications, so the whole machine does \[\text{size}\;\times\;\tfrac{4}{9}\;\times\;\tfrac{15}{8}.\] Two multiplications back to back are just one multiplication by their product, which is exactly the student's point. So the single fraction hiding inside the machine is \(\tfrac{4}{9}\times\tfrac{15}{8}\). Multiply it out by cancelling across the top and bottom first. The \(4\) and the \(8\) share a factor of \(4\), and the \(15\) and the \(9\) share a factor of \(3\): $$\frac{4}{9}\times\frac{15}{8}=\frac{\cancel{4}^{\,1}}{\cancel{9}^{\,3}}\times\frac{\cancel{15}^{\,5}}{\cancel{8}^{\,2}}=\frac{1\times 5}{3\times 2}=\frac{5}{6}.$$ So the two settings really are one setting, multiply by \(\tfrac{5}{6}\), and the student was right. Now feed in the part of size \(12\): $$12\times\frac{5}{6}=\frac{12\times 5}{6}=\frac{60}{6}=10.$$ Notice what just happened. A multiply and a divide, two separate operations, folded into a single clean fraction \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) with no remainder left over and no decimal in sight. That is the quiet reason fraction arithmetic stays so tidy. Multiply fractions, divide fractions, chain them as long as you like, and the result is always just another fraction. You never escape the fractions. \(\boxed{10}\)
Fun fact
Percent = Latin per centum; "30 percent of" always meant \(\times\tfrac{30}{100}\). Egyptians used reciprocals for 3000+ years; the Rhind Papyrus is a long unit-fraction table.

Review problems

Review 1. \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) of a parking lot is shaded; \(\tfrac{2}{9}\) of shaded spaces are reserved. What fraction of the whole lot is shaded-reserved? Write in lowest terms as \(a/b\).
Show solution
The phrase two ninths of three quarters is a multiplication, \(\tfrac{2}{9}\times\tfrac{3}{4}\). Before multiplying, hunt for shared factors across the tops and bottoms, the same cancelling trick from 4.2. The \(3\) on top and the \(9\) on the bottom share a \(3\), leaving \(1\) and \(3\). The \(2\) on top and the \(4\) on the bottom share a \(2\), leaving \(1\) and \(2\). Now multiply the trimmed tops and bottoms, $$\frac{2}{9}\times\frac{3}{4}=\frac{1\times 1}{3\times 2}=\frac{1}{6}.$$ You can check without cancelling, \(\tfrac{2\times 3}{9\times 4}=\tfrac{6}{36}=\tfrac{1}{6}\), the same point. So the shaded staff spaces make up \(\boxed{1/6}\) of the whole lot.
Review 2. Three quarters of \(20\) students ride bikes. Split \(20\) into \(4\) equal groups, keep three. How many students ride bikes?
Show solution
The word of tells you to multiply, so you want \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) of \(20\). The quiet trick with a fraction of a whole is to handle the bottom number first. The denominator \(4\) says split the \(20\) into four equal groups, and \(20 \div 4 = 5\), so each quarter is \(5\) students. The numerator \(3\) says keep three of those quarters, which is \(3 \times 5 = 15\). You can also write it as one multiplication, \(\tfrac{3}{4} \times 20 = \tfrac{3 \times 20}{4} = \tfrac{60}{4} = 15\), and it lands in the same place. Either way, \(\boxed{15}\) students ride bikes.
Review 3. Light through two panes: outer passes \(\tfrac{2}{3}\); inner passes \(\tfrac{6}{7}\). Cancel \(3\) and \(6\) first. What fraction gets through?
Show solution
Set up the product of the two pass-through fractions, \(\tfrac{2}{3}\times\tfrac{6}{7}\). Instead of multiplying right away, look for a shared factor across the diagonal. The \(6\) on top and the \(3\) on the bottom both divide by \(3\), so the \(6\) becomes \(2\) and the \(3\) becomes \(1\). That leaves \(\tfrac{2}{1}\times\tfrac{2}{7}\). Now multiply straight across, \(2\times 2 = 4\) on top and \(1\times 7 = 7\) on the bottom, giving \(\tfrac{4}{7}\). Since \(4\) and \(7\) share no factor bigger than \(1\), it is already in lowest terms. Cancelling first kept the numbers small and meant there was nothing left to reduce at the end. \(\boxed{4/7}\)
Review 4. Each of \(8\) ornaments uses \(\tfrac{3}{10}\) m of wire. Total wire is \(8\times\tfrac{3}{10}\). How many meters are needed? Give as an improper fraction \(a/b\) in lowest terms.
Show solution
Multiplying by 8 is the same as laying down 8 copies of \(\tfrac{3}{10}\) and adding them up. When you multiply a whole number by a fraction, you scale the numerator and leave the denominator alone, since the size of each piece does not change, only how many pieces you have. So \(8\times\tfrac{3}{10}=\tfrac{8\times 3}{10}=\tfrac{24}{10}\). Now reduce. Both 24 and 10 share a factor of 2, and \(\gcd(24,10)=2\), so divide top and bottom by 2 to get \(\tfrac{24}{10}=\tfrac{12}{5}\). The 8 ornaments use \(\boxed{12/5}\) meters of wire.
Review 5. Compute \(\dfrac{9}{14}\times\dfrac{7}{12}\). Cancel \(7\) from \(7\) and \(14\), and \(3\) from \(9\) and \(12\). What fraction \(a/b\) in lowest terms do you get?
Show solution
Cancel the crossing pairs first. The \(7\) and the \(14\) share a \(7\), so they become \(1\) and \(2\). The \(9\) and the \(12\) share a \(3\), so they become \(3\) and \(4\). The problem turns into $$\frac{3}{2}\times\frac{1}{4}.$$ Now multiply straight across. The tops give \(3\times1=3\) and the bottoms give \(2\times4=8\), so the product is \(\tfrac{3}{8}\). Since \(\gcd(3,8)=1\), it is already in lowest terms. $$\boxed{3/8}$$
Review 6. A jug is \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) full. You pour off \(\tfrac{2}{5}\) of the juice inside. The two \(5\)s cancel. What fraction of the full jug did you pour out?
Show solution
Pouring off \(\tfrac{2}{5}\) of what is in the jug, while \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) is in there, is \(\tfrac{2}{5}\times\tfrac{5}{8}\). Instead of multiplying straight across into \(\tfrac{10}{40}\) and reducing later, cancel first. The 5 on the bottom of \(\tfrac{2}{5}\) and the 5 on the top of \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) cancel to 1, leaving \(\tfrac{2}{1}\times\tfrac{1}{8}=\tfrac{2}{8}\). One more pair, the 2 against the 8, gives \(\tfrac{1}{4}\). So you poured out \(\boxed{1/4}\) of the full jug.
Review 7. Chain: \(\tfrac{4}{5}\times\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{6}{7}\times\tfrac{7}{8}\). Most tops and bottoms cancel in pairs. What single fraction \(a/b\) does the chain equal?
Show solution
Write the product as one big fraction by collecting all the tops and all the bottoms. $$\frac{4}{5}\times\frac{5}{6}\times\frac{6}{7}\times\frac{7}{8}=\frac{4\times5\times6\times7}{5\times6\times7\times8}.$$ Now look down the two rows. The \(5\) on top cancels the \(5\) on the bottom, the \(6\) cancels the \(6\), and the \(7\) cancels the \(7\). Each scaling handed its new number straight to the next one, so every middle piece pairs off and disappears. $$\frac{4\times\cancel{5}\times\cancel{6}\times\cancel{7}}{\cancel{5}\times\cancel{6}\times\cancel{7}\times8}=\frac{4}{8}.$$ Only the first top, \(4\), and the last bottom, \(8\), survive. That is the telescoping idea, the chain collapses to first-top over last-bottom. Finally \(\dfrac{4}{8}\) reduces by dividing both parts by \(4\), giving \(\dfrac{1}{2}\). $$\boxed{1/2}$$
Review 8. A stretch setting multiplies by \(\tfrac{9}{4}\). What single fraction \(a/b\) multiplies with \(\tfrac{9}{4}\) to give exactly \(1\)?
Show solution
You want the number that multiplies with \(\tfrac{9}{4}\) to land on exactly \(1\). That partner is the reciprocal, and you find it by swapping the top and bottom. Flipping \(\tfrac{9}{4}\) gives \(\tfrac{4}{9}\). Check it by multiplying straight across. \[\frac{9}{4}\times\frac{4}{9}=\frac{9\times 4}{4\times 9}=\frac{36}{36}=1.\] The product is \(1\), so the flip really does undo the stretch. The undo setting is \(\boxed{4/9}\).
Review 9. A \(4\) L drum is emptied with a \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) L dispenser. How many full pumps empty the drum?
Show solution
The question is how many \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) liter pumps fit inside \(4\) liters, so we compute \(4 \div \tfrac{1}{3}\). Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal, and the reciprocal of \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) is \(3\). So \(4 \div \tfrac{1}{3} = 4 \times 3 = 12\). You can also see it directly. One liter holds \(3\) of the small pumps, and there are \(4\) liters, which gives \(4 \times 3 = 12\). The drum empties after \(\boxed{12}\) full pumps.
Review 10. A tank is \(\tfrac{7}{10}\) full. Each truck carries \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) of a full tank. How many truckloads does the current water fill? Give as \(a/b\) in lowest terms.
Show solution
The water sitting in the tank is \(\tfrac{7}{10}\) of a full tank, and each truck hauls away \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) of a full tank. Counting truckloads means counting how many half-tanks fit inside seven tenths, which is the division \(\tfrac{7}{10} \div \tfrac{1}{2}\). To divide by a fraction, flip it and multiply. The flip of \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) is \(\tfrac{2}{1}\), so \(\tfrac{7}{10} \div \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{7}{10} \times \tfrac{2}{1}\). Multiply straight across to get \(\tfrac{7 \times 2}{10 \times 1} = \tfrac{14}{10}\). Both numbers share a factor of \(2\), and dividing top and bottom by \(2\) gives \(\tfrac{7}{5}\). That makes sense, since a bit more than one full half-tank should come out, and \(\tfrac{7}{5}\) is just past \(1\). $$\boxed{7/5}$$
Review 11. Tray A holds \(\tfrac{10}{21}\) of a ream; Tray B holds \(\tfrac{5}{14}\). Compute \(\tfrac{10}{21}\div\tfrac{5}{14}\) and give the result as \(a/b\) in lowest terms.
Show solution
Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal, so flip the divisor. \(\tfrac{10}{21}\div\tfrac{5}{14}=\tfrac{10}{21}\times\tfrac{14}{5}\). Now cancel before multiplying. The \(10\) on top and the \(5\) on the bottom share a factor of \(5\), which turns them into \(2\) and \(1\). The \(14\) on top and the \(21\) on the bottom share a factor of \(7\), which turns them into \(2\) and \(3\). That leaves \(\tfrac{2}{3}\times\tfrac{2}{1}=\tfrac{4}{3}\). Multiplying straight across without canceling gives \(\tfrac{140}{105}\), and since \(\gcd(140,105)=35\), this also reduces to \(\tfrac{4}{3}\), so both paths agree. $$\boxed{4/3}$$
Review 12. Priya is \(\tfrac{3}{5}\) through her novel, which is \(27\) pages. Since \(\tfrac{3}{5}\) of the whole \(=27\), multiply \(27\) by \(\tfrac{5}{3}\). How many pages total?
Show solution
The clue is the word of. Three fifths of the whole book is the same as \(\tfrac{3}{5}\) times the total, and that product is the 27 pages Priya has read. So the total is whatever number, when shrunk to three fifths of itself, lands on 27. To undo multiplying by \(\tfrac{3}{5}\), multiply by its reciprocal \(\tfrac{5}{3}\). $$27\times\frac{5}{3}$$ Write \(27\) as \(\tfrac{27}{1}\) and cancel before multiplying. The \(3\) in the bottom divides the \(27\) on top, since \(27\div 3=9\). That leaves \(9\times 5=45\). A quick sanity check: three fifths of 45 is \(\tfrac{3}{5}\times 45 = 27\), which matches her journal. The whole book is \(\boxed{45}\) pages long.
Review 13. A spool holds \(\tfrac{15}{4}\) m; each bow needs \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) m. Compute \(\tfrac{15}{4}\div\tfrac{3}{8}\). How many whole bows does the ribbon make?
Show solution
Start by rewriting the division as multiplication by the reciprocal. Flip \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) to \(\tfrac{8}{3}\): $$\frac{15}{4}\div\frac{3}{8}=\frac{15}{4}\times\frac{8}{3}.$$ Now cancel before multiplying. The \(8\) and the \(4\) share a factor of \(4\), so they become \(2\) and \(1\). The \(15\) and the \(3\) share a factor of \(3\), so they become \(5\) and \(1\): $$\frac{15}{4}\times\frac{8}{3}=\frac{5}{1}\times\frac{2}{1}=10.$$ The quotient is a whole number, which means the ribbon divides evenly with nothing left over. So you can tie exactly \(\boxed{10}\) bows.
Review 14. Five eighths of a \(24\) g block is measured out and split into jars of \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) g each. How many jars does the merchant fill?
Show solution
This problem hides two steps, a multiply and a divide, inside one story. Take them in order. First, how much saffron does she measure out? That is five eighths of \(24\) grams, so \(24 \times \tfrac{5}{8}\). Since \(24 \div 8 = 3\), this is \(3 \times 5 = 15\) grams. Now she splits those \(15\) grams into jars holding \(\tfrac34\) gram each. Asking how many \(\tfrac34\) gram scoops fit into \(15\) grams is a division, \(15 \div \tfrac34\). Flip the divisor and multiply, so \(15 \times \tfrac43 = \tfrac{60}{3} = 20\). The two operations pull in opposite directions, multiplying by a fraction less than \(1\) shrank the amount, then dividing by a fraction less than \(1\) grew the count back up. She fills \(\boxed{20}\) jars.
Review 15. Three filters: \(\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{6}{7}\times\tfrac{7}{8}\). The chain telescopes. Divide that result by \(\tfrac{5}{8}\). What single number is the answer?
Show solution
Start with the three filters. Multiplying straight across, \(\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{6}{7}\times\tfrac{7}{8}=\dfrac{5\times 6\times 7}{6\times 7\times 8}\). The \(6\) and the \(7\) each appear once on top and once on the bottom, so they cancel and leave \(\dfrac{5}{8}\). This is a telescoping chain, where each filter's bottom is swallowed by the next filter's top. Now divide by the baseline. Dividing by \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) means multiplying by its reciprocal \(\tfrac{8}{5}\), so we have \(\dfrac{5}{8}\times\dfrac{8}{5}=\dfrac{5\times 8}{8\times 5}\). The \(5\) cancels with the \(5\) and the \(8\) cancels with the \(8\), so nothing is left but the number itself. Any nonzero fraction times its own reciprocal is \(1\), which makes sense because the surviving signal turned out to be exactly the baseline. $$\boxed{1}$$
