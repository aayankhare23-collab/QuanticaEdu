Prealgebra · Lesson 7.4

Proportions

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Writing \(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}\) is a proportion. Two quantities are proportional when their ratio stays constant. The coming shortcut, cross-multiplication, is just clearing denominators from Chapter 6.

A fountain pours at 9 liters per minute. You timed it for 2 minutes and caught 18 liters; a friend measured 45 liters in 5 minutes. Keeping that same flow, how many liters pour out in 8 minutes?
Show solution
Set up a proportion with matching units in matching positions, minutes over liters on both sides, and let \(x\) be the liters in \(8\) minutes. $$\frac{2}{18} = \frac{8}{x}$$ Cross-multiply, which is just clearing both denominators at once. For \(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}\) the rule \(a\cdot d = b\cdot c\) holds, so $$2\cdot x = 18\cdot 8$$ $$2x = 144$$ Divide both sides by \(2\). $$x = \frac{144}{2} = 72$$ A quick sanity check, the steady rate is \(9\) liters per minute, and \(9\times 8 = 72\), which matches. So in \(8\) minutes the fountain pours out \(\boxed{72}\) liters.
Key idea
Proportional quantities keep the same ratio at every scale, \(9\) liters per minute whether you wait \(2\) minutes or \(50\). When a ratio stays steady like that no matter how big or small the amounts grow, the two quantities are proportional. Setting one of those equal ratios next to another, like \(\tfrac{9}{1} = \tfrac{x}{8}\), gives a proportion, an equation that says two ratios are equal. That steady ratio is the whole heart of it, since once you know it holds you can build a proportion to reach any new value.
A camp kitchen uses 2 cups of rice for every 5 campers. Tonight 15 campers arrive. How many cups of dry rice are needed?
Show solution
Write the two ratios as a proportion, with cups on top and campers on the bottom in both, $$\frac{2}{5} = \frac{x}{15}.$$ The fastest path here is scaling, the same move from equivalent ratios. The campers went from \(5\) to \(15\), which is \(\times 3\), so the cups go up by the same \(\times 3\), giving \(2 \times 3 = 6\). To prove it with the new tool, cross-multiply. Setting the cross-products equal gives \(5x = 2 \times 15\), so \(5x = 30\). Divide both sides by \(5\) to get \(x = 6\). Check that the cross-products match, \(2 \times 15 = 30\) and \(5 \times 6 = 30\), so the ratios truly are equal. The cook needs \(\boxed{6}\) cups of dry rice.
Key idea
Scaling a proportion. When one matching pair is easy to compare, find the number you multiply the known side by to land on the new side, then do the very same multiplication to its partner, and the unknown falls out. It is the equivalent-ratio idea from 7.1 written as an equation. The catch is that this only feels quick when the multiplier jumps out, like a clean doubling or tripling. When one part grows from, say, \(14\) to \(20\), there is no tidy whole-number jump to spot, and you will need a method that does not lean on seeing one.
A paint mix calls for 8 jars of pigment in 14 liters of base. A big order needs 20 jars. Set up \(\frac{8}{14} = \frac{20}{x}\) and solve. How many liters of base are needed?
Show solution
Set up the proportion with jars on top and liters on the bottom in both ratios. $$\frac{8}{14} = \frac{20}{x}$$ Back in lesson 6.2 you cleared a fraction by multiplying the whole equation by its denominator. Do that for both denominators here. Multiply both sides by \(14\) and by \(x\). On the left the \(14\) cancels, and on the right the \(x\) cancels, leaving \(8 \cdot x\) on one side and \(14 \cdot 20\) on the other. $$8 \cdot x = 14 \cdot 20$$ That shortcut, where each numerator pairs with the other side's denominator, is called cross-multiplication. It works because it is just clearing the fractions like any ordinary equation. Now finish solving. $$8x = 280$$ $$x = \frac{280}{8} = 35$$ The big order needs \(\boxed{35}\) liters of base. Check the cross-products. \(8 \times 35 = 280\) and \(14 \times 20 = 280\), so they match.
A 3D printer uses 5 grams of filament for 8 layers. How many grams does a 6-layer piece need? (There is no clean whole-number scale from 8 to 6, so use cross-multiplication.)
Show solution
Because the rate is proportional, grams and layers stay in the same ratio. Put grams on top and layers on the bottom in both fractions, with the known test on one side and the unknown grams \(x\) on the other. $$\frac{5}{8} = \frac{x}{6}$$ There is no whole-number multiplier from \(8\) to \(6\), so scaling stalls. Cross-multiply instead, which multiplies each numerator by the opposite denominator and clears both fractions. $$8 \times x = 5 \times 6$$ $$8x = 30$$ Now divide both sides by \(8\). $$x = \frac{30}{8} = \frac{15}{4}$$ Check the cross-products to be sure, \(5 \times 6 = 30\) and \(8 \times \frac{15}{4} = 30\), so the ratios truly match. As an exact decimal, \(\frac{15}{4} = 3.75\), so the \(6\)-layer piece needs \(\boxed{15/4}\) grams of filament.
Key idea
Cross-multiplication. When two ratios are set equal, \(\tfrac{a}{b} = \tfrac{c}{d}\), multiply each numerator by the opposite denominator to get \(ad = bc\), and the fractions are gone. It works because cross-multiplying is really multiplying both sides by both denominators at once, so they cancel and the balance is untouched, the same clearing move from 6.2. Scaling is faster when you spot an easy whole-number jump, but cross-multiplication needs no tidy jump, so it solves every proportion, even when the answer is a fraction or decimal.
A map uses the scale 6 cm = 10 km. Two overlooks are 9 km apart on the trail. How many centimeters apart are they on the map?
Show solution
Keep real kilometers on top and map centimeters on the bottom in both ratios, which puts the unknown map distance in a denominator. $$\frac{10}{6} = \frac{9}{x}$$ Cross-multiply, top times opposite bottom on each side. $$10x = 6 \times 9 = 54$$ The unknown was on the bottom, but cross-multiplication treated it exactly like a numerator, and now one division finishes the job. Divide both sides by \(10\). $$x = \frac{54}{10} = \frac{27}{5}$$ As a decimal that is \(5.4\) centimeters, which is sensible since \(9\) kilometers is a bit less than the \(10\) kilometers that spanned \(6\) centimeters. The two overlooks are \(\boxed{27/5}\) centimeters apart on the map.
A 3-inch mast represents 90 real inches; the boom is 5 inches. A student writes \(\frac{3}{90} = \frac{x}{5}\) with mixed units. Set it up correctly. What is the real boom length in inches?
Show solution
Match the units before you write anything. Put real inches on top and model inches on the bottom for both ratios, so each side is the same kind of rate. The mast tells you the rate, \(\frac{90 \text{ real}}{3 \text{ model}}\), and the boom follows the same rate, \(\frac{x \text{ real}}{5 \text{ model}}\). Setting them equal gives the proportion $$\frac{90}{3} = \frac{x}{5}.$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, which gives \(3x = 90 \times 5\), so \(3x = 450\). Divide both sides by \(3\) to get \(x = 150\). Check the matching positions, real over model equals real over model, and the cross-products agree since \(90 \times 5 = 450\) and \(3 \times 150 = 450\). The real boom is \(\boxed{150}\) inches long.
Key idea
Match units before cross-multiplying. A proportion only works when matching units sit in matching positions, so the tops of both sides measure the same kind of thing and the bottoms do too. Then both sides really are the same rate, real over model equals real over model, or miles over inches equals miles over inches. If you flip one side by accident, cross-multiplication still does its job, it just faithfully multiplies the wrong quantities and hands you an answer that cannot be right. Read both sides aloud first to confirm.
A harbor kiosk trades at one fixed rate: 7 dollars gets 91 shells. How many shells does the traveler get for 11 dollars?
Show solution
Since the kiosk uses one fixed rate, dollars and shells are proportional, so the morning trade and the afternoon trade form a single proportion. Keep dollars on top and shells on the bottom in both fractions so the units match. $$\frac{7 \text{ dollars}}{91 \text{ shells}} = \frac{11 \text{ dollars}}{x \text{ shells}}$$ Cross-multiply, which clears both denominators and leaves a plain equation. $$7 \cdot x = 91 \cdot 11$$ $$7x = 1001$$ Divide both sides by \(7\). $$x = \frac{1001}{7} = 143$$ You can check it. The first trade gives \(\frac{7}{91} = \frac{1}{13}\), one dollar for thirteen shells, and \(\frac{11}{143} = \frac{1}{13}\) is the same rate. She gets \(\boxed{143}\) shells.
A trail-snack recipe uses 5 scoops oats for every 2 scoops cashews. A big batch needs 12 scoops of oats. How many scoops of cashews keep the ratio right?
Show solution
Set up the proportion with oats on top and cashews on the bottom on both sides, $$\frac{5}{2} = \frac{12}{x}.$$ The scale from \(5\) to \(12\) is not a whole number, so cross-multiply to clear the denominators. Multiply each numerator by the opposite denominator, $$5 \cdot x = 2 \cdot 12.$$ That gives \(5x = 24\). Divide both sides by \(5\) to get \(x = \frac{24}{5}\), which is the exact decimal \(4.8\). Check the ratio, \(\frac{12}{4.8} = \frac{5}{2}\), so the taste stays the same. The troop needs \(\boxed{24/5}\) scoops of cashews.
A park ranger measures a 6-foot rod casting an 8-foot shadow. Across the field, a fire-watch antenna casts a 28-foot shadow at the same instant. How tall is the antenna in feet?
Show solution
Both objects stand straight up and their rays slant the same way, so the height-to-shadow ratio is the same for each. Put the rod's ratio equal to the antenna's ratio, with height on top and shadow on the bottom in both spots. $$\frac{6}{8} = \frac{h}{28}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, multiplying each top by the other bottom. $$8 \cdot h = 6 \cdot 28$$ That gives \(8h = 168\). Divide both sides by \(8\). $$h = \frac{168}{8} = 21$$ The fire-watch antenna stands \(\boxed{21}\) feet tall.
Fun fact
Thales reportedly found a pyramid's height by measuring its shadow when the sun made height equal shadow. Parallel sun rays create similar triangles, the proportion method in this lesson.
At a craft fair, 6 bundles cost 15 dollars at a fixed price. You want 10 bundles. How many dollars do 10 bundles cost?
Show solution
Write the known price and the wanted price as two equal ratios, keeping bundles over dollars in both positions so the units match. Let \(x\) be the cost of 10 bundles. $$\frac{6}{15} = \frac{10}{x}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, which gives \(6 \cdot x = 15 \cdot 10\), so \(6x = 150\). Divide both sides by 6 to find \(x = \frac{150}{6} = 25\). You can check this the unit-rate way from 7.3. One bundle costs \(15 \div 6 = 2.5\) dollars, and 10 of them cost \(2.5 \times 10 = 25\) dollars. Both routes land on the same number, so 10 bundles cost \(\boxed{25}\) dollars.
Key idea
Two routes, same answer. A proportion is just two equal rates side by side, so cross-multiplying is not the only honest way through. You can find the per-one value first, the unit rate from 7.3, then scale it up to whatever amount you want. Both routes land on the same answer every time, so pick whichever fits the numbers in front of you. Cross-multiplication is the dependable workhorse, and the unit-rate route is the easy one when one side divides cleanly into a nice per-one amount.
A design is 8 inches wide and 5 inches tall. The printed banner will be 20 inches wide. What height keeps the same shape without stretching?
Show solution
Match the units in matching positions. Both ratios are width over height, so the design gives \(\frac{8}{5}\) and the banner gives \(\frac{20}{x}\), and setting them equal gives the proportion $$\frac{8}{5} = \frac{20}{x}.$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, which gives \(8x = 5 \cdot 20\), so \(8x = 100\). Divide both sides by \(8\) to get \(x = \frac{100}{8} = \frac{25}{2}\). As a quick check, that ratio equals the exact decimal \(12.5\) inches, and the cross-products match since \(8 \cdot \frac{25}{2} = 100\) and \(5 \cdot 20 = 100\). The height of the printed banner is \(\boxed{25/2}\).
A mural is 5 ft tall and 12 ft wide. A scaled-up copy is 20 ft tall. How many feet wide is the big copy?
Show solution
Because the two rectangles are similar, their height-to-width ratios are equal. Keep matching positions together, small height over small width equals big height over big width, and let \(x\) be the unknown width. $$\frac{5}{12} = \frac{20}{x}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$5 \cdot x = 12 \cdot 20$$ The right side is \(240\), so \(5x = 240\). Divide both sides by \(5\). $$x = \frac{240}{5} = 48$$ Check the pairing, \(\frac{5}{12}\) and \(\frac{20}{48}\) both reduce to \(\frac{5}{12}\), so the shape stays true. The big copy is \(\boxed{48}\) feet wide.

Review problems

Review 1. A fruit punch keeps juice to water in the ratio \(4:10\). A bigger batch uses 6 cups of juice. Set up \(\frac{4}{10} = \frac{6}{x}\) and solve. How many cups of water keep the same ratio?
Show solution
Both batches must hold the same juice to water ratio, so set the two ratios equal with the unknown water as x. Keep juice on top and water on the bottom in both fractions. $$\frac{4}{10} = \frac{6}{x}$$ Cross-multiply, which means multiply each numerator by the other denominator. $$4 \cdot x = 10 \cdot 6$$ $$4x = 60$$ Divide both sides by 4. $$x = \frac{60}{4} = 15$$ Check the cross-products to be sure. \(4 \cdot 15 = 60\) and \(10 \cdot 6 = 60\), which match, so the ratio holds. The bigger batch needs \(\boxed{15}\) cups of water.
Review 2. A stage paint uses 3 oz pigment for every 7 oz base. A bigger batch uses 21 oz base. Set up \(\frac{3}{7} = \frac{x}{21}\). How many ounces of teal pigment are needed?
Show solution
Start from the proportion that keeps the color constant, \(\frac{3}{7} = \frac{x}{21}\). Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, which gives \(7 \cdot x = 3 \cdot 21\), so \(7x = 63\). Divide both sides by 7 to get \(x = \frac{63}{7} = 9\). You can sanity check by scaling. The base grew from 7 to 21, a clean \(\times 3\), and the pigment grows the same way, \(3 \times 3 = 9\). They need \(\boxed{9}\) ounces of teal pigment.
Review 3. A pottery glaze uses 6 scoops of clay powder for every 5 cups of water. A big batch uses 40 cups of water. How many scoops of clay powder are needed?
Show solution
Keep the ratio of scoops of clay powder to cups of water the same. Let \(x\) be the scoops needed for 40 cups, so $$\frac{6}{5} = \frac{x}{40}.$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, giving \(5x = 6 \times 40\), so \(5x = 240\). Divide both sides by 5 to get \(x = 48\). The batch needs \(\boxed{48}\) scoops of clay powder.
Review 4. A recipe uses 7 g of yeast for every 10 dinner rolls. The baker wants just 4 rolls. How many grams of yeast are needed?
Show solution
The grams of yeast per roll never changes, so the two ratios are equal. Put grams on top and rolls on the bottom in both fractions, which gives \(\frac{7}{10} = \frac{x}{4}\). Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, so \(10x = 7 \times 4\), which is \(10x = 28\). Divide both sides by \(10\) to get \(x = \frac{28}{10}\). Reduce that fraction by dividing top and bottom by \(2\), leaving \(x = \frac{14}{5}\), the same as \(2.8\) grams. So the bakery needs \(\boxed{14/5}\) grams of yeast for \(4\) rolls.
Review 5. A perfume sample uses 12 drops rose oil for every 21 drops sandalwood. A tester vial uses only 8 drops of rose oil. How many drops of sandalwood are in the tester?
Show solution
Both mixes keep the same ratio of rose oil to sandalwood oil, so write a proportion with rose on top and sandalwood on the bottom in each fraction. $$\frac{12}{21} = \frac{8}{x}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$12 \cdot x = 21 \cdot 8$$ $$12x = 168$$ Divide both sides by \(12\). $$x = \frac{168}{12} = 14$$ The tester vial holds \(\boxed{14}\) drops of sandalwood oil.
Review 6. Two flower beds keep the same ratio of 9 tulips to 12 daffodils. The second bed has 15 tulips. How many daffodils are in the second bed?
Show solution
Put tulips on top and daffodils on the bottom in both fractions so the units match in matching positions. Let \(x\) be the number of daffodils in the second bed. $$\frac{9}{12} = \frac{15}{x}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$9 \cdot x = 12 \cdot 15$$ $$9x = 180$$ Divide both sides by \(9\). $$x = \frac{180}{9} = 20$$ The second bed has \(\boxed{20}\) daffodils.
Review 7. A board game café trades 5 dollars for 65 tokens at a fixed rate. You hand over 8 dollars. How many tokens do you get?
Show solution
Because the rate is fixed, the ratio of dollars to tokens is the same for both swaps. Keep dollars on top and tokens on the bottom in both ratios. $$\frac{5}{65} = \frac{8}{x}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$5 \times x = 65 \times 8$$ $$5x = 520$$ Divide both sides by \(5\). $$x = \frac{520}{5} = 104$$ So \(8\) dollars gives you \(\boxed{104}\) tokens.
Review 8. A pancake batter uses 5 cups flour for every 2 cups sugar. A big batch calls for 12 cups of sugar. How many cups of flour does it need?
Show solution
The flour to sugar ratio stays the same, so set the recipe ratio equal to the big-batch ratio with flour on top and sugar on the bottom in both places. $$\frac{5}{2} = \frac{x}{12}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$2x = 5 \times 12$$ $$2x = 60$$ Divide both sides by \(2\). $$x = \frac{60}{2} = 30$$ The giant batch needs \(\boxed{30}\) cups of flour.
Review 9. A hiking map: 2 cm stands for 45 km. Two ranger stations sit 5 cm apart on the map. How many kilometers apart are they in real life?
Show solution
Keep map centimeters on top and real kilometers on the bottom in both ratios, with \(x\) standing for the real distance you are after. $$\frac{2}{45} = \frac{5}{x}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$2 \cdot x = 45 \cdot 5$$ $$2x = 225$$ Divide both sides by \(2\). $$x = \frac{225}{2} = 112.5$$ So the two stations are \(\boxed{112.5}\) kilometers apart in real life.
Review 10. A garden gnome (4 ft tall) casts a 5-foot shadow. A nearby water tower casts a 50-foot shadow at the same moment. How tall is the water tower in feet?
Show solution
Both objects share the same height-to-shadow ratio, so I set up a proportion with height on top and shadow length on the bottom in both spots. Letting \(h\) be the tower's height, $$\frac{4}{5} = \frac{h}{50}.$$ Cross-multiplying clears the denominators, \(5h = 4 \times 50\), so \(5h = 200\). Dividing both sides by \(5\) gives \(h = 40\). The water tower is \(\boxed{40}\) feet tall.
Review 11. A van holds a steady speed, covering 60 miles in 4 hours. At that same speed, how many miles does it cover in 7 hours?
Show solution
Since the van holds a steady speed, miles and hours stay in a constant ratio, so I set up a proportion with miles over hours on both sides. $$\frac{60 \text{ miles}}{4 \text{ hours}} = \frac{x \text{ miles}}{7 \text{ hours}}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$4x = 60 \times 7$$ $$4x = 420$$ Now divide both sides by \(4\). $$x = \frac{420}{4} = 105$$ So in \(7\) hours the van covers \(\boxed{105}\) miles.
Review 12. A glassworker enlarges a 6-inch-wide by 9-inch-tall panel so the new width is 22 inches. Keeping the same shape, how tall is the enlarged panel in inches?
Show solution
Keeping the panel the same shape means the width-to-height comparison cannot change, so the two panels form a proportion. Write width on top and height on the bottom for both, the original on the left and the enlargement on the right. $$\frac{6}{9} = \frac{22}{x}$$ Here \(x\) is the height of the enlarged panel in inches. To solve, cross-multiply, which clears both denominators just like in equation solving. Multiply the top of each side by the bottom of the other. $$6 \times x = 9 \times 22$$ The right side is \(9 \times 22 = 198\), so the equation becomes \(6x = 198\). Now divide both sides by \(6\) to get \(x\) by itself. $$x = \frac{198}{6} = 33$$ A quick check shows the comparison held. The enlargement is \(22:33\), and dividing both parts by \(11\) gives \(2:3\), the very same shape as \(6:9\). So the enlarged panel is \(\boxed{33}\) inches tall.
Review 13. A sourdough starter uses 3 g starter for every 8 g flour. Tomorrow the baker uses 5 g flour. How many grams of starter should go in?
Show solution
Keep starter on top and flour on the bottom in both ratios, so the proportion is $$\frac{3}{8} = \frac{x}{5}.$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators, which gives \(8x = 3 \times 5\), so \(8x = 15\). Divide both sides by \(8\) to get \(x = \frac{15}{8}\). As a check, \(\frac{15}{8} = 1.875\) grams of starter, and the cross-products match since \(3 \times 5 = 15\) and \(8 \times \frac{15}{8} = 15\). So the baker needs \(\boxed{15/8}\) grams of starter.
Review 14. Two similar triangular sails: the small sail has a 12 cm bottom edge and 8 cm slanted edge. The enlarged sail has a 27 cm bottom edge. How long is its slanted edge in centimeters?
Show solution
Because the sails are similar, the bottom edge and the slanted edge keep a constant ratio. Put bottom edges on top and slanted edges on the bottom in both ratios. $$\frac{12}{8} = \frac{27}{x}$$ Cross-multiply to clear the denominators. $$12x = 8 \times 27$$ $$12x = 216$$ Divide both sides by \(12\). $$x = \frac{216}{12} = 18$$ The slanted edge of the enlarged sail is \(\boxed{18}\) centimeters.
