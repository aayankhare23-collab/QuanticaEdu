Prealgebra · Lesson 4.8

Comparing Fractions

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Two climbers sit at \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) and \(\tfrac{8}{11}\) of the way up. Eight is more than seven, so the second seems ahead, but elevenths are thinner slivers than ninths, so it is stacking more pieces that are each smaller. More thin pieces against fewer fat pieces is a genuine standoff. This lesson builds a small kit of moves for fights exactly like this, cross-multiplying, matching numerators, and leaning on landmarks, and the real skill is reaching for the one move that turns a given standoff into something you can just read.

North crew: \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) of a path. East crew: \(\tfrac{7}{12}\). Rewrite over a common denominator, find which crew leads, then report the gap as a single fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
The trap here is comparing \(5\) against \(7\) directly. Those numbers count pieces, but an eighth of the path and a twelfth of the path are not the same length, so the counts are not yet on speaking terms. The fix is to chop both into identical pieces. Since \(8\) and \(12\) both divide \(24\), twenty-fourths do the job. Scale each fraction without changing its value, \(\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{5\times 3}{8\times 3}=\tfrac{15}{24}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{12}=\tfrac{7\times 2}{12\times 2}=\tfrac{14}{24}\). Now every piece is one twenty-fourth of the path, so the fractions really are just counts of the same thing. The north crew has \(15\) of those pieces and the east crew has \(14\), so the north crew is in the lead. To find how far ahead, subtract over the shared denominator, \(\tfrac{15}{24}-\tfrac{14}{24}=\tfrac{15-14}{24}=\tfrac{1}{24}\). Since \(\gcd(1,24)=1\), that fraction is already in simplest form. The north crew is ahead by exactly one twenty-fourth of the path, a gap so thin you could only ever spot it by making the pieces match. \(\boxed{1/24}\)
Key idea
Compare by common denominator. Two fractions feel hard to compare only because their pieces are different sizes, the way you cannot tell whether twelve dimes beats two quarters until you count everything in cents. Give both fractions a common denominator and every piece becomes the same size, so the question turns into plain counting and the bigger numerator wins, sitting farther right on the line. The least common denominator keeps the numbers small, but any shared denominator works just as well. This method is slower than the shortcuts ahead, yet it never lies and works on any pair of fractions you will ever meet.
Compare \(\tfrac{4}{7}\) and \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) via the diagonal products \(4\times 8\) and \(7\times 5\). Which is larger, and what is the gap in simplest form?
Show solution
Rewrite both fractions over \(56\) without changing their value. The \(\tfrac{4}{7}\) becomes \(\tfrac{4\times 8}{56}=\tfrac{32}{56}\), and \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) becomes \(\tfrac{7\times 5}{56}=\tfrac{35}{56}\). Notice the tops, \(32\) and \(35\), are precisely the diagonal products of the original fractions. Once both fractions share the bottom \(56\), the bottom carries no information anymore, so comparing the fractions is the same as comparing those two tops. Since \(35\gt 32\), the fraction \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) sits farther to the right, so \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) is the larger one. The gap is \(\tfrac{35}{56}-\tfrac{32}{56}=\tfrac{3}{56}\). The numbers \(3\) and \(56\) share no common factor, so this is already in simplest form. \(\boxed{3/56}\)
Key idea
Cross-multiplication for ordering. When you compare \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) and \(\tfrac{c}{d}\) over the shared bottom \(bd\), the two tops become \(a\times d\) and \(b\times c\), and that bottom is the same for both, so it never decides anything. You can skip writing it and just look at the two diagonal products, with the bigger product belonging to the bigger fraction. It is the very same diagonals from before, when \(ad=bc\) told us two fractions landed on the same point. Now they tell us which point sits farther right, as long as both bottoms are positive, so if a denominator is negative, tidy the minus sign up onto the top before cross-multiplying.
Settle the wall: \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) vs \(\tfrac{8}{11}\). Compare \(7\times 11\) against \(9\times 8\). Which is higher, and by how much? Gap in simplest form.
Show solution
Cross-multiply by sending each numerator across to the opposite denominator. The left marker \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) gives \(7\times 11=77\), and the right marker \(\tfrac{8}{11}\) gives \(9\times 8=72\). Since \(77\gt 72\), the product tied to \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) is the larger one, so \(\tfrac{7}{9}\gt\tfrac{8}{11}\). The left marker sits higher. Now measure the gap. Both products were really numerators over the shared bottom \(9\times 11=99\), so \(\tfrac{7}{9}=\tfrac{77}{99}\) and \(\tfrac{8}{11}=\tfrac{72}{99}\). Subtracting, \(\tfrac{77}{99}-\tfrac{72}{99}=\tfrac{5}{99}\). Check for common factors. The number \(99\) is \(9\times 11\), and \(5\) shares no factor with either, so the fraction is already in simplest form. The left marker beats the right by \(\boxed{5/99}\) of the route.
\(\tfrac{6}{11}\) vs \(\tfrac{6}{13}\): same tops. Elevenths are fatter than thirteenths, so six fat slices win. Find the gap in simplest form.
Show solution
Both fractions have a top of \(6\), so this is a common-numerator comparison. When the tops match, each fraction is just six copies of one piece, and the size of that piece is set by the bottom. A bottom of \(11\) cuts the canister into eleven pieces, a bottom of \(13\) cuts it into thirteen, and cutting into fewer pieces makes each piece bigger. So an eleventh beats a thirteenth, and six elevenths beats six thirteenths. That makes \(\tfrac{6}{11}\) the larger share. Notice we settled the direction without finding a common bottom at all, just by reading the denominators. Now for the gap. To subtract we do need a shared bottom, so use \(11 \times 13 = 143\). Scaling up, \(\tfrac{6}{11} = \tfrac{6 \times 13}{143} = \tfrac{78}{143}\) and \(\tfrac{6}{13} = \tfrac{6 \times 11}{143} = \tfrac{66}{143}\). Subtract the tops, \(78 - 66 = 12\), which gives \(\tfrac{12}{143}\). Check whether it reduces. Since \(143 = 11 \times 13\) and \(12 = 2 \times 2 \times 3\) share no common factor, \(\gcd(12,143) = 1\) and the fraction is already in simplest form. So the larger plan, \(\tfrac{6}{11}\), beats the other by \(\boxed{12/143}\).
Key idea
Equal numerators trick. You do not have to match the bottoms, you can match the tops instead. Once two fractions have the same numerator they hold the same number of pieces, so whichever one has the bigger pieces is the bigger fraction. And bigger pieces means a smaller denominator, because cutting a whole into fewer parts leaves each part fatter. So when the tops are equal, the smaller bottom wins, which feels backwards until you remember that an eleventh is a fatter slice than a thirteenth.
Blue \(\tfrac{4}{15}\), red \(\tfrac{3}{10}\). Match tops to \(12\): \(\tfrac{12}{45}\) vs \(\tfrac{12}{40}\). Smaller bottom wins. Which share is larger, and by how much? Gap in simplest form.
Show solution
Follow the plan and match the tops. The least common multiple of the numerators \(4\) and \(3\) is \(12\). Scale each fraction so its top becomes \(12\). For the blue ribbon, multiply top and bottom by \(3\) to get \(\tfrac{4}{15}=\tfrac{12}{45}\). For the red ribbon, multiply top and bottom by \(4\) to get \(\tfrac{3}{10}=\tfrac{12}{40}\). Now both fractions describe \(12\) equal pieces of something. The blue version breaks its whole into \(45\) parts, the red version into only \(40\) parts. Cutting the same \(12\) pieces from fewer, fatter parts means each piece is bigger, so \(\tfrac{12}{40}\) is the larger fraction. That means the red ribbon, \(\tfrac{3}{10}\), is the bigger share of its roll. Now find the gap by subtracting the smaller from the larger. A common bottom for \(10\) and \(15\) is \(30\). Rewrite both originals as thirtieths, \(\tfrac{3}{10}=\tfrac{9}{30}\) and \(\tfrac{4}{15}=\tfrac{8}{30}\). Subtract the tops, \(\tfrac{9}{30}-\tfrac{8}{30}=\tfrac{1}{30}\). Since \(1\) and \(30\) share no common factor, it is already in simplest form. The red ribbon is the larger share, and it beats the blue ribbon by \(\boxed{1/30}\) of a roll.
Six scores: \(\tfrac{5}{9},\tfrac{4}{9},\tfrac{7}{15},\tfrac{9}{16},\tfrac{11}{20},\tfrac{8}{17}\). Beat \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) when top \(>\) half bottom. How many qualify?
Show solution
The whole trick is that \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) of something split into \(b\) parts is \(\tfrac{b}{2}\) of those parts. So a fraction \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) is more than \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) exactly when its top \(a\) is more than half of its bottom \(b\). No common denominators, no arithmetic beyond comparing one number to another. Let us walk the board. For \(\tfrac{5}{9}\), half of \(9\) is \(4\tfrac{1}{2}\), and \(5\) clears it, so this one wins. For \(\tfrac{4}{9}\), half of \(9\) is still \(4\tfrac{1}{2}\), and \(4\) falls short, so it loses. For \(\tfrac{7}{15}\), half of \(15\) is \(7\tfrac{1}{2}\), and \(7\) does not reach it, so it loses. For \(\tfrac{9}{16}\), half of \(16\) is \(8\), and \(9\) beats \(8\), so it wins. For \(\tfrac{11}{20}\), half of \(20\) is \(10\), and \(11\) beats \(10\), so it wins. For \(\tfrac{8}{17}\), half of \(17\) is \(8\tfrac{1}{2}\), and \(8\) lands just under, so it loses. The winners are \(\tfrac{5}{9}\), \(\tfrac{9}{16}\), and \(\tfrac{11}{20}\). Notice how close \(\tfrac{8}{17}\) came, missing by a single half a part, a good reminder that the benchmark catches near misses the eye might wave through. Counting the wins gives \(\boxed{3}\).
Key idea
Landmark comparison. Instead of building a common denominator right away, ask where each fraction sits next to an easy landmark like \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) or a whole \(1\). A fraction beats \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) the moment its top climbs past half its bottom, and it only hits \(1\) when its top finally catches its bottom. So if one fraction parks above the landmark and the other parks below, the bigger one is settled at a glance, with no cross-multiplying and no shared denominator in sight.
Phone 1: \(\tfrac{7}{13}\). Phone 2: \(\tfrac{9}{20}\). Use \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) as landmark, confirm with cross-multiply. By how much is the more-charged phone ahead? Simplest form.
Show solution
Start with the landmark, because it splits the pair without any heavy work. A fraction beats \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) exactly when twice its numerator is more than its denominator. For \(\tfrac{7}{13}\), twice 7 is 14, and 14 is more than 13, so \(\tfrac{7}{13}\) sits just above half. For \(\tfrac{9}{20}\), twice 9 is 18, and 18 is less than 20, so \(\tfrac{9}{20}\) sits just below half. One is above the line and one is below, so \(\tfrac{7}{13}\) is the bigger charge and we did not have to compute anything exact yet. Cross-multiplication confirms it. Compare \(7 \times 20 = 140\) against \(9 \times 13 = 117\). Since \(140 \gt 117\), the fraction holding the 140, namely \(\tfrac{7}{13}\), is indeed larger. Now for the exact gap. The cross-multiplication already handed us both numerators over the shared denominator \(13 \times 20 = 260\). So \(\tfrac{7}{13} = \tfrac{140}{260}\) and \(\tfrac{9}{20} = \tfrac{117}{260}\). Subtract along the top, \(140 - 117 = 23\), which gives a difference of \(\tfrac{23}{260}\). Check whether it reduces. The number 23 is prime, and 260 factors as \(2^2 \times 5 \times 13\), which has no 23 in it, so the fraction is already in lowest terms. The more-charged battery leads by \(\boxed{23/260}\).
Tank 1: \(\tfrac{8}{9}\) (missing \(\tfrac{1}{9}\)). Tank 2: \(\tfrac{11}{12}\) (missing \(\tfrac{1}{12}\)). Smaller gap means fuller. Which is fuller, and by how much? Simplest form.
Show solution
Comparing \(\tfrac{8}{9}\) and \(\tfrac{11}{12}\) head on means wrestling with two different sized pieces. The clever move is to notice that each tank is exactly one piece short of full. The first is missing \(\tfrac{1}{9}\), and the second is missing \(\tfrac{1}{12}\). Both gaps have a numerator of 1, so they are easy to rank. A twelfth of a tank is a thinner sliver than a ninth, so \(\tfrac{1}{12}\lt\tfrac{1}{9}\). The second tank has less empty room, so it is the fuller one, and \(\tfrac{11}{12}\gt\tfrac{8}{9}\). Now for the gap between the fill levels. Here is the neat part. The difference in how full the tanks are is the very same as the difference in how empty they are, because each missing piece is measured from the same full top. So the gap is \(\tfrac{1}{9}-\tfrac{1}{12}\), the difference of the two empty pieces. The smallest common bottom for 9 and 12 is 36, since \(9=3\cdot 3\) and \(12=3\cdot 4\) share a single factor of 3. Rewrite each piece, \(\tfrac{1}{9}=\tfrac{4}{36}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{12}=\tfrac{3}{36}\), then subtract to get \(\tfrac{4}{36}-\tfrac{3}{36}=\tfrac{1}{36}\). Since 1 and 36 share no common factor, it is already in simplest form. The second tank is fuller, by \(\boxed{1/36}\) of a tank.
Key idea
One-piece-short trick. When two fractions each sit just one piece short of a whole, you do not have to wrestle with the fractions at all. Just look at the piece each one is missing. The fraction with the smaller missing piece has less ground left to cover, so it sits closer to \(1\) and wins. With \(\tfrac{8}{9}\) and \(\tfrac{11}{12}\), the gaps are \(\tfrac{1}{9}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{12}\), and since \(\tfrac{1}{12}\) is the smaller gap, \(\tfrac{11}{12}\) is the larger fraction. Compare the gaps, not the fractions, and the smaller gap belongs to the bigger number.
Bars \(\tfrac{13}{14}\) and \(\tfrac{14}{15}\): gaps \(\tfrac{1}{14}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{15}\). Smaller gap is closer to done. Report the gap between the two bars in simplest form.
Show solution
Both bars are racing toward the same finish line, the whole \(1\). So instead of asking how full each bar is, ask how empty it is. The top bar has \(\tfrac{1}{14}\) left to fill, the bottom bar has \(\tfrac{1}{15}\) left. With unit fractions the smaller denominator means the bigger piece, so \(\tfrac{1}{14}\gt\tfrac{1}{15}\). The bottom bar has the smaller piece remaining, which means the bottom bar at \(\tfrac{14}{15}\) is the one closer to finished. Now the clever part. The gap between the two bars is the same as the gap between their leftover pieces, since both leftovers are measured from that same finish line. Sliding both measurements down from \(1\) does not change the distance between them. So the answer is \(\tfrac{1}{14}-\tfrac{1}{15}\). Subtracting unit fractions has a tidy shortcut. \(\tfrac{1}{14}-\tfrac{1}{15}=\tfrac{15-14}{14\times 15}=\tfrac{1}{210}\). The denominator \(210\) is the very product we were trying to dodge, but here it shows up only once, at the end, instead of forcing us to rewrite both bars as fourteenths-of-fifteenths from the start. The numerator is \(1\), so the fraction is already in simplest form. The two bars are exactly \(\boxed{1/210}\) apart, with the \(\tfrac{14}{15}\) bar in the lead.
Fun fact
Archimedes (c. 250 BC) trapped \(\pi\) between \(\tfrac{223}{71}\) and \(\tfrac{22}{7}\) with no decimals, needing to verify \(\tfrac{223}{71}<\tfrac{22}{7}\), the same tools you used all chapter.
Temperatures \(-\tfrac{3}{5}\) and \(-\tfrac{5}{8}\): both left of \(0\). Warmer is nearer \(0\). Which is the larger value, and how far apart are they? Positive fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Negative readings flip the usual instinct. With ordinary positive fractions, the bigger one wins, but down here below \(0\) the value that is larger is the one closer to \(0\), not the one with the bigger size. So the plan is to compare the sizes first, then mirror that result across \(0\). Put both sizes over a common denominator of \(40\). That gives \(\tfrac{3}{5}=\tfrac{24}{40}\) and \(\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{25}{40}\). Since \(\tfrac{25}{40}\gt\tfrac{24}{40}\), the size \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) is the bigger one, which means \(-\tfrac{5}{8}\) dipped deeper below the baseline and sits farther to the left. The shallower dip, \(-\tfrac{3}{5}\), sits nearer to \(0\), so \(-\tfrac{3}{5}\gt -\tfrac{5}{8}\) and the warmer morning, the larger value, is \(-\tfrac{3}{5}\). Now the gap. The distance between two points on the number line is the same whether you measure the readings or their mirror images, so the gap is just the difference of the sizes, \(\tfrac{25}{40}-\tfrac{24}{40}=\tfrac{1}{40}\). The numerator \(1\) and denominator \(40\) share no common factor, so it is already in simplest form. The two mornings were a razor-thin \(\tfrac{1}{40}\) of a degree apart. \(\boxed{1/40}\)
Key idea
Comparing negative fractions. On the right of \(0\) the bigger fraction is the one that reaches farther out, but on the left that whole feeling turns inside out. The negatives are the mirror image of the positives across \(0\), so farther from \(0\) now means deeper below it, and deeper below means smaller. So between two negative fractions the one hugging closer to \(0\) is the larger value, and the more negative one, sitting farther left, is the smaller. The safe habit is to compare the two as if both were positive, using whatever trick you like, then flip the verdict, since the bigger size is the one that drops you farther down.
Three runners are near the finish. Mara is missing \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) of the course and Priya is missing \(\tfrac{1}{12}\), so settle those two by the smaller missing piece. Dev is at \(\tfrac{7}{9}\), so place him with a cross-multiply. Order all three, then report the gap between the leader and the runner in last, in simplest form.
Show solution
Start with the two that the missing-piece view settles instantly. Mara is \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) of the course short of the finish, and Priya is \(\tfrac{1}{12}\) short. The runner with the smaller piece left has gone farther, and \(\tfrac{1}{12}\) is smaller than \(\tfrac{1}{6}\), so Priya is ahead of Mara.

Now place Dev. Compare \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) with Mara's \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) by cross-multiplying. On Dev's side \(7\times 6 = 42\), on Mara's side \(5\times 9 = 45\). Since \(42 \lt 45\), we get \(\tfrac{7}{9} \lt \tfrac{5}{6}\), so Dev is behind Mara. That already puts Dev behind Priya too, since Mara was behind Priya.

So from least finished to most finished the order is Dev \(\left(\tfrac{7}{9}\right)\), then Mara \(\left(\tfrac{5}{6}\right)\), then Priya \(\left(\tfrac{11}{12}\right)\). The leader is Priya and the runner in last is Dev.

The gap between them is \(\tfrac{11}{12} - \tfrac{7}{9}\). A common denominator for \(12\) and \(9\) is \(36\). Rewrite each fraction, \(\tfrac{11}{12} = \tfrac{33}{36}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{9} = \tfrac{28}{36}\). Subtracting gives \(\tfrac{33}{36} - \tfrac{28}{36} = \tfrac{5}{36}\). Since \(5\) and \(36\) share no common factor, it is already in simplest form.

\(\boxed{5/36}\)
\(\tfrac{98}{99}\) misses \(\tfrac{1}{99}\); \(\tfrac{100}{101}\) misses \(\tfrac{1}{101}\). Smaller missing piece is larger rate. Which rate wins, and what is the gap in simplest form?
Show solution
Start with the picture instead of the arithmetic. Both rates are almost a whole, each one a single unit fraction short. The rate \(\tfrac{98}{99}\) falls short by \(\tfrac{1}{99}\), and the rate \(\tfrac{100}{101}\) falls short by \(\tfrac{1}{101}\). Since \(99\) parts are bigger than \(101\) parts of the same whole, \(\tfrac{1}{99}\) is the larger gap. The fraction carrying the larger gap sits further below \(1\), so \(\tfrac{100}{101}\) is the larger rate. No four-digit comparison needed, just a glance at the two missing pieces. Now the gap between the rates. The distance from \(\tfrac{98}{99}\) up to \(1\) is \(\tfrac{1}{99}\) and the distance from \(\tfrac{100}{101}\) up to \(1\) is \(\tfrac{1}{101}\), so the space between the two rates is the difference of those gaps, \(\tfrac{1}{99}-\tfrac{1}{101}\). Put them over the common denominator \(99\times 101 = 9999\). That gives \(\tfrac{101}{9999}-\tfrac{99}{9999} = \tfrac{2}{9999}\). Check for simplifying. Here \(9999 = 9\times 11\times 101\), which has no factor of \(2\), so \(\tfrac{2}{9999}\) is already in lowest terms. The denominator we dreaded computing only had to appear once, at the very end, instead of inside a comparison. So the larger rate is \(\tfrac{100}{101}\), and the two rates differ by \(\boxed{2/9999}\).

Review problems

Review 1. Rosa: \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) of her wall. Ben: \(\tfrac{5}{7}\) of his. Find the common denominator, compare, give the gap as a fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Since \(4\) and \(7\) share no common factor, the smallest shared denominator is \(4 \times 7 = 28\). Rewrite each share over \(28\). For Rosa, \(\tfrac{3}{4} = \tfrac{3 \times 7}{4 \times 7} = \tfrac{21}{28}\). For Ben, \(\tfrac{5}{7} = \tfrac{5 \times 4}{7 \times 4} = \tfrac{20}{28}\). Now the comparison is easy because the pieces are the same size. Rosa has \(21\) of them and Ben has \(20\), so Rosa has painted the larger share. The gap is \(\tfrac{21}{28} - \tfrac{20}{28} = \tfrac{1}{28}\). Since \(1\) and \(28\) share no common factor, this is already in simplest form. The gap between the two shares is \(\boxed{1/28}\).
Review 2. Compare \(\tfrac{8}{11}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{10}\) by cross-multiplying. Which is larger, and how much greater is it? Report the gap as a fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Cross-multiplying sends each numerator up the diagonal across the other fraction's denominator. For \(\tfrac{8}{11}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{10}\) the diagonal products are \(8\times 10=80\) and \(11\times 7=77\). Since \(80\gt 77\), the fraction sitting under the larger product wins, so \(\tfrac{8}{11}\) is the larger fraction. Here is why those numbers settle it. Both diagonal products are really the numerators you would get over the shared denominator \(11\times 10=110\). That gives \(\tfrac{8}{11}=\tfrac{80}{110}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{10}=\tfrac{77}{110}\), now wearing the same denominator. The gap is \(\tfrac{80}{110}-\tfrac{77}{110}=\tfrac{3}{110}\). Since \(\gcd(3,110)=1\), this is already in simplest form. So the larger rate beats the smaller by exactly \(\boxed{3/110}\).
Review 3. Find \(k\) so that \(\tfrac{6}{k}=\tfrac{15}{20}\). Set diagonal products equal: \(6\times 20=k\times 15\). What is \(k\)?
Show solution
For \(\tfrac{6}{k}\) to equal \(\tfrac{15}{20}\), the cross products have to match. The top-left times the bottom-right equals the bottom-left times the top-right, so \(6\times 20 = k\times 15\). That gives \(120 = 15k\). Now find the \(k\) that fits, \(15k = 120\) means \(k = 120\div 15 = 8\). Quick check, \(\tfrac{6}{8}\) reduces to \(\tfrac{3}{4}\), and \(\tfrac{15}{20}\) also reduces to \(\tfrac{3}{4}\), so the two really are the same fraction. \(\boxed{8}\)
Review 4. Compare \(\tfrac{8}{15}\) and \(\tfrac{8}{13}\): same numerator, different denominators. Which is larger? Report how much more that person has as a fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Both fractions have the same top, 8. Think about what the bottom tells you. Cutting a ribbon into 13 strands makes each strand bigger than cutting it into 15 strands, since you are sharing the same ribbon among fewer pieces. Eight bigger strands beat eight smaller strands, so \(\tfrac{8}{13} \gt \tfrac{8}{15}\). The person with the 13-strand ribbon has more. Now measure the gap. A common bottom for both is \(15 \times 13 = 195\). Rewriting, \(\tfrac{8}{13} = \tfrac{8 \times 15}{195} = \tfrac{120}{195}\) and \(\tfrac{8}{15} = \tfrac{8 \times 13}{195} = \tfrac{104}{195}\). The difference is \(\tfrac{120}{195} - \tfrac{104}{195} = \tfrac{16}{195}\). Check that this is in lowest terms. The number 16 is \(2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2\), and 195 is \(3 \times 5 \times 13\), so they share no common factor and \(\gcd(16, 195) = 1\). The gap is \(\boxed{16/195}\).
Review 5. \(\tfrac{6}{35}\) and \(\tfrac{9}{40}\) each rewritten with numerator \(18\): \(\tfrac{18}{105}\) vs \(\tfrac{18}{80}\). Smaller bottom wins. Gap between them in simplest form?
Show solution
First decide which recipe wins. The matching tops are already set up, \(\tfrac{6}{35}=\tfrac{18}{105}\) and \(\tfrac{9}{40}=\tfrac{18}{80}\). Same top, so compare bottoms. Cutting the jar into \(80\) pieces gives larger pieces than cutting it into \(105\) pieces, and we take \(18\) of them either way, so \(\tfrac{18}{80}\gt\tfrac{18}{105}\). The second recipe, \(\tfrac{9}{40}\), uses more. Now measure the gap by subtracting. Use a bottom that both \(40\) and \(35\) divide into, and \(280\) works since \(40\times 7=280\) and \(35\times 8=280\). Rewrite each piece. \(\tfrac{9}{40}=\tfrac{9\times 7}{280}=\tfrac{63}{280}\) and \(\tfrac{6}{35}=\tfrac{6\times 8}{280}=\tfrac{48}{280}\). Subtract the smaller from the larger, \(\tfrac{63}{280}-\tfrac{48}{280}=\tfrac{15}{280}\). Both top and bottom share a factor of \(5\), so \(\tfrac{15}{280}=\tfrac{3}{56}\). The second recipe uses \(\tfrac{3}{56}\) more of the jar. \(\boxed{3/56}\)
Review 6. Scores: \(\tfrac{7}{12}\), \(\tfrac{5}{11}\), \(\tfrac{10}{19}\), \(\tfrac{6}{13}\), \(\tfrac{9}{17}\). Beat \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) when top \(>\) half the bottom. How many exceed \(\tfrac{1}{2}\)?
Show solution
The clean test is to compare twice the top with the bottom. A fraction is more than \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) exactly when twice its top is larger than its bottom, because \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) is the same as having a top equal to half the bottom. Go down the list. For \(\tfrac{7}{12}\), twice \(7\) is \(14\), which is more than \(12\), so it clears the bar. For \(\tfrac{5}{11}\), twice \(5\) is \(10\), which is less than \(11\), so it falls short. For \(\tfrac{10}{19}\), twice \(10\) is \(20\), which is more than \(19\), so it clears. For \(\tfrac{6}{13}\), twice \(6\) is \(12\), which is less than \(13\), so it falls short. For \(\tfrac{9}{17}\), twice \(9\) is \(18\), which is more than \(17\), so it clears. Three of the five passed the test, namely \(\tfrac{7}{12}\), \(\tfrac{10}{19}\), and \(\tfrac{9}{17}\). The count of scores more than \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) is \(\boxed{3}\).
Review 7. Green trail checkpoint: \(\tfrac{8}{15}\). Orange: \(\tfrac{7}{16}\). Use \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) as a landmark to decide which is farther, then find the gap. Give your answer as a fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Start with the landmark \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). A fraction beats \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) exactly when twice its numerator is more than its denominator. For \(\tfrac{8}{15}\), twice the numerator is \(2 \times 8 = 16\), and \(16\) is more than \(15\), so \(\tfrac{8}{15} \gt \tfrac{1}{2}\). For \(\tfrac{7}{16}\), twice the numerator is \(2 \times 7 = 14\), and \(14\) is less than \(16\), so \(\tfrac{7}{16} \lt \tfrac{1}{2}\). The landmark splits them cleanly, which means \(\tfrac{8}{15}\) is the larger fraction without any common denominator yet. So the green trail checkpoint is farther. Now find the gap. A denominator both \(15\) and \(16\) share is \(15 \times 16 = 240\). Rewrite each fraction. \(\tfrac{8}{15} = \tfrac{8 \times 16}{240} = \tfrac{128}{240}\), and \(\tfrac{7}{16} = \tfrac{7 \times 15}{240} = \tfrac{105}{240}\). Subtract to get the gap. \(\tfrac{128}{240} - \tfrac{105}{240} = \tfrac{23}{240}\). Since \(23\) is prime and does not divide \(240\), the fraction is already in simplest form. \(\boxed{23/240}\)
Review 8. Cup 1: \(\tfrac{9}{10}\) full (gap \(\tfrac{1}{10}\)). Cup 2: \(\tfrac{10}{11}\) full (gap \(\tfrac{1}{11}\)). Smaller gap means fuller. Find the gap between fill levels in simplest form.
Show solution
Comparing the fills directly is awkward, so compare the gaps at the top instead. The first cup leaves \(\tfrac{1}{10}\) empty and the second leaves \(\tfrac{1}{11}\) empty. Both gaps have numerator \(1\), and when the numerator is fixed, a larger denominator cuts the whole into more pieces, so each piece is smaller. Since \(11 \gt 10\), the gap \(\tfrac{1}{11}\) is smaller than \(\tfrac{1}{10}\). A smaller empty gap means more liquid, so the second cup at \(\tfrac{10}{11}\) is the fuller one. Now for the distance between the levels. The first level sits \(\tfrac{1}{10}\) below the brim and the second sits \(\tfrac{1}{11}\) below the brim, so the space between them is \(\tfrac{1}{10}-\tfrac{1}{11}\). A common denominator is \(110\), giving \(\tfrac{11}{110}-\tfrac{10}{110}=\tfrac{1}{110}\). The gap denominators differed by one step, and that single step is worth exactly \(\tfrac{1}{110}\) of a cup. So the fuller cup is \(\tfrac{10}{11}\), and the levels are \(\boxed{1/110}\) of a cup apart.
Review 9. Survey 1: missing \(\tfrac{1}{50}\). Survey 2: missing \(\tfrac{1}{51}\). Smaller missing piece is further along. Gap between completion fractions in simplest form?
Show solution
Instead of forcing a giant common denominator, look at what each survey still has left. The survey at \(\tfrac{49}{50}\) is one fiftieth short of done, and the survey at \(\tfrac{50}{51}\) is one fifty-first short of done. A leftover piece of \(\tfrac{1}{51}\) is smaller than a leftover piece of \(\tfrac{1}{50}\), since cutting a whole into more parts makes each part tinier. Less left to do means more done, so the survey at \(\tfrac{50}{51}\) is the one closer to finished. Now the gap. Both surveys are being compared to the same finished survey, so the distance between their completion fractions is exactly the distance between their missing pieces. That gives \(\tfrac{1}{50}-\tfrac{1}{51}\). A common denominator of \(50\) and \(51\) is \(50\times 51=2{,}550\), so \(\tfrac{1}{50}=\tfrac{51}{2550}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{51}=\tfrac{50}{2550}\). Subtracting gives \(\tfrac{51}{2550}-\tfrac{50}{2550}=\tfrac{1}{2550}\). Since \(2{,}550\) has factors of \(2\), \(3\), \(5\), and \(17\) while the numerator is \(1\), the fraction is already in simplest form. The two surveys are a hair apart, and the one at \(\tfrac{50}{51}\) leads by \(\boxed{1/2550}\).
Review 10. Temperatures \(-\tfrac{5}{9}\) and \(-\tfrac{4}{7}\). Warmer is closer to \(0\). Which reading is warmer? Report how far apart the readings are as a positive fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
First compare the two sizes, ignoring the minus signs. The denominators are \(9\) and \(7\), and \(9 \times 7 = 63\), so rewrite both over \(63\). We get \(\tfrac{5}{9} = \tfrac{35}{63}\) and \(\tfrac{4}{7} = \tfrac{36}{63}\). Since \(36 \gt 35\), the size \(\tfrac{4}{7}\) is larger. Now bring the signs back. A larger size pushes a negative number further from \(0\), so \(-\tfrac{4}{7}\) sits lower than \(-\tfrac{5}{9}\). That makes \(-\tfrac{5}{9}\) the warmer reading, the one nearer to \(0\). To find how far apart the two readings are, subtract the smaller size from the larger size, since the gap between \(-\tfrac{35}{63}\) and \(-\tfrac{36}{63}\) is one \(\tfrac{1}{63}\) step. So the distance is \(\tfrac{36}{63} - \tfrac{35}{63} = \tfrac{1}{63}\), which is already in simplest form. The two readings are \(\boxed{1/63}\) apart.
Review 11. Four sprinters: \(\tfrac{7}{12}\), \(\tfrac{5}{8}\), \(\tfrac{11}{18}\), \(\tfrac{2}{3}\). Use the fastest comparison tool for each pair. Write the single largest fraction in its original form.
Show solution
Pick a common denominator that all four fit into. The denominators are \(12\), \(8\), \(18\), and \(3\), and the smallest number they all divide is \(72\). Rewrite each runner's distance over \(72\). For Aanya, \(\tfrac{7}{12}=\tfrac{42}{72}\). For Beck, \(\tfrac{5}{8}=\tfrac{45}{72}\). For Cory, \(\tfrac{11}{18}=\tfrac{44}{72}\). For Dev, \(\tfrac{2}{3}=\tfrac{48}{72}\). Now the numerators tell the whole story, since the pieces are the same size. Lined up from least to most they go \(\tfrac{42}{72}\), \(\tfrac{44}{72}\), \(\tfrac{45}{72}\), \(\tfrac{48}{72}\), which is Aanya, then Cory, then Beck, then Dev. The leader is \(\tfrac{48}{72}\), and that came from \(\tfrac{2}{3}\). So the largest of the four, in its original form, is \(\boxed{2/3}\).
Review 12. Sand: gains \(\tfrac{3}{4}\), loses \(\tfrac{1}{3}\). Find what remains, compare to \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). By how much does it differ from \(\tfrac{1}{2}\)? Simplest form.
Show solution
Start with the subtraction. The denominators \(4\) and \(3\) both fit into \(12\), so rewrite each fraction over \(12\). We get \(\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{9}{12}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{4}{12}\). Taking the leak away leaves \(\tfrac{9}{12}-\tfrac{4}{12}=\tfrac{5}{12}\) of a scoop in the jar. Now compare that with \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). Written over the same denominator, \(\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{6}{12}\). Since \(\tfrac{5}{12}\lt\tfrac{6}{12}\), the sand that remains is a little less than half a scoop. The gap is \(\tfrac{6}{12}-\tfrac{5}{12}=\tfrac{1}{12}\), and \(\tfrac{1}{12}\) is already in simplest form because \(\gcd(1,12)=1\). So the remaining sand falls short of the halfway mark by \(\boxed{1/12}\) of a scoop.
Review 13. Maya: \(\tfrac{13}{16}\) of her goal. Theo: \(\tfrac{17}{20}\). Both past halfway, so use cross-multiplication. By what fraction, in simplest form, is the leader ahead?
Show solution
The two denominators are 16 and 20. A denominator they share is \(80\), since \(16 \times 5 = 80\) and \(20 \times 4 = 80\). Rewrite each fraction in eightieths. For Maya, \(\tfrac{13}{16} = \tfrac{13 \times 5}{80} = \tfrac{65}{80}\). For Theo, \(\tfrac{17}{20} = \tfrac{17 \times 4}{80} = \tfrac{68}{80}\). Now the comparison is plain. Out of \(80\) equal parts Theo has \(68\) and Maya has \(65\), so Theo is further toward his goal and he is the leader. The gap between them is the difference of the two amounts, \(\tfrac{68}{80} - \tfrac{65}{80} = \tfrac{3}{80}\). Check whether \(\tfrac{3}{80}\) can be reduced. The top is \(3\), and \(80 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 5\) has no factor of \(3\), so \(\gcd(3,80) = 1\) and the fraction is already in simplest form. The leader is ahead by \(\boxed{3/80}\).
Review 14. Mosaic 1: \(\tfrac{123}{124}\) done (missing \(\tfrac{1}{124}\)). Mosaic 2: \(\tfrac{199}{200}\) (missing \(\tfrac{1}{200}\)). Smaller missing piece wins. Report the gap in simplest form.
Show solution
Chasing the filled amounts \(\tfrac{123}{124}\) and \(\tfrac{199}{200}\) head on means a denominator of \(124\times 200\), which is huge and easy to fumble. There is a cleaner way in. Each mosaic is one tile from done, so what is left undone is \(\tfrac{1}{124}\) for the first and \(\tfrac{1}{200}\) for the second. With the same numerator of \(1\), the fraction with the larger denominator is the smaller hole. Since \(200\gt 124\), the leftover \(\tfrac{1}{200}\) is smaller than \(\tfrac{1}{124}\), so the second mosaic has less missing and is the more complete one. That means \(\tfrac{199}{200}\gt\tfrac{123}{124}\). Now for the gap. The distance between the two completions equals the distance between the two leftover holes, because filling more of a mosaic just shrinks its hole by the same amount. So the gap is \(\tfrac{1}{124}-\tfrac{1}{200}\). The least common denominator of \(124\) and \(200\) is \(6200\), since \(124=4\times 31\) and \(200=8\times 25\) share only the factor \(4\), giving \(\gcd=4\) and \(\text{lcm}=\tfrac{124\times 200}{4}=6200\). Rewrite each piece, \(\tfrac{1}{124}=\tfrac{50}{6200}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{200}=\tfrac{31}{6200}\). Subtract to get \(\tfrac{50}{6200}-\tfrac{31}{6200}=\tfrac{19}{6200}\). Since \(19\) is prime and does not divide \(6200\), the fraction is already in lowest terms. The second mosaic leads, and the gap between the two is \(\boxed{19/6200}\).
