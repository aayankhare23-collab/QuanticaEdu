Prealgebra · Lesson 3.6

Counting a Number's Divisors

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Lesson 3.1 found that perfect squares alone have an odd divisor count. Lesson 3.3 gave every number a unique prime fingerprint. Now: can the fingerprint alone count divisors exactly, without listing a single one?

A machine builds divisors of \(12 = 2^2 \times 3\) by choosing how many 2s (0, 1, or 2) and how many 3s (0 or 1) to multiply together. How many different divisors can it build?
Show solution
Every divisor of \(12\) is built by deciding how many \(2\)s to include and how many \(3\)s, and nothing else, since \(12 = 2^2 \times 3\) is made of only those primes. The number of \(2\)s can be \(0\), \(1\), or \(2\), that is three choices, and the number of \(3\)s can be \(0\) or \(1\), that is two choices. The two decisions are completely independent, so the divisors number $$3 \times 2 = \boxed{6}.$$ Listing them as \(2^i \times 3^j\) confirms it, $$2^0 3^0 = 1,\ \ 2^1 3^0 = 2,\ \ 2^2 3^0 = 4,\ \ 2^0 3^1 = 3,\ \ 2^1 3^1 = 6,\ \ 2^2 3^1 = 12,$$ exactly the six divisors of \(12\) and no others. Quick check, the count of \(2\)s ran over \(0,1,2\), which is \(2 + 1 = 3\) values, one more than the exponent, and the count of \(3\)s ran over \(0,1\), which is \(1 + 1 = 2\) values. The general idea is that a divisor is just an independent choice of how many of each prime to take, so the number of divisors is a product of choice counts.
Key idea
Add-one-and-multiply rule. If \(n = p^a \times q^b \times \cdots\), the number of positive divisors is \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\). A divisor is built by choosing how many copies of each prime to take, anywhere from \(0\) up to that prime's power, so prime \(p\) offers \(a+1\) choices, prime \(q\) offers \(b+1\), and so on. Since the choices are independent they multiply, and that single product counts every divisor at once because each divisor matches exactly one way of filling in the choices.
The fingerprint of 360 is \(360 = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5\). Use the add-one-and-multiply rule to count how many positive divisors 360 has without listing them.
Show solution
The rule extends to any number of primes without change. For \(360 = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5^1\), each prime offers one more choice than its exponent. The \(2\)s give \(3 + 1 = 4\) choices, the \(3\)s give \(2 + 1 = 3\), and the single \(5\) gives \(1 + 1 = 2\). Multiplying the independent choices, $$(3+1)(2+1)(1+1) = 4 \times 3 \times 2 = \boxed{24}.$$ So \(360\) has \(24\) positive divisors, and we found that without writing even one of them down. Quick check, the smallest few divisors \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10\) already pile up fast, and the full list does run to exactly \(24\) entries. The general idea is that the add-one-and-multiply rule scales to as many primes as a number carries, turning a long listing chore into one quick multiplication.
Compare \(96 = 2^5 \times 3\) and \(100 = 2^2 \times 5^2\). Apply the counting rule to each. Which number has more divisors?
Show solution
Run the rule on each fingerprint. For \(96 = 2^5 \times 3\), the divisor count is $$(5+1)(1+1) = 6 \times 2 = 12.$$ For \(100 = 2^2 \times 5^2\), it is $$(2+1)(2+1) = 3 \times 3 = 9.$$ So \(96\) has \(12\) divisors while the larger \(100\) has only \(9\), and the winner is \(\boxed{96}\). The reason is in the shape of the fingerprints. A tall stack of one prime, like the five \(2\)s in \(96\), spreads into many divisors, while \(100\) spends its size on two medium stacks that multiply to a smaller count. Quick check, \(100\) really does have just nine divisors, \(1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20, 25, 50, 100\), three short of \(96\)'s dozen. The general idea is that the number of divisors depends on the shape of the prime fingerprint, the exponents, not on how big the number is.
Find the smallest whole number with exactly 12 divisors by breaking 12 into exponent-plus-one pieces, building the smallest number from each, and comparing.
Show solution
Since the divisor count is \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\), a number with exactly \(12\) divisors needs exponents whose plus-one values multiply to \(12\). Each way of writing \(12\) as such a product gives a recipe, and to make the number itself small we hand the largest exponents to the smallest primes. $$12 = 12 \ \Rightarrow\ 2^{11} = 2048$$ $$12 = 6 \times 2 \ \Rightarrow\ 2^{5} \times 3 = 96$$ $$12 = 4 \times 3 \ \Rightarrow\ 2^{3} \times 3^{2} = 72$$ $$12 = 3 \times 2 \times 2 \ \Rightarrow\ 2^{2} \times 3 \times 5 = 60$$ The smallest result is \(\boxed{60}\). Quick check, \(60 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5\) gives \((2+1)(1+1)(1+1) = 3 \times 2 \times 2 = 12\) divisors, and no arrangement of exponents summing to that product lands lower, since spreading the choices across the primes \(2, 3, 5\) is cheaper than stacking them high on \(2\) alone. The general idea is that to hit a target divisor count with the smallest number, you factor the target into exponent-plus-one pieces and load the biggest exponents onto the smallest primes.
The divisor count \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\) is odd only when every exponent is even, the fingerprint of a perfect square. How many numbers from 1 to 50 have an odd number of divisors?
Show solution
The divisor count is the product \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\), and a product of whole numbers comes out odd only when every factor in it is odd. Each factor \(a+1\) is odd exactly when the exponent \(a\) is even. So a number has an odd divisor count precisely when every exponent in its fingerprint is even, and that is exactly the fingerprint of a perfect square, the very fact lesson 3.1 discovered through self-pairing. So the question becomes how many perfect squares lie from \(1\) to \(50\). They are $$1,\ 4,\ 9,\ 16,\ 25,\ 36,\ 49,$$ the squares of \(1\) through \(7\), since \(8^2 = 64\) overshoots. That is \(\boxed{7}\) numbers. Quick check, \(36 = 2^2 \times 3^2\) has \((2+1)(2+1) = 9\) divisors, an odd count, just as promised. The general idea is that the counting rule re-proves the old result, since an odd divisor count forces every exponent even, which is the fingerprint of a perfect square.
Key idea
A number has an odd number of divisors exactly when it is a perfect square. From 3.1: only a perfect square has a self-paired factor. From the counting rule: the product \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\) is odd only when every exponent is even. Two stories, one conclusion.
\(720=2^4\times3^2\times5\). An even divisor needs at least one 2, so restrict the 2-choices to \{1,2,3,4\} and keep the full range for 3 and 5. How many even divisors does 720 have?
Show solution
An even divisor of \(720 = 2^4 \times 3^2 \times 5\) must carry at least one factor of \(2\). In the counting rule, the \(2\)s normally offer five choices, taking \(0, 1, 2, 3,\) or \(4\) of them, but ruling out the choice of zero leaves only the four choices \(1, 2, 3, 4\). The \(3\)s still offer \(2 + 1 = 3\) choices and the \(5\) still offers \(1 + 1 = 2\), so the even divisors number $$4 \times 3 \times 2 = \boxed{24}.$$ A nice way to confirm it is to count from the other side. All of \(720\) has \((4+1)(2+1)(1+1) = 5 \times 3 \times 2 = 30\) divisors, and the odd ones use no \(2\)s at all, so they are the divisors of \(3^2 \times 5\), numbering \((2+1)(1+1) = 6\). Subtracting, \(30 - 6 = 24\), matching exactly. Quick check, both routes land on \(24\). The general idea is that you can steer the counting rule by restricting one prime's choices, which lets you count special families of divisors like the even ones or the multiples of a given factor.
Fun fact
Highly composite numbers beat every smaller number in divisor count: 1, 2, 4, 6, 12, 24, 36, 60, 120, 360. Civilizations pick these for time and angles because many divisors make sharing easy.

The chapter followed one thread: look inside a number, find its primes, read the fingerprint, and everything follows, divisibility, perfect powers, GCD, LCM, and the exact divisor count. Chapter 4 turns to fractions.

Review problems

Review 1. A number is given as \(2^4 \times 5^2\). Use the add-one-and-multiply rule to count its positive divisors.
Show solution
Each prime offers one more choice than its exponent, so for \(2^4 \times 5^2\) the count is $$(4+1)(2+1) = 5 \times 3 = \boxed{15}.$$ Quick check, the divisors are \(2^i \times 5^j\) with \(i\) from \(0\) to \(4\) and \(j\) from \(0\) to \(2\), which is \(5\) by \(3\) combinations, exactly \(15\). The general idea is that the divisor count is the product of each exponent plus one.
Review 2. Factor \(600 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5^2\) and apply the counting rule. How many positive divisors does 600 have?
Show solution
Reading the fingerprint \(600 = 2^3 \times 3^1 \times 5^2\), the counting rule gives $$(3+1)(1+1)(2+1) = 4 \times 2 \times 3 = \boxed{24}.$$ Quick check, \(600\) shares the count \(24\) with \(360\) from the lesson, since both spread their primes into the same shape of choices. The general idea is that once a number is in fingerprint form, its divisor count is a single multiplication away.
Review 3. Which has more divisors, \(64 = 2^6\) or \(90 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 5\)? Count each and enter the number with more.
Show solution
A single tall stack counts poorly. For \(64 = 2^6\), the divisor count is \((6+1) = 7\). For \(90 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 5\), it is \((1+1)(2+1)(1+1) = 2 \times 3 \times 2 = 12\). So the smaller-looking \(90\) wins with \(12\) divisors against \(64\)'s \(7\), and the answer is \(\boxed{90}\). Quick check, \(64\) really has only the seven divisors \(1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64\). The general idea is that spreading primes out makes far more divisors than stacking one prime high, so divisor count tracks fingerprint shape, not size.
Review 4. How many whole numbers from 1 to 100 have an odd number of divisors?
Show solution
A divisor count \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\) is odd only when every exponent is even, which is the fingerprint of a perfect square. So the numbers with an odd divisor count from \(1\) to \(100\) are exactly the perfect squares there, $$1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100,$$ the squares of \(1\) through \(10\). That is \(\boxed{10}\) numbers. Quick check, \(100 = 2^2 \times 5^2\) has \((2+1)(2+1) = 9\) divisors, odd as expected. The general idea is that odd divisor counts belong to perfect squares and nothing else, so counting them is just counting squares.
Review 5. Find the smallest whole number with exactly 8 divisors.
Show solution
A number with exactly \(8\) divisors needs exponents whose plus-one values multiply to \(8\). The recipes, each loading the biggest exponents onto the smallest primes, are $$8 = 8 \ \Rightarrow\ 2^7 = 128, \qquad 8 = 4 \times 2 \ \Rightarrow\ 2^3 \times 3 = 24, \qquad 8 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \ \Rightarrow\ 2 \times 3 \times 5 = 30.$$ The smallest is \(\boxed{24}\). Quick check, \(24 = 2^3 \times 3\) gives \((3+1)(1+1) = 8\) divisors, namely \(1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24\). The general idea is that to reach a target divisor count with the least number, you factor the target and give the largest exponents to the smallest primes.
Review 6. How many positive divisors does \(2^{10}\) have?
Show solution
A power of a single prime \(2^{10}\) has divisors \(2^0, 2^1, \ldots, 2^{10}\), one for each exponent from \(0\) to \(10\). The counting rule says the same, \((10+1) = \boxed{11}\). Quick check, the list \(1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1024\) holds exactly eleven numbers. The general idea is that a prime raised to the power \(a\) has \(a+1\) divisors, the simplest case of the rule.
Review 7. A number has exactly three divisors only when it is the square of a prime. How many numbers from 1 to 50 have exactly three divisors?
Show solution
The divisor count \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\) equals \(3\) only when the product is the single factor \(3\), since \(3\) is prime and cannot be split into smaller whole-number factors above \(1\). That forces exactly one prime with exponent \(2\) and nothing else, so the number must be the square of a prime, \(p^2\), whose divisors are just \(1\), \(p\), and \(p^2\). The prime squares up to \(50\) are $$2^2 = 4,\quad 3^2 = 9,\quad 5^2 = 25,\quad 7^2 = 49,$$ since \(11^2 = 121\) overshoots. That is \(\boxed{4}\) numbers. Quick check, \(25\) has exactly the three divisors \(1, 5, 25\). The general idea is that a number has exactly three divisors precisely when it is the square of a prime, a clean fingerprint forced by the counting rule.
Review 8. How many of 360's divisors are even, given \(360 = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5\)?
Show solution
An even divisor of \(360 = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5\) must include at least one factor of \(2\). The \(2\)s normally give four choices, \(0, 1, 2, 3\), but ruling out zero leaves three, \(1, 2, 3\). The \(3\)s give \(2 + 1 = 3\) choices and the \(5\) gives \(1 + 1 = 2\), so the even divisors number $$3 \times 3 \times 2 = \boxed{18}.$$ Checking from the other side, all of \(360\) has \(4 \times 3 \times 2 = 24\) divisors, and the odd ones are the divisors of \(3^2 \times 5\), numbering \(3 \times 2 = 6\), so even ones are \(24 - 6 = 18\), a match. Quick check, both routes give \(18\). The general idea is that requiring a prime to appear shrinks that prime's choice count by one, which counts a chosen family of divisors directly.
Review 9. Divisor counting rule: for \(n=p^a q^b\cdots\), count is \((a+1)(b+1)\cdots\). Depends on exponent shape, not size. Odd count ↔ perfect square. Trim one prime's choices for a sub-family. Run backward to design a number with a target count.
Review 10. Locker \(n\) is flipped once per divisor, so it ends open only when it has an odd divisor count, exactly the perfect squares. Open lockers: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100.
