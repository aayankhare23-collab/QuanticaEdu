Prealgebra · Lesson 5.5

Repeating Decimals

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

\(\tfrac{1}{3}\) can never settle into a stopping decimal because \(3\) is neither \(2\) nor \(5\). So what do its digits do? Are they random, or stuck in a loop? The answer hides inside the remainders of long division.

Long-divide \(1 \div 3\). At each step the remainder decides the next digit. What number is the remainder at the end of every step?
Show solution
Watch the remainder, not the digit, and the mystery dissolves. We are computing \(1 \div 3\). Since \(3\) does not divide \(1\), we place the decimal point and bring down a zero, which makes the running number \(10\). The largest multiple of \(3\) that fits in \(10\) is \(9\), so the quotient digit is \(3\) and the remainder is \(10 - 9 = 1\). Now bring down the next zero. The remainder \(1\) becomes \(10\) once more, the digit is \(3\) again, and \(10 - 9 = 1\) again. Here is the key observation. The new remainder, \(1\), is exactly the number we started this whole process with. So step three must be an identical copy of step two, step four a copy of step three, and so on with no end. The remainder is locked at \(1\), which locks the quotient digit at \(3\), forever. That is precisely why \(\tfrac{1}{3} = 0.\overline{3}\), the bar marking the \(3\) that can never escape. The single number that returns at the end of every step is \(\boxed{1}\).
Key idea
Why decimals repeat. A decimal stops when long division finally hands back a remainder of \(0\) and quits. Dividing \(1\) by \(3\) never gets to, because every step ends on the same leftover of \(1\). An identical leftover makes the next step identical, and an identical step prints the same digit again, so when the remainder repeats, the digit has no choice but to repeat too. One third runs forever not because its digits are unlucky but because its remainder is stuck in a loop and can never reach \(0\). That is the gatekeeper rule from 5.3 seen from the inside.
Long-dividing \(1 \div 7\) cycles through remainders \(1, 3, 2, 6, 4, 5\) before \(1\) returns, giving \(\tfrac{1}{7} = 0.\overline{142857}\). How many digits are in the repeating block?
Show solution
Each step of long division produces a remainder, and that remainder is what decides the next digit. So if a remainder ever repeats, the step after it must produce the same digit and the same next remainder as before, and the pattern is locked into a loop from that point on. The length of the repeating block is just how many steps happen between a remainder and its first return. Track the remainders for \(\tfrac{1}{7}\). Start with \(1\). Bring down a zero to get \(10\), and \(10 \div 7\) gives digit \(1\) with remainder \(3\). Then \(30 \div 7\) gives \(4\) remainder \(2\). Then \(20 \div 7\) gives \(2\) remainder \(6\). Then \(60 \div 7\) gives \(8\) remainder \(4\). Then \(40 \div 7\) gives \(5\) remainder \(5\). Then \(50 \div 7\) gives \(7\) remainder \(1\). The remainder \(1\) is back, the one we started with, so the loop closes here. The remainders we passed through were \(1, 3, 2, 6, 4, 5\), which is six of them, and the digits they produced were \(1, 4, 2, 8, 5, 7\). That gives \(\tfrac{1}{7} = 0.\overline{142857}\), a block of six digits. Notice the block could not have been longer than six, since only the six remainders \(1\) through \(6\) are available before a \(0\) would end it, and here all six get used. The repeating block has \(\boxed{6}\) digits.
Key idea
Repeating is unavoidable. When you divide by \(b\), every remainder is smaller than \(b\), so there are at most \(b-1\) non-zero values that can ever turn up. If the division never lands on \(0\), it keeps stepping forever with only that small handful to choose from, so sooner or later a remainder you already saw comes back, and the instant it does, every step after copies a step before and the digits lock into a loop. A decimal that refuses to stop is never wandering at random, it is always settling into a block that repeats, so every non-terminating decimal from a fraction is periodic. Truly random endless decimals exist, but no fraction ever spells one.
The long division \(1 \div 11\) gives \(\tfrac{1}{11} = 0.\overline{09}\). The bar covers both the \(0\) and the \(9\). How many digits are in the repeating block? Give the period.
Show solution
The period is the length of the repeating block, so the real task is to find that block and then count its digits. The cleanest way is to track remainders during long division, because the moment a remainder repeats, the digits must repeat too. Start with \(1 \div 11\). Since \(1\) is smaller than \(11\), we write \(0.\) and carry a remainder of \(1\). Bring down a zero to make \(10\). Now \(10 \div 11 = 0\) with remainder \(10\), so the first digit after the point is \(0\). Bring down another zero to make \(100\). Now \(100 \div 11 = 9\) with remainder \(1\), so the next digit is \(9\). That remainder \(1\) is exactly the remainder we began with, so the process repeats forever from here. The digits it keeps spitting out are \(0, 9, 0, 9, \dots\), which gives $$\frac{1}{11} = 0.\overline{09}.$$ The bar covers \(09\), and that leading zero is essential. Writing \(0.\overline{9}\) would mean \(0.999\dots\), a completely different number. So the repeating block is \(09\), and counting its digits gives \(0\) and \(9\), which is \(\boxed{2}\) digits. The period of \(\tfrac{1}{11}\) is \(\boxed{2}\).
In \(\tfrac{1}{6} = 0.1\overline{6}\), only the \(6\) repeats. How many digits come before the repeating block begins, meaning how many are not under the bar?
Show solution
Long division tells the whole story through its remainders. Dividing \(1\) by \(6\), bring down a zero to get \(10\). Then \(10\div 6 = 1\) remainder \(4\), so the first digit after the point is \(1\). Bring down another zero to get \(40\). Then \(40\div 6 = 6\) remainder \(4\). The remainder is \(4\) again, the same one we already saw, so the process is now stuck in a loop and the digit \(6\) repeats endlessly. The decimal is $$\tfrac{1}{6} = 0.1\overline{6} = 0.16666\ldots$$ The bar sits over the \(6\) only, because the loop does not begin until after that first digit. Why one settling digit and not zero? Look at the denominator. \(6 = 2\times 3\). From 5.3, factors of \(2\) and \(5\) are the part that wants to terminate, and they are what create non-repeating head digits. The single factor of \(2\) here produces exactly one such digit, the \(1\), before the factor of \(3\) takes over and forces the endless \(6\). One \(2\) buys one settling digit. So the digits not under the bar number exactly one. $$\boxed{1}$$
Key idea
Reading the decimal from the denominator. Factor the denominator of a fraction in lowest terms and you can read its decimal before you ever divide. The 2s and 5s are the stopping primes, and whichever of those two counts is larger sets the length of a short non-repeating head. Every other prime is a looping prime, building the repeating tail. So a denominator with only \(2\)s and \(5\)s gives a clean stop, one with none of them repeats from the very first digit, and one with both gives a head followed by a loop.
Long division gives \(\tfrac{1}{12} = 0.08\overline{3}\). What is the repeating block? Type the digit or digits under the bar.
Show solution
Long division is really a hunt for a repeated remainder, so let us track the remainders carefully. Dividing \(1\) by \(12\), the first step gives \(10 \div 12 = 0\) remainder \(10\), digit \(0\). The second step gives \(100 \div 12 = 8\) remainder \(4\), digit \(8\). The third step gives \(40 \div 12 = 3\) remainder \(4\). That remainder \(4\) is the very same one we just had, so the division has entered a cycle. From here on we will always be computing \(40 \div 12\) again, writing another \(3\), and handing back remainder \(4\). The \(3\) loops, while the \(0\) and the \(8\) at the front each appeared only once and stay put. So \(\tfrac{1}{12} = 0.08\overline{3}\), where the bar sits over the lone digit \(3\). Here is the satisfying part. The head \(08\) is two digits long, and that is no accident. Since \(12 = 2^2 \times 3\), the two factors of \(2\) force exactly two non-repeating digits at the start, and the leftover factor \(3\) is what generates the looping tail. The block under the bar is a single digit. \(\boxed{3}\)

Every fraction produces an endless repeating decimal, but the reverse trip is also possible. The shift-and-subtract trick lines up two copies of the same tail so they cancel, collapsing an infinite decimal into a finite fraction.

Let \(x = 0.\overline{7}\). Multiply by \(10\) to get \(10x = 7.\overline{7}\). The tails of \(7\)s cancel when you subtract: \(9x = 7\). Write \(0.\overline{7}\) as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by naming the number. Let \(x = 0.\overline{7}\), so \(x = 0.7777\ldots\) with the \(7\) repeating forever. The reason you cannot just read off a fraction is that the tail never ends, so the trick is to manufacture a second copy of that same tail and line it up. Multiply both sides by \(10\). One factor of \(10\) shifts the decimal point exactly one place, which is just enough to move past the first \(7\) and expose an identical tail underneath. $$10x = 7.7777\ldots$$ Now write the two facts together and subtract the bottom from the top. $$\begin{array}{r} 10x = 7.7777\ldots \\ -\;\;\;x = 0.7777\ldots \\ \hline 9x = 7.0000\ldots \end{array}$$ Watch what happened on the right. Past the decimal point, \(10x\) had \(7777\ldots\) and \(x\) had \(7777\ldots\), the very same endless string, so digit by digit they cancel to nothing. The infinite part vanishes, and all that survives is \(7 - 0 = 7\). On the left, \(10x - x = 9x\). So the endless decimal has collapsed into the clean statement \(9x = 7\). Solve it. Divide both sides by \(9\) to get \(x = \tfrac{7}{9}\). Since \(7\) is prime and does not divide \(9\), the fraction is already in lowest terms. And it makes sense as a finale, every infinitely repeating decimal you will ever meet is secretly a fraction in disguise, and the shift-and-subtract move is the key that unlocks it. $$\boxed{7/9}$$
Let \(x = 0.\overline{12}\). Multiply by \(100\) to get \(100x = 12.\overline{12}\). Subtracting gives \(99x = 12\), so \(x = \tfrac{12}{99}\). Write \(0.\overline{12}\) as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by naming the number. Let \(x = 0.\overline{12} = 0.121212\ldots\) The block has two digits, so shift the decimal point two places by multiplying by \(100\). $$100x = 12.121212\ldots$$ Now line the two equations up and subtract, so the matching infinite tails cancel. $$\begin{array}{r} 100x = 12.121212\ldots \\ -\ \ \ x = \ 0.121212\ldots \\ \hline 99x = 12 \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \end{array}$$ Everything after the decimal point is gone, which is the whole point of shifting by the full length of the block. Solve for \(x\). $$x = \frac{12}{99}$$ This is the block over two nines, exactly the pattern. But \(\tfrac{12}{99}\) is not in lowest terms yet. Both numbers are divisible by \(3\), since \(1+2 = 3\) and \(9+9 = 18\) are each multiples of \(3\). $$\frac{12}{99} = \frac{12 \div 3}{99 \div 3} = \frac{4}{33}$$ Now \(4 = 2 \times 2\) and \(33 = 3 \times 11\) share no common factor, so this is fully reduced. As a sanity check, dividing \(4\) by \(33\) gives \(0.121212\ldots\), right back where we started. \(0.\overline{12} = \boxed{4/33}\)
Key idea
Block-over-nines shortcut. A pure repeating decimal becomes the repeating block sitting over a string of \(9\)s, one \(9\) for each digit in the block, and then you reduce. That is why \(0.\overline{7}=\tfrac{7}{9}\), \(0.\overline{12}=\tfrac{12}{99}\), and \(0.\overline{481}=\tfrac{481}{999}\). It works because shifting by exactly one block length lines the endless tails up so they cancel, and dividing by that power of ten minus \(1\) is the same as dividing by \(9\), \(99\), or \(999\). The scary infinite tail turns out to be the easy part, since it cancels with a copy of itself.
Write \(0.41\overline{6}\) as a fraction in lowest terms. Multiply by \(1{,}000\) and by \(100\), subtract to cancel the \(\overline{6}\) tails, then solve and reduce.
Show solution
Let \(x = 0.41\overline{6} = 0.41666\ldots\). The trouble is the \(41\) up front. It does not repeat, so if we shift to line up tails carelessly we will trap that head inside the subtraction. The fix is to shift past it first, then shift one more block. Multiply by \(100\) to slide the point right up to the edge of the loop. $$100x = 41.\overline{6} = 41.666\ldots$$ Now everything to the left of the point is settled, and the leftover tail is exactly \(0.666\ldots\). Multiply the original by \(1{,}000\), which is one extra block further. $$1{,}000x = 416.\overline{6} = 416.666\ldots$$ This tail is also exactly \(0.666\ldots\), the same as before, because the \(6\) loop never changes no matter how far you walk into it. The two numbers share an identical \(.666\ldots\) tail, so subtracting cancels it cleanly. $$1{,}000x - 100x = 416.666\ldots - 41.666\ldots$$ $$900x = 375$$ Solve for \(x\) and reduce. Both \(375\) and \(900\) share a factor of \(75\), since \(375 = 75 \cdot 5\) and \(900 = 75 \cdot 12\). $$x = \frac{375}{900} = \frac{5}{12}$$ A quick sanity check, \(5 \div 12 = 0.41666\ldots\), which is \(0.41\overline{6}\) exactly. The head shifted out, the tails cancelled, and the whole chapter folds shut. \(\boxed{5/12}\)
Let \(x = 0.\overline{9}\). Multiply by \(10\): \(10x = 9.\overline{9}\). Subtract: \(9x = 9\). Divide by \(9\). What does \(0.\overline{9}\) equal?
Show solution
Set \(x = 0.\overline{9}\), so \(x = 0.9999\dots\) with the nines never stopping. Multiply by 10, which slides the decimal point one place to the right. $$10x = 9.\overline{9} = 9.9999\dots$$ Here is the key observation. Both \(10x\) and \(x\) end in the very same endless string of nines after the decimal point, \(.9999\dots\). They are not nearly the same, they are exactly the same tail. So when you subtract, that whole infinite tail cancels cleanly. $$10x - x = 9.9999\dots - 0.9999\dots = 9$$ On the left, \(10x - x = 9x\), which gives $$9x = 9.$$ Divide both sides by 9. $$x = \frac{9}{9} = 1.$$ Look back at what just happened. Every step was ordinary algebra, the same algebra that sent \(0.\overline{7}\) to \(\tfrac{7}{9}\). Nowhere did you round, drop a piece, or settle for "close enough". The answer is not a number creeping up toward 1 and not a sliver below it. It is 1, on the nose. So \(0.\overline{9}\) and 1 are simply two different names for the same number, and that is a fitting place to close the chapter. $$\boxed{1}$$
Key idea
\(0.\overline{9} = 1\) exactly. Run the same procedure that turned \(0.\overline{7}\) into \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) and \(0.\overline{3}\) into \(\tfrac{1}{3}\), and when the repeating block is \(9\) it simply hands back \(\tfrac{9}{9}=1\). The endless string of nines is not creeping toward \(1\) and never quite landing, it has already landed, because an endless decimal is one fixed number, not a journey toward something. The thirds say the same thing: \(\tfrac{1}{3}=0.\overline{3}\), so three of them make \(0.\overline{9}\), yet three thirds also make \(1\). So \(0.\overline{9}\) and \(1\) are not close, they are the very same number wearing two names.
\(\tfrac{1}{7} = 0.\overline{142857}\), a block of \(6\) digits. Divide \(50 \div 6\) to find the position within the block. What is the 50th digit after the decimal point?
Show solution
The decimal is \(\tfrac{1}{7} = 0.\overline{142857}\), so the same block of six digits, \(142857\), repeats forever. Label the spots inside one block by position. The \(1\)st digit is \(1\), the \(2\)nd is \(4\), the \(3\)rd is \(2\), the \(4\)th is \(8\), the \(5\)th is \(5\), and the \(6\)th is \(7\). After that the block starts over, so position \(7\) is \(1\) again, position \(8\) is \(4\), and the cycle keeps turning. The key move is that every group of \(6\) positions returns you to the same place in the block. So to find position \(50\), see how many full groups of \(6\) fit and what is left over. $$50 = 6 \times 8 + 2$$ Eight complete blocks carry you up through position \(48\), which is the end of the eighth block, a \(7\). Then you take \(2\) more steps into a fresh block. Step one lands on \(1\) (position \(49\)) and step two lands on \(4\) (position \(50\)). The remainder \(2\) points straight at the \(2\)nd digit of \(142857\), and that digit is \(4\). No fifty rounds of division needed, just one little division to find your place in the loop. \(\boxed{4}\)
Key idea
Finding a far digit. Once you know the repeating block and its length, the decimal is settled forever, so finding the digit at any spot is just asking where that spot lands inside one trip around the block. Divide the position by the period and read the remainder: remainder \(1\) points to the first digit of the block, remainder \(2\) to the second, and a remainder of \(0\) means you stopped right on the last digit. The remainder is what made the digits loop in the first place, so the same loop that trapped the division is the one you ride straight to any position, no long division needed beyond computing the period.
Fun fact
In base \(7\), the fraction \(\tfrac{1}{7}\) would simply be \(0.1\), while \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) would repeat. Whether a decimal terminates depends on the base, not the fraction.
Which of \(\tfrac{1}{16}\), \(\tfrac{1}{20}\), \(\tfrac{1}{25}\), \(\tfrac{1}{15}\) repeats? Factor each denominator and apply the \(2\)s-and-\(5\)s test. Give the denominator.
Show solution
The whole question lives in the prime factorizations, so factor all four denominators and never touch long division. \(16 = 2^4\), made of nothing but \(2\)s. \(20 = 2^2 \times 5\), only \(2\)s and a \(5\). \(25 = 5^2\), only \(5\)s. \(15 = 3 \times 5\), and there it is, a \(3\) hiding among the friendly primes. The rule from 5.3, sharpened by this lesson, says a lowest-terms fraction terminates exactly when its denominator is built solely from \(2\)s and \(5\)s. Those are the only primes that divide a power of \(10\), so a denominator of that shape can always be scaled up into a clean power of ten, which is what a terminating decimal really is. A \(3\) can never be turned into a \(10\) by piling on more \(2\)s and \(5\)s, so it has no way to clear out. In the long division for \(\tfrac{1}{15}\) the remainders cycle instead of reaching \(0\), and you get \(\tfrac{1}{15} = 0.0\overline{6}\), where the \(6\) loops without end. Meanwhile the other three stop cold, \(\tfrac{1}{16} = 0.0625\), \(\tfrac{1}{20} = 0.05\), and \(\tfrac{1}{25} = 0.04\). One stray prime was the entire story. The repeating fraction is \(\tfrac{1}{15}\), so the denominator you want is \(\boxed{15}\).
Find \(0.\overline{72} - 0.\overline{27}\) as a single fraction in lowest terms. Convert each to a fraction over \(99\), subtract, then reduce.
Show solution
Start by converting each runner with the block-over-nines move you discovered earlier in the chapter. A repeating two-digit block always sits over \(99\), so $$0.\overline{72} = \frac{72}{99}, \qquad 0.\overline{27} = \frac{27}{99}.$$ Here is the satisfying part. Both fractions already share the denominator \(99\), so there is no common-denominator work to do. You just subtract the numerators and keep the \(99\) underneath. $$\frac{72}{99} - \frac{27}{99} = \frac{72 - 27}{99} = \frac{45}{99}.$$ Now reduce all the way down using the greatest-common-factor habit from Chapter 4. Both \(45\) and \(99\) are divisible by \(9\), since \(45 = 9 \times 5\) and \(99 = 9 \times 11\). Dividing top and bottom by \(9\) gives $$\frac{45}{99} = \frac{45 \div 9}{99 \div 9} = \frac{5}{11}.$$ Since \(5\) is prime and does not divide \(11\), this is fully reduced. As a quick sanity check, \(\frac{5}{11}\) is itself the repeating decimal \(0.\overline{45}\), and indeed \(0.727272\dots\) minus \(0.272727\dots\) leaves \(0.454545\dots\) at every place. The first runner beats the second by \(\boxed{5/11}\).

Review problems

Review 1. Long-divide \(2 \div 3\). Each step ends with the same remainder. What single digit repeats forever in the decimal \(0.\overline{6}\)?
Show solution
Long division is really a remainder machine, and the moment a remainder shows up twice, you know the digits will loop. Start with \(2 \div 3\). Since \(3\) is bigger than \(2\), the whole-number part is \(0\), and we carry a remainder of \(2\) past the decimal point. Now we divide \(20\) by \(3\). Because \(3 \times 6 = 18\), the digit is \(6\) and the leftover is \(20 - 18 = 2\). That leftover of \(2\) is exactly the remainder we began with, so the next step is identical, and so is the one after that. The machine is stuck in a loop, printing a \(6\) on every turn. This gives the endless decimal $$2 \div 3 = 0.6666\ldots = 0.\overline{6}.$$ The bar sits over the block that repeats, and here that block is a single digit. So the digit that goes on forever is \(\boxed{6}\).
Review 2. Long-divide \(1 \div 9\). The remainder returns immediately, giving \(\tfrac{1}{9} = 0.\overline{1}\). How many digits are in the repeating block? Give the period.
Show solution
Do the long division and watch the remainder. Since \(9\) does not go into \(1\), place a decimal point and bring down a zero to make \(10\). Now \(9\) goes into \(10\) one time, since \(9 \times 1 = 9\), and the remainder is \(10 - 9 = 1\). That remainder of \(1\) is exactly the number we started with, so the next step repeats the same digit, and so does the step after that, with no end. Every step writes a single \(1\). So the decimal is $$\frac{1}{9} = 0.1111\ldots = 0.\overline{1}.$$ The bar covers just one digit, the lone \(1\). A repeating block that is a single digit has a period of one, and \(\tfrac{1}{9}\) is the simplest pure repeater there is. The number of digits in the repeating block is \(\boxed{1}\).
Review 3. Long-divide \(1 \div 11\). The remainder returns after two digits, giving \(\tfrac{1}{11} = 0.\overline{09}\). Write the repeating block as a digit string, including the leading zero.
Show solution
Long division is really a remainder chase, and the moment a remainder repeats, the whole pattern repeats with it. Start the division \(1\div 11\). The first remainder is \(1\). Attach a zero to get \(10\), and ask how many times \(11\) fits into \(10\). It fits \(0\) times, so the first decimal digit is \(0\) and the remainder stays \(10\). Attach another zero to get \(100\). Now \(11\) fits \(9\) times since \(9\times 11=99\), so the second decimal digit is \(9\) and the new remainder is \(100-99=1\). That remainder of \(1\) is exactly the one we started with, so from here the digits \(0\) then \(9\) will cycle forever. That gives \(\frac{1}{11}=0.0909\ldots=0.\overline{09}\). The repeating block is both digits together, and the leading zero is part of the block, not decoration. So the two-digit repeating block, written as a digit string, is \(\boxed{09}\).
Review 4. \(5 \div 6 = 0.8\overline{3}\). The digit \(8\) settles before the loop. How many digits come before the repeating block begins?
Show solution
Long division shows the whole story. Bring down to get \(50 \div 6\), which is \(8\) with a remainder of \(2\). That \(8\) is the first decimal digit. Now carry the remainder \(2\) forward, so \(20 \div 6\) is \(3\) with a remainder of \(2\) again. Since the remainder is back to \(2\), the same step repeats, so the digit \(3\) loops forever. That gives \(\frac{5}{6} = 0.8333\ldots = 0.8\overline{3}\). The bar sits over the \(3\) because the \(3\) is the part that repeats. The \(8\) appears once and is never under the bar, so it is the non-repeating head. Counting the digits outside the bar, there is exactly one, the \(8\). A decimal like this is called eventually repeating. It settles into its loop after a short run-up rather than right away, and here that run-up is a single digit. \(\boxed{1}\)
Review 5. Long-divide \(7 \div 30\) to find \(\tfrac{7}{30} = 0.2\overline{3}\). The \(2\) settles first. What single digit is the repeating block?
Show solution
Do the long division of \(7 \div 30\) and keep an eye on the remainders. First, \(70 \div 30 = 2\) with remainder \(10\), so the first decimal digit is \(2\). This is the digit that settles, thanks to the factors of \(2\) and \(5\) in \(30\). Next, bring down a zero to get \(100\). Now \(100 \div 30 = 3\) with remainder \(10\). The digit is \(3\), and the remainder is \(10\) again, the same remainder we just had. Once a remainder repeats, the process is locked into a cycle. Every step from here produces another \(3\) with remainder \(10\), over and over. So \(\tfrac{7}{30} = 0.2333\ldots = 0.2\overline{3}\). The \(2\) is the settling digit and the part under the bar is the repeating block. That block is a single digit, so the repeating block is \(\boxed{3}\).
Review 6. Let \(x = 0.\overline{5}\). Multiply by \(10\): \(10x = 5.\overline{5}\). Subtract: \(9x = 5\). Write \(0.\overline{5}\) as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start by naming the mystery number. Let \(x = 0.\overline{5} = 0.5555\ldots\). The whole point is that this tail runs on forever with the same digit, and that is exactly what we will use against it. Multiply both sides by \(10\) so every digit shifts one place to the left, which gives \(10x = 5.5555\ldots\). Now line the two equations up. $$10x = 5.5555\ldots$$ $$x = 0.5555\ldots$$ Both numbers end in the identical endless string of fives, so when we subtract the bottom from the top those tails cancel perfectly and nothing is left dangling. On the left \(10x - x = 9x\), and on the right \(5.5555\ldots - 0.5555\ldots = 5\). That leaves the clean little equation \(9x = 5\). Divide both sides by \(9\) to free \(x\), so \(x = \tfrac{5}{9}\). Finally check that the fraction is already in lowest terms. The factors of \(5\) are just \(1\) and \(5\), and \(9 = 3 \times 3\) shares none of them, so nothing reduces. The endless decimal you started with was secretly this exact fraction the whole time, \(\boxed{5/9}\).
Review 7. Using the block-over-nines shortcut, \(0.\overline{2}\) has a one-digit block, so it equals \(\tfrac{2}{9}\). Write \(0.\overline{2}\) as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
The repeating block is \(2\), and it is a single digit, so it goes over one nine. That gives \(0.\overline{2} = \tfrac{2}{9}\). The last step is to reduce. The factors of \(2\) are \(1\) and \(2\), and the factors of \(9\) are \(1\), \(3\), and \(9\). The only number they share is \(1\), so the fraction is already as small as it gets. To see why the shortcut is honest, let \(x = 0.\overline{2}\). Then \(10x = 2.\overline{2}\), and subtracting gives \(10x - x = 2.\overline{2} - 0.\overline{2}\), so \(9x = 2\) and \(x = \tfrac{2}{9}\). The nine in the denominator is exactly the \(10 - 1\) hiding behind the trick. \(\boxed{2/9}\)
Review 8. The decimal \(0.\overline{81}\) equals \(\tfrac{81}{99}\). Reduce \(\tfrac{81}{99}\) to lowest terms. What fraction do you get?
Show solution
Start by reading the bar. The notation \(0.\overline{81}\) is short for \(0.818181\ldots\), with the two-digit block 81 repeating forever. A repeating block of length two always sits over \(99\), so the fraction you begin with is $$0.\overline{81}=\frac{81}{99}.$$ This value is right, but \(\dfrac{81}{99}\) is not in lowest terms yet, because the top and the bottom share a common factor. The greatest common factor of \(81\) and \(99\) is \(9\), since \(81=9\times 9\) and \(99=9\times 11\). Dividing both parts by \(9\) gives $$\frac{81}{99}=\frac{81\div 9}{99\div 9}=\frac{9}{11}.$$ You can check the result by turning it back into a decimal. Long division of \(9\div 11\) gives \(0.818181\ldots\), the same repeating \(81\), so the conversion is sound. And \(9\) and \(11\) share no common factor other than \(1\), so this is as far as it reduces. The decimal that looked endless was just a neat fraction in disguise, \(\boxed{9/11}\).
Review 9. Write \(0.0\overline{45}\) as a fraction in lowest terms. Use \(1{,}000x\) and \(10x\) to cancel the repeating tails, giving \(990x = 45\), then reduce.
Show solution
Call the number \(x = 0.0\overline{45} = 0.0454545\ldots\). The trouble is that single leading zero, since it appears once and never repeats, so a plain \(100x\) trick will not line the tails up. The fix is to shift twice. Slide the decimal three places to land just before a fresh \(45\), giving \(1{,}000x = 45.454545\ldots\). Slide it one place to clear only the head zero, giving \(10x = 0.454545\ldots\). Both now have the identical repeating tail \(.454545\ldots\). Subtract the smaller from the larger and the tails vanish. $$1{,}000x - 10x = 45.4545\ldots - 0.4545\ldots = 45$$ The left side is \(990x\), so \(990x = 45\) and \(x = \tfrac{45}{990}\). Reduce by the greatest common factor. Both share \(45\), since \(45 = 45 \cdot 1\) and \(990 = 45 \cdot 22\), giving \(\tfrac{45}{990} = \tfrac{1}{22}\). A quick check confirms it, since long division of \(1 \div 22\) gives \(0.0454545\ldots\), the zero once and then \(45\) on a loop. So \(0.0\overline{45} = \boxed{1/22}\).
Review 10. Let \(x = 0.\overline{9}\). Multiply by \(10\): \(10x = 9.\overline{9}\). Subtract and solve. What single whole number does \(0.\overline{9}\) equal?
Show solution
Start by naming the mystery value. Let \(x = 0.\overline{9}\). The bar means the nines repeat forever with no end. Multiplying by \(10\) slides every digit one spot to the left, so $$10x = 9.\overline{9}.$$ The clever part is the subtraction. Line the two equations up and take the difference. $$10x - x = 9.\overline{9} - 0.\overline{9}$$ On the right, the part after the decimal point is an identical endless string of nines in both numbers, so it cancels completely and only the \(9\) in front survives. On the left, \(10x - x = 9x\). That leaves $$9x = 9.$$ Divide both sides by \(9\) to get \(x = 1\). Since \(x\) was \(0.\overline{9}\) the whole time, this says \(0.\overline{9}\) and \(1\) are not close, they are the exact same number written two different ways. A quick second check agrees. From the work in this chapter, \(0.\overline{1} = \tfrac{1}{9}\), so nine of those repeating tenths give \(9 \cdot \tfrac{1}{9} = \tfrac{9}{9} = 1\), and \(9 \cdot 0.\overline{1} = 0.\overline{9}\). Both roads land in the same place. \(\boxed{1}\)
Review 11. \(\tfrac{1}{13} = 0.\overline{076923}\), a block of \(6\) digits. Divide \(100 \div 6\) to place the position within the block. What is the 100th digit after the decimal point?
Show solution
Start with what the decimal actually is. Doing the long division, \(\tfrac{1}{13} = 0.\overline{076923}\), a pure repeater whose block \(076923\) has length \(6\). So the stream of digits after the decimal point is \(076923\,076923\,076923\dots\), the same six digits over and over with nothing else mixed in. Because the pattern restarts every \(6\) digits, the position of a digit only matters up to multiples of \(6\). Position \(6\) ends the first block, position \(12\) ends the second, and in general every position that is a multiple of \(6\) holds the last digit of the block. To place position \(100\), divide by the block length. \(100 = 6 \times 16 + 4\), because \(6 \times 16 = 96\) and \(100 - 96 = 4\). That means after \(96\) digits we have just finished \(16\) complete blocks and landed exactly on a block boundary, and then we count \(4\) more digits into a fresh copy of \(076923\). Reading that block, the 1st digit is \(0\), the 2nd is \(7\), the 3rd is \(6\), and the 4th is \(9\). So the digit sitting in the 100th place is \(\boxed{9}\). The remainder is the whole trick. The quotient \(16\) just counts how many full blocks went by and never affects which digit you land on, while the remainder \(4\) points straight at the 4th slot of the block. A nice way to feel sure is to notice that position \(96\) is a multiple of \(6\), so it holds the \(6\)th digit \(3\), and then positions \(97, 98, 99, 100\) read off \(0, 7, 6, 9\), landing on \(9\) again.
Review 12. \(\frac{4}{33} = 0.\overline{12}\), a block of \(2\) digits. Divide \(99 \div 2\) to find the position in the block. What is the 99th digit after the decimal point?
Show solution
The decimal \(\frac{4}{33} = 0.\overline{12}\) repeats in a block of length \(2\), so every digit is determined by its position within that block. To locate position \(99\), divide by the block length. We get \(99 = 2 \times 49 + 1\), so after \(49\) complete blocks of \(12\) there is a remainder of \(1\). A remainder of \(1\) means we land on the first digit of the block \(12\), which is \(1\). You can sanity check this with the odd-even pattern. Odd positions \(1, 3, 5, \ldots\) always hold the \(1\), and even positions hold the \(2\). Since \(99\) is odd, the digit is the \(1\). $$\frac{4}{33} = 0.\underbrace{12}_{1}\underbrace{12}_{2}\ldots, \qquad 99 \div 2 = 49 \text{ r } 1$$ So the 99th digit is \(\boxed{1}\).
Review 13. Factor each denominator: \(8=2^3\), \(40=2^3\times5\), \(50=2\times5^2\), \(24=2^3\times3\). Which denominator contains a prime other than \(2\) or \(5\), forcing its decimal to repeat?
Show solution
The gatekeeper test says a fraction in lowest terms gives a terminating decimal exactly when its denominator, once fully factored, contains only the primes 2 and 5. Any other prime down there means the decimal repeats forever. So I do not need to divide at all. I just read the prime factorizations. \(8 = 2^3\), all 2s, so \(\tfrac{1}{8}\) terminates. \(40 = 2^3 \times 5\), only 2s and 5s, so \(\tfrac{1}{40}\) terminates. \(50 = 2 \times 5^2\), again only 2s and 5s, so \(\tfrac{1}{50}\) terminates. That leaves \(24 = 2^3 \times 3\). The 3 is a prime that is neither 2 nor 5, and it cannot cancel because the fraction is already in lowest terms. That single 3 is the troublemaker that keeps the decimal going. If you want proof, the long division of \(\tfrac{1}{24}\) settles into \(0.041\overline{6} = 0.041666\ldots\), a decimal that truly never ends. The denominator that repeats is \(\boxed{24}\).
Review 14. Find \(0.\overline{81} - 0.\overline{36}\) as a single fraction in lowest terms. Both two-digit repeaters sit over \(99\); subtract the numerators and reduce.
Show solution
Every repeating block of two digits turns into that block over two nines, so \(0.\overline{81}=\dfrac{81}{99}\) and \(0.\overline{36}=\dfrac{36}{99}\). They were built on the same denominator, which is the lucky part. Subtracting is just subtracting the numerators. $$\frac{81}{99}-\frac{36}{99}=\frac{81-36}{99}=\frac{45}{99}$$ Now reduce. Both \(45\) and \(99\) share the factor \(9\), since \(45=9\times 5\) and \(99=9\times 11\). Dividing top and bottom by \(9\) gives \(\dfrac{45}{99}=\dfrac{5}{11}\). You can sanity check it by dividing \(5\) by \(11\), which loops as \(0.454545\dots=0.\overline{45}\), exactly the gap between the two decimals you started with. \(\boxed{5/11}\)
