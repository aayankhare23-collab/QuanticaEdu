Prealgebra · Lesson 1.5

Division

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Two operations are tamed, and each came with a partner that undoes it. Subtraction turned out to be addition in a costume, since a − b is only a + (−b), and the opposite −b is the number that carries you back to 0. That leaves the fourth and final operation, division. Here is a fair guess to keep in your pocket before we start. If subtraction was addition in disguise, division will be multiplication in disguise, and it will need a partner of its own. The rest of this lesson earns that guess by using it.

A blueprint app has one button that scales every length by 5, blowing a drawing up to five times its size. You tap it by accident. You need a second scaling that puts the drawing back exactly as it was, neither bigger nor smaller. Multiplying by $1$ is the scaling that changes nothing, so what number must you scale by to undo the \(\times 5\)?
Show solution
Scaling up by 5 and back down acts like multiplying by 1, so you need the number that turns 5 into 1.$$5 \times \frac{1}{5} = 1$$The undo scales by \(\boxed{\frac{1}{5}}\), the reciprocal of 5.
Key idea
Every number except 0 has a reciprocal, written \(\frac{1}{b}\), the partner that multiplies with it to give 1, so \(b \times \frac{1}{b} = 1\). The reciprocal is to multiplication what the opposite was to addition. The opposite carries a number home to 0, the number that does nothing when you add. The reciprocal carries it home to 1, the number that does nothing when you multiply.
Here is a pattern worth predicting before you compute. Last chapter, the opposite of the opposite of a number brought you right back to the number, since two flips across zero return home. Now guess the reciprocal of a reciprocal, then test the guess. What is the reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{6}\)?
Show solution
Flip \(\frac{1}{6}\) and it becomes 6.$$\frac{1}{6} \times 6 = 1$$So the reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{6}\) is \(\boxed{6}\). Flipping twice returns the original, so the reciprocal of a reciprocal is the number itself.
Key idea
Two facts fall out at once. The reciprocal of a fraction is that fraction flipped, since the swapped top and bottom always cancel. And the reciprocal of a reciprocal is the original number, since flipping twice returns you home, just as taking the opposite twice did. A reciprocal is a round trip waiting to happen.
One number refuses to play. Try to find the reciprocal of $0$, the partner that would multiply with 0 to give 1. Hunt for it, and say what goes wrong.
Show solution
The reciprocal of 0 would have to turn 0 into 1, but \(0 \times (\text{any number}) = 0\), never 1. So 0 has no reciprocal, the one number locked out of this game.
Last chapter, exactly one number was its own opposite, the number 0, sitting right on its own mirror image. Now ask the matching question for reciprocals. How many numbers are their own reciprocal, equal to the very number that multiplies with them to give 1? Test small cases and watch for a surprise.
Show solution
A number is its own reciprocal when it times itself gives 1. \(1 \times 1 = 1\), and \((-1) \times (-1) = 1\), so 1 and \(-1\) both qualify. Nothing else does, since any number above 1 has a reciprocal below 1. That leaves exactly \(\boxed{2}\) such numbers.
Key idea
A number and its reciprocal always share a sign, because their product is 1, a positive, and a positive product cannot come from one positive and one negative factor. So the reciprocal of a negative number is itself negative. Two numbers are their own reciprocal, 1 and −1, and one number, 0, has no reciprocal at all.

Now we hold the partner, so we can build the operation. Watch the symmetry with subtraction line up. Subtraction added the opposite. Division, as you are about to see, multiplies by the reciprocal. First let’s check the strange sounding rule against the plain division you already trust, and then let’s turn it loose where ordinary division would leave you stuck.

A relay team of $4$ runners splits a $12$ km course into equal legs. Plain division says each leg is \(12 \div 4 = 3\) km, no surprise. Now compute it the new way instead. Take \(\frac{1}{4}\) of 12, that is \(12 \times \frac{1}{4}\). How long is each leg this way?
Show solution
Each runner covers one quarter of 12 km, found by multiplying.$$12 \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{12}{4} = \boxed{3}$$Dividing 12 by 4 and multiplying 12 by \(\frac{1}{4}\) are two names for one number.
Key idea
Division is multiplication by the reciprocal. For any number a and any nonzero b, $a\div b=a\times\frac1b$. This is the twin of subtraction, where $a-b=a+(-b)$. And here at last is why you can never divide by 0. Dividing by b means multiplying by the reciprocal of b, but 0 has no reciprocal, so dividing by 0 is not hard, it is meaningless.
A jar holds 3 cups of flour. Your scoop holds \(\frac{1}{4}\) cup. How many full scoops fill the jar? In symbols, find $$3 \div \frac{1}{4}.$$
Show solution
Turn the division into multiplication by the reciprocal of \(\frac{1}{4}\), which is 4.$$3 \div \frac{1}{4} = 3 \times 4 = \boxed{12}$$The answer grew past 3 because we divided by a number below 1, whose reciprocal is bigger than 1.
Key idea
Dividing by a number between 0 and 1 makes the result larger, not smaller. There is no contradiction once you read division correctly. Dividing by 1/4 is multiplying by 4. The smaller the piece you divide by, the more of them fit, so the bigger the count.
Over $6$ equal stages of a dive, a submarine’s depth changed by a total of $-240$ m (a downward move counts as negative, as it did two lessons ago). The stages were identical, so what was the depth change per stage? Compute $$-240 \div 6.$$
Show solution
Rewrite the division as multiplication by \(\frac{1}{6}\).$$-240 \div 6 = -240 \times \frac{1}{6}$$One negative factor makes the result negative.$$-240 \times \frac{1}{6} = \boxed{-40}$$Division borrows every sign rule from multiplication, since it is multiplication by the reciprocal.
Structure beats grinding, as always. Compute $$(16 \times 21) \div (8 \times 3)$$ without doing the two multiplications first. Look for parts of the top that pair off neatly with parts of the bottom.
Show solution

The slow road multiplies \(16 \times 21 = 336\) and \(8 \times 3 = 24\), then divides \(336 \div 24 = 14\). It works, but the big products were a detour. Read the division as multiplying by reciprocals and the factors sort themselves into friendly pairs.

$$(16 \times 21) \times \frac{1}{8} \times \frac{1}{3} = \left(16 \times \frac{1}{8}\right) \times \left(21 \times \frac{1}{3}\right)$$

Each pair collapses on its own, \(16 \times \frac{1}{8} = 2\) and \(21 \times \frac{1}{3} = 7\), so the whole thing is

$$2 \times 7 = \boxed{14}$$

Every pair that cancels leaves a clean 1 behind and quietly disappears, the same way a number plus its opposite vanished to 0 last chapter. Pair first, multiply last.

Key idea
In a quotient of products, pair each factor on top with a factor on the bottom that it cancels with, and clear those pairs before multiplying anything. Each cancelled pair leaves a 1 behind, the do nothing of multiplication, exactly as each opposite pair left a 0 behind under addition. The whole point is to never build the big products at all.
Sometimes the top and bottom share a factor so ugly you would dread computing it. Find $$(637 \times 50) \div (637 \times 10).$$ The 637 is bait. Do not take it.
Show solution

Read the division as multiplication by reciprocals and the 637 meets its own reciprocal.

$$637 \times 50 \times \frac{1}{637} \times \frac{1}{10} = \left(637 \times \frac{1}{637}\right) \times \left(50 \times \frac{1}{10}\right)$$

That first pair is \(637 \times \frac{1}{637} = 1\), no matter what 637 happens to be, so it simply evaporates and you never multiply it out.

$$1 \times (50 \div 10) = \boxed{5}$$

An identical factor on top and bottom always cancels to 1, whatever its size, the way a number and its opposite always cancelled to 0. The 637 was there to scare you into hard arithmetic you did not need.

A signal runs through a pipeline that multiplies it by $14$, then by $27$, then by $10$. Three filters then divide it by $7$, by $9$, and by $5$. By what single factor does the signal change overall? Compute $$(14 \times 27 \times 10) \div (7 \times 9 \times 5)$$ by pairing, not by grinding.
Show solution

Spread the three divisions out as reciprocals and the six numbers fall into three tidy couples.

$$14 \times 27 \times 10 \times \frac{1}{7} \times \frac{1}{9} \times \frac{1}{5}$$

Pair each top factor with the bottom factor built to cancel it.

$$(14 \div 7) \times (27 \div 9) \times (10 \div 5) = 2 \times 3 \times 2 = \boxed{12}$$

The signal comes out 12 times bigger, and not one of the original six multiplications had to be carried out. A long chain of times and divided by collapses the instant you match each factor with its partner.

Addition and multiplication let you reorder and regroup at will. Division is stricter, and it is worth catching in the act. First, is \(12 \div 3\) the same as \(3 \div 12\)? Then settle a grouping fight. Compute \(24 \div (4 \div 2)\) and compare it to \((24 \div 4) \div 2\). Enter the value of $$24 \div (4 \div 2).$$
Show solution

Order first. \(12 \div 3 = 4\), but \(3 \div 12 = \frac{1}{4}\), nowhere near equal. So you cannot swap the two numbers in a division. Now grouping. Inside the parentheses, \(4 \div 2 = 2\), so

$$24 \div (4 \div 2) = 24 \div 2 = \boxed{12}$$

But moving the parentheses changes everything.

$$(24 \div 4) \div 2 = 6 \div 2 = 3$$

Twelve against three, from the same three numbers. Division simply does not reorder or regroup the way addition and multiplication do. The reason is clean. Division flips only the number you divide by, so moving the numbers around changes which one gets flipped.

Key idea
Division will not let you shuffle freely, but there is a fix you have used once already. Last chapter, the trick for messy subtractions was to turn every minus into adding the opposite, and then all the addition freedoms came back. Do the same here. Turn every ÷ b into × 1/b first. Once the whole expression is one long product, you may reorder, regroup, and cancel it however you like.
Put every tool in one place. Compute $$(-45 \times 16) \div (-9 \times 4)$$ in your head. Mind the signs, convert the division, then pair off.
Show solution

Start with the sign. There is one negative on top and one on the bottom, two negatives in all, so the answer is positive and you can set the signs aside.

$$(-45 \times 16) \div (-9 \times 4) = (45 \times 16) \div (9 \times 4)$$

Now convert and pair. Spread the bottom out as reciprocals and match the factors that cancel.

$$45 \times 16 \times \frac{1}{9} \times \frac{1}{4} = (45 \div 9) \times (16 \div 4) = 5 \times 4 = \boxed{20}$$

Signs handled by the flip rule, division turned into multiplication, factors paired off to cancel. Three lessons, one short computation.

Key idea
Every number but 0 has a reciprocal, the partner that multiplies with it to give 1. Flip a fraction to find it, $\frac{a}{b}\to\frac{b}{a}$, and flipping twice returns the original. Only 1 and −1 are their own reciprocal, a number shares its reciprocal’s sign, and 0 has none.
Key idea
Dividing by b is multiplying by 1/b, so $a\div b=a\times\frac1b$, the twin of $a-b=a+(-b)$. Every sign rule from multiplication carries straight over. And dividing by a number between 0 and 1 makes the result larger, since its reciprocal is bigger than 1.
Key idea
In a quotient of products, pair each top factor with a bottom factor that cancels it and clear the pairs before multiplying. Identical factors vanish to 1 whatever their size. Division will not reorder or regroup the way + and × do, so turn every ÷ into × the reciprocal first, then shuffle and cancel freely.

Review problems

Review 1. Compute $$(9 + 18 + 27 + 36) \div (36 + 27 + 18 + 9).$$
Show solution
The numbers above the line and below it are identical, only listed in reverse, so the two sums are equal. Any nonzero amount divided by itself is \(\boxed{1}\), you never have to add a single thing.
Review 2. Compute $$5{,}600{,}000 \div 70{,}000$$ by cancelling, not by long division.
Show solution
Strike four matching zeros from the top and bottom: \(5{,}600{,}000 \div 70{,}000\) becomes \(560 \div 7 = \boxed{80}\). Cancelling equal factors of 10 from numerator and denominator never changes a quotient.
Review 3. Compute $$(317 \times 317) \div 317$$ without ever multiplying 317 by itself.
Show solution
Dividing a product by one of its own factors simply hands back the other factor, so the answer is \(\boxed{317}\). Multiplying by a number and then dividing by it is a round trip straight back to the start.
Review 4. A ribbon is $6$ metres long. How many \(\frac{1}{5}\)-metre bookmarks can you cut from it? Compute $$6 \div \frac{1}{5}.$$
Show solution
Five fifths fit in every metre, so six metres give \(6 \times 5 = \boxed{30}\) bookmarks. Dividing by \(\frac{1}{5}\) means multiplying by its reciprocal, 5, and the count grows past 6 because the piece you cut is tiny.
Review 5. Compute $$20 \div \frac{2}{5}.$$
Show solution
Flip the divisor and multiply: \(20 \div \frac{2}{5} = 20 \times \frac{5}{2} = \frac{100}{2} = \boxed{50}\).
Review 6. What number is 4 less than the value of \(48 \div \frac{3}{4}\)?
Show solution
\(48 \div \frac{3}{4} = 48 \times \frac{4}{3} = 64\), and \(64 - 4 = \boxed{60}\). Dividing by a number below 1 makes the result bigger, which catches almost everyone off guard the first time.
Review 7. Compute $$(13 \times 40 \times 9) \div (9 \times 13)$$ by cancelling first.
Show solution
The 9 and the 13 on top each meet a copy on the bottom and cancel to 1, leaving the lone \(\boxed{40}\). Neither big product was ever worth forming.
Review 8. Compute $$(20 \times 50 \times 90) \div (2 \times 5 \times 9).$$
Show solution
Pair and divide: \(20 \div 2 = 10\), \(50 \div 5 = 10\), \(90 \div 9 = 10\). So the whole expression is \(10 \times 10 \times 10 = \boxed{1000}\). Scaling every factor up by ten multiplies the quotient by ten, once for each factor.
Review 9. Compute $$(4{,}000{,}000 + 36) \div 4$$ by sharing the division across the sum.
Show solution
Divide each piece on its own: \(4{,}000{,}000 \div 4 + 36 \div 4 = 1{,}000{,}000 + 9 = \boxed{1{,}000{,}009}\). Splitting the dividend into friendly chunks beats one monstrous long division.
Review 10. Compute $$(72 + 56 + 40 + 24) \div (9 + 7 + 5 + 3)$$ by comparing the two sums.
Show solution
Every top term is eight times its partner (\(72 = 8 \times 9\), \(56 = 8 \times 7\), \(40 = 8 \times 5\), \(24 = 8 \times 3\)), so the whole top is eight times the whole bottom. A quantity divided by one-eighth of itself is \(\boxed{8}\), and not a single sum had to be worked out.
Review 11. Compute $$96 \div \frac{1}{2} \div \frac{1}{2} \div \frac{1}{3},$$ working left to right.
Show solution
Left to right: \(96 \div \frac{1}{2} = 192\), then \(192 \div \frac{1}{2} = 384\), then \(384 \div \frac{1}{3} = \boxed{1152}\). Two doublings and a tripling multiply the start by \(2 \times 2 \times 3 = 12\), and \(96 \times 12 = 1152\).
Review 12. Compute $$(45 \times 28) \div (35 \times 18)$$ without multiplying either product out.
Show solution
Cancel in stages. 45 and 35 share 5, leaving 9 and 7; 28 and 18 share 2, leaving 14 and 9. Now \((9 \times 14) \div (7 \times 9)\): the 9 cancels and \(14 \div 7 = 2\), so the answer is \(\boxed{2}\). Clearing shared factors first turns four awkward numbers into a single digit.
Review 13. Watch what division does as the divisor shrinks. $12\div1=12$, then $12\div\frac12=24$, then $12\div\frac14=48$, then $12\div\frac{1}{100}=1200$. The nearer the divisor creeps to $0$, the more violently the quotient explodes, with no ceiling anywhere. That runaway is the real reason you cannot divide by $0$: there is no single number waiting at the top of the climb, only an endless rush toward the infinite.
