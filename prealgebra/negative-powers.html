Prealgebra · Lesson 2.4

Negative Powers

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Every power you have met so far makes a number bigger. But exponents do not have to stop at zero. The ladder of powers keeps climbing down past the bottom rung, and the steps below zero hide a surprise that turns out to be the whole point of this section.

A glacier lab logs ice thickness in powers of 5 centimetres. Reading down the column the entries are \(5^3 = 125\), \(5^2 = 25\), \(5^1 = 5\), and each step down divides the thickness by 5. The next entry is \(5^0\). What number is \(5^0\)?
Show solution
Each step down divides by 5. From \(5^1 = 5\), one more step gives \(5 \div 5 = 1\), so \(5^0 = \boxed{1}\). The zero power sits exactly where the dividing-by-5 pattern says it should.
Keep going down the same column. Below \(5^0 = 1\) the lab writes \(5^{-1}\), still made by dividing the entry above it by 5. What number is \(5^{-1}\)? Write it as a fraction.
Show solution
One more step down divides \(5^0 = 1\) by 5, giving \(1 \div 5 = \dfrac{1}{5}\). So \(5^{-1} = \boxed{\dfrac{1}{5}}\). A negative exponent has flipped the base into the bottom of a fraction.
Key idea
Negative powers. A nonzero base raised to a negative exponent is 1 over the base raised to the matching positive exponent. $$a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^{n}} \quad (a \neq 0)$$ The descending ladder forces it. Each step down divides by the base, and dividing past \(a^0 = 1\) sends the base under the fraction bar.

There is a second way to see the same fact, and it uses a law you already own. The quotient rule subtracts exponents when you divide a common base. Let the bottom exponent be the larger one and watch where the rule points.

A delivery drone climbs in stages, each multiplying its altitude by 4. Compute \(\dfrac{4^2}{4^5}\) two ways. First as a plain fraction by writing out the factors and cancelling, then by the quotient rule which subtracts \(2 - 5\). What negative power of 4 do both ways give? Give the exponent only.
Show solution
Writing the factors, two 4s cancel and three remain underneath, so \(\dfrac{4^2}{4^5} = \dfrac{1}{4^3}\). The quotient rule agrees, \(4^{2-5} = 4^{-3}\). The two views match, \(4^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{4^3}\), and the exponent is \(\boxed{-3}\).
Key idea
Both routes land in the same place. The quotient rule turns \(\dfrac{a^m}{a^n}\) into \(a^{m-n}\), and when \(n\) is the larger exponent the leftover factors all sit in the denominator. So a negative exponent is just the bookkeeping for factors that ended up downstairs.
A pollen grain seen under a microscope has width \(3^{-4}\) of a millimetre. Write \(3^{-4}\) as a single fraction.
Show solution
Flip the base under the bar with its matching positive power. $$3^{-4} = \dfrac{1}{3^4} = \dfrac{1}{81}$$ So the width is \(\boxed{\dfrac{1}{81}}\) of a millimetre.

A negative exponent means a reciprocal, and that single idea also runs in reverse. If the base is already a fraction, a negative exponent flips it back up. The next two problems push on that.

A zoom lens reverses its setting. The reduced view was \(\dfrac{1}{4}\), and reversing it raises that to the power \(-3\). Evaluate \(\left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right)^{-3}\) as a whole number.
Show solution
A negative exponent flips the base over. The reciprocal of \(\dfrac{1}{4}\) is 4, so $$\left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right)^{-3} = 4^{3} = 64.$$ The fraction turned right side up and grew. The answer is \(\boxed{64}\).
Key idea
Reciprocal bases. A fraction to a negative power flips upright and the exponent turns positive. $$\left(\dfrac{1}{a}\right)^{n} = a^{-n} \qquad \left(a^{-1}\right)^{n} = a^{-n}$$ A power simply flips the sign of its exponent each time it crosses a fraction bar.
A relay stores the reciprocal of 5 as \(5^{-1}\), then a reset raises that stored value to the power \(-2\). Evaluate \(\left(5^{-1}\right)^{-2}\) as a whole number.
Show solution
The exponent flips its sign each time it crosses the bar, so \(5^{-1}\) raised to \(-2\) comes back upright. $$\left(5^{-1}\right)^{-2} = 5^{(-1)\times(-2)} = 5^{2} = 25$$ Two reciprocals undo each other. The answer is \(\boxed{25}\).
Fun fact
Scientists lean on negative powers of ten constantly. A nanometre is \(10^{-9}\) of a metre, the rough width of a few atoms side by side, and a picosecond is \(10^{-12}\) of a second. Light travels less than a millimetre in one of those. The same negative exponents that shrink fractions also name the smallest things people have ever measured.

Here is where negative powers really earn their keep. Once exponents can go negative, the product and quotient rules work across zero without any new rules. Adding exponents is just adding numbers, and some of those numbers are now below zero.

A cipher key is built as \(7^{6} \times 7^{-4}\). Use the product rule to combine it into a single power of 7, then give that power's value.
Show solution
Add the exponents straight across zero. $$7^{6} \times 7^{-4} = 7^{6 + (-4)} = 7^{2} = 49$$ The negative exponent subtracted from the positive one. The value is \(\boxed{49}\).
A camera meter sets two opposing exposure stops that multiply to \(9^{5} \times 9^{-5}\). Combine them into a single power of 9 and give its value.
Show solution
The exponents are opposites and add to zero. $$9^{5} \times 9^{-5} = 9^{5 + (-5)} = 9^{0} = 1$$ A power times its reciprocal collapses to \(\boxed{1}\).

The sign rules from negative bases meet negative exponents now, and the parentheses matter more than ever. The next problem lines up three look-alikes where only one comes out positive. Read exactly what each exponent grips and what each minus sign covers.

Exactly one of these three is a positive number. $$-2^{-4}, \qquad (-5)^{-2}, \qquad (-2)^{-3}$$ Find the one that is positive and give its value as a fraction.
Show solution
Read each one carefully. In \(-2^{-4}\) the exponent touches only the 2, so \(-2^{-4} = -\dfrac{1}{2^4} = -\dfrac{1}{16}\), which is negative. In \((-2)^{-3}\) the sign is inside but the odd power keeps it negative, \((-2)^{-3} = \dfrac{1}{(-2)^3} = -\dfrac{1}{8}\). Only \((-5)^{-2}\) is positive, because the parentheses hold the sign and an even power turns it positive, \((-5)^{-2} = \dfrac{1}{(-5)^2} = \boxed{\dfrac{1}{25}}\).
A spinning rotor multiplies its energy by 4 each round. After one round the energy is \(4^1\) units, but the formula divides by the rotor's idle reading \(4^{-3}\), giving \(\dfrac{4^1}{4^{-3}}\). Write the result as a single power of 4 and give its value.
Show solution
The quotient rule subtracts the bottom exponent, and subtracting a negative adds. $$\dfrac{4^{1}}{4^{-3}} = 4^{1 - (-3)} = 4^{4} = 256$$ Dividing by a negative power climbed the exponent. The value is \(\boxed{256}\).

Letters behave exactly like numbers here. The same exponent laws collect variable powers across zero and into the negatives, with the coefficients riding along untouched. The closer below brings the whole section together.

A growth model uses the expression \(t^{3} \times t^{-7}\). Simplify it to a single power of \(t\), written with one exponent. Give the exponent.
Show solution
Add the exponents, sign and all. $$t^{3} \times t^{-7} = t^{3 + (-7)} = t^{-4}$$ The exponent is \(\boxed{-4}\). Written without a negative, that is \(\dfrac{1}{t^4}\).
Four dials each set a magnification, all secretly powers of 3. They read $$9^{-3}, \qquad \left(\tfrac{1}{27}\right)^{2}, \qquad \left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right)^{-5}, \qquad \left(3^{2}\right)^{-4}.$$ Rewrite each as a single power of 3, then say which dial gives the smallest magnification. Give that dial's value as a power of 3, for example in the form 3^7.
Show solution
Convert every dial to base 3. First \(9^{-3} = (3^2)^{-3} = 3^{-6}\). Next \(\left(\tfrac{1}{27}\right)^{2} = 27^{-2} = (3^3)^{-2} = 3^{-6}\). Then \(\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right)^{-5}\) flips upright to \(3^{5}\). Last \(\left(3^{2}\right)^{-4} = 3^{2\times(-4)} = 3^{-8}\). Now one base rules them all, so the smallest exponent gives the smallest magnification. Among \(-6, -6, 5, -8\) the smallest is \(-8\), so the smallest dial is \(\boxed{3^{-8}}\), which equals \(\dfrac{1}{6561}\).

Review problems

Review 1. You already know the exponent ladder climbs by multiplying, and that any nonzero base raised to the zero power lands on \(1\). The questions below walk that same ladder the other way, below zero, where each step still divides by the base. Once you see what a negative exponent really asks for, a whole shelf of reciprocal tricks opens up.
Review 2. A scientist photographs a snowflake at a magnification of \(5^2\). To study a real raindrop instead, she wants the opposite, a view shrunk by that same factor, which she writes as \(5^{-2}\). Continue her ladder by hand. Starting from \(5^2=25\), each step down divides by \(5\), so \(5^1=5\) and then \(5^0=1\). Take two more steps down and give the value of \(5^{-2}\) as a fraction.
Show solution
Walking the ladder down, each rung divides by \(5\). $$5^2=25,\quad 5^1=5,\quad 5^0=1,\quad 5^{-1}=\tfrac{1}{5},\quad 5^{-2}=\tfrac{1}{25}$$ Past zero the values keep shrinking by the same factor, so a negative exponent is just a positive power waiting underneath a \(1\). $$5^{-2}=\dfrac{1}{5^2}=\boxed{\dfrac{1}{25}}$$
Review 3. The ladder never breaks at zero. For any nonzero base \(b\), one more step down from \(b^0=1\) keeps dividing, so a negative exponent means a reciprocal. $$b^{-n}=\dfrac{1}{b^n}$$ Read \(b^{-n}\) as one over \(b\) raised to the matching positive power.
Review 4. A relay tower's signal fades by a factor of \(2\) every kilometer. At the tower the strength reads \(2^3\) units. A listener sits far enough away that the strength has fallen to \(2^{-4}\) units. Written as a single fraction, how strong is the signal where the listener sits?
Show solution
A negative exponent sends the power to the denominator. $$2^{-4}=\dfrac{1}{2^4}=\boxed{\dfrac{1}{16}}$$ The lonely \(2^3\) at the start was never needed, it only set the scene. What matters is that the minus sign in the exponent means a reciprocal.
Review 5. A jeweler grades polishing grit by powers of \(7\), where a finer grit carries a smaller number. The finest cloth on the bench is labeled \(7^{-3}\). Rewrite that label with a positive exponent only, then give its value as a fraction.
Show solution
Flipping the sign of the exponent flips the power across the bar. $$7^{-3}=\dfrac{1}{7^3}=\dfrac{1}{343}$$ With a positive exponent the label reads \(\dfrac{1}{7^3}\), which equals \(\boxed{\dfrac{1}{343}}\).
Review 6. Two filters sit in a beam of light. The first passes \(4^{2}\) of every batch through, the second passes \(4^{-5}\) of whatever reaches it. Multiply the two factors and write the combined factor as a single power of \(4\).
Show solution
Same base, so multiplying adds the exponents and the minus sign rides along. $$4^{2}\times4^{-5}=4^{2+(-5)}=\boxed{4^{-3}}$$ The law that added exponents above zero keeps working straight across zero and into the negatives.
Review 7. Every law of exponents you have met still holds when an exponent goes negative. Adding exponents, subtracting them, and multiplying them all carry the minus sign along without complaint. A negative exponent is a fully grown number on the ladder, not a special case.
Review 8. A timekeeper records a tiny span as \(\dfrac{10^{2}}{10^{6}}\) of a day. Use the quotient rule to write this as a single power of \(10\), then say how many places after the decimal point its leading digit sits.
Show solution
Subtract the exponents, bottom from top. $$\dfrac{10^2}{10^6}=10^{2-6}=10^{-4}$$ That value is \(\dfrac{1}{10^4}=0.0001\), so the leading \(1\) sits in the \(\boxed{4}\)th place after the decimal point. The quotient rule lands you below zero whenever the bottom exponent is the larger one.
Review 9. This is exactly how scientists write the very small. A nanometer is \(10^{-9}\) of a meter, and a single water molecule is only a few of them across. The minus sign is doing real work, it counts how many times you divided by ten to shrink a meter down to something you could never see.
Review 10. A clockmaker winds a spring \(6\) full turns, recorded as \(9^{6}\), then unwinds it \(6\) turns, recorded as \(9^{-6}\). Combine the two as a single power of \(9\) and give the resulting value.
Show solution
Same base, so add the exponents. $$9^{6}\times9^{-6}=9^{6+(-6)}=9^{0}=\boxed{1}$$ A power and its exact opposite annihilate, landing right back on \(9^0=1\). The spring ends precisely where it began.
Review 11. A photographer halves the light at each click of a dial. One full sweep dims the light to \(\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^{5}\) of the start. Her assistant insists the same dimming can be written as \(2^{-5}\). Show they agree by giving the single fraction both equal.
Show solution
Distribute the exponent over the fraction. $$\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^5=\dfrac{1^5}{2^5}=\dfrac{1}{2^5}=2^{-5}=\dfrac{1}{32}$$ Both ways of writing it give \(\boxed{\dfrac{1}{32}}\). Raising a reciprocal to the \(n\) is the very same thing as raising the base to the \(-n\).
Review 12. Flipping the base undoes the negative sign on an exponent. $$\left(\dfrac{1}{b}\right)^{n}=b^{-n}\qquad\text{and}\qquad (b^{-1})^{n}=b^{-n}$$ A reciprocal in the base and a minus in the exponent are two names for the same flip.
Review 13. A vault dial is calibrated so that the setting \(\dfrac{8}{8^{-3}}\) opens it. Simplify that to a single power of \(8\) and give its exponent.
Show solution
Write the top as \(8^1\) and subtract the bottom exponent. Subtracting a negative adds. $$\dfrac{8}{8^{-3}}=\dfrac{8^1}{8^{-3}}=8^{1-(-3)}=8^{4}$$ So the exponent is \(\boxed{4}\). Dividing by a negative power launches the exponent up, because a power flips the sign of its exponent the moment it crosses the bar.
Review 14. In a program, a variable holds \(x^{6}\) and gets multiplied by \(x^{-2}\), then the result is multiplied once more by \(x^{-1}\). Write the final expression as a single power of \(x\).
Show solution
All one base, so the exponents simply add. $$x^{6}\times x^{-2}\times x^{-1}=x^{6+(-2)+(-1)}=x^{6-3}=\boxed{x^{3}}$$ The negative exponents pull the total down without changing how the rule works.
Review 15. Musicians live on this ladder without naming it. Drop an octave and a note's frequency is halved, which is one step of \(2^{-1}\). Drop another and you are at \(2^{-2}\). The lowest pipe on a giant organ is many such steps below middle ground, a stack of reciprocals you can feel in your chest more than hear.
Review 16. A careless student claims \(-2^{-4}\) and \((-2)^{-4}\) are equal. Work out each value exactly, then state the distance between them on the number line.
Show solution
The negative exponent makes each a reciprocal, but the sign rules split them apart. In \(-2^{-4}\) the power touches only the \(2\), so $$-2^{-4}=-\dfrac{1}{2^4}=-\dfrac{1}{16}.$$ In \((-2)^{-4}\) the parentheses keep the sign inside, and a negative base to an even power comes out positive, so $$(-2)^{-4}=\dfrac{1}{(-2)^4}=\dfrac{1}{16}.$$ They sit on opposite sides of zero, a distance of $$\dfrac{1}{16}+\dfrac{1}{16}=\dfrac{2}{16}=\boxed{\dfrac{1}{8}}$$ apart. The exponent grabs exactly what it touches, even when it is negative.
Review 17. Three lenses are labeled by how much they enlarge an image. Lens A is \(\left(\dfrac{1}{8}\right)^{-5}\), lens B is \(\left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right)^{-7}\), and lens C is \(16^{3}\). Rewrite all three as powers of \(2\) and name the single strongest lens.
Show solution
Each reciprocal base with a negative exponent flips into a plain power, then everything goes to base \(2\). $$\left(\dfrac{1}{8}\right)^{-5}=8^{5}=(2^3)^5=2^{15}$$ $$\left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right)^{-7}=4^{7}=(2^2)^7=2^{14}$$ $$16^{3}=(2^4)^3=2^{12}$$ One base now rules all three, so the biggest exponent is the strongest lens. Since \(15>14>12\), the winner is lens A, $$\left(\dfrac{1}{8}\right)^{-5}=\boxed{2^{15}}.$$ Two flips and a shared base turned a guessing game into a glance.
Review 18. Collapse this into a single power of \(2\), exponent and all. $$\dfrac{(2^{-3})^{-4}\times 4^{-5}}{8^{-1}}$$
Show solution
Send every piece to base \(2\). The power of a power multiplies exponents, and two negatives make a positive. $$(2^{-3})^{-4}=2^{(-3)\times(-4)}=2^{12}$$ Next \(4^{-5}=(2^2)^{-5}=2^{-10}\) and \(8^{-1}=(2^3)^{-1}=2^{-3}\). The numerator is $$2^{12}\times2^{-10}=2^{12+(-10)}=2^{2}.$$ Dividing by \(2^{-3}\) subtracts that exponent, and subtracting a negative adds. $$\dfrac{2^{2}}{2^{-3}}=2^{2-(-3)}=2^{5}=\boxed{2^{5}}$$ All four laws took a turn, and every minus sign behaved exactly like a number, which is the whole lesson of this section.
