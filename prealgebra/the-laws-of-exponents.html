Prealgebra · Lesson 2.2

The Laws of Exponents

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

You can always compute \(2^{13}\) by multiplying thirteen 2s in a row. But for \(2^{100}\) that approach is hopeless. There is a faster path hiding inside the exponents themselves, and finding it is what this section is about.

A strip of paper starts as a single layer. You fold it in half 3 times, creating \(2^3\) layers. Without unfolding, you fold it 4 more times. How many layers does it have now?
Show solution
After the first three folds the stack holds \(2^3 = 8\) layers. Each of the four new folds doubles every layer already there, so those rounds multiply the whole stack by \(2^4\). Two batches of doublings stacked end to end are just doublings in a row. $$2^3 \times 2^4 = 8 \times 16 = \boxed{128}$$ Count the exponents: \(3\) doublings then \(4\) more is \(7\) doublings, and \(2^7 = 128\). That is the product rule at its plainest: two runs of the same factor, one after the other, merge into one run whose length is the sum of theirs.
Key idea
Product rule. When you multiply two powers with the same base, add the exponents. $$a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$$ Counting explains it: \(m\) copies of \(a\) followed by \(n\) more copies gives \(m+n\) copies altogether.
Find the missing exponent. $$6^{\,?} \times 6^8 = 6^{15}$$
Show solution
The product rule says the two exponents add up to the combined exponent. Here the combined exponent is 15, so $$? + 8 = 15.$$ Reading that backward, \(? = 15 - 8 = \boxed{7}\). The product rule runs in both directions: forward it fuses two powers into one; backward it recovers a missing exponent from the finished product.

The product rule handles multiplication. What happens to exponents when you divide? When you cancel factors instead of collecting them, something is subtracted rather than added. The next two problems let you discover what.

Verify the subtraction pattern by cancelling factors directly. Write \(\dfrac{5^7}{5^4}\) as a fraction with every factor of 5 listed in numerator and denominator, then cancel matching pairs. What single power of 5 survives?
Show solution
Write every factor out and let matching pairs cancel. Seven 5s on top, four on the bottom: $$\frac{5^7}{5^4} = \frac{5 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 5}{5 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 \cdot 5}$$ Four cancellations wipe out four factors from the top, and three 5s are left standing. $$= 5^3 = \boxed{125}$$ The subtraction \(7 - 4 = 3\) counted the survivors exactly. The quotient rule is a shorthand for this cancellation: whatever is in the denominator is subtracted from the top exponent.
Key idea
Quotient rule. When you divide two powers with the same base, subtract the exponents. $$a^m \div a^n = a^{m-n} \quad (a \neq 0)$$ Division cancels equal factors from top and bottom, and subtraction counts what is left.
Simplify \(\dfrac{2^{13} \times 2^4}{2^{10}}\) to a single power of 2, then give its value.
Show solution
Two laws in sequence: product rule first, then quotient rule. $$\text{Numerator: } 2^{13} \times 2^4 = 2^{13+4} = 2^{17}$$ $$\text{Divide: } \frac{2^{17}}{2^{10}} = 2^{17-10} = 2^7 = \boxed{128}$$ Adding and then subtracting is all the arithmetic needed. The laws never conflict because they govern different operations: multiplying same-base powers adds the exponents, dividing them subtracts.

The product and quotient rules both involve two separate powers being combined. There is one more situation: a power raised to another power, like \((3^4)^5\). Unpacking it reveals yet another pattern waiting in the exponents.

Compute \((2^3)^4\) by first evaluating \(2^3\), then raising that result to the 4th power. You should arrive at 4096. Write 4096 as a power of 2 and look at the relationship between that final exponent and the original two exponents, 3 and 4.
Show solution
Work it out directly. \(2^3 = 8\), and \(8^4 = 4096\). Now place \(4096\) on the ladder of powers of 2: \(4096 = 2^{12}\). The final exponent is 12, and \(12 = 3 \times 4\). Four copies of \(2^3\) each contribute 3 factors of 2, so there are \(4 \times 3 = 12\) factors altogether. $$(2^3)^4 = 2^{3 \times 4} = 2^{12} = \boxed{4096}$$ A power raised to another power multiplies the two exponents, because the outer exponent calls for that many copies of the inner power, and each copy adds the inner exponent's worth of factors.
Key idea
Power rule. When you raise a power to another power, multiply the exponents. $$(a^m)^n = a^{m \times n}$$ The outer exponent calls for \(n\) copies of \(a^m\). Each copy contributes \(m\) factors of \(a\), so \(n\) copies contribute \(m \times n\) factors altogether.
Since \(64 = 2^6\), rewrite \(64^{10}\) as a single power of 2. What is the exponent?
Show solution
Since \(64 = 2^6\), the base is a power of 2 in disguise. Substituting that disguise stacks a second exponent on top, and stacked exponents multiply. $$64^{10} = (2^6)^{10} = 2^{6 \times 10} = 2^{60}$$ The exponent is \(\boxed{60}\). Swapping an unfamiliar base for a power of a known base and multiplying the exponents is the core move whenever two different-looking numbers are secretly built from the same one.
A square floor is tiled with square tiles. Each tile has a side length of \(4^2\) centimetres, and the floor has \(4^3\) tiles along each side. Find the area of the floor in square centimetres, expressed as \(4^n\). What is \(n\)?
Show solution
Build the side length before squaring the floor. Each tile contributes \(4^2\) cm to one side, and \(4^3\) tiles run across that side, so the product rule finds the total width. $$4^3 \times 4^2 = 4^{3+2} = 4^5 \text{ cm per side}$$ The floor is a square, so its area is that side length squared. Now the power rule fires. $$(4^5)^2 = 4^{5 \times 2} = 4^{10} \text{ sq cm}$$ The exponent is \(n = \boxed{10}\). Product rule first, then power rule, each handling the operation it was built for.

The three rules so far all require the same base throughout. But a power can also sit over a product, like \((2 \times 5)^3\). The next problem asks whether the exponent distributes over the multiplication or not.

Compute \((2 \times 5)^3\) two ways: first as \(10^3\), then as \(2^3 \times 5^3\). Do you get the same answer?
Show solution
Compute the same product both ways and watch them agree. Collapsing the base first: \(2 \times 5 = 10\), and \(10^3 = 1000\). Distributing the exponent instead: \(2^3 \times 5^3 = 8 \times 125 = 1000\). $$(2 \times 5)^3 = 2^3 \times 5^3 = \boxed{1000}$$ Both routes land on 1000. The outer exponent can drop onto each factor separately because cubing the pair is the same as pairing the cubes, and that equivalence is exactly what the power-of-a-product rule captures.
Key idea
Power of a product. An exponent distributes over multiplication. $$(ab)^n = a^n \times b^n$$ This works because \((ab)^n\) means \(n\) copies of the pair \(a \cdot b\), and you can regroup to gather all the \(a\)s together and all the \(b\)s together.
Find the positive integer \(n\) such that \((9^n)^4 = 9^{20}\).
Show solution
The power rule turns a stacked power into one by multiplying the exponents. $$(9^n)^4 = 9^{n \times 4}$$ For that to equal \(9^{20}\) the exponents must match. $$n \times 4 = 20 \implies n = \boxed{5}$$ Reading the power rule in reverse: if the outer exponent is 4 and the result carries exponent 20, the inner exponent must have been \(20 \div 4\). The law runs forward and backward.
Compute \(6^4 \div (2^4 \times 3^4)\) without multiplying anything out.
Show solution
The key is recognising \(6 = 2 \times 3\). The power-of-a-product rule distributes the outer exponent across the multiplication. $$6^4 = (2 \times 3)^4 = 2^4 \times 3^4$$ So the numerator is identical to the denominator. $$6^4 \div (2^4 \times 3^4) = \frac{2^4 \times 3^4}{2^4 \times 3^4} = \boxed{1}$$ Any nonzero expression over an identical copy of itself is 1. The trick was seeing that \(6^4\) was already hiding its own factors inside.

You now have four tools: add exponents when multiplying same-base powers, subtract when dividing, multiply when raising a power to a power, and distribute when a power sits over a product. The last two problems combine all four.

Simplify \(\dfrac{(3^5)^4}{3^{11} \times 3^5}\) to a single power of 3, then give its value.
Show solution
Three laws, each handling one operation. $$\text{Power rule (numerator): }(3^5)^4 = 3^{5 \times 4} = 3^{20}$$ $$\text{Product rule (denominator): }3^{11} \times 3^5 = 3^{11+5} = 3^{16}$$ $$\text{Quotient rule: }\frac{3^{20}}{3^{16}} = 3^{20-16} = 3^4 = \boxed{81}$$ Each law handled its own operation without interfering with the others. The skill is seeing which law belongs to which part of the expression before touching any arithmetic.
Compute $$\frac{6^{20} \times 4^{15}}{8^{10} \times 3^{20}}.$$ The result is a power of 2. What is the exponent?
Show solution
Send every base to its prime factors before touching any law. $$6^{20} = (2 \times 3)^{20} = 2^{20} \times 3^{20}$$ $$4^{15} = (2^2)^{15} = 2^{30}, \qquad 8^{10} = (2^3)^{10} = 2^{30}$$ Separate the powers of 2 from the powers of 3 and simplify each strand. $$\frac{2^{20} \times 3^{20} \times 2^{30}}{2^{30} \times 3^{20}} = \frac{2^{50} \times 3^{20}}{2^{30} \times 3^{20}}$$ The 3s cancel entirely, and \(2^{50} \div 2^{30} = 2^{20}\). The exponent is \(\boxed{20}\). Four different bases, one unifying move: reduce everything to prime factors first, then let the laws handle the rest.

Review problems

Review 1. A kaleidoscope adds mirrors in two stages. The first tube holds \(4\) mirrors, and each mirror doubles the number of visible copies of a bead, so the tube shows \(2^4\) copies. You twist on a second tube of \(6\) more doubling mirrors. Written as a single power of \(2\), how many copies of the bead do you see through the whole instrument?
Show solution
Each mirror doubles, so the first tube multiplies by \(2^4\) and the second by \(2^6\). Sending one bead through both means doubling \(4\) times, then \(6\) more, which is \(10\) doublings all told. Multiplying powers of the same base adds the exponents. $$2^4\times2^6=2^{4+6}=\boxed{2^{10}}$$ That is \(1024\) copies, a number you never had to build by hand.
Review 2. That \(2^{10}=1024\) is the reason a kilobyte is not quite \(1000\) bytes. Computers count in doublings, and ten doublings overshoot a thousand by just \(24\). Engineers shrugged, borrowed the word kilo anyway, and the small fib has stuck ever since.
Review 3. A library's main hall holds \(3^9\) books and a small reading room holds \(3^5\) books. The hall holds how many times as many books as the room? Give your answer as a power of \(3\).
Show solution
Asking how many times as many is asking for a quotient. $$\dfrac{3^9}{3^5}=3^{9-5}=3^4=\boxed{81}$$ Dividing powers of the same base subtracts the exponents, so the towering \(3^9\) never has to be written out.
Review 4. Two identical servers each store \(2^{19}\) files. Put together, they store how many files in total? Write the total as a single power of \(2\). Careful, the answer is not \(4^{19}\).
Show solution
Two equal stacks add to twice one stack. $$2^{19}+2^{19}=2\times2^{19}=\boxed{2^{20}}$$ Doubling nudges the exponent up by one and leaves the base alone. The base stays \(2\); it never turns into \(4\).
Review 5. Watch which operation you are in. Multiplying two copies of \(2^{19}\) adds the exponents and lands far off at \(2^{38}\). Adding two copies only doubles. When equal powers are added you pull the number of copies out in front, so \(2^{19}+2^{19}=2\times2^{19}\), and only because that leading \(2\) matches the base does the exponent climb to \(2^{20}\).
Review 6. A microscope magnifies by \(10^3\) at one setting. To study a single cell you stack that same setting \(4\) times in a row, each stage feeding its image into the next, for a magnification of \((10^3)^4\). Write that as a single power of \(10\).
Show solution
Four stages each multiply by \(10^3\), so the total is \(10^3\) multiplied by itself four times, which is \((10^3)^4\). A power of a power multiplies the exponents. $$(10^3)^4=10^{3\times4}=\boxed{10^{12}}$$ A trillion-fold, written without a single zero.
Review 7. A square mosaic is \(6\) tiles wide and \(6\) tiles tall. An artist refines it so that every tile is rebuilt as a \(5\)-by-\(5\) block of smaller tiles. How many small tiles fill the finished mosaic? Find it by writing the count as \((6\times5)^2\) and letting the exponent land on each factor.
Show solution
Each side once spanned \(6\) tiles, and every tile is now \(5\) small tiles across, so a side runs \(6\times5=30\) small tiles. The mosaic holds \((6\times5)^2\) of them, and an exponent on a product drops onto each factor. $$(6\times5)^2=6^2\times5^2=36\times25=\boxed{900}$$ The same square, counted two ways, agrees.
Review 8. Three gears feed one counter. Gear A multiplies it by \(8\), gear B by \(4\), and gear C by \(2\). You crank gear A three times, gear B twice, and gear C once. By what single power of \(2\) has the counter grown?
Show solution
Rewrite every gear in base \(2\). Cranking them gives $$8^3\times4^2\times2=(2^3)^3\times(2^2)^2\times2^1=2^9\times2^4\times2^1.$$ Now there is one base, so add the exponents, \(9+4+1=14\), for \(\boxed{2^{14}}\). Three different-looking gears were the same gear in disguise.
Review 9. That is the move worth keeping. When the bases look different but are secretly powers of one number, rewrite every piece in that one base. The moment a single base rules the expression, the laws take over and the whole thing folds down to one tidy power.
Review 10. Two students simplify the same thing and get opposite signs. One writes \((-2)^4\times2^3\), the other writes \(-2^4\times2^3\). Work out each value, then say how far apart the two results sit.
Show solution
The parentheses decide everything. In \((-2)^4\) the sign is inside the power, and four negatives make a positive, so \((-2)^4=16\). In \(-2^4\) the exponent touches only the \(2\), and the minus waits outside, so \(-2^4=-(16)=-16\). Multiplying each by \(2^3=8\), $$16\times8=128,\qquad -16\times8=-128.$$ They sit \(128+128=\boxed{256}\) apart. The exponent grabs exactly what it touches, no more.
Review 11. A puzzle claims that \(4^{12}\) and \(8^8\) are exactly the same size. Settle it by rewriting each as a power of \(2\). What single power of \(2\) do they share?
Show solution
Send both to base \(2\). $$4^{12}=(2^2)^{12}=2^{24},\qquad 8^8=(2^3)^8=2^{24}$$ Same base, same exponent, so the puzzle is right, both equal \(\boxed{2^{24}}\). Forcing a shared base turns a staring contest into a single glance.
Review 12. A number is built as \(2^6\times5^9\). How many digits does it have once it is written out in full?
Show solution
A \(2\) and a \(5\) make a \(10\), but here the \(5\)s outnumber the \(2\)s. The six \(2\)s pair with six \(5\)s, leaving \(5^3\) on its own. $$2^6\times5^9=(2^6\times5^6)\times5^3=10^6\times125=125000000$$ That is \(125\) trailing six zeros, so \(\boxed{9}\) digits in all.
Review 13. Every trailing zero on a whole number is a hidden \(10\), and every \(10\) is a \(2\) holding hands with a \(5\). That is the whole secret behind round numbers. To learn how many zeros end a giant product, you do not multiply it out, you just count how many \(2\)-and-\(5\) pairs hide inside.
Review 14. Collapse this tower into a single power of \(2\). $$\dfrac{8^6\times16^3}{4^5\times2^4}$$
Show solution
Send every base to \(2\). On top, \(8^6=(2^3)^6=2^{18}\) and \(16^3=(2^4)^3=2^{12}\), so the numerator is \(2^{18}\times2^{12}=2^{30}\). On the bottom, \(4^5=(2^2)^5=2^{10}\) and \(2^4=2^4\), so the denominator is \(2^{10}\times2^4=2^{14}\). Dividing same bases subtracts the exponents. $$\dfrac{2^{30}}{2^{14}}=2^{30-14}=\boxed{2^{16}}$$ A four-story tower flattened to one power, \(65536\), and all four laws took a turn.
Review 15. This sum refuses to merge, because the powers are not equal. $$2^{10}+2^{11}+2^{12}$$ Find its exact value.
Show solution
Equal powers can be added in a single step, but these climb. The fix is to factor out the smallest power they all contain. $$2^{10}+2^{11}+2^{12}=2^{10}(1+2+4)=2^{10}\times7=1024\times7=\boxed{7168}$$ Pulling out the lowest power is the move whenever a sum of powers will not collapse on its own.
Review 16. Which is larger, \(2^{40}\) or \(3^{30}\)? Decide with no calculator by giving both a shared exponent.
Show solution
When bases differ, a shared exponent works as well as a shared base. Both exponents are multiples of \(10\), so factor a \(10\) out of each with the power-of-a-power rule. $$2^{40}=(2^4)^{10}=16^{10},\qquad 3^{30}=(3^3)^{10}=27^{10}$$ The exponents match, so the larger base wins. Since \(27>16\), the bigger number is \(\boxed{3^{30}}\).
