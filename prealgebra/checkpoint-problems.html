Prealgebra · Lesson 1.4

Checkpoint Problems

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Three lessons, three toolkits: reordering and pairing sums, taming signs, and multiplication with its distributive bridge. Time to mix them. The problems below carry no labels: nothing announces which tool a problem wants, and several want two at once. That is the point: real problems never tell you which chapter they came from. Read slowly, look for structure first, and trust that every one of these cracks open without paper.

For 14 days, a bakery celebrates its birthday: on day 1 it gives away 1 free roll, on day 2 it gives 2, and so on up to 14 rolls on day 14, and it runs the same giveaway at each of its $5$ branches. How many rolls does the whole chain give away?
Show solution
Pair the days, \(1 + 14, 2 + 13, \ldots, 7 + 8\), into seven 15s, so \(7 \times 15 = 105\) rolls per branch. Then \(5 \times 105 = \boxed{525}\).
Zoe rings up six items on Monday costing $41, 52, 63, 74, 85, 96$. On Tuesday she rings up $39, 50, 61, 72, 83, 94$. Without finding either total, work out how much more Monday’s bill is than Tuesday’s.
Show solution
Match the lists item by item: every Monday item costs 2 more, and there are six items, so Monday’s bill is \(6 \times 2 = \boxed{12}\) more. Neither total was needed.
A freight scale reports that crate A outweighs crate B by $9$ kg; that is, \(A - B = 9\). The mover wants the comparison the other way around: what is \(B - A\)?
Show solution
Swapping the order of a subtraction takes the opposite: \(B - A = -(A - B) = \boxed{-9}\).
Key idea
None of those answers needed a grand total. Matching two lists term by term, or flipping a difference to its opposite, replaces a page of arithmetic with a single observation. Hunt for structure before you start to grind.
A puzzle: split the numbers $3, 5, 8, 0$ into two pairs, multiply inside each pair, then add the two results. What is the largest total you can reach?
Show solution
Zero erases whatever it multiplies, so pair it with the smallest number. Pairing 0 with 3 leaves \(5 \times 8 = 40\), then \(40 + 0 = \boxed{40}\). The other pairings give only 15 and 24.
Compute $$(445 - (4 \times 111)) \times 777.$$
Show solution
Inside, \(4 \times 111 = 444\), so the parentheses hold \(445 - 444 = 1\), and the whole expression is \(1 \times 777 = \boxed{777}\). A big expression can hide a factor of 1.
Twins Ria and Kio race through a worksheet. Ria computes \(46 \times 45\); Kio computes \(46 \times 44 + 46\). Their teacher asks for Ria’s answer minus Kio’s. Find it the lazy way.
Show solution
Kio’s \(46 \times 44 + 46\) is forty-four 46s plus one more, which is forty-five 46s, exactly Ria’s \(46 \times 45\). The two are equal, so their difference is \(\boxed{0}\).
Key idea
Big expressions often hide a tiny secret: a factor equal to 0 or 1, or two products that are secretly identical. Thirty seconds of X-ray vision can beat ten minutes of multiplying. Make “what is this expression really saying?” your first question, not your last.
A frog sits at 0 on a number line. It makes $8$ jumps of \(+5\), then $6$ jumps of \(-7\). Where does the frog land?
Show solution
The jumps are two products: \(8 \times 5 = 40\) forward, then \(6 \times (-7) = -42\) back. The frog lands at \(40 + (-42) = \boxed{-2}\).
Compute $$18 \times 7 + 18 \times 21 + 18 \times 12.$$
Show solution
All three products share the 18, so factor it out: \(18 \times (7 + 21 + 12) = 18 \times 40 = \boxed{720}\).
Compute $$(294 + 295 + 296 + \cdots + 306) - (13 \times 294).$$
Show solution
Each number from 294 to 306 is 294 plus a small excess: \(294 + 0, 294 + 1, \ldots, 294 + 12\). Subtracting \(13 \times 294\) strips the 294 from every term, leaving \(1 + 2 + \cdots + 12\), which pairs into six 13s: \(\boxed{78}\).
Two siblings argue. Asha says \(399 \times 501\) and \(400 \times 500\) must be equal, “because you just move a 1 from one number to the other.” Settle it: compute the larger minus the smaller.
Show solution
Peel a layer off each: \(399 \times 501 = 399 \times 500 + 399\) and \(400 \times 500 = 399 \times 500 + 500\). They share the same bulk, so \(400 \times 500\) is larger by \(500 - 399 = \boxed{101}\).
Key idea
Look back: collapsing three products, stripping thirteen copies of 294 out of a long sum, peeling a layer off 399 × 501. Every one was the distributive bridge $a(b+c)=ab+ac$ worn a different way. Whenever a problem mixes × with + or −, ask where the bridge is before you compute anything.
Compute $$64 \times 36 - 63 \times 35$$ without long multiplication. (Careful: no factor is shared… yet.)
Show solution
Write \(64 \times 36 = (63 + 1) \times 36 = 63 \times 36 + 36\). Then \(63 \times 36 + 36 - 63 \times 35 = 63 \times (36 - 35) + 36 = 63 + 36 = \boxed{99}\). When no shared factor exists, build one.

If any of those three still feels wobbly, revisit its lesson before moving on. Up next is the last of the four operations, division, and it will fall to the same playbook.
