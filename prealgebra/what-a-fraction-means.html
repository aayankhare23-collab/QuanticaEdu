Prealgebra · Lesson 4.1

What a Fraction Means

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Every division so far came out clean. Now split \(3\) bars of clay among \(4\) friends and whole numbers stall, since handing out one bar each leaves the fourth friend empty-handed. Yet a fair share clearly exists between the whole-number marks, and that share must be a new kind of number.

Two campers share one strip of dried mango by making a single cut at the middle, giving each person an equal piece. The amount one camper holds is \(1\div 2\). Write that share as a fraction \(a/b\).
Show solution
The hidden move here is that a division and a fraction are the very same thing wearing two different outfits. When the campers ask "one strip shared fairly between two people," they are asking \(1 \div 2\), and the finished answer to that division is what we call the fraction. Watch what the single cut does. Before the cut there is 1 whole strip. The cut at the middle turns it into 2 pieces that are exactly equal, so neither camper can complain that the other got more. One camper walks away holding exactly 1 of those 2 equal parts. That sentence, 1 of 2 equal parts, is precisely what the notation \(a/b\) is built to record. The bottom number tells you how many equal parts the whole was broken into, which is \(2\). The top number tells you how many of those parts you are holding, which is \(1\). Stacking them gives \(\frac{1}{2}\). So \(1 \div 2\) does not stay an unfinished question with a remainder hanging off the end. Its answer is one clean number, and that number is $$1 \div 2 = \frac{1}{2}.$$ The share each camper holds is \(\boxed{1/2}\) of the strip.
Three clay bars are shared equally among \(4\) friends. Slice every bar into \(4\) equal strips, deal one strip from each bar to each friend. Write each friend's share as a fraction \(a/b\).
Show solution
The trick is to make every piece the same size so sharing becomes plain counting. Cutting each of the \(3\) bars into \(4\) equal strips turns the whole problem into strips, with \(3 \times 4 = 12\) of them, and every strip is \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) of a bar. \nNow share. There are \(12\) strips and \(4\) friends, so each friend gets \(12 \div 4 = 3\) strips, and nothing is wasted because \(12\) splits evenly. One way to see those \(3\) strips is that each friend pulls exactly one strip out of each bar, one from the first, one from the second, one from the third. \nSo one friend's pile is \(3\) strips, each worth \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) of a bar, which adds up to $$\tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{3}{4}.$$ \nThat is the whole point. The question \(3 \div 4\) does not get stuck with a leftover. The answer is a single amount, three quarters of a bar, and the bar in \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) is just the division sign in disguise. \(3\) shared among \(4\) is \(\boxed{3/4}\).
A workbench has \(5\) equal color bands; blue tape covers \(2\) of them. The \(5\) counts equal pieces in the whole; the \(2\) counts how many the tape covers. Write the fraction as \(a/b\).
Show solution
The whole bench has been cut into \(5\) equal bands, and those \(5\) bands rebuild it exactly. That word equal is doing the heavy lifting. Because every band is the same size, one band is a fair, honest fifth of the bench. So the \(5\) is not really counting tape, it is setting the size of a piece. It answers the question how many equal pieces make one whole, and the answer is \(5\), so each piece is one fifth. Now the \(2\) does a completely different job. It does not chop anything or set any size. It simply counts. The tape sits on \(2\) of those fifth-sized bands and not a sliver more, so we have \(2\) copies of one fifth. Putting the count on top of the size, the tape covers \(2\) pieces, each of size one fifth, which is $$\frac{2}{5}.$$ Notice the two roles. The bottom number, \(5\), built the pieces by cutting the whole into equal parts. The top number, \(2\), grabbed some of them. Same numbers, two honest jobs. \(\boxed{2/5}\)
A chocolatier pours \(7\) scoops across \(4\) trays. Each tray holds \(7\div 4\), and the same amount can be found by counting quarter-parts on one tray. Write that amount as a fraction \(a/b\).
Show solution
Watch the two helpers carefully, because they are about to land on the exact same number from two directions.

The first helper, sharing. She has 7 equal scoops and pours them fairly across 4 trays. One tray's amount is whatever you get from \(7\) shared among \(4\), which is \(7 \div 4\). That is the meaning of the fraction \(\tfrac{7}{4}\) right away. Sharing \(a\) things among \(b\) groups gives \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) in each group, so here each tray holds \(\tfrac{7}{4}\).

The second helper, counting quarter parts. She declares one tray's worth to be the whole and slices that whole into 4 equal pieces, each of size \(\tfrac14\). Now she deals these quarter parts onto one tray until all 7 scoops are used up. How many quarter parts is that? Each of the 7 scoops, cut into quarters, becomes 4 quarter parts, so there are \(7 \times 4 = 28\) quarter parts in all. Spread fairly over the 4 trays, a single tray collects \(28 \div 4 = 7\) of them. So one tray holds 7 quarter parts, and 7 pieces each of size \(\tfrac14\) is

$$7 \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{7}{4}.$$

The collision. The first helper read \(\tfrac{7}{4}\) as "cut nothing, just share 7 among 4." The second read it as "cut the whole into 4 and take 7 of those pieces." They argued, but they were always describing the same caramel, and both readings give \(7 \div 4 = \tfrac74\). Notice this tray holds more than one whole, since \(\tfrac74\) is past 1, which is exactly why both helpers had to deal out extra quarter parts beyond a single full tray.

One tray holds \(\boxed{7/4}\).
Key idea
Fraction as division. A fraction \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) is one number, the exact answer to \(a\div b\), and the bar in the middle is the divide sign doing the work. It is never a division stuck halfway. You can read it two ways and they always land on the same spot. Cut one whole into \(b\) equal pieces and keep \(a\) of them, or take \(a\) whole things and split them fairly among \(b\). Same number both times, and since that number is a real fair share with a real size, it sits at one definite point between the whole numbers.
Key idea
Numerator and denominator. The bottom number names the size of each piece, since it says how many equal pieces one whole was cut into, so we call it the denominator. The top number counts how many of those pieces you actually took, so we call it the numerator. The bar between them is a hidden divide sign, which is why \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) really means \(a\div b\). And the surprising part holds true. A bigger denominator makes each piece smaller, not bigger, because cutting the whole into more pieces leaves less in every one.
A beetle starts at \(0\) and hops toward \(1\) in equal steps, landing exactly on \(1\) after \(6\) hops. Write the beetle's position after a single hop as a fraction \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
Call the length of one hop \(h\). The beetle makes \(6\) hops of length \(h\) and lands exactly on \(1\), so those \(6\) equal lengths must rebuild the whole distance from \(0\) to \(1\). That single fact pins everything down. $$6 \times h = 1.$$ So \(h\) is the number that stacks up \(6\) times to make \(1\). There is only one such number, and we give it a name and a symbol. We call it one sixth and write it \(\tfrac{1}{6}\). The \(6\) underneath records that the trip to \(1\) was cut into \(6\) equal steps, and the \(1\) on top records that the beetle has taken just one of those steps.

Notice what this does. The hop is not a thing sitting beside the line, it is a place on the line. After one hop the beetle is resting at the point \(\tfrac{1}{6}\), the very first mark you would make if you were building a ruler for this trip. The number \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) is the home of that point. So the beetle's position after a single hop is $$\boxed{1/6}.$$
The beetle hops in steps of \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) and makes \(5\) hops. It ends up past the halfway mark but short of \(1\). Write the beetle's position after \(5\) hops as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
Splitting the path from \(0\) to \(1\) into \(6\) equal hops fixes the step size at \(\tfrac{1}{6}\). That denominator is doing one job, it sets how big a single step is. The numerator does the other job, it counts the steps. After \(5\) hops the beetle has taken \(5\) steps, each of size \(\tfrac{1}{6}\), so its position is $$5 \times \tfrac{1}{6} = \tfrac{5}{6}.$$ The second reading agrees. The beetle has covered \(5\) of the \(6\) equal pieces of the path, which is exactly the number you get by dividing \(5\) by \(6\), namely \(\tfrac{5}{6}\). Both readings land on the same spot, which is the whole point of writing it as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\). The sanity check holds too. Halfway is \(3\) steps, written \(\tfrac{3}{6}\), and \(5\) steps is more than \(3\) but fewer than the full \(6\). So the beetle sits past the halfway mark and still short of \(1\), which is just where \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) should be. \(\boxed{5/6}\)
Key idea
Reading a fraction on the number line. The two numbers in \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) do two different jobs, best seen as a walk along the line. The denominator sets your step size, chopping the stretch from \(0\) to \(1\) into \(b\) equal pieces each one unit fraction \(\tfrac{1}{b}\) wide, so a bigger bottom means smaller steps. The numerator just counts. Starting at \(0\), you walk out \(a\) of those steps, and the spot where you stop is \(\tfrac{a}{b}\). The denominator builds the ruler, the numerator counts the marks, and that one move places any fraction you will ever meet.
Walk \(\tfrac{7}{4}\): four quarter-steps reach \(1\), then \(3\) more land between \(1\) and \(2\). Enter the larger whole number \(\tfrac{7}{4}\) falls between.
Show solution
The trick is to stop seeing \(\tfrac{7}{4}\) as one frozen thing and start seeing it as a count of steps. The denominator \(4\) sets the size of each step, \(\tfrac{1}{4}\). The numerator \(7\) sets how many of those steps you take from \(0\). So \(\tfrac{7}{4}\) is just the place you land after walking $$\underbrace{\tfrac14+\tfrac14+\tfrac14+\tfrac14}_{\text{4 steps}}+\underbrace{\tfrac14+\tfrac14+\tfrac14}_{\text{3 steps}}.$$ Watch when you cross the whole numbers. Whole numbers are exactly the places where the quarter steps fill up perfectly, and that happens every \(4\) steps, since \(\tfrac{4}{4}=1\) and \(\tfrac{8}{4}=2\). Your \(7\) steps clear the first checkpoint at step \(4\), putting you on \(1\). The next checkpoint, the whole number \(2\), lives at step \(8\). You only have \(7\) steps, which is one short of \(8\), so you finish past \(1\) but before \(2\). That pins \(\tfrac{7}{4}\) strictly between the consecutive whole numbers \(1\) and \(2\). The larger of the two is \(\boxed{2}\).
Fun fact
The bar in a fraction is the vinculum, introduced by al-Hassar in the 1100s and spread by Fibonacci. It makes \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) both the question (\(3\div 4\)) and the finished answer at once.
A machine lights a lamp when the top divided by \(8\) is a whole number. It tries \(\frac{8}{8}\), \(\frac{24}{8}\), \(\frac{40}{8}\), \(\frac{52}{8}\). How many tops light the lamp?
Show solution
A fraction \(\frac{a}{8}\) is just the answer to the question, how many whole groups of \(8\) fit into \(a\), and the lamp lights only when the answer is a clean whole number. That happens exactly when \(8\) divides \(a\) with no remainder, which is the same divisibility idea from the last chapter. So I check each top against the multiples of \(8\), which are \(8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, 56\), without ever doing a real division. \(8\) is \(8 \times 1\), so \(\frac{8}{8} = 1\). Clean, lamp on. \(24\) is \(8 \times 3\), so \(\frac{24}{8} = 3\). Clean, lamp on. \(40\) is \(8 \times 5\), so \(\frac{40}{8} = 5\). Clean, lamp on. \(52\) sits between \(48 = 8 \times 6\) and \(56 = 8 \times 7\), so it is not a multiple of \(8\). In fact \(52 = 48 + 4\), leaving a remainder of \(4\), so \(\frac{52}{8}\) is not a whole number. Lamp off. \nThree of the four tops are multiples of \(8\), so three lamps light. The point worth keeping is that a fraction is secretly a whole number exactly when the bottom divides the top, which turns a question about fractions straight back into a question about divisors. \(\boxed{3}\)
Key idea
Fractions and whole numbers. Since \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) means \(a\) steps each of size \(\tfrac{1}{b}\), you land right on a whole-number tick only when those steps fill complete groups of \(b\) with nothing left over, and that is exactly when \(b\) divides \(a\) evenly. So this is the same divisibility from last chapter seen from a new seat. When the bottom divides the top, the fraction is a whole number in disguise parked on a labeled tick. When it does not, the fraction is a brand new number resting between the ticks. Either way you can tell which you have just by asking whether the bottom divides the top.
A diver descends \(7\) kicks of \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) m, landing at \(-\tfrac{7}{3}\) m between two whole marks. Which whole number is deeper? Enter it with its minus sign.
Show solution
Each kick is \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) of a meter and they all point the same way, straight down, so seven kicks land her at \(-\tfrac{7}{3}\). The whole idea is that a negative fraction is just the same sized step walked backward across \(0\), so I can count the steps in groups instead of guessing. \nThirds clump into whole meters three at a time, since \(\tfrac{3}{3}=1\). Splitting the seven thirds that way gives $$-\frac{7}{3} = -\frac{3}{3} - \frac{3}{3} - \frac{1}{3} = -1 - 1 - \frac{1}{3}.$$\nThe first six thirds carry her down to exactly \(-2\), a clean whole meter mark. Then one leftover third nudges her a bit deeper, so she rests at \(-2-\tfrac{1}{3}\), which is just below \(-2\) but not all the way to \(-3\). \nThat pins her between the marks \(-2\) and \(-3\). On the left side of \(0\) the numbers grow in size as they go down, so the deeper mark, the one farther from the surface, is the more negative one. The shallower neighbor is \(-2\) and the deeper neighbor is \(-3\). The deeper whole meter mark is $$\boxed{-3}.$$
Ava: \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). Ben: \(\tfrac{12}{-5}\). Cleo: \(-\!\left(\tfrac{12}{5}\right)\). All claim the same point left of \(0\). How many distinct points do they name?
Show solution
First nail down where the chalk really is. Twelve backward steps of size \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) means \(12\) copies of \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) measured to the left, so the mark sits at \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). Everything below is a test of whether each student's rule lands there too. Ava: \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). She put the minus in front of the whole fraction. Reading it as a position, that is \(\tfrac{12}{5}\) units to the left of \(0\), which is exactly the mark. Landing spot \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). Ben: \(\tfrac{12}{-5}\). He buried the minus in the bottom. His picture is honest. One step is \(\tfrac{1}{-5}\), a single fifth that points backward, and he takes \(12\) of them. Twelve backward fifths land at \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). The arithmetic agrees: \(\tfrac{12}{-5}=\tfrac{-12}{5}=-\tfrac{12}{5}\), since flipping the sign of the bottom flips the sign of the whole quotient. Landing spot \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). Cleo: \(-\left(\tfrac{12}{5}\right)\). She walks to \(\tfrac{12}{5}\) on the right, then reflects across \(0\). Reflecting a point across \(0\) just swaps its sign, so \(\tfrac{12}{5}\) becomes \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). Landing spot \(-\tfrac{12}{5}\). \nThree rules, three honest trips, and all three stop on the same chalk mark. The minus sign has three legal homes, out front, on top, or on the bottom, and a single home on the bottom or the top moves the same point. None of those moves changes where you land. So the three expressions name one and the same point. $$-\frac{12}{5}=\frac{-12}{5}=\frac{12}{-5}=-\left(\frac{12}{5}\right)$$ \nThe count of distinct points is \(\boxed{1}\).
Key idea
Sign placement in a fraction. A fraction has just one minus sign to spend, and it does not care which of three seats it takes. Up top as \(\tfrac{-a}{b}\), down bottom as \(\tfrac{a}{-b}\), or out front as \(-\tfrac{a}{b}\), every version lands on the very same point to the left of \(0\). It is one number wearing its sign in a different chair, not three different numbers. The clean habit is to pull the minus out front so the top and bottom stay positive. The only way a sign actually changes the answer is if you use two of them, since a negative on top and a negative on bottom cancel and walk you back to the positive side.

Pin the numerator and let the denominator roam over \(1,2,3,\dots\); count how many choices land on a whole number. That counting question is divisor-counting from last chapter, now seen through fractions.

A pogo stick hops \(\tfrac{1}{8}\) m; a flag marks every whole-meter landing from hop \(1\) to \(40\). How many of the \(40\) hops plant a flag?
Show solution
After \(n\) hops the pogo stick has traveled \(n\) copies of \(\tfrac{1}{8}\), so it rests at position \(\tfrac{n}{8}\) of a meter. A flag is planted only when this lands on a whole number, and \(\tfrac{n}{8}\) is a whole number exactly when \(n\) splits cleanly into groups of \(8\) with nothing left over. In other words, \(n\) has to be a multiple of \(8\). So the question quietly turns into a counting question. How many multiples of \(8\) are there between \(1\) and \(40\)? They are \(8, 16, 24, 32, 40\), which are \(8 \times 1, 8 \times 2, 8 \times 3, 8 \times 4, 8 \times 5\). Each one matches a counting number from \(1\) up to \(5\), and the largest, \(40\), is the last hop counted, so it just makes the cut. A quick way to see the total without listing is to divide. The biggest multiple of \(8\) that fits inside \(40\) is \(40\) itself, and \(40 \div 8 = 5\), so there are \(5\) such multiples. The flags rise at the \(8\)th, \(16\)th, \(24\)th, \(32\)nd, and \(40\)th touchdowns. $$\boxed{5}$$
Machine places \(\tfrac{60}{n}\) for \(n=1\) to \(50\); green light when result is whole. One divisor of \(60\) exceeds \(50\). How many values of \(n\) light the lamp?
Show solution
The green light is a divisor detector. The marker \(\frac{60}{n}\) lands on a whole number exactly when \(n\) divides \(60\) with no remainder, so I just need the divisors of \(60\) that fall inside the range \(1\) to \(50\). The clean way to find every divisor is to hunt in pairs, since divisors of \(60\) always come matched so that the two of them multiply to \(60\). I walk \(n\) upward and write down each partner I find. $$1 \times 60, \quad 2 \times 30, \quad 3 \times 20, \quad 4 \times 15, \quad 5 \times 12, \quad 6 \times 10.$$ After \(6\) the next candidate \(7\) does not divide \(60\), \(8\) does not, \(9\) does not, and \(10\) is already in my list as the partner of \(6\). That is the signal to stop. Pulling out both numbers from every pair gives the full set of divisors. $$1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 5,\ 6,\ 10,\ 12,\ 15,\ 20,\ 30,\ 60.$$ That is \(12\) divisors in all. Now the careful part. The machine only ever tries \(n\) from \(1\) to \(50\), so any divisor larger than \(50\) never gets fed in and its light never blinks. Scanning the list, exactly one number breaks the ceiling, which is \(60\) itself. The reason it pokes out is the pairing. Its partner is \(1\), the smallest possible divisor, so \(60\) is forced to be the largest, and a large enough divisor can land beyond the range while its tiny partner stays safely inside. So I drop \(60\) and keep the other eleven. $$12 - 1 = 11.$$ Eleven values of \(n\) turn the green light on. $$\boxed{11}$$
Key idea
Summary. A fraction \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) is an address on the number line, the single number you land on when you split \(a\) into \(b\) equal pieces and count out. The bottom sets how big one step is, the top says how many steps to walk from \(0\), and a minus sign anywhere only flips the direction you walk. The address sits right on a whole number exactly when the bottom divides the top, the same divisibility from last chapter, so asking how many whole \(n\) make \(\tfrac{60}{n}\) whole is really asking how many divisors \(60\) has. And here is the strange part. One step of size \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) lands on the same spot as two steps of size \(\tfrac{1}{4}\), so \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) and \(\tfrac{2}{4}\) are two different-looking fractions pointing at one address.

Review problems

Review 1. A spool is clamped at \(9\) evenly spaced points, cutting it into \(9\) equal lengths. The share-reading for one length is the answer to \(1\div 9\). Write it as a fraction \(a/b\).
Show solution
A fraction is just a way to record the answer to a division. When you split \(1\) whole coil into \(9\) equal pieces, each piece is \(1 \div 9\), and that division is exactly what the fraction \(\frac{1}{9}\) means. The top number, \(1\), is the one whole coil you began with. The bottom number, \(9\), is the number of equal pieces the clamps made. So the length of wire at one clamp is $$1 \div 9 = \frac{1}{9}.$$ The share reading at one clamp is \(\boxed{1/9}\).
Review 2. Five sheets of gold leaf are shared equally among \(8\) picture frames, nothing wasted. Write how much gold leaf one frame receives as a fraction \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
The share reading of a fraction tells us that \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) means \(a\) wholes shared equally among \(b\) of them. So the top number counts what we are handing out and the bottom number counts how many fair shares we cut it into. Here we hand out 5 sheets of gold leaf and split them equally among 8 frames. That is exactly 5 shared among 8, which the share reading writes as \(\tfrac{5}{8}\). A quick sanity check helps. Each frame gets less than one full sheet, since 5 sheets cannot give 8 frames a whole sheet each. And \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) is indeed less than 1 because the top is smaller than the bottom, so the size feels right. $$\boxed{5/8}$$
Review 3. A hose is cut into \(7\) equal sections. A sticker covers exactly \(4\) of them. Using the cut-and-take reading, write the fraction of the whole hose the sticker covers as \(a/b\).
Show solution
A fraction is a cut and take reading. First you cut the whole into equal pieces, and the number of pieces is the bottom of the fraction. Here the hose is cut into \(7\) equal sections, so the whole hose is \(\tfrac{7}{7}\) and each section is \(\tfrac{1}{7}\). Then you take some of those pieces, and how many you take is the top of the fraction. The sticker covers \(4\) of the sections, so it takes \(4\) copies of \(\tfrac{1}{7}\), which is \(\tfrac{4}{7}\). The sticker covers \boxed{4/7} of the whole hose.
Review 4. A beetle reaches \(1\) in exactly \(12\) equal steps, each of size \(\tfrac{1}{12}\). How many of these steps does the beetle take to travel from \(0\) to \(1\)?
Show solution
Each step is the same size, and the steps together cover the distance from \(0\) to \(1\) with nothing left over. That means \(1\) is being split into equal pieces, and the size of one piece is the size of one step. A unit fraction like \(\tfrac{1}{12}\) means one piece out of \(12\) equal pieces that fill a whole. The bottom number \(12\) is the count of pieces it takes to rebuild the \(1\). So if one step is \(\tfrac{1}{12}\), then stacking those steps gives \(\tfrac{1}{12}+\tfrac{1}{12}+\cdots\) until they add up to \(1\), which happens after \(12\) of them. You can check it directly. Twelve steps of size \(\tfrac{1}{12}\) cover \(12\times\tfrac{1}{12}=\tfrac{12}{12}=1\), landing the beetle exactly on \(1\). The number of steps and the denominator are the same number, because the denominator was telling you how many equal pieces make the whole all along. \(\boxed{12}\)
Review 5. A token is \(17\) steps of \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) out from \(0\), at address \(\tfrac{17}{5}\). It falls between two whole numbers. Enter the smaller of those two whole numbers.
Show solution
Five steps of size \(\tfrac{1}{5}\) add up to one whole unit, so whole numbers live at step counts that are multiples of \(5\). Counting out from \(0\), the whole-number stops are at \(0, 5, 10, 15, 20\) steps, which are the addresses \(0, 1, 2, 3, 4\). The token is at \(17\) steps. The largest multiple of \(5\) not past \(17\) is \(15\), and \(15\) steps is the address \(3\). The next whole number is at \(20\) steps, which is the address \(4\). So \(\tfrac{17}{5}\) sits between \(3\) and \(4\). You can check this directly, since \(3 = \tfrac{15}{5}\) and \(4 = \tfrac{20}{5}\), and \(15 < 17 < 20\). The smaller of the two whole numbers is \(\boxed{3}\).
Review 6. A hold sits at height \(\tfrac{11}{3}\) m, between two whole meter marks. Enter the larger of the two whole numbers that \(\tfrac{11}{3}\) falls between.
Show solution
To see where \(\frac{11}{3}\) sits, think of it as eleven steps of one third. Whole meters come in groups of three thirds, since \(\frac{3}{3}=1\). Pull out as many whole meters as you can. Three whole meters use up \(\frac{9}{3}\), which leaves \(\frac{11}{3}-\frac{9}{3}=\frac{2}{3}\) still to go. So \(\frac{11}{3}=3+\frac{2}{3}\), which is \(3\) and a little more. The hold has climbed past the \(3\) meter mark but has not reached \(4\), so it lands between \(3\) and \(4\). The larger of those two whole numbers is \(\boxed{4}\).
Review 7. A sensor hangs at \(-\tfrac{9}{4}\) m, between two whole meter marks. Which whole meter mark is the deeper (lower) of the two? Give it with its minus sign.
Show solution
Start by reading the fraction as a position. Since \(\tfrac{9}{4}=2\tfrac14\), the address \(-\tfrac{9}{4}\) is \(-2\tfrac14\). So the sensor sits a quarter meter below the \(-2\) mark. Now find the two whole meter marks it falls between. Going up from \(-2\tfrac14\) the next whole mark is \(-2\). Going down the next whole mark is \(-3\). So the sensor rests between \(-3\) and \(-2\). The question asks for the deeper one, the lower whole number. Deeper means more negative, and \(-3\) is below \(-2\) on the line. So the deeper mark is $$\boxed{-3}$$
Review 8. A log reads 84/7 bins. \(\tfrac{84}{7}\) lands on a whole number when \(7\) divides \(84\) exactly. Check divisibility. If it does, what whole number does \(\tfrac{84}{7}\) equal?
Show solution
A fraction is just a division waiting to happen, so \(\tfrac{84}{7}\) is asking how many groups of 7 fit inside 84. First check that it comes out clean. Counting up in sevens gives \(7,14,21,28,35,42,49,56,63,70,77,84\), and 84 shows up right on the list, so 7 divides 84 with nothing left over. That means \(\tfrac{84}{7}\) really is a whole number. Counting how many sevens it took to reach 84, we land on the twelfth step, so \(84\div 7 = 12\). The clerk filled exactly \(\boxed{12}\) bins.
Review 9. Four ribbons laid end to end fill \(0\) to \(4\). Split that stretch equally among \(3\) friends. Where does the first friend's piece end? Write that address as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
The whole length from \(0\) to \(4\) measures \(4\) units. Sharing it fairly among \(3\) people means cutting that length of \(4\) into \(3\) equal pieces, and the size of one piece is \(4 \div 3\). That is the whole point of a fraction. Splitting \(4\) into \(3\) equal parts is written \(\tfrac{4}{3}\), where the top number is how much you are sharing and the bottom number is how many equal parts you are sharing it into. The first friend's piece starts at \(0\) and runs one full piece length, so its far end sits at address \(\tfrac{4}{3}\) on the ruler. That is a little past \(1\), which makes sense since \(4\) split three ways gives each person a bit more than one whole ribbon. $$\boxed{4/3}$$
Review 10. Three cards on a number line: \(-\tfrac{8}{3}\), \(\tfrac{8}{-3}\), \(-\left(\tfrac{8}{3}\right)\). Trace where each points. How many distinct points do they name in total?
Show solution
Read each card as one signed number. The first card is already \(-\tfrac{8}{3}\). For the second card, \(\tfrac{8}{-3}\) means a positive 8 divided by a negative 3, and a positive over a negative is negative, so \(\tfrac{8}{-3} = -\tfrac{8}{3}\). For the third card, \(-\left(\tfrac{8}{3}\right)\) is the opposite of \(\tfrac{8}{3}\), which is \(-\tfrac{8}{3}\). All three cards simplify to the same value \(-\tfrac{8}{3}\), a single spot two and two thirds units left of zero. Putting a minus sign on top of a fraction, on the bottom, or in front of the whole thing all do the same job, they flip the sign once. So the three cards point at the same location, and the number of distinct points is \(\boxed{1}\).
Review 11. Five addresses: \(\frac{80}{5}\), \(\frac{80}{6}\), \(\frac{80}{16}\), \(\frac{80}{10}\), \(\frac{80}{3}\). A locker is real when the fraction is whole. How many are real lockers?
Show solution
Since the top is always 80, an address is a whole number exactly when its bottom number divides 80 with no remainder. Check them one at a time. \(\frac{80}{5}=16\), even, so this one counts. \(\frac{80}{6}\) leaves a remainder since 6 does not divide 80, so it does not count. \(\frac{80}{16}=5\), even, so it counts. \(\frac{80}{10}=8\), even, so it counts. \(\frac{80}{3}\) leaves a remainder, so it does not count. The ones that land on a whole number are \(\frac{80}{5}\), \(\frac{80}{16}\), and \(\frac{80}{10}\), which is \boxed{3}.
Review 12. Tiles \(72\) into \(n\) equal rows: needs \(n\mid 72\). For how many whole numbers \(n\geq 1\) does \(\tfrac{72}{n}\) land on a whole number?
Show solution
Asking when \(\tfrac{72}{n}\) is a whole number is the same as asking when \(n\) divides 72 evenly, so the count we want is just the number of divisors of 72. A tidy way to find them all is to hunt in pairs that multiply to 72. Going up from 1, we get \(1\times 72\), \(2\times 36\), \(3\times 24\), \(4\times 18\), \(6\times 12\), and \(8\times 9\). Once the two partners in a pair meet in the middle, like 8 and 9 here, we have caught everything. Listing the values gives \(1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36, 72\). Counting them, there are \(\boxed{12}\) whole numbers \(n\) that work.
Review 13. A loader accepts \(n\) trays when \(\tfrac{n+7}{n}=1+\tfrac{7}{n}\) is a whole number, i.e., when \(n\) divides \(7\). For how many whole numbers \(n\geq 1\) does the loader accept the stack?
Show solution
Rewrite the fraction by splitting the top. We get $$\frac{n+7}{n} = \frac{n}{n} + \frac{7}{n} = 1 + \frac{7}{n}.$$ The \(1\) is already a whole number, so the whole expression is a whole number exactly when \(\frac{7}{n}\) is a whole number. That happens only when \(n\) divides evenly into \(7\). The number \(7\) is prime, so the only whole numbers that go into it with no remainder are \(1\) and \(7\). Checking, \(n=1\) gives \(\frac{8}{1} = 8\) and \(n=7\) gives \(\frac{14}{7} = 2\), both whole. That is \(\boxed{2}\) values of \(n\).
