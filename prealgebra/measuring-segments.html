Prealgebra · Lesson 11.4

Measuring Segments

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

You have spent three lessons on angles, the turn inside a shape. But a shape is made of two things, and the other one is its sides. A segment is just the straight path between two points, and its length is the distance across it. Over the next few lessons you will split a segment into pieces, add those pieces back together, and walk the whole way around a shape to measure how far you traveled.

Key idea
Segments and their length. A segment is the straight piece connecting two endpoints, and we name it by those endpoints, like segment \(AB\). Its length is the distance between the two endpoints, and we write it simply as \(AB\). In geometry we usually drop the units and just work with the numbers, so \(AB = 12\) means the segment is 12 units long. Naming the segment and naming its length look almost the same, and that is on purpose. When you see \(AB\) sitting in an equation with a number, it means the length. When two segments carry matching tick marks, that is a picture telling you their lengths are equal, so \(AB = CD\) without measuring either one.
Key idea
The midpoint. The midpoint of a segment is the point exactly halfway between its two endpoints. It splits the segment into two equal halves, so each half is half the whole length and the whole length is twice either half. If \(M\) is the midpoint of \(AB\), then \(AM = MB\), each equal to \(\frac{1}{2}AB\), and \(AB = 2\,AM\). Equal tick marks on the two halves, like the ones in the figure above, are the usual signal that a point is a midpoint.
In the diagram above, \(M\) is the midpoint of \(AB\) and the tick marks show the two halves are equal. Given \(AM = 7\), find \(AB\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
The midpoint \(M\) cuts \(AB\) into two equal halves, so \(MB = AM = 7\). The whole segment is both halves together, which means \(AB = 2 \times 7\). So \(AB = \boxed{14}\).
Key idea
Adding segments. When a point \(B\) lies on segment \(AC\), somewhere between \(A\) and \(C\), it splits the whole into two pieces. Those two pieces add back to the whole. $$AB + BC = AC$$ Read it forward and you combine two known lengths into the total. If \(AB = 5\) and \(BC = 8\), then \(AC = 13\). Read it backward and you find a missing piece by subtracting from the whole. If you know \(AC = 13\) and \(AB = 5\), then \(BC = 13 - 5 = 8\). This is the segment version of the angle-addition idea you used earlier, where the two smaller angles at a point added up to the big one. Same move, lengths instead of angles.
In the diagram above, \(B\) lies on \(AC\) with \(AB = 8\), and the bracket shows the whole segment \(AC = 20\). Find \(BC\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
Point \(B\) splits \(AC\) into \(AB\) and \(BC\), and those two pieces add up to the whole, so \(AB + BC = AC\). Fill in what you know, \(8 + BC = 20\), and subtract to get $$BC = 20 - 8 = \boxed{12}.$$ So \(BC = 12\).
Key idea
Congruent segments. Two segments are congruent when they have the same length. A figure shows this with tick marks. Segments carrying the same number of ticks are equal to each other, so one tick on \(AB\) and one tick on \(CD\) tells you \(AB = CD\). When several segments in a row each carry a single tick, they are all the same length. The whole they build is that one length times the number of pieces, so five equal pieces of length \(3\) make a whole of \(5 \times 3 = 15\).
In the diagram above, \(AB\), \(BC\), and \(CD\) each carry one tick mark, so the three pieces are equal, and \(AD = 27\). Find the length of \(AB\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
The three tick marks mean \(AB\), \(BC\), and \(CD\) are equal, and stacked end to end they make the whole segment \(AD = 27\). So three equal pieces add to \(27\), and each one is $$27 \div 3 = \boxed{9}.$$ So \(AB = 9\).
In the diagram above, \(M\) is the midpoint of \(AB\) with \(AB = 24\), and \(N\) is the midpoint of \(MB\). Find \(AN\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
Since \(M\) is the midpoint of \(AB\), it cuts the whole in half, so \(AM = MB = 12\). Now \(N\) is the midpoint of \(MB\), which cuts that half in half again, so \(MN = 6\). Going from \(A\) to \(N\) covers \(AM\) then \(MN\), so $$AN = AM + MN = 12 + 6 = \boxed{18}.$$ So \(AN = 18\).
Key idea
Perimeter. The perimeter of a closed figure is the total distance around it, and you find it by adding up the lengths of all its sides. For the triangle in the figure above with sides \(5\), \(6\), and \(7\), the perimeter is \(5 + 6 + 7 = 18\). Walk the outside of the shape and add each edge you cross, and the running total when you arrive back where you started is the perimeter. For a regular polygon every side is the same length, so there is no need to add them one by one. Multiply one side by the number of sides. A regular hexagon with side \(4\) has perimeter \(6 \times 4 = 24\).
The diagram above shows a rectangle that is \(8\) units wide and \(5\) units tall. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of all four side lengths. A rectangle has two sides of length \(8\) and two of length \(5\), so $$8 + 5 + 8 + 5 = 26.$$ A quicker way is to add one width and one height, then double it, \(2\times(8+5) = 26\). The perimeter is \(\boxed{26}\).
The diagram above shows an L-shaped figure with its six side lengths marked \(6\), \(2\), \(4\), \(3\), \(2\), and \(5\). Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of all the sides, so add the six marked lengths. $$6 + 2 + 4 + 3 + 2 + 5 = 22$$ The perimeter is \(\boxed{22}\).
The diagram above shows an isosceles triangle whose two equal legs, marked with ticks and labelled \(2x\), are each twice its base, labelled \(x\). The perimeter is \(40\). Find the base. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of the three sides, so \(2x + 2x + x = 40\). Combining the like terms gives \(5x = 40\), so \(x = 8\). The base is \(x\), which is \(\boxed{8}\).
The diagram above shows a regular hexagon. All six sides are equal, marked by the ticks, and its perimeter is \(66\). Find the length of one side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A regular hexagon has 6 equal sides, so the perimeter is one side length added six times. To get a single side, split the perimeter into 6 equal parts. $$66 \div 6 = 11$$ So one side is \(\boxed{11}\).
Key idea
The triangle inequality. Three lengths can only close into a triangle if the two shorter ones together are longer than the longest. The two short sides have to stretch across the long one and still reach each other to meet at a corner, and if they fall short, no triangle forms. Put simply, any two sides of a triangle add to more than the third. The figure above labels a triangle's sides, and you can check every pair against this rule. A handy consequence is that the third side must be less than the sum of the other two and more than their difference, which pins down exactly how long a side is allowed to be.
In the diagram above, two sides of a triangle are \(7\) and \(9\), and the third side (marked with a question mark) is a whole number. Find the largest the third side could be. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Any side of a triangle must be less than the sum of the other two. The two given sides add to \(7 + 9 = 16\), so the third side must satisfy the third side \(< 16\). The largest whole number under \(16\) is \(15\). If the third side were \(16\), the triangle would flatten into a straight line, so it truly has to stay below \(16\). The answer is \(\boxed{15}\).
In the diagram above, two sides of a triangle are \(7\) and \(9\), and the third side (marked with a question mark) is a whole number. What is the smallest the third side could be? Give the number of units.
Show solution
By the triangle inequality, the third side has to be more than the difference of the other two, or the triangle collapses flat. That difference is \(9 - 7 = 2\), so the third side must be more than \(2\). The smallest whole number bigger than \(2\) is \(\boxed{3}\).
In the diagram above, two sides of a triangle are \(4\) and \(9\), and the third side (the question mark) is a whole number. How many different whole-number lengths are possible for it? Give the number of whole-number lengths.
Show solution
The triangle inequality traps the third side between the sum and the difference of the other two sides. It has to be less than \(9 + 4 = 13\) and more than \(9 - 4 = 5\), so $$5 < \text{third side} < 13.$$ The whole numbers strictly between \(5\) and \(13\) are \(6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12\). That is \(\boxed{7}\) possible lengths.
Fun fact
The word perimeter comes straight from Greek. "Peri" means around and "metron" means measure, so perimeter literally says "measure around". That same "peri" turns up in a periscope, which lets you see around a corner, and in a perimeter fence that runs all the way around a property. Once you spot the "peri", you can hear the meaning hiding inside the word.
In the diagram above is a triangular garden with sides \(13\), \(14\), and \(15\) feet. Fencing costs \(\$4\) per foot. Find the total cost to fence it. Give the number of dollars.
Show solution
Fencing wraps the whole border, so first find the perimeter by adding the three sides. \(13 + 14 + 15 = 42\) feet. Every foot costs \(\$4\), so multiply.$$42 \times 4 = \boxed{168}$$The garden costs \(168\) dollars to fence.
In the diagram above, points \(B\) and \(C\) lie on segment \(AD\) with \(AC\) and \(CD\) in the ratio \(3 : 1\), and \(B\) is the midpoint of \(AC\). The bracket shows \(BC = 6\). Find \(AD\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
The midpoint \(B\) splits \(AC\) into two equal halves, so \(BC\) is half of \(AC\). That gives \(AC = 2 \times 6 = 12\). The ratio \(AC : CD = 3 : 1\) means \(CD\) is a third of \(AC\), so \(CD = 12 \div 3 = 4\). Then \(AD = AC + CD = 12 + 4 = \boxed{16}\).
Key idea
The segment toolkit. You now have everything you need to measure the parts of a figure. A segment is named by its two endpoints, like \(AB\), and measured by its length, written \(AB\) with no units. Its midpoint splits it into two equal halves. When a point lies on a segment, the pieces add up to the whole, \(AB + BC = AC\), so you can build the whole by adding forward or find a missing piece by subtracting backward. Tick marks are the diagram's way of telling you two segments are congruent, so they carry the same length even when only one is labelled. The perimeter of a figure is just the sum of its side lengths, and when the figure is a regular polygon every side matches, so the perimeter is one side times the number of sides. Finally, three lengths close into a triangle only when any two of them add to more than the third, which pins the unknown side strictly between the sum and the difference of the other two. Add forward, subtract backward, read the ticks, sum the sides, and check the triangle. Those five moves handle almost every length question you will meet.

Review problems

Review 1.
In the diagram above, \(M\) is the midpoint of \(PQ\) and \(PM = 11\). Find \(PQ\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
Since \(M\) is the midpoint of \(PQ\), the two halves \(PM\) and \(MQ\) are equal, so \(PQ\) is twice one half. $$PQ = 2 \times 11 = \boxed{22}$$ So \(PQ = 22\).
Review 2.
In the diagram above, \(B\) lies on \(AC\) with \(AB = 13\), and the bracket marks the whole length \(AC = 30\). Find \(BC\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
The point \(B\) splits \(AC\) into two pieces, \(AB\) and \(BC\), that add up to the whole. So \(BC = AC - AB\). Reading the diagram, \(AC = 30\) and \(AB = 13\), which gives $$BC = 30 - 13 = \boxed{17}.$$
Review 3.
In the diagram above, the tick marks show that \(XY\), \(YZ\), \(ZW\), and \(WV\) are all equal, and the whole length \(XV = 32\). Find \(XY\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
The four tick marks mean \(XY\), \(YZ\), \(ZW\), and \(WV\) are equal pieces that add up to \(XV\). So one piece is a quarter of the whole. $$XY = \frac{32}{4} = \boxed{8}$$
Review 4.
In the diagram above, \(M\) is the midpoint of \(AB\) with \(AB = 20\), and \(N\) is the midpoint of \(AM\). Find \(NB\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
Each midpoint cuts a segment in half. Since \(M\) is the midpoint of \(AB\), we get \(AM = \frac{20}{2} = 10\). Since \(N\) is the midpoint of \(AM\), we get \(AN = \frac{10}{2} = 5\). Then \(N\) sits \(5\) units from \(A\), so $$NB = AB - AN = 20 - 5 = \boxed{15}.$$
Review 5.
The diagram above shows a rectangle that is \(9\) wide and \(6\) tall. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of all four sides. A rectangle has two sides of length \(9\) and two of length \(6\), so add one of each and double it. $$2\times(9+6) = 2\times 15 = 30$$ The perimeter is \(\boxed{30}\).
Review 6.
The diagram above shows an L-shaped figure with its six sides labelled. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of every side. Reading the six lengths off the figure and adding them, $$7 + 2 + 4 + 3 + 3 + 5 = \boxed{24}.$$ So the perimeter is \(24\).
Review 7.
In the diagram above, the two equal legs of the isosceles triangle each measure \(2x\), the base measures \(x\), and the perimeter is \(35\). Find the base. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of the three sides, so add the two legs and the base. Each leg is \(2x\) and the base is \(x\), which gives $$2x + 2x + x = 35.$$ Combine the like terms to get \(5x = 35\), so \(x = 7\). The base is \(x\), so its length is \(\boxed{7}\).
Review 8.
The diagram above shows a regular octagon with perimeter \(72\). Since all eight sides are equal, find the length of one side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
All eight sides of a regular octagon are equal, so one side is the perimeter split into \(8\) equal parts. $$72 \div 8 = 9$$ One side has length \(\boxed{9}\).
Review 9.
In the diagram above, two sides of the triangle measure \(8\) and \(11\), and the third side is a whole number. What is the largest length the third side can be? Give the number of units.
Show solution
By the triangle inequality, the two known sides must sum to more than the third side, so the third side is less than \(8 + 11 = 19\). The largest whole number below \(19\) is \(\boxed{18}\).
Review 10.
In the diagram above, a triangle has two sides of length \(8\) and \(11\), and its third side is a whole number of units. What is the smallest length that third side can be? Give the number of units.
Show solution
For a triangle to close up, the third side must be longer than the difference of the other two, so \(11 - 8 = 3\). The side has to be more than \(3\), and the smallest whole number that beats \(3\) is \(\boxed{4}\).
Review 11.
In the diagram above, two sides of a triangle are \(6\) and \(10\), and the third side is a whole number. How many different whole-number lengths could the third side be? Give the number of whole-number lengths.
Show solution
By the triangle inequality, the third side must be less than the sum of the other two and more than their difference. So it is more than \(10-6=4\) and less than \(10+6=16\), giving \(4 < \text{third} < 16\). The whole numbers strictly between \(4\) and \(16\) run from \(5\) to \(15\), which is \(15-5+1=11\) values. The answer is \(\boxed{11}\).
Review 12.
The diagram above shows a square garden that measures \(14\) feet on each side. Fencing costs \(\$5\) per foot, and you want to fence the whole garden. How much will the fencing cost? Give the number of dollars.
Show solution
Fencing follows the border, so start with the perimeter. A square has four equal sides, so the perimeter is \(4 \times 14 = 56\) feet. Each foot costs \(\$5\), so the fencing costs $$56 \times 5 = \boxed{280}$$ dollars.
Review 13.
In the diagram above, \(B\) and \(C\) lie on segment \(AD\) with \(AB : BC : CD = 3 : 4 : 5\), and \(AD = 36\). Find \(BC\). Give the number of units.
Show solution
The ratio splits \(AD\) into \(3 + 4 + 5 = 12\) equal pieces. So one piece is $$36 \div 12 = 3.$$ Now \(BC\) is worth \(4\) of those pieces, so \(BC = 4 \times 3 = \boxed{12}\).
