Prealgebra · Lesson 7.1

What Is a Ratio?

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A smoothie owner keeps the flavor steady by using the same comparison every time: three mango chunks for every two yogurt scoops. That balance between two quantities is a ratio, and it holds whether she is making one cup or filling a pitcher.

A trail stand fills each bag with 5 almonds and 3 dried cherries. A customer orders 4 scoops. How many dried cherries are in the bag?
Show solution
The almonds are along for the ride here. What matters is that every scoop is identical, so the cherries simply repeat, three of them at a time. One scoop brings \(3\) cherries. Four scoops bring four copies of that same group, which is \(4\) groups of \(3\). So the count is \(4 \times 3 = 12\). Notice we never had to touch the almonds to answer this, the cherry side of the pairing carries its own steady count. The bag holds \(\boxed{12}\) dried cherries.
At a kite festival the rule is 3 yellow kites for every 5 red ones. One cluster has 9 yellow kites. How many red kites are in the cluster?
Show solution
The rule for every \(3\) yellow there are \(5\) red is the ratio \(3:5\), and a ratio works much like a fraction in simplest form. As long as you scale both parts by the same number, the comparison stays exactly the same. The yellow part has grown from \(3\) to \(9\). To find the multiplier, ask what \(3\) was multiplied by to reach \(9\). Since \(3\times 3 = 9\), the multiplier is \(3\), which means there are \(3\) groups of the for every unit. Now apply that same \(\times 3\) to the red part, because both colors rise together. That gives \(5\times 3 = 15\) red kites. You can sanity check it. The scaled comparison \(9:15\) reduces back to \(3:5\), so the ratio held steady. \(\boxed{15}\)
Key idea
A ratio compares two amounts by their relative size. Writing \(3:2\) means for every \(3\) of the first there are \(2\) of the second, and that pairing holds no matter how big the batch grows. On its own a ratio never tells you how many there are altogether, since \(3:2\) could mean \(3\) and \(2\) or \(30\) and \(20\). The same comparison can be written three ways, as \(3:2\) with a colon, as "3 to 2" in words, or as the fraction \(\tfrac{3}{2}\), and the colon form is the one you will reach for most. Order carries meaning, so mango to yogurt is a different comparison than yogurt to mango.
A garden plants basil and mint in the ratio \(4:5\). One full repeat of the pair uses \(4+5=9\) plants, so every valid total is a multiple of \(9\). What is the only possible total between 30 and 40?
Show solution
Each full repeat of the ratio brings \(4\) basil and \(5\) mint, a bundle of \(4+5=9\) plants. Since the garden is built from whole bundles, the total number of plants must be a multiple of \(9\). The multiples of \(9\) are \(9, 18, 27, 36, 45,\) and so on. The only one that sits between \(30\) and \(40\) is \(36\), which is exactly \(4\) bundles. Check it. Four bundles give \(4\times 4=16\) basil and \(4\times 5=20\) mint, and \(16+20=36\), still in the comparison \(4:5\). So the only possible total is \(\boxed{36}\).
Key idea
One ratio fits a whole family of totals. If the comparison is \(a:b\), the real counts could be \(a\) and \(b\), or twice that, or any matched scale-up, so the ratio alone never says how many things there are in all. What it does pin down is the size of one full repeat, which is \(a+b\), and that is why every honest total lands on a whole number of those repeats. To turn a ratio into actual amounts you need one extra fact, either the total or one of the two quantities.
A cooling fluid should compare water to coolant as \(2:3\). Six ratios were recorded: \(4:6\), \(8:10\), \(6:9\), \(9:12\), \(10:15\), \(14:21\). How many are equivalent to \(2:3\)?
Show solution
Reduce each ratio to its simplest form and compare it with \(2:3\). For \(4:6\), both numbers divide by \(2\), giving \(2:3\). Match. For \(8:10\), both divide by \(2\), giving \(4:5\). Not a match. For \(6:9\), both divide by \(3\), giving \(2:3\). Match. For \(9:12\), both divide by \(3\), giving \(3:4\). Not a match. For \(10:15\), both divide by \(5\), giving \(2:3\). Match. For \(14:21\), both divide by \(7\), giving \(2:3\). Match. The ratios \(4:6\), \(6:9\), \(10:15\), and \(14:21\) all reduce to \(2:3\), while \(8:10\) and \(9:12\) do not. That gives \(\boxed{4}\) matching ratios.
Key idea
Equivalent ratios. A ratio acts like a fraction, so multiplying both parts of \(a:b\) by the same number gives an equivalent ratio, the very same comparison in bigger numbers, the way \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) and \(\tfrac{6}{8}\) are the same amount. Running that backward, dividing both parts by a factor they share keeps the comparison the same too. Dividing by their greatest common factor, as far as it will go, writes the ratio in simplest form, which is reached when the two parts share no common factor larger than \(1\).
A board game ships tokens in the ratio \(18:24\). Reduce it to lowest terms. Write your answer as a:b.
Show solution
Treat the ratio like a fraction you want in simplest form. First find the greatest common factor of the two parts. The factors of \(18\) are \(1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18\), and the factors of \(24\) are \(1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24\). The largest number in both lists is \(6\), so the greatest common factor is \(6\). Now divide each part by \(6\). For the red tokens, \(18\div 6 = 3\). For the blue tokens, \(24\div 6 = 4\). That turns \(18:24\) into \(3:4\). Finally, check that you are truly finished. The only common factor of \(3\) and \(4\) is \(1\), so nothing larger divides both and the ratio cannot shrink any further. The simplest form of the comparison is \(\boxed{3:4}\).
A school library returned books on time to late in the ratio \(45:75\). Reduce to simplest form. Write as a:b.
Show solution
The trick is to find the greatest common factor of \(45\) and \(75\), the largest number that divides both with nothing left over. It is tempting to stop at \(5\) since both numbers end in a \(5\), but you can do better. List what fits into each. The number \(45\) breaks into \(3 \times 3 \times 5\), and \(75\) breaks into \(3 \times 5 \times 5\). They share one \(3\) and one \(5\), so their greatest common factor is \(3 \times 5 = 15\). Now divide both parts of the ratio by \(15\). On time becomes \(45 \div 15 = 3\), and late becomes \(75 \div 15 = 5\). That gives \(3:5\). Finally, confirm it is fully reduced. The only number that divides both \(3\) and \(5\) is \(1\), so there is nothing left to cancel. The library's month came down to a clean \(\boxed{3:5}\), three on time for every five late.
A bakery uses \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) cup almond flour and \(\tfrac{4}{9}\) cup tapioca starch. Write the ratio in lowest terms as a:b.
Show solution
Start with the ratio of almond flour to tapioca starch, which is \(\tfrac{2}{3}:\tfrac{4}{9}\). Fractions make a ratio hard to read, so clear them first. Multiplying both parts of a ratio by the same number gives an equivalent ratio, and the number \(9\) works for both denominators. $$\tfrac{2}{3}\times 9 = 6 \qquad \tfrac{4}{9}\times 9 = 4$$ So \(\tfrac{2}{3}:\tfrac{4}{9}\) is the same comparison as \(6:4\). Now reduce, exactly like putting a fraction in simplest form. Both parts share a common factor of \(2\), so divide each part by \(2\). $$6\div 2 = 3 \qquad 4\div 2 = 2$$ That leaves \(3:2\), and since \(3\) and \(2\) share no common factor larger than \(1\), it is fully reduced. For every \(3\) parts of almond flour the blend has \(2\) parts of tapioca starch. \(\boxed{3:2}\)
Key idea
Clear, then reduce. If a ratio shows up wearing fractions, multiply every part by a common denominator to turn them into whole numbers. If it shows up wearing decimals, multiply by a power of ten. That scaling is safe because stretching both parts by the same factor never changes what they are comparing, exactly like building equivalent fractions. Then divide both whole numbers by their greatest common factor to land in lowest terms. Clear first, then reduce.
A trail map records two legs as \(0.75\,\text{km}\) and \(1.25\,\text{km}\). Write \(0.75:1.25\) as a whole-number ratio in lowest terms, in the form a:b.
Show solution
Start with the decimal ratio \(0.75:1.25\). Both numbers stop at the hundredths place, so multiplying each part by \(100\) clears the decimals. $$0.75:1.25 = (0.75\times 100):(1.25\times 100) = 75:125$$ Multiplying both parts by the same number does not change the comparison, just like scaling the top and bottom of a fraction by \(100\) leaves its value alone, so \(75:125\) describes the very same trail. Now reduce \(75:125\) to lowest terms. The greatest common factor of \(75\) and \(125\) is \(25\), so divide each part by \(25\). $$75:125 = (75\div 25):(125\div 25) = 3:5$$ Since \(3\) and \(5\) share no factor larger than \(1\), the ratio is fully reduced. The two legs compare as \(\boxed{3:5}\).
Fun fact
A screen\'s aspect ratio is width to height: \(16:9\) for modern TVs, \(4:3\) for older monitors. Scaling \(4:3\) to \(16:12\) shows \(16:9\) is shorter and wider. One ratio covers every screen size.

Turn a ratio into a count. So far a ratio has fixed only the relative sizes, never the totals. The move that unlocks real amounts is to read a ratio as a recipe built from equal parts, \(a\) parts of one thing and \(b\) parts of the other, every part the same size. Once you find what a single part is worth, every quantity in the story drops out by simple multiplication.

A bag holds 56 pieces of dried fruit mixed as raisins to pumpkin seeds in the ratio \(5:3\). How many pumpkin seeds are in the bag?
Show solution
The ratio \(5:3\) tells you the bag is made of \(5\) equal parts of raisins and \(3\) equal parts of pumpkin seeds. Notice that the ratio by itself does not tell you the totals, it only tells you how the pieces share out. To bring in the real count, add the parts. There are \(5+3=8\) equal parts, and all \(8\) of them together must add up to the whole bag of \(56\) pieces. So each part is worth $$56 \div 8 = 7 \text{ pieces.}$$ Now scale each side by that value. The raisins are \(5\) parts, which is \(5\times 7 = 35\) pieces, and the pumpkin seeds are \(3\) parts, which is \(3\times 7 = 21\) pieces. As a quick check, \(35+21=56\), exactly the size of the bag, and \(35:21\) reduces back to \(5:3\). The bag holds \(\boxed{21}\) pumpkin seeds.
Key idea
The parts method. Read \(a:b\) as a pile of \(a+b\) equal parts. If you know the total, divide it by \(a+b\) to find what one part is worth, then hand \(a\) of those parts to one side and \(b\) to the other to get the real amounts. If instead you know one quantity, divide it by its part count to find the value of one part, then scale the other side by that same multiplier. Once you know what one part is, the whole comparison falls open.
A box of 84 stickers is split between two friends in the ratio \(5:7\), bigger share to the friend who paid more. How many stickers are in the smaller share?
Show solution
The ratio \(5:7\) tells you the box is shared in \(5 + 7 = 12\) equal parts. To find the size of one part, divide the total by the number of parts, so \(84 \div 12 = 7\) stickers per part. The smaller share is the \(5\)-part side, which is \(5 \times 7 = 35\) stickers. As a quick check, the larger share is \(7 \times 7 = 49\), and \(35 + 49 = 84\), the whole box. The smaller share holds \(\boxed{35}\) stickers.
A lemonade recipe uses lemon juice to water in the ratio \(2:9\). One batch uses 6 ounces of lemon juice. How many ounces does the whole batch hold?
Show solution
Start from the side you actually know. The lemon juice is \(2\) parts in the ratio, and in this batch those \(2\) parts come out to six ounces. So each single part must be worth \(6 \div 2 = 3\) ounces. That number \(3\) is the multiplier, the amount one part is really worth. Now scale the whole recipe by it. One complete repeat of the ratio is \(2 + 9 = 11\) parts, so the finished batch is \(11 \times 3 = 33\) ounces. You can check it by parts. The lemon juice is \(2 \times 3 = 6\) ounces, the water is \(9 \times 3 = 27\) ounces, and \(6 + 27 = 33\) ounces, which matches. The whole batch is \(\boxed{33}\) ounces.
An aquarium opens with 40 fish, blue to green in \(3:2\). The keeper adds only green fish until the ratio reads \(6:5\). How many green fish must the keeper add?
Show solution
Start by turning the opening ratio into real counts. The ratio \(3:2\) means \(3+2=5\) parts, and \(40\div 5=8\) fish per part, so there are \(3\times 8=24\) blue and \(2\times 8=16\) green. Only green fish get added, so the blue stays anchored at \(24\) forever. Now read the target comparison \(6:5\) the same way. Blue is the \(6\) side and it equals \(24\) fish, so one part is \(24\div 6=4\) fish. The green side is \(5\) parts, which is \(5\times 4=20\) green fish. The tank already holds \(16\) green, so the keeper needs \(20-16=4\) more. Check it, with \(24\) blue and \(20\) green the comparison is \(24:20\), and dividing both by \(4\) gives \(6:5\), exactly the goal. So the keeper must add \(\boxed{4}\) green fish.

Review problems

Review 1. A vendor packs gift bags with 4 glitter stickers and 6 mini erasers each. She fills 5 bags. How many mini erasers does she use in total?
Show solution
A ratio inside one bag pairs the stickers and erasers, but this question asks only about the erasers, so the sticker count is a distraction. Each bag holds the same six mini erasers, and she fills five identical bags. Five bags each carrying six erasers gives \(5 \times 6 = 30\) erasers in all. So she uses \(\boxed{30}\) mini erasers across the five bags.
Review 2. A swim club keeps 2 coaches for every 9 swimmers. This morning 36 swimmers arrived. How many coaches are on deck?
Show solution
The staffing is fixed at \(2:9\), two coaches for every nine swimmers. To keep the comparison the same when more people show up, both parts of the ratio scale by the same multiplier. The swimmers went from \(9\) to \(36\), and \(36 \div 9 = 4\), so the for every unit repeats four times. The coaches must repeat four times as well, which gives \(2 \times 4 = 8\) coaches. As a check, \(8:36\) reduces by dividing both parts by \(4\), and \(8 \div 4 = 2\) with \(36 \div 4 = 9\), so \(8:36\) is the same comparison as \(2:9\). There are \(\boxed{8}\) coaches on deck.
Review 3. A flower stand bundles tulips and daffodils in the ratio \(5:6\). One full repeat is \(5+6=11\) stems, so every valid total is a multiple of 11. What is the only total between 70 and 80?
Show solution
Each full repeat of the \(5:6\) comparison uses \(5+6=11\) stems, so any real total must be made of complete groups of \(11\). That means the total has to be a multiple of \(11\). Checking the multiples of \(11\) near the range, \(11\times 6=66\) is below \(70\) and \(11\times 8=88\) is above \(80\), while \(11\times 7=77\) lands neatly between them. So the only total that splits evenly into \(5:6\) bundles here is \(77\), which gives \(7\) repeats, meaning \(35\) tulips and \(42\) daffodils. The single possible total is \(\boxed{77}\).
Review 4. A fabric border repeats wide stripes to narrow stripes in the ratio \(9:12\). Reduce first, then scale up so the wide count reaches 24. How many narrow stripes go with 24 wide ones?
Show solution
Start by reducing the comparison. Both parts of \(9:12\) share a common factor of \(3\), so dividing each part gives \(\tfrac{9}{3}:\tfrac{12}{3}\), which is \(3:4\). That tells you the simplest pattern is \(3\) wide stripes for every \(4\) narrow ones. Now scale up to reach \(24\) wide stripes. To turn the \(3\) wide stripes into \(24\), multiply by \(8\), since \(3\times 8 = 24\). To keep the comparison the same, multiply the narrow part by that very same factor, so \(4\times 8 = 32\). You can check it against the original numbers too. Going from \(9\) wide to \(24\) wide is a factor of \(\tfrac{24}{9} = \tfrac{8}{3}\), and \(12\times \tfrac{8}{3} = 32\) as well. Either path lands in the same place. So \(24\) wide stripes go with \(\boxed{32}\) narrow stripes.
Review 5. A juice bar restocks mango and guava pods in the ratio \(24:36\). Simplify to lowest terms. Write your answer as a:b.
Show solution
A ratio behaves like a fraction, so simplifying it works the same way you reduced fractions in Chapter 4. You divide both parts by the same number until nothing is left to cancel. The fastest route is to divide by the greatest common factor of the two parts at once. The greatest common factor of \(24\) and \(36\) is \(12\), since \(12\) divides both and no larger number does. Dividing each part by \(12\) gives \(24 \div 12 = 2\) and \(36 \div 12 = 3\), so the ratio becomes \(2:3\). Now check that it is fully reduced. The only positive factor shared by \(2\) and \(3\) is \(1\), so there is nothing left to cancel and the ratio is in lowest terms. The simplified ratio is \(\boxed{2:3}\).
Review 6. A bakery sells sourdough to rye in the ratio \(42:54\). Simplify to lowest terms. Write your answer as a:b.
Show solution
A ratio behaves like a fraction, so simplest form means the two parts share no common factor larger than \(1\). Start by finding the greatest common factor of \(42\) and \(54\). The factors they share are \(2\) and \(3\), and \(2\times 3=6\), so the greatest common factor is \(6\). Now divide each part of the ratio by \(6\). For the sourdough, \(42\div 6=7\), and for the rye, \(54\div 6=9\). That gives the simplified ratio \(7:9\). Since \(7\) and \(9\) share no common factor larger than \(1\), this is fully reduced. So the bakery sells sourdough and rye in the ratio \(\boxed{7:9}\).
Review 7. A smoothie uses \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) cup berries and \(\tfrac{5}{9}\) cup banana. Clear the fractions and reduce to lowest terms. Write the ratio of berries to banana as a:b.
Show solution
The two amounts being compared are \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) cup of berries and \(\tfrac{5}{9}\) cup of banana, so the ratio is \(\tfrac{5}{6}:\tfrac{5}{9}\). A ratio does not change when you multiply both parts by the same number, just like an equivalent fraction. Pick a number that clears both denominators. Both \(6\) and \(9\) divide evenly into \(18\), so multiply each part by \(18\). The berries part becomes \(\tfrac{5}{6}\times 18 = 15\), and the banana part becomes \(\tfrac{5}{9}\times 18 = 10\). That gives the whole-number ratio \(15:10\). Now reduce it. The greatest common factor of \(15\) and \(10\) is \(5\), so divide both by \(5\) to get \(15\div 5 = 3\) and \(10\div 5 = 2\). The ratio in lowest terms is \(\boxed{3:2}\).
Review 8. A window glaze uses \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) cup fast-drying liquid and \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) cup slow-drying liquid. Multiply both by 8, then reduce. Write the ratio in the form a:b.
Show solution
The two amounts are \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) cup and \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) cup, so the ratio is \(\tfrac{1}{2} : \tfrac{3}{8}\). Scaling both parts of a ratio by the same number keeps the ratio equivalent, just like building up equivalent fractions. Multiply each part by \(8\). The first part becomes \(\tfrac{1}{2} \times 8 = 4\), and the second becomes \(\tfrac{3}{8} \times 8 = 3\). That turns the comparison into \(4 : 3\). Now check for common factors. The factors of \(4\) are \(1, 2, 4\) and the factors of \(3\) are \(1, 3\), so the only shared factor is \(1\). Since nothing larger divides both, \(4 : 3\) is already in lowest terms. The ratio of fast-drying to slow-drying liquid is \(\boxed{4:3}\).
Review 9. Two garden rows measure \(0.6\) m and \(1.5\) m. Rewrite \(0.6:1.5\) as a whole-number ratio in simplest form. Write your answer as a:b.
Show solution
Start with \(0.6:1.5\). A ratio works the same way a fraction does, so multiplying both parts by the same number keeps the comparison the same. Both numbers have one decimal place, so multiply both parts by \(10\) to clear the decimals. That gives \(6:15\). Now reduce to lowest terms. The largest number that divides both \(6\) and \(15\) is \(3\), so divide both parts by \(3\). You get \(6\div 3 = 2\) and \(15\div 3 = 5\), which is \(2:5\). Since \(2\) and \(5\) share no common factor larger than \(1\), this is fully reduced. The rows compare in the ratio \(\boxed{2:5}\).
Review 10. A soup base uses \(2.4\) cups broth and \(0.9\) cups cream. Multiply by 10 to clear decimals, then reduce. Write the ratio as a:b.
Show solution
Start by clearing the decimals. Multiplying both parts of \(2.4:0.9\) by \(10\) keeps the comparison the same, since scaling both parts by the same amount gives an equivalent ratio. That gives \(24:9\). Now reduce to lowest terms, just like simplifying a fraction. The greatest common factor of \(24\) and \(9\) is \(3\), so divide both parts by \(3\). You get \(24\div 3 = 8\) and \(9\div 3 = 3\), which leaves \(8:3\). Since \(8\) and \(3\) share no common factor larger than \(1\), this is fully reduced. The recipe holds \(8\) parts broth for every \(3\) parts cream, so the answer is \(\boxed{8:3}\).
Review 11. A bracelet has 72 beads, copper to jade in the ratio \(4:5\). How many jade beads are on the bracelet?
Show solution
The ratio \(4:5\) tells you copper takes \(4\) parts and jade takes \(5\) parts, so the bracelet is divided into \(4+5=9\) equal parts. Those nine parts share the \(72\) beads evenly, so one part is worth $$72\div 9 = 8 \text{ beads}.$$ Jade is the five-part color, so it gets five of those equal parts, \(5\times 8 = 40\) beads. As a quick check, copper gets \(4\times 8 = 32\) beads, and \(40+32=72\), which matches the whole bracelet. So the number of jade beads is \(\boxed{40}\).
Review 12. A 90-minute playlist splits jazz to folk in the ratio \(7:3\). The jazz piece is the larger share. How many minutes long is the jazz piece?
Show solution
The ratio \(7:3\) tells you the playlist is split into \(7 + 3 = 10\) equal parts, with jazz getting \(7\) of them and folk getting \(3\). Find the value of one part by sharing the ninety minutes among the ten parts, \(90 \div 10 = 9\) minutes per part. The jazz piece is seven parts, so \(7 \times 9 = 63\) minutes. As a quick check, folk gets \(3 \times 9 = 27\) minutes, and \(63 + 27 = 90\), the whole playlist. The jazz piece is \(\boxed{63}\) minutes.
Review 13. A potting soil blend mixes compost to sand in the ratio \(3:8\). One batch uses 15 kg of compost, where one part equals one kilogram. What is the total mass of the batch?
Show solution
The ratio \(3:8\) means compost takes \(3\) parts and sand takes \(8\) parts, so one full repeat of the mix is \(3 + 8 = 11\) kilograms. The compost is the \(3\) part, and here it actually weighs \(15\) kilograms. To find the multiplier, ask what \(3\) is multiplied by to give \(15\). That is \(15 \div 3 = 5\), so the real batch is \(5\) copies of the basic mix. Now scale the whole mix by that same multiplier. One repeat weighs \(11\) kilograms, so the full batch is \(11 \times 5 = 55\) kilograms. You can check it by parts. Compost is \(3 \times 5 = 15\) kilograms and sand is \(8 \times 5 = 40\) kilograms, and \(15 + 40 = 55\) kilograms. The total mass of the batch is \(\boxed{55}\).
Review 14. A reef tank holds 35 fish in the ratio \(3:4\) blue to gold. The keeper adds only blue fish until the count is \(1:1\). How many blue fish must she add?
Show solution
The ratio \(3:4\) divides the tank into \(3+4=7\) equal parts. With \(35\) fish in all, one part is \(35\div 7=5\) fish. So the tank starts with \(3\times 5=15\) blue fish and \(4\times 5=20\) gold fish, which checks out since \(15+20=35\). Now only blue fish get added, so the gold count stays locked at \(20\). An even split of \(1:1\) means blue must climb until it matches the gold, so blue needs to reach \(20\). It began at \(15\), so the keeper adds \(20-15=5\) blue fish. \(\boxed{5}\)
