Prealgebra · Lesson 7.3

Unit Rates and Speed

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A rate compares two quantities in different units, like dollars per pound or miles per hour. The word per signals a rate. A unit rate is the per-one value, how much of the first thing goes with exactly one unit of the second.

Dried cherries cost 20 dollars for 4 pounds. What is the price per pound, in dollars?
Show solution
Four pounds together cost \(20\) dollars, and the cherries all sell at the same rate, so to find the price of one pound I share those dollars equally across the pounds by dividing. That gives \(\tfrac{20 \text{ dollars}}{4 \text{ pounds}} = 5\) dollars per pound. So one pound costs \(\boxed{5}\) dollars.
A reader finishes a 426-page novel in 6 days at a steady pace. How many pages per day did she read?
Show solution
A rate compares two quantities in different units, and the unit rate is the amount per one of the second quantity. The second quantity is days, so divide the pages by the days. $$\frac{426 \text{ pages}}{6 \text{ days}} = 71 \text{ pages per day}.$$ You can check it by going the other way, since \(71 \text{ pages per day} \times 6 \text{ days} = 426\) pages, the whole novel. So she read \(\boxed{71}\) pages per day.
Key idea
Rate vs. unit rate. A rate compares two quantities measured in different units, not the same-kind comparison from 7.1 and 7.2, and the word "per" is the giveaway, dollars per pound, pages per day, miles per hour. The unit rate is the per-one version, the amount of the first quantity that rides with a single unit of the second, and you find it by dividing the first quantity by the second. $$\tfrac{20\,\text{dollars}}{4\,\text{pounds}} = 5\text{ dollars per pound}$$ That is what makes a unit rate so handy, it strips a messy comparison down to one clean number.
Two olive-oil bottles: 15 oz for \$6.00, or 24 oz for \$8.40. Find the price per ounce of each and identify the better buy. What is the price per ounce of the better buy, in dollars?
Show solution
Find the price per ounce of each bottle by dividing dollars by ounces. First bottle: $$\frac{\$6.00}{15 \text{ oz}} = \$0.40 \text{ per ounce}.$$ Second bottle: $$\frac{\$8.40}{24 \text{ oz}} = \$0.35 \text{ per ounce}.$$ Now compare the two unit rates. The second bottle costs \(\$0.35\) for each ounce while the first costs \(\$0.40\) for each ounce, so the second bottle is the better buy because you pay less for the same amount of oil. The price per ounce of the better buy is \(\boxed{0.35}\) dollars.
Key idea
Best buy via unit rate. When two deals come in different sizes at different prices, you cannot rank them at a glance, since a bigger price might only mean a bigger package. Divide each price by its quantity to find what one single unit costs, one ounce or one pound or one anything. Once both are written as a price per one, the comparison is instant, and the better buy is whichever has the lower price per unit. The same per-one move ranks miles per gallon, words per minute, or anything measured this way.
A drive covers 195 miles in 3 hours at a steady pace. What is the speed in miles per hour?
Show solution
Speed is distance divided by time, and the unit miles per hour spells out exactly that division. Put the miles on top and the hours on the bottom, then divide. $$\frac{195 \text{ miles}}{3 \text{ hours}} = 65 \text{ miles per hour}$$ So in every single hour the car covers 65 miles, which is the meaning of the "per one hour". The speed is \(\boxed{65}\) miles per hour.
A van cruises at 48 miles per hour for 7 hours straight. How many miles did it travel?
Show solution
Speed is distance per hour, so to recover the distance you multiply the speed by the number of hours. That gives \(\text{distance} = \text{speed} \times \text{time}\). Plug in the steady pace and the driving time, \(48 \tfrac{\text{miles}}{\text{hour}} \times 7 \text{ hours} = 336 \text{ miles}\). The "hours" in the rate cancels the "hours" of driving, leaving plain miles, which is exactly the unit the question wants. The van covered \(\boxed{336}\) miles.
Key idea
Speed is distance ÷ time. Speed is just the rate of distance to time, and its unit label is an instruction in disguise, the thing before "per" is what you measured and the thing after it is what you divide by. So "miles per hour" means exactly \(\tfrac{\text{miles}}{\text{hours}}\). Rearranged, distance \(= \text{speed} \times \text{time}\) and time \(= \tfrac{\text{distance}}{\text{speed}}\). Because a speed in miles per hour is already a per-one-hour amount, knowing any two of the three gives you the third.
An e-bike can hold 56 mph on a 392-mile route. How many hours does the ride take?
Show solution
Speed says distance divided by time, so to get the time you rearrange it the other way and use time equals distance divided by speed. Drop in the numbers, \(\tfrac{392 \text{ miles}}{56 \text{ miles per hour}} = 7 \text{ hours}\). You can check it by going forward, since \(56 \text{ miles per hour} \times 7 \text{ hours} = 392 \text{ miles}\), which matches the route exactly. The ride takes \(\boxed{7}\) hours.
Key idea
The speed triangle. Speed, distance, and time are three names for one fact that rearranges to give whichever name is missing. $$\text{speed} = \tfrac{d}{t}, \qquad d = s \times t, \qquad t = \tfrac{d}{s}$$ A handy trick is to cover the quantity you are missing and look at the other two. If the missing one sits on top, multiply the two below it. If it sits under the line, divide. As soon as you know any two of the three, the last one drops right out.
A scooter covers 21 miles in 45 minutes. Convert 45 minutes to hours first, then find the speed in miles per hour.
Show solution
First handle the time, staying inside the one dimension of time. Forty five minutes out of the \(60\) minutes in an hour is \(\tfrac{45}{60} = \tfrac{3}{4}\) of an hour, which is \(0.75\) hours. Now the units line up, miles on top and hours on the bottom, so divide. $$\frac{21 \text{ miles}}{0.75 \text{ hours}} = 28 \text{ miles per hour}$$ Dividing by \(0.75\) is the same as dividing by \(\tfrac{3}{4}\), and \(21 \div \tfrac{3}{4} = 21 \times \tfrac{4}{3} = 28\), so the units check out and the speed is \(\boxed{28}\) miles per hour.
A commuter train travels at 64 mph for 105 minutes. Convert 105 minutes to hours, then find the distance in miles.
Show solution
First make the units agree. The speed is in miles per hour, so the time has to be in hours. Since one hour is \(60\) minutes, divide the minutes by \(60\). $$105 \text{ minutes} = \tfrac{105}{60} \text{ hours} = 1.75 \text{ hours}$$ Now the units match, so apply distance \(=\) speed \(\times\) time. $$64 \tfrac{\text{miles}}{\text{hour}} \times 1.75 \text{ hours} = 112 \text{ miles}$$ The "hours" on top and bottom cancel, leaving miles, which is exactly the unit a distance should have. The train traveled \(\boxed{112}\) miles.
Key idea
Unit mismatch fix. The units of a rate quietly tell you what to feed it. A speed in miles per hour wants its time measured in hours, so when a time shows up wearing minutes you turn it into hours first by dividing the minutes by \(60\). That is how \(90\) minutes becomes \(1.5\) hours, \(45\) minutes becomes \(0.75\) hours, and \(40\) minutes becomes \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) hour. Keep the fix inside one kind of measurement, and once the time unit matches the rate the answer falls out in exactly the unit you wanted.
A road trip has two legs: 84 miles in 2 hours, then 161 miles in 3 hours. What is the average speed for the whole trip in miles per hour?
Show solution
Average speed is the total distance covered divided by the total time spent, not the mean of the two leg speeds. First gather the totals. The whole distance is $$84 \text{ miles} + 161 \text{ miles} = 245 \text{ miles}.$$ The whole time is $$2 \text{ hours} + 3 \text{ hours} = 5 \text{ hours}.$$ Now divide the total distance by the total time. $$\frac{245 \text{ miles}}{5 \text{ hours}} = 49 \text{ miles per hour}.$$ Notice why averaging the two speeds would mislead you. The city leg ran at \(\tfrac{84 \text{ miles}}{2 \text{ hours}} = 42\) miles per hour and the highway leg ran at \(\tfrac{161 \text{ miles}}{3 \text{ hours}} \approx 53.7\) miles per hour. The honest trip average sits at \(\boxed{49}\) miles per hour, found from the totals.
Key idea
Average speed for a whole trip is the total distance divided by the total time, the one steady speed that would carry you the same way over that same time. It is almost never the average of the two leg speeds, because you spend more time at the slower speed, and that extra time pulls the average down toward the slow part. So gather the entire trip first, add up every mile and add up every hour, and only then divide. Total over total, not the middle of the two speeds.
A courier rides 210 miles out at 30 mph (headwind) and 210 miles back at 70 mph (tailwind). The simple midpoint of 50 mph is wrong. What is the average speed for the whole round trip?
Show solution
Average speed is total distance divided by total time, not the mean of the two speeds, so start by finding how long each leg takes. Time is distance divided by speed. The trip out covers \(210\) miles at \(30\) miles per hour, which takes $$\frac{210 \text{ miles}}{30 \text{ miles per hour}} = 7 \text{ hours}.$$ The trip back covers the same \(210\) miles at \(70\) miles per hour, which takes $$\frac{210 \text{ miles}}{70 \text{ miles per hour}} = 3 \text{ hours}.$$ Add the times to get the total time, \(7 + 3 = 10\) hours. Add the two equal legs to get the total distance, \(210 + 210 = 420\) miles. Now divide total distance by total time, $$\frac{420 \text{ miles}}{10 \text{ hours}} = 42 \text{ miles per hour}.$$ Notice this lands below \(50\), pulled toward the slow speed because she spent \(7\) of her \(10\) hours crawling into the wind. The average speed for the round trip is \(\boxed{42}\) miles per hour.
Printer A outputs 1,800 pages in 40 minutes; Printer B outputs 1,450 pages in 25 minutes. Find each printer's pages-per-minute rate. What is the rate of the faster printer?
Show solution
Bring each printer to its per one minute rate by dividing pages by minutes. For Printer A, \(\tfrac{1{,}800 \text{ pages}}{40 \text{ minutes}} = 45\) pages per minute. For Printer B, \(\tfrac{1{,}450 \text{ pages}}{25 \text{ minutes}} = 58\) pages per minute. Now they are on equal footing, both measured over a single minute. Since 58 pages per minute is more than 45 pages per minute, Printer B is the faster machine, churning out \(\boxed{58}\) pages every minute.
Fun fact
Ship speed is measured in knots, one knot is one nautical mile per hour. The name comes from sailors counting knots on a rope timed with a sandglass. A knot is itself a unit rate.
Two trucks 300 miles apart drive toward each other at 45 mph and 55 mph. Together they close the gap at \(45+55=100\) mph. How many hours until they meet?
Show solution
Each hour the two trucks together remove both of their speeds from the gap, so the gap closes at the combined speed \(45 + 55 = 100\) miles per hour. The meeting time is the starting gap divided by that closing speed, \(\tfrac{300 \text{ miles}}{100 \text{ miles per hour}} = 3 \text{ hours}\). You can check it. In \(3\) hours the slower truck covers \(45 \times 3 = 135\) miles and the faster truck covers \(55 \times 3 = 165\) miles, and \(135 + 165 = 300\) miles, exactly the road between them. So they meet after \(\boxed{3}\) hours.
Key idea
Two movers approaching each other. When two things head straight toward each other, do not follow each one separately, just watch the gap between them, because every hour that gap loses both distances at once, one bite taken from each end. So the gap shrinks at the combined speed, the two speeds added together, exactly as if one mover covered the whole gap alone at that pace. The time to meet is then the starting gap divided by the combined speed, plain distance over speed with the combined rate doing the work.

Review problems

Review 1. A fruit cart sells 7 pounds of apples for 28 dollars. What is the price per pound, in dollars?
Show solution
A unit rate tells you the cost of one of the second quantity, here one pound. The sign gives a price for many pounds at once, and since every pound costs the same you can share the total evenly. Divide the dollars by the pounds, \(\tfrac{28 \text{ dollars}}{7 \text{ pounds}} = 4\) dollars per pound. You can check this by going backward, since \(4 \text{ dollars per pound} \times 7 \text{ pounds} = 28\) dollars, which matches the sign. So one pound costs \(\boxed{4}\) dollars.
Review 2. A resting heart beats 312 times in 4 minutes. What is the heart rate in beats per minute?
Show solution
A unit rate is the amount per one of the second quantity, here per one minute, so I divide the total beats by the total minutes. $$\frac{312 \text{ beats}}{4 \text{ minutes}} = 78 \text{ beats per minute}$$ So the heart beats at \(\boxed{78}\) beats per minute.
Review 3. Two bags of brown rice: 12 oz for \$3.60, or 20 oz for \$5.00. Find the price per ounce of each and identify the better buy. What is that price per ounce, in dollars?
Show solution
Turn each bag into a price for one ounce by dividing the cost by the number of ounces. The smaller bag gives \(\tfrac{\$3.60}{12 \text{ ounces}} = \$0.30\) per ounce. The larger bag gives \(\tfrac{\$5.00}{20 \text{ ounces}} = \$0.25\) per ounce. Now compare. Since \(0.25\) is less than \(0.30\), the larger bag is the better buy, and its price per ounce is \(\boxed{0.25}\) dollars.
Review 4. Machine A makes 240 parts in 8 hours; Machine B makes 350 parts in 10 hours. What is the rate of the faster machine, in parts per hour?
Show solution
Find the unit rate for each machine by dividing its parts by its hours. For machine A, \(\tfrac{240 \text{ parts}}{8 \text{ hours}} = 30\) parts per hour. For machine B, \(\tfrac{350 \text{ parts}}{10 \text{ hours}} = 35\) parts per hour. Now compare the two per-hour numbers. Machine B makes 35 parts every hour and machine A makes only 30, so machine B is the faster one. Its rate is \(\boxed{35}\) parts per hour.
Review 5. A commuter train covers 280 miles in 5 hours at a steady pace. What is the train's speed in miles per hour?
Show solution
Speed is a rate, the distance covered for each hour of travel. Divide the total distance by the total time. $$\frac{280 \text{ miles}}{5 \text{ hours}} = 56 \text{ miles per hour}$$ A quick check, \(56 \times 5 = 280\) miles, which matches the distance given. So the train's speed is \(\boxed{56}\) miles per hour.
Review 6. A maglev shuttle holds a steady 62 miles per hour for 4 hours. How many miles does it travel?
Show solution
Speed stays steady, so distance equals speed times time. The speed is \(62\) miles per hour and the time is \(4\) hours, so the miles per hour multiply by the number of hours and the hours cancel out, leaving miles. $$62 \text{ miles per hour} \times 4 \text{ hours} = 248 \text{ miles}$$ The shuttle covers \(\boxed{248}\) miles.
Review 7. A tour bus has 414 miles ahead and cruises at 46 miles per hour. How many hours does the trip take?
Show solution
Speed is distance divided by time, so rearranged it gives time equals distance divided by speed. The bus covers 414 miles at 46 miles per hour, so $$\text{time} = \frac{414 \text{ miles}}{46 \text{ miles per hour}} = 9 \text{ hours}.$$ The miles cancel against the miles in "miles per hour" and leave hours, which is exactly the unit we want. So the trip takes \(\boxed{9}\) hours.
Review 8. A cyclist covers 35 miles in 50 minutes. Convert 50 minutes to hours (\(\tfrac{5}{6}\) hr), then find the speed in miles per hour.
Show solution
First put the time into hours. Fifty minutes is \(\tfrac{50}{60} = \tfrac{5}{6}\) of an hour. Speed is distance divided by time, so $$\frac{35 \text{ miles}}{\tfrac{5}{6} \text{ hour}} = 35 \times \frac{6}{5} = \frac{210}{5} = 42 \text{ miles per hour}.$$ The speed is \(\boxed{42}\).
Review 9. A survey drone flies at 72 mph for 25 minutes. Convert 25 minutes to hours first, then find the distance covered in miles.
Show solution
The speed is given per hour, so first change \(25\) minutes into hours. One hour is \(60\) minutes, so \(25\) minutes is \(\tfrac{25}{60}=\tfrac{5}{12}\) of an hour. Distance is speed times time, so \(72 \text{ miles per hour} \times \tfrac{5}{12} \text{ hour} = \tfrac{72 \times 5}{12} = \tfrac{360}{12} = 30\) miles. The drone covers \(\boxed{30}\) miles.
Review 10. A scooter rides at 36 mph to a drop-off 9 miles away. Find the travel time in minutes.
Show solution
Start with time equals distance divided by speed. The distance is \(9\) miles and the speed is \(36\) miles per hour, so the time in hours is $$\frac{9 \text{ miles}}{36 \text{ miles per hour}} = \tfrac{1}{4} \text{ hour}.$$ Now turn that quarter of an hour into minutes. One hour is \(60\) minutes, so multiply. $$\tfrac{1}{4} \text{ hour} \times 60 \text{ minutes per hour} = 15 \text{ minutes}.$$ The trip takes \(\boxed{15}\) minutes.
Review 11. A van drives two legs: 90 miles in 2 hours through hills, then 140 miles in 3 hours on the highway. What is the average speed for the whole route in miles per hour?
Show solution
First gather the whole trip. The total distance is \(90 \text{ miles} + 140 \text{ miles} = 230 \text{ miles}\), and the total time is \(2 \text{ hours} + 3 \text{ hours} = 5 \text{ hours}\). Average speed is the total distance divided by the total time, so $$\frac{230 \text{ miles}}{5 \text{ hours}} = 46 \text{ miles per hour}.$$ Notice this is not the mean of the two leg speeds, which would be \(\tfrac{45 + 46\tfrac{2}{3}}{2} = 45\tfrac{5}{6}\), because the van spent different amounts of time on each leg. The average speed for the whole route is \(\boxed{46}\) miles per hour.
Review 12. A kayaker paddles 60 miles upstream at 20 mph, then 60 miles downstream at 30 mph. The simple midpoint of 25 mph is wrong. What is the average speed for the whole round trip?
Show solution
Average speed is total distance over total time, not the average of the two speeds, because the slower leg eats up more of the clock. Handle each leg with time = distance divided by speed. Upstream takes \(\tfrac{60 \text{ miles}}{20 \text{ miles per hour}} = 3\) hours. Downstream takes \(\tfrac{60 \text{ miles}}{30 \text{ miles per hour}} = 2\) hours. The round trip covers \(60 + 60 = 120\) miles in \(3 + 2 = 5\) hours. So the average speed is \(\tfrac{120 \text{ miles}}{5 \text{ hours}} = 24\) miles per hour. Notice it landed below the midpoint of \(25\), pulled down because the slow upstream leg lasted longer. \(\boxed{24}\)
Review 13. Two drones start from opposite ends of a 264-mile tunnel and fly toward each other at 38 mph and 50 mph. How many hours until they meet?
Show solution
When two movers travel straight toward each other, you do not have to track them one at a time. The space between them is what matters, and that space disappears at the two speeds working together. So first add the speeds to get the combined closing speed, \(38 + 50 = 88\) miles per hour. That means the gap shrinks by \(88\) miles every hour. The gap starts at \(264\) miles, and time is distance divided by speed, so divide the starting gap by the closing speed, \(\tfrac{264 \text{ miles}}{88 \text{ miles per hour}} = 3\) hours. You can check it. In \(3\) hours the first drone covers \(38 \times 3 = 114\) miles and the second covers \(50 \times 3 = 150\) miles, and \(114 + 150 = 264\) miles, exactly the length of the tunnel, so they meet right as the gap closes. The drones meet after \(\boxed{3}\) hours.
Review 14. Two cyclists start 350 miles apart and pedal toward each other at 30 mph and 40 mph. Find when they meet, then calculate how far the faster cyclist has traveled.
Show solution
The two cyclists approach each other, so the distance between them drops at the combined speed. Add the speeds to get \(30 + 40 = 70\) miles per hour. The 350 mile gap closes at that rate, so the time until they meet is \(\tfrac{350 \text{ miles}}{70 \text{ miles per hour}} = 5\) hours. Now use distance equals speed times time for the faster cyclist, who rides at 40 miles per hour. In 5 hours that cyclist covers \(40 \times 5 = 200\) miles. As a check, the slower cyclist covers \(30 \times 5 = 150\) miles, and \(200 + 150 = 350\) miles, the full gap, so the meeting works out. The faster cyclist has traveled \(\boxed{200}\) miles.
