Prealgebra · Lesson 3.1

Factors and Multiples

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A factor of a number is a side of a rectangle with that area, every row the same length, nothing left over. Factors travel in pairs, since each side has a matching partner on the opposite edge whose product is the original number.

A class arranges 24 floor cushions into one full rectangular block, every row the same length, no cushion left over. How many different row counts work?
Show solution
A number of rows works exactly when it splits 24 into equal rows with nothing left over, which is the same as saying that number divides 24 evenly. Walk upward and write each working count beside the row length it forces. $$1\times24,\quad 2\times12,\quad 3\times8,\quad 4\times6$$ Each arrangement names two numbers at once, the rows and the row length, and their product is always 24. Reading both numbers out of every rectangle gives the full list of row counts that work. $$1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 6,\ 8,\ 12,\ 24$$ These are precisely the numbers that divide 24 evenly. We call them the factors, or divisors, of 24, and there are $$\boxed{8}$$ of them. Quick check, the 4-by-6 rectangle holds \(4\times6=24\) cushions, exactly the pile we started with.
Find all the factors of 48 by writing factor pairs \(1\times48, 2\times24, \ldots\) and stopping once the two sides of a pair would cross. How many factors does 48 have?
Show solution
Start at 1 and climb, writing each factor with the partner it forces, since \(d\) rows of length \(48\div d\) is the very same wall. $$1\times48,\quad 2\times24,\quad 3\times16,\quad 4\times12,\quad 6\times8$$ Each small side instantly summons its larger partner, so the factors come out already sorted into matched pairs whose product is 48. Now watch where to stop. After 6 we try 7, but 7 does not divide 48. The next number that divides, 8, is already standing on the right of the pair \(6\times8\). Once the side you are about to test would be larger than the partner it would summon, you have already met every rectangle, just mirrored, so the search is finished. Reading both numbers out of every pair gives all the factors. $$1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 6,\ 8,\ 12,\ 16,\ 24,\ 48$$ There are $$\boxed{10}$$ of them. Quick check, the middle pair \(6\times8=48\), and we stopped right after 6 because 8 was already accounted for.
Key idea
Factor (divisor). A whole number \(f\) is a factor of \(n\), also called a divisor of \(n\), when \(n = f \times k\) for some whole number \(k\), equivalently when \(n \div f\) leaves no remainder, equivalently when \(n\) objects fill a rectangle with \(f\) along one side. Factors travel in pairs, since every side \(f\) is matched by the partner side \(n \div f\) on the opposite edge and the two multiply to \(n\). That pairing is why you never search past \(\sqrt{n}\): below that crossing each small factor you find hands you its large partner for free, and once a side would exceed its own partner you have met every rectangle already, just mirrored.
The factors of 36 pair as \(1\times36, 2\times18, 3\times12, 4\times9, 6\times6\). Does 49 have an odd or even number of factors? What about 35?
Show solution
Walk the pairs that fill a \(36\)-tile rectangle, each side handing you its partner. $$1\times36,\quad 2\times18,\quad 3\times12,\quad 4\times9,\quad 6\times6$$ The first four pairs each name two different factors, eight factors so far. The last one is different. The square \(6\times6\) uses the same number on both sides, so \(6\) is its own partner. It adds a single new factor, not a fresh pair. Counting them all, $$1,2,3,4,6,9,12,18,36,$$ gives \(\boxed{9}\) factors, an odd total. Here is the reason in one breath. Factors come two at a time, a small side and its larger partner, so they would always count up to an even number. The only way to break that is for one factor to pair with itself, \(d\times d=n\), which happens exactly when \(n\) is a perfect square. That lone self-paired factor is counted once, tipping the total to odd. So \(49=7\times7\) self-pairs at \(7\) and has an odd number of factors, while \(35\) splits only into the distinct pairs \(1\times35\) and \(5\times7\), four factors, an even count. Quick check on \(35\). Its factors are \(1,5,7,35\), and \(4\) is indeed even.
Key idea
Perfect squares have an odd factor count. Almost every factor drags a different partner along, so factors come two at a time and the count is even. The one way to break that is for a factor to pair with itself, \(f \times f = n\), which happens exactly when \(n\) is a perfect square. That lone self-paired factor is counted once, tipping the total to odd. So a whole number has an odd number of factors precisely when it is a perfect square, and the unpaired factor is the side of that square. An odd factor count is a fingerprint, and the print belongs to a square.

If \(6\) is a factor of \(36\), then \(36\) is a multiple of \(6\), it sits exactly \(6, 12, 18, 24, 30, 36\) steps out from \(0\). Factor and multiple are one relationship read from its two ends.

A train starts at stone 0 and rolls forward exactly 7 stones at a time. How many of the four stones 28, 35, 50, 63 does it land on?
Show solution
Skip forward by sevens and watch where the markers fall. $$7,\ 14,\ 21,\ 28,\ 35,\ 42,\ 49,\ 56,\ 63,\ \ldots$$ Stones \(28, 35,\) and \(63\) all appear, but \(50\) does not, since the train steps from \(49\) straight to \(56\) and skips right over it. The landings are exactly the stones reached by a whole number of \(7\)-steps. $$28=4\times7,\qquad 35=5\times7,\qquad 63=9\times7$$ Each landing reads two ways. Take \(28\). Read one direction, \(7\) is a factor of \(28\), since \(28=4\times7\). Read the other direction, \(28\) is a multiple of \(7\), since it is four \(7\)-steps out from \(0\). The same double reading holds for \(35\) and \(63\). Stone \(50\) gets neither, because \(7\) is not a factor of it and it is not a multiple of \(7\). The train lands on $$\boxed{28,\ 35,\ 63}.$$
Key idea
Multiple. A whole number \(m\) is a multiple of \(n\) when \(m\) sits a whole number of \(n\)-steps out from \(0\), that is \(m = k \times n\) for some whole number \(k\). This is the exact mirror of a factor: \(m\) is a multiple of \(n\) precisely when \(n\) is a factor of \(m\). Factor and multiple are one arrow, not two relationships. Read it forward and you say multiple, read it backward and you say factor.
For each pair, decide whether the first is a factor of the second, a multiple, both, or neither. (a) 4 and 12 (b) 15 and 5 (c) 8 and 8 (d) 6 and 9
Show solution
Each pair asks one question twice, once in each direction. The first number is a factor of the second when the second is a whole-number times the first, and the first is a multiple of the second when the first is a whole-number times the second. (a) \(4\) and \(12\). Is \(12\) a whole-number times \(4\)? Yes, \(12 = 4 \times 3\), so \(4\) is a factor of \(12\). Going the other way, \(4\) is not \(12\) times any whole number, so it is not a multiple of \(12\). (b) \(15\) and \(5\). Is \(15\) a whole-number times \(5\)? Yes, \(15 = 5 \times 3\), so \(15\) is a multiple of \(5\). It cannot be a factor of \(5\), since a factor never exceeds the number it divides. (c) \(8\) and \(8\). Here \(8 = 8 \times 1\) read both ways at once. \(8\) divides \(8\) evenly, so it is a factor, and \(8\) is one copy of \(8\), so it is a multiple. A number is always both a factor and a multiple of itself. (d) \(6\) and \(9\). Does \(6\) divide \(9\) evenly? No, \(9 \div 6\) leaves a remainder. Is \(6\) a whole-number times \(9\)? No, \(6\) is smaller than \(9\). So the answer is neither. Notice that \(4\) and \(12\) tell the same story as \(15\) and \(5\), just told from opposite ends. Whenever one number is a factor of another, that other number is automatically a multiple of the first. Factor and multiple are not two relationships, they are one relationship read in two directions.
Priya lists every factor of 20; Owen lists every multiple of 20. Who finishes, who never does, and why? Write the list that can be finished, and say whether 20 appears on both lists.
Show solution
Priya finishes, Owen never does. Every factor of \(20\) is a side of a rectangle of area \(20\), and a side can never be longer than the area allows, so no factor can climb past \(20\) itself. Hunting in pairs gives \(1\times 20\), \(2\times 10\), \(4\times 5\), and the list closes. $$1,\ 2,\ 4,\ 5,\ 10,\ 20$$ That is six factors, the largest being \(20\), and Priya is done. Owen's list has no last entry. The multiples of \(20\) are \(20, 40, 60, 80, 100, \ldots\) and from any one of them he can always take one more step of \(20\) and reach a new one. There is no biggest multiple, so he never finishes. Notice \(20\) appears on both lists at once. It is its own largest factor, since \(20=1\times 20\), and it is its own smallest positive multiple, since \(20=20\times 1\). And right at the front of Priya's list sits \(1\), a factor of \(20\) because one long row of \(20\) is a perfectly good rectangle. The finishable list is $$\boxed{1,\ 2,\ 4,\ 5,\ 10,\ 20}$$ Quick check, each pairs cleanly, \(2\times 10=20\) and \(4\times 5=20\), so nothing is missing and nothing is extra.
Key idea
Factors are finite; multiples are infinite. Every factor of \(n\) is at least \(1\) and at most \(n\) itself, since its partner side is no smaller than \(1\), so the factor list closes. But from any multiple you can always step once more and reach a fresh one, so multiples stretch forever. Three quiet facts ride along. \(1\) is a factor of every \(n\), since \(n = 1 \times n\). Every \(n\) is both a factor and a multiple of itself, since \(n = n \times 1\). And \(0\) is a multiple of every \(n\), since \(0 = n \times 0\), so it waits at the front of every multiple list.
A lighthouse flashes every 8th second. How many flashes fall in the range 100 through 250 inclusive?
Show solution
The flashes sit exactly at the multiples of \(8\), so this is a counting question about a stretch of the multiples line. Rather than list them all, count up to the top of the window and then subtract everything that falls below the bottom. Up to \(250\), the largest multiple of \(8\) that fits is \(248=31\times 8\), since \(32\times 8=256\) overshoots. So \(31\) multiples of \(8\) sit in \(1\) through \(250\). Below \(100\), the largest multiple of \(8\) is \(96=12\times 8\), so \(12\) multiples of \(8\) sit in \(1\) through \(99\). The flashes actually inside the window are what is left after removing the early ones. $$31-12=\boxed{19}$$ One caution worth naming, always check whether the endpoints are themselves multiples, since an endpoint that lands on a multiple has to be counted. Here neither \(100\) nor \(250\) is a multiple of \(8\), so no endpoint needs special care. Quick check, the first flash inside the window is \(104=13\times 8\) and the last is \(248=31\times 8\), and the flashes run \(13\times 8\) up through \(31\times 8\), which is \(31-13+1=19\) of them. The two counts agree.
Key idea
Counting multiples in a range. The number of multiples of \(n\) from \(1\) up to a value \(V\) is how many whole \(n\)-steps fit, which is \(V \div n\) with the remainder thrown away. So count the multiples up to the top of the range, then subtract the multiples below the bottom, and what survives lies inside. Round the division down for the largest multiple no greater than a bound, and up for the smallest multiple at least as big as a bound. Keep one eye on whether each endpoint is itself a multiple, since that decides whether it joins the count.

Now picture two multiples of \(n\) added or subtracted. The shared factor \(n\) can be pulled out, so the result is still a multiple of \(n\). But adding a non-multiple onto a multiple always lands off the trail.

A machine dispenses and swallows tokens only in packs of 9. Starting at 0, a player receives 153, then 72 more, then returns 18. Find the final count, and explain why it must be a multiple of 9.
Show solution
The straight count is \(153+72-18=207\). Now for the part that matters. Every amount the machine touches is a whole number of \(9\)s, so write them that way, \(153=9\times17\), \(72=9\times8\), and \(18=9\times2\). The running count is then $$9\times17+9\times8-9\times2=9\times(17+8-2)=9\times23=\boxed{207}.$$ The \(9\) was a shared factor of every piece, so it slid out in front of the whole combination and left a whole number, \(23\), behind. That is the heart of it. Adding or subtracting whole numbers of \(9\)s can only ever build another whole number of \(9\)s. In general, if two amounts are \(9a\) and \(9b\), then \(9a+9b=9(a+b)\) and \(9a-9b=9(a-b)\), still a whole number of \(9\)-steps. Picture it as the machine stacking full rows of \(9\). Two such blocks set on top of each other make one taller block, still full rows of \(9\) all the way up. Quick check, \(9\times23=207\), and \(153+72-18=207\) too.
A turnstile reads 240, a multiple of 12. A glitch adds 17. Explain why 240 + 17 cannot be a multiple of 12, then find the smallest positive number to add to restore a multiple of 12.
Show solution
Suppose, just to test it, that \(240+17\) were itself a multiple of \(12\). Both \(240\) and that supposed total would then be multiples of \(12\), so their difference would be a multiple of \(12\) as well, by the closure you just found. But that difference is exactly \(17\), and \(17\) is not a multiple of \(12\). The supposition collapses, so \(240+17=257\) is not a multiple of \(12\). The same reasoning works every time. A multiple plus a non-multiple is always a non-multiple. To repair the count, find the next multiple of \(12\) above \(257\). Since \(12\times21=252\) and \(12\times22=264\), the count \(257\) sits just past \(252\), and the next marker is \(264\). So you must add $$264-257=\boxed{7}.$$ Here is the quiet idea underneath. The stray \(17\) is \(5\) past a multiple of \(12\), since \(17=12+5\). That \(5\) is all that knocked the count off the trail, so the count now sits \(5\) past a marker and needs \(12-5=7\) more to reach the next one. The remainder of the stray number alone decided the whole gap. Check, \(257+7=264=12\times22\).
Key idea
Closure. Add or subtract two multiples of \(n\) and you land on a multiple of \(n\) again, because the shared \(n\) factors out: \(na \pm nb = n(a \pm b)\). Turn it around for the sharper half. A multiple of \(n\) plus a number that is not a multiple of \(n\) can never be a multiple of \(n\), because if it were, subtracting the two multiples would force the stray number to be a multiple too, and it is not. Once you toss a non-multiple onto a multiple you are stuck off the trail until you add back exactly the missing amount.
Envelope \(A\) holds a multiple of 8 dollars; envelope \(B\) holds a multiple of 8 dollars. Show that the two cannot total exactly 250 dollars, and find the nearest achievable total.
Show solution
You do not need either amount. Both envelopes hold multiples of \(8\), so by closure their sum \(A+B\) is forced to be a multiple of \(8\) as well. The whole question becomes a single fact about \(250\). Is \(250\) a multiple of \(8\)? Since \(8\times31=248\) and \(8\times32=256\), the number \(250\) lands between two neighbouring multiples of \(8\) and is not one of them. So no two such envelopes can ever sum to \(250\), and the announcement is impossible. The nearest total they could actually reach is the closer of those two neighbours, $$\boxed{248},$$ which is just \(2\) below \(250\) and is reachable, for instance with \(A=240\) and \(B=8\). Notice what closure did here. It let you prove something about a sum whose two pieces you could not even see.

Suppose the multiples of one number coincide with the multiples of a factor it contains. A multiple of 12, is it automatically a multiple of 3? The next problem settles which direction this inheritance runs.

Bundles of 12 beeswax sheets, with \(12 = 4 \times 3\). (1) Must a whole number of bundles be a multiple of 3? (2) Of 4? (3) Is every multiple of 3 a multiple of 12? Give a counterexample.
Show solution
This whole problem is one idea, chaining. A multiple of a multiple is a multiple, so once \(12\) is built from \(3\) and \(4\), anything built from \(12\) inherits those smaller pieces. (1) A delivery of full bundles is some whole number of bundles, say \(n\) of them, so the sheet count is \(n\times12\). Replace each bundle by its \(3\) rows of \(4\). Every bundle becomes \(4+4+4\), a whole number of threes, so $$n\times12 = n\times(4\times3) = (n\times4)\times3,$$ which is \(3\) multiplied by a whole number. So the count is a multiple of \(3\). The threes that hid inside one bundle are still there when you stack many bundles. (2) Yes, by the same chaining, and now using the other layout. Each bundle is \(3\) rows of \(4\), so $$n\times12 = n\times(3\times4) = (n\times3)\times4,$$ which is \(4\) times a whole number. So a multiple of \(12\) is always a multiple of \(4\) too. (3) No, the arrow does not run backward. Being a multiple of \(3\) does not force a pile to be a multiple of \(12\). A pile of $$\boxed{6}$$ sheets splits cleanly into \(2\) groups of \(3\), so it is a multiple of \(3\), yet \(6\) is less than one full bundle of \(12\), so it is not a multiple of \(12\). Quick check, \(6=3\times2\) confirms the multiple of \(3\), and \(6\) sitting between \(0\) and \(12\) confirms it is not a multiple of \(12\). (The very smallest multiple of \(3\) that misses \(12\) is actually \(3\) itself, so \(6\) is just one easy witness, not the smallest.) Chaining only flows one way, from the bigger building block down to the pieces inside it, never back up.
Key idea
Chaining. If \(a\) is a factor of \(b\), then every multiple of \(b\) is automatically a multiple of \(a\). The multiple carries \(b\) inside it, and \(b\) carries \(a\) inside it, so \(a\) is in there too: stepping in \(b\)-sized steps is secretly stepping in \(a\)-sized steps. A multiple of \(12\), for instance, is always a multiple of \(1, 2, 3, 4, 6\) as well, every factor of \(12\) inherited for free. The chain runs only downward toward the smaller factors, never upward, which is why a multiple of \(3\) need not be a multiple of \(12\). This one inheritance is exactly what the divisibility shortcuts of the next section lean on.
A secret number is a multiple of 6, a perfect square, and lies between 100 and 400 inclusive. Find every possible value.
Show solution
The two conditions, perfect square and multiple of \(6\), squeeze together harder than they first look. Suppose the number is \(k\times k\) and is a multiple of \(6\). A multiple of \(6\) carries a factor of \(2\) and a factor of \(3\). But \(2\) and \(3\) cannot be conjured from nowhere. For the square \(k\times k\) to contain them, the side \(k\) must already supply a factor of \(2\) and a factor of \(3\) on its own, which makes \(k\) a multiple of \(6\). Then \(k\times k\) is a multiple of \(6\times6=36\). So the hunt collapses to squaring multiples of \(6\) and keeping the ones in range. Walking them, \(6^2=36\) is below \(100\), \(12^2=144\) and \(18^2=324\) both land between \(100\) and \(400\), and \(24^2=576\) overshoots. Nothing else can appear, since any valid number had to be the square of a multiple of \(6\) and those are the only such squares in the window. The secret number is therefore $$\boxed{144 \text{ or } 324}.$$ Check, \(144=12^2\) and \(144=6\times24\), while \(324=18^2\) and \(324=6\times54\), so both are genuine perfect squares and genuine multiples of \(6\) inside the range. The pleasing twist is that asking for a square that is also a multiple of \(6\) quietly forced a multiple of \(36\).
Key idea
Summary. One relationship carried this lesson, read from two ends. A factor of \(n\) is a side of a rectangle of area \(n\), so factors arrive in pairs that multiply to \(n\), with a perfect square's self-paired side the lone exception that tips its factor count to odd. Turn the arrow around and the small number is a factor while the large one is a multiple. From there flow the quiet facts: finitely many factors but infinitely many multiples, \(1\) divides everything, \(0\) is a multiple of everything. To count multiples in a window, count up to the top and subtract the ones below the bottom. By closure, sums and differences of multiples of \(n\) stay multiples of \(n\). By chaining, a multiple of a multiple is a multiple, the inheritance running only toward smaller factors, which is exactly what the divisibility tests ahead are built on.

Review problems

Review 1. A school fits 40 lockers into one full rectangular block. List all factor pairs of 40 and report how many factors there are in all.
Show solution
Climb from \(1\) and let each small factor summon its partner, since a side of \(d\) lockers forces \(40\div d\) on the opposite edge. $$1\times 40,\qquad 2\times 20,\qquad 4\times 10,\qquad 5\times 8$$ The number \(3\) is skipped since it does not divide \(40\), and so are \(6\) and \(7\). Each small side that works instantly hands you its larger partner, so the factors come out already sorted into matched pairs whose product is \(40\). Now watch where to stop. After \(5\) the next try would be \(6\), but \(6\) would summon a partner smaller than \(6\), one already standing inside the pair \(5\times 8\). Once the side you are about to test would be larger than the partner it summons, you have already met every block, just mirrored, so the search is finished. Reading both numbers out of every pair and sorting them gives $$1,\ 2,\ 4,\ 5,\ 8,\ 10,\ 20,\ 40,$$ which is \(\boxed{8}\) factors. They come in four clean pairs, no side meeting itself, so the count is even, exactly what you expect from a number that is not a perfect square. Check the middle pair, \(5\times 8 = 40\), the very pile we started with. The general idea is that factors travel in partner pairs that multiply to the number, so you only hunt up to where a side would meet its own partner, and each small factor you find drags its larger partner along for free.
Review 2. A ring-toss scoreboard only ever adds 8 points per throw. Five friends claim scores of 24, 30, 48, 56, and 60. How many of these could the game actually show?
Show solution
Since the board moves up \(8\) at a time and never by anything smaller, a score can show up only if it is built from a whole number of \(8\)-point throws, which is exactly what it means to be a multiple of \(8\). So I test each score by dividing by \(8\) and asking whether it comes out clean. $$24 = 8 \times 3, \quad 48 = 8 \times 6, \quad 56 = 8 \times 7$$ Those three land squarely on a multiple of \(8\), so they are scores the game really could display, reached in \(3\), \(6\), and \(7\) successful throws. The other two miss. Dividing \(30\) by \(8\) gives \(3\) with \(6\) left over, since \(8 \times 3 = 24\) and \(8 \times 4 = 32\) overshoots, so \(30\) is stranded between two multiples and can never appear. Dividing \(60\) by \(8\) gives \(7\) with \(4\) left over, since \(8 \times 7 = 56\) and \(8 \times 8 = 64\), so \(60\) is impossible too. The possible scores are $$\boxed{24,\ 48,\ 56}$$ Check the largest one, \(8 \times 7 = 56\), seven clean throws and not a point wasted. The general idea is that a number is a multiple of \(8\) exactly when \(8\) divides it with zero remainder, so testing for a multiple is just one division and a glance at what, if anything, is left over.
Review 3. Decide whether each claim about 61 is true or false. (a) 1 is a factor of 61. (b) 61 is a multiple of 61. (c) 0 is a multiple of 61. (d) 61 is a factor of 0. Enter your four verdicts in order.
Show solution
The whole question turns on one definition, that \(b\) is a factor of \(a\) when \(a = b \times (\text{whole number})\), and in the same breath \(a\) is then a multiple of \(b\). So I just hunt for that equation in each claim. For (a), \(61 = 1 \times 61\), so \(1\) is a factor of \(61\), and in fact \(1\) is a factor of every whole number since multiplying by \(1\) changes nothing. True. For (b), \(61 = 61 \times 1\), so \(61\) is a multiple of itself, just as every number is, since one copy of a number is already a multiple of it. True. Now (c) and (d) are not two separate facts at all, they are the single line $$0 = 61 \times 0$$ read from its two ends. Read with \(0\) as the result, it says \(0\) is a multiple of \(61\), so (c) is True, and indeed \(0\) is a multiple of every number, because zero copies of anything still land on \(0\). Read with \(61\) as the thing doing the multiplying, the very same line says \(61\) is a factor of \(0\), so (d) is True, and every number is a factor of \(0\). All four claims hold, so the verdict is \(\boxed{\text{all four true}}\). Check the quiet equation that powers the last two, \(61 \times 0 = 0\), one sentence wearing two names. The general idea is that \(1\) divides everything, every number is a multiple of itself, and \(0\) is the universal multiple that every number divides, with "factor" and "multiple" being the same relationship seen from opposite sides.
Review 4. Find the smallest and largest factors of 56, and give a one-line reason for each.
Show solution
Every factor of \(56\) travels with a partner, the two side lengths of a rectangle, and the two numbers always multiply to \(56\). Start at the bottom end. The number \(1\) divides every whole number cleanly, since \(56=1\times56\), so \(1\) is always present and nothing smaller than \(1\) is a positive whole number, which makes \(1\) the smallest factor of any number. Now climb to the top end. Suppose some factor \(f\) were larger than \(56\) itself. Its partner would be \(56\div f\), and since \(f\) is bigger than \(56\) that partner would have to be a whole number smaller than \(1\), which simply does not exist. So no factor can exceed the number, and the largest factor is the number reaching all the way up to its own size, namely \(56\), paired with \(1\) on the other side. $$\text{smallest}=1\times56,\qquad \text{largest}=56\times1$$ The two bookends are therefore $$\boxed{1\ \text{and}\ 56}.$$ Check, \(1\times56=56\) and \(56\times1=56\), both rebuild the original bed, and the same reasoning would hand back \(1\) and the number itself for any whole number you choose. The general idea is that the factors of a number are bookended by \(1\) on the low end and the number on the high end, because \(1\) divides everything and a factor can never climb past the number it divides.
Review 5. Two bookends sit on every factor list. \(1\) is always the smallest factor, the single full-length row, and the number itself is always the largest, since no side of a rectangle can outrun its own area. So a factor of \(n\) is always trapped between \(1\) and \(n\), which is why a factor hunt can never wander off forever.
Review 6. Which of 72, 81, and 98 has an odd number of factors? Explain through self-pairing.
Show solution
The quick way to spot an odd factor count is to remember how factors travel, since each factor \(d\) of a number \(n\) walks arm in arm with its partner \(n \div d\), and the two multiply back to \(n\). Pair off this way and the factors normally come two at a time, which makes the total even. The only escape is a factor whose partner is itself, where \(d = n \div d\), and that forces \(d \times d = n\). So a number breaks the even pattern exactly when it is a perfect square, because then its square root stands alone with no separate partner to swap with. The whole question becomes which of \(72\), \(81\), \(98\) is a whole number times itself. Squaring the nearby whole numbers gives $$8 \times 8 = 64, \qquad 9 \times 9 = 81, \qquad 10 \times 10 = 100,$$ and only \(81\) appears, sitting right at \(9 \times 9\). For \(81\) the side \(9\) pairs with itself, the lone unpaired factor that tips the count to odd, while the rest still pair up as \(1 \times 81\) and \(3 \times 27\). The number \(72\) falls between \(64\) and \(81\) and \(98\) falls between \(81\) and \(100\), so neither is any whole number squared, which means every one of their factors finds a distinct partner and their counts stay even. The puzzle with an odd number of factors is \(\boxed{81}\). Check that \(81\) really is special by listing its factors, \(1, 3, 9, 27, 81\), which is five of them, an odd count, with \(9\) standing in the middle as its own partner. The general idea is that an odd factor count is the fingerprint of a perfect square, since only a perfect square owns a factor that pairs with itself.
Review 7. A ribbon is marked every 23 centimeters. What is the largest multiple of 23 below 500?
Show solution
The largest multiple of \(23\) below a cap is found by fitting as many whole \(23\)-steps under the cap as I can, so I divide and round down. $$500 \div 23 = 21 \text{ remainder } 17$$ The remainder \(17\) tells me \(500\) is not itself a multiple of \(23\), and that exactly \(21\) full steps fit beneath it. Those \(21\) steps land the cut at $$21 \times 23 = 483.$$ The very next mark would be \(22 \times 23 = 506\), which sails past \(500\), so there is no multiple of \(23\) hiding between \(483\) and \(500\). The tailor cuts at \(\boxed{483}\) centimeters. Check, \(483\) is under \(500\) and \(483 + 23 = 506\) is over, so \(483\) really is the last mark before the cap. The general idea is that the greatest multiple of \(n\) below a bound is the bound divided by \(n\) with the remainder dropped, then multiplied back by \(n\).
Review 8. A bakery packs rolls in trays of 26. What is the smallest three-digit multiple of 26?
Show solution
Climb the multiples of 26 one tray at a time and watch for the moment the total first reaches three digits. $$26\times 1 = 26,\qquad 26\times 2 = 52,\qquad 26\times 3 = 78,\qquad 26\times 4 = 104$$ Three trays give \(78\), which is still two digits, so it falls short of the \(100\) mark. The very next tray jumps to \(104\), the first total that reaches three digits, so the answer is \(\boxed{104}\). Here is the quicker way to see it without listing, which is the real idea. A three-digit number is anything from \(100\) upward, so I ask how many trays it takes to reach \(100\) by dividing, \(100 \div 26 \approx 3.8\). Since I cannot bake \(3.8\) trays, I round that count up to the next whole tray, \(4\), and then \(26 \times 4 = 104\). Rounding up is the key move, because rounding down to \(3\) would land below the bound at \(78\). Check the answer is genuinely a multiple of 26 by confirming \(26 \times 4 = 104\), and that \(104 \div 26 = 4\) with nothing left over. The general idea is that the smallest multiple of \(n\) at or above a bound \(B\) comes from dividing \(B\) by \(n\) and rounding the quotient up to a whole number, then multiplying back by \(n\).
Review 9. Two-digit codes lit from inside are multiples of 6 whose digits sum to 6. How many codes light up?
Show solution
The slick move is to build the codes from the digit rule first, since the condition that the two digits add to \(6\) is far more restrictive than the multiple of \(6\) condition and leaves only a tiny list to check. A two-digit code has a tens digit and a units digit, and the tens digit cannot be \(0\), so let the tens digit run from \(1\) up to \(6\). Each choice forces the units digit, because the two digits must total \(6\), which gives the pairs \(1\) and \(5\), then \(2\) and \(4\), then \(3\) and \(3\), then \(4\) and \(2\), then \(5\) and \(1\), then \(6\) and \(0\). A tens digit of \(7\) or more is impossible since even a units digit of \(0\) would already push the sum past \(6\), so the complete list of two-digit numbers with digit sum \(6\) is $$15,\ 24,\ 33,\ 42,\ 51,\ 60,$$ exactly six of them and no more. Now bring in the multiple of \(6\) requirement, and here is the shortcut that makes the whole thing easy. A number is a multiple of \(6\) precisely when it is both even and a multiple of \(3\), and every code on this short list has digit sum \(6\), which is itself a multiple of \(3\), so every one of these six is already divisible by \(3\) for free. The only gate left is being even, so I just keep the codes that end in an even digit, which are \(24\), \(42\), and \(60\), while \(15\), \(33\), and \(51\) end in odd digits and fall away. The lit floats therefore carry the codes $$\boxed{24,\ 42,\ 60}.$$ Check each one, \(24=6\times 4\) with \(2+4=6\), \(42=6\times 7\) with \(4+2=6\), and \(60=6\times 10\) with \(6+0=6\), all three passing both rules, and since I built the candidate list from every possible digit pair that sums to \(6\), nothing was skipped and no fourth code can exist. The general idea is that when two rules overlap, you start from whichever rule produces the shorter list, and a digit sum being fixed at \(6\) handily settles the divisibility by \(3\) on its own, so the search collapses to a single even or odd check.
Review 10. Find the largest three-digit multiple of 15 whose three digits are all different.
Show solution
Since \(15 = 3 \times 5\), a multiple of 15 must be divisible by both 3 and 5, so it has to end in \(0\) or \(5\). The largest three-digit multiple of 15 sits right below 1000, and \(1000 \div 15\) is not a whole number, so I climb down to the nearest one. $$990 = 15 \times 66$$ That is the greatest three-digit multiple of 15, but its digits are \(9, 9, 0\), and the two 9s repeat, so it fails the all-different rule. Stepping down by 15 lands me on the next candidate. $$975 = 15 \times 65$$ Its digits are \(9, 7, 5\), all three different from one another, so this one qualifies. Because I am walking downward from the very top and 975 is the first survivor I meet, nothing larger can work, and the answer is \(\boxed{975}\). Check that 975 really is a multiple of 15 by testing both factors, its digit sum is \(9 + 7 + 5 = 21\) which is divisible by 3, and it ends in 5 so it is divisible by 5, so it is divisible by 15. The general idea is that you can hunt for the best number meeting a rule by searching from the extreme end and walking inward, stopping the instant the extra constraint, here all digits distinct, is finally satisfied.
Review 11. An amphitheater has 7 rows with 14, 28, 42, … seats. Find the total number of seats.
Show solution
Start by writing down what sits in each row as you climb. The front row has \(14\), the next has \(28\), then \(42\), and since every row adds another \(14\), the seven counts are \(14, 28, 42, 56, 70, 84, 98\). The slow way is to add those seven biggish numbers straight across, but there is a much friendlier move waiting in plain sight, since every one of them is \(14\) times its row position counting from the front, $$14 = 14\times 1,\quad 28 = 14\times 2,\quad 42 = 14\times 3,\quad \dots,\quad 98 = 14\times 7.$$ Because the same factor of \(14\) rides along with every term, I can pull it out front and rewrite the whole total as \(14\times(1+2+3+4+5+6+7)\). Now the hard part has shrunk to adding the plain counting numbers \(1\) through \(7\), and the pairing trick finishes that in a breath, since \(1+7=8\), \(2+6=8\), and \(3+5=8\), which is three pairs of \(8\) with the lonely \(4\) sitting in the middle, so \(3\times 8 + 4 = 28\). That leaves a single clean multiplication, $$14\times 28 = \boxed{392}.$$ Check by grouping the original row counts the same friendly way, \(14+98 = 112\), \(28+84 = 112\), \(42+70 = 112\), three pairs of \(112\) plus the middle row of \(56\), and \(3\times 112 + 56 = 336 + 56 = 392\), which matches. The general idea is that when a list of quantities all share a common step, you can factor that step out front and only sum the small counting numbers that remain, often pairing them from the ends to land the answer in your head.
Review 12. A runner claps at every 13th metre. How many claps fall strictly between metres 150 and 850?
Show solution
The claps sit exactly at the multiples of \(13\), so this is a counting question about a stretch of the multiples line. Rather than list every post, count up to the top of the window and then subtract everything that falls below the bottom. For the top, the largest multiple of \(13\) that stays strictly under \(850\) is \(845 = 13\times 65\), since \(13\times 66 = 858\) sails past \(850\). So \(65\) multiples of \(13\) sit from the start of the line up through \(845\). For the bottom, the largest multiple of \(13\) that stays at or below \(150\) is \(143 = 13\times 11\), since \(13\times 12 = 156\) already pokes above \(150\). So \(11\) multiples of \(13\) sit below the window. The claps actually inside the stretch are what is left after removing those early ones. $$65 - 11 = \boxed{54}$$ One caution always worth naming, check whether either endpoint is itself a multiple, since an endpoint landing on a marker would have to be counted. Here \(150 = 13\times 11 + 7\) and \(850 = 13\times 65 + 5\), so neither \(150\) nor \(850\) is a multiple of \(13\), and the word strictly costs us nothing. Quick check, the first clap inside the window is \(156 = 13\times 12\) and the last is \(845 = 13\times 65\), and the markers run from the \(12\)th through the \(65\)th, which is \(65 - 12 + 1 = 54\) of them. Both counts agree. The general idea is that to count the multiples of \(n\) inside a range you count how many sit up to the top and subtract how many sit below the bottom, keeping one eye on whether each endpoint is itself a multiple.
Review 13. Two trucks each carry a multiple of 15 apples. Show 400 is impossible, then find the nearest reachable total.
Show solution
Write each truck as a multiple of \(15\), say \(15a\) apples on one and \(15b\) on the other, where \(a\) and \(b\) are whole numbers. Their total factors a \(15\) right out front. $$15a+15b=15(a+b)$$ Since \(a+b\) is again a whole number, the total is itself a multiple of \(15\). This is closure, adding two multiples of \(15\) can never escape the multiples of \(15\). So the only question is whether \(400\) is one of them. Walk the multiples of \(15\) near \(400\). $$15\times 26=390,\qquad 15\times 27=405$$ The number \(400\) lands strictly between these two neighbors, with no multiple of \(15\) in the gap, so \(400\) is not a multiple of \(15\) and the dispatcher's claim is impossible. Now hunt for the reachable total nearest \(400\). The candidates are exactly its two neighbors, \(390\) and \(405\). Measure each gap. $$400-390=10,\qquad 405-400=5$$ So \(405\) wins, sitting only \(5\) away while \(390\) is twice as far. It remains to confirm \(405\) is genuinely reachable as a sum of two trucks, each a multiple of \(15\). It is, for instance one truck with a single crate and the other with \(26\) crates. $$15+390=405$$ So the total cannot be \(400\), and the nearest total two such trucks could actually carry is \(\boxed{405}\). Check, \(405=15\times 27\), a clean multiple of \(15\), and \(15+390\) builds it from two valid trucks. The general idea is that a closed set, here the multiples of \(15\), can prove a target is out of reach the moment the target fails the membership test, and the best you can do is the nearest member to it.
Review 14. A bead artist works only in kits of 24. For each of 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, and 16, decide whether the bead count must be a multiple of that number. How many of the six are guaranteed?
Show solution
The whole game is to see which numbers are baked into \(24\), because a multiple of \(24\) carries along every factor that \(24\) already has, and nothing more is promised. Start by laying out the factors of \(24\) in pairs, $$1\times 24,\qquad 2\times 12,\qquad 3\times 8,\qquad 4\times 6,$$ so the factors of \(24\) are \(1,\ 2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 6,\ 8,\ 12,\ 24\). Here is the chaining idea that does all the work. The bead count is some \(24\times k\). If \(d\) is a factor of \(24\), then \(24 = d\times m\) for a whole number \(m\), so the count is \(24\times k = d\times(mk)\), and that shows \(d\) divides the count. In words, whatever divides \(24\) divides every multiple of \(24\), and the inheritance only flows downward to \(24\)'s own factors. Run the six sizes through this test. Since \(2,\ 3,\ 4,\) and \(8\) are all on the factor list of \(24\), the count must be a multiple of each of them. But \(9\) is not a factor of \(24\), because \(24 = 9\times 2 + 6\) leaves a remainder of \(6\), and \(16\) is not a factor either, since \(24 = 16 + 8\) leaves a remainder of \(8\). So neither \(9\) nor \(16\) is guaranteed, and the cleanest counterexample for both is \(24\) itself, which is a multiple of \(24\) yet is divisible by neither \(9\) nor \(16\). Collecting the guaranteed sizes gives $$\boxed{2,\ 3,\ 4,\ 8}.$$ Check the trickiest survivor, \(8\), on a real multiple, \(24\times 5 = 120 = 8\times 15\), divisible just as promised, while \(120\) is not a multiple of \(16\) since \(120 = 16\times 7 + 8\). The general idea is that being a multiple of \(n\) only forces divisibility by the factors of \(n\), so divisibility chains downward through \(n\)'s factor list and never reaches a number that is not a factor of \(n\).
Review 15. Find every perfect square that is a multiple of 45 and stays below 2000. How many such numbers are there?
Show solution
First listen to what "multiple of 45" really demands. Since \(45 = 9 \times 5 = 3^2 \times 5\), every multiple of 45 must carry at least two factors of 3 and one factor of 5. Now suppose the code is a perfect square, so I can write it as \(s \times s\) for some whole number \(s\). The clever part is to track each prime through that product. The square needs a factor of 5, but 5 is a prime, so the two copies of \(s\) cannot share it loosely; for \(s\times s\) to contain a 5 at all, one of the copies of \(s\) must contain a 5, and then the other copy contains one too, so the square actually carries \(5\times 5\). The same pressure works for 3. The square needs a factor of 3, and since one copy of \(s\) must supply it, both copies do, so the square carries \(3 \times 3\) just from a single 3 living in \(s\). Putting those together, \(s\) must contain a 3 and a 5, which means \(s\) is a multiple of \(3 \times 5 = 15\), and therefore the square is a multiple of \(15^2 = 225\). That is the quiet twist, the two separate demands for a 9 and a 5 combine and force the code to be a multiple of 225, not merely of 45. Now I only have to walk through the multiples of 15 and square them, watching the 2000 ceiling. $$15^2 = 225,\qquad 30^2 = 900,\qquad 45^2 = 2025$$ The first two sit safely below 2000, but \(45^2 = 2025\) overshoots, and every later square is larger still, so the search is complete. Nothing was left out, because any valid code had to be \(15^2\) times a perfect square, and \(225 \times 1\) and \(225 \times 4\) are the only such values under 2000. The codes are \(\boxed{225 \text{ and } 900}\). Check that each truly fits, since \(225 = 15^2\) is a square and \(225 = 45 \times 5\) is a multiple of 45, while \(900 = 30^2\) is a square and \(900 = 45 \times 20\) is a multiple of 45. The general idea is that a perfect square must contain every prime to an even power, so demanding a square that is a multiple of a number forces the square to be a multiple of that number's "squared-up" version, here 45 rounding up to 225.
