Prealgebra · Lesson 7.5

Converting Units

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

When the top and bottom of a fraction are the same real amount in different units, that fraction equals \(1\). Such a fraction is a conversion factor. Multiplying by it swaps the unit label without changing the true quantity.

One hand equals 4 inches. A pony is 36 inches tall. Multiply by \(\frac{1\,\text{hand}}{4\,\text{in}}\) so the inches cancel. How many hands tall is the pony?
Show solution
Start with the height you know and multiply by the conversion factor that puts inches on the bottom, so inches can cancel and hands remain. $$36 \text{ in} \times \frac{1 \text{ hand}}{4 \text{ in}} = \frac{36 \cancel{\text{ in}}}{1} \times \frac{1 \text{ hand}}{4 \cancel{\text{ in}}}$$ The inches cancel, leaving only hands as the unit. Now handle the numbers, \(\frac{36}{4} = 9\), so the pony is \(9\) hands tall. $$36 \div 4 = \boxed{9}$$
Key idea
A conversion factor equals 1. A conversion factor is a fraction whose top and bottom are the exact same real amount written in two different units, like \(\tfrac{12\,\text{in}}{1\,\text{ft}}\). Since \(12\) inches is one foot, that fraction is one amount divided by itself, which is \(1\). Multiplying any quantity by \(1\) cannot change how much you have, so the real amount sits untouched while the only thing that swaps is the unit it is wearing. It changes the costume, not the amount.
One fathom equals 6 feet. An anchor rope is 23 fathoms long. Using the factor \(\frac{6\,\text{ft}}{1\,\text{fathom}}\), convert to feet. How many feet long is the rope?
Show solution
Start with the length you were given and attach a conversion factor that turns fathoms into feet. The factor is \(\frac{6 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ fathom}}\), which equals \(1\) because 6 feet and 1 fathom are the same distance. Orient it so the unwanted unit cancels. Fathom is on top in the starting amount and on the bottom in the factor, so it cancels. $$23 \text{ fathom} \times \frac{6 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ fathom}} = 23 \times 6 \text{ ft} = 138 \text{ ft}$$ The fathom labels divided out and feet is the only unit left, exactly what the question asked for. The rope is \(\boxed{138}\) feet long.
One stone equals 14 pounds. Luggage weighs 91 pounds. Build a conversion factor so the pounds cancel and stone survives. What is the weight in stone? (The answer is not a whole number.)
Show solution
Start with the weight you have and multiply by the conversion factor turned so that pounds sits on the bottom, ready to cancel the pounds you started with. $$91 \text{ pounds} \times \frac{1 \text{ stone}}{14 \text{ pounds}}$$ The word "pounds" appears once on top and once on the bottom, so it cancels and only stone remains. $$91 \cancel{\text{ pounds}} \times \frac{1 \text{ stone}}{14 \cancel{\text{ pounds}}} = \frac{91}{14} \text{ stone}$$ Now reduce the fraction. Both 91 and 14 share a factor of 7, since \(91 = 7 \times 13\) and \(14 = 7 \times 2\). $$\frac{91}{14} = \frac{7 \times 13}{7 \times 2} = \frac{13}{2}$$ So the luggage weighs \(\frac{13}{2}\) stone, which is the same as \(6.5\) stone. The conversion factor did its job perfectly even though the answer landed between two whole numbers. $$\boxed{13/2}$$
270 loose jars need to be packed into crates of 18. Use \(\frac{1\,\text{crate}}{18\,\text{jars}}\) so jars cancel and crates survive. How many full crates do 270 jars make?
Show solution
Pick the factor that puts the unwanted unit, jars, on the bottom so it can cancel, which is \(\frac{1 \text{ crate}}{18 \text{ jars}}\). Write the starting amount with its unit and multiply. $$270 \text{ jars} \times \frac{1 \text{ crate}}{18 \text{ jars}}$$ The word jars sits on top in the first piece and on the bottom in the factor, so it cancels and leaves crates standing. $$270 \cancel{\text{ jars}} \times \frac{1 \text{ crate}}{18 \cancel{\text{ jars}}} = \frac{270}{18} \text{ crates}$$ Now just do the arithmetic. $$\frac{270}{18} = 15$$ So \(270\) jars fill \(\boxed{15}\) full crates. Notice the other factor, \(\frac{18 \text{ jars}}{1 \text{ crate}}\), would have left you with units of \(\frac{\text{jars}^2}{\text{crate}}\), which is nonsense, and that is exactly how the units tell you when you have flipped the factor the wrong way.
Key idea
Two orientations, one true fact. One fact about two units gives you two conversion factors, the fraction and its flip, and both equal \(1\) because the top and bottom are the same amount in different units. So never guess which way to write it. Put the unit you want gone on the opposite side from where it sits now, so it cancels and leaves the unit you actually wanted standing. The units check your work for free, since if the surviving unit is the one the question asked for, the setup was right.
A racecourse is 4 furlongs. Use \(1\,\text{furlong} = 220\,\text{yd}\) and \(1\,\text{yd} = 3\,\text{ft}\). Chain two factors through yards. How many feet is 4 furlongs?
Show solution
There is no single furlong to feet factor, so chain two of them through yards. Each conversion factor is a ratio equal to \(1\), oriented so the unwanted unit lands on the bottom and cancels. Lay the chain out left to right. $$4 \text{ furlong} \times \frac{220 \text{ yd}}{1 \text{ furlong}} \times \frac{3 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ yd}}$$ Watch the units cancel, furlong against the first denominator and yard against the second, which leaves only feet. $$4 \cancel{\text{ furlong}} \times \frac{220 \text{ yd}}{1 \cancel{\text{ furlong}}} \times \frac{3 \text{ ft}}{1 \cancel{\text{ yd}}} = 4 \times 220 \times 3 \text{ ft}$$ Now multiply the numbers across. $$4 \times 220 = 880, \qquad 880 \times 3 = 2{,}640$$ So \(4\) furlongs is \(\boxed{2640}\) feet.
Key idea
Chaining conversion factors. When no single factor reaches from where you start to where you want to land, hop through a middle unit instead, stringing several factors together and letting each hand off to the next. Every factor is its own \(1\), so multiplying a whole row of them still multiplies by \(1\) and the real amount never budges. Set each factor so the unit you just produced sits on the bottom and cancels, and keep going until only the target unit is left standing.
A bolt of cloth is 6 yards long. Using \(1\,\text{yd} = 3\,\text{ft}\) and \(1\,\text{ft} = 12\,\text{in}\), chain two factors. How many inches long is the cloth?
Show solution
There is no yard-to-inch fact, so chain two conversion factors and let the units do the bookkeeping. First turn yards into feet, then feet into inches. Each factor is just a ratio equal to \(1\), oriented so the unwanted unit cancels. $$6 \text{ yd} \times \frac{3 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ yd}} \times \frac{12 \text{ in}}{1 \text{ ft}}$$ Watch the units cancel. The "yd" on top cancels the "yd" on the bottom, and the "ft" that appears cancels the "ft" below it, leaving only "in". $$6 \times \frac{3}{1} \times \frac{12}{1} \text{ in} = 6 \times 36 \text{ in} = 216 \text{ in}$$ The cloth is \(\boxed{216}\) inches long.
A lantern festival lasts 1 fortnight (14 days). Using \(1\,\text{day} = 24\,\text{hr}\) and \(1\,\text{hr} = 60\,\text{min}\), convert through three factors. How many minutes long is 1 fortnight?
Show solution
Chain the three conversion factors so each unwanted unit cancels in turn, leaving minutes. $$1 \text{ fortnight} \times \frac{14 \text{ days}}{1 \text{ fortnight}} \times \frac{24 \text{ hr}}{1 \text{ day}} \times \frac{60 \text{ min}}{1 \text{ hr}}$$ Watch the relay. The first factor cancels \(\text{fortnight}\), the second cancels \(\text{day}\), and the third cancels \(\text{hr}\), so the only unit left standing is \(\text{min}\). $$1 \times \frac{14}{1} \times \frac{24}{1} \times \frac{60}{1} \text{ min}$$ Multiply the top numbers along the line. First \(14 \times 24 = 336\), then \(336 \times 60 = 20{,}160\). So 1 fortnight is \(\boxed{20160}\) minutes.
Fun fact
In 1999 NASA lost the \$300-million Mars Climate Orbiter because one team sent pound-force seconds while the software expected newton seconds, off by 4.45. A conversion error destroyed a spacecraft.
Key idea
Exchange rates are conversion factors. A rate like "17 pesos per dollar" is really a ratio, and a ratio can be flipped over and still describe the same deal. That makes it a conversion factor exactly like \(\tfrac{12\,\text{in}}{1\,\text{ft}}\), so you can stand it on its head and multiply, choosing the side that cancels the unit you want gone. Converting money, a price, or a speed is all one move, multiply by the rate turned the right way and watch the leftover unit be the one you asked for.
An arcade: 1 dollar buys 4 tokens, and 1 token buys 3 prize tickets. No direct dollar-to-ticket rate is posted. Chain two factors through tokens. How many prize tickets are 9 dollars worth?
Show solution
There is no dollars-to-tickets rate given, so chain the two exchanges through tokens. Start with the \(9\) dollars and multiply by a conversion factor that cancels dollars, then by one that cancels tokens. $$9 \text{ dollars} \times \frac{4 \text{ tokens}}{1 \text{ dollar}} \times \frac{3 \text{ tickets}}{1 \text{ token}}$$ The dollars in the first numerator cancel the dollars in the first factor's denominator, and the tokens left behind cancel the tokens in the second factor's denominator. $$9 \, \cancel{\text{dollars}} \times \frac{4 \, \cancel{\text{tokens}}}{1 \, \cancel{\text{dollar}}} \times \frac{3 \text{ tickets}}{1 \, \cancel{\text{token}}}$$ Only tickets survive, which is the target unit. Now multiply the numbers, \(9 \times 4 = 36\) tokens worth of value, then \(36 \times 3 = 108\) tickets. $$9 \times 4 \times 3 = 108 \text{ tickets}$$ So \(9\) dollars is worth \(\boxed{108}\) prize tickets.
A cyclist rides at 45 miles per hour. Use \(1\,\text{mi} = 5{,}280\,\text{ft}\) and \(1\,\text{hr} = 3{,}600\,\text{s}\) to convert. What is the speed in feet per second?
Show solution
Write the speed as a fraction, then multiply by two conversion factors, each equal to \(1\), arranged so the unwanted units cancel. $$\frac{45 \text{ mi}}{1 \text{ hr}} \times \frac{5{,}280 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ mi}} \times \frac{1 \text{ hr}}{3{,}600 \text{ s}}$$ The \(\cancel{\text{mi}}\) on top cancels the \(\cancel{\text{mi}}\) on the bottom, and the \(\cancel{\text{hr}}\) on the bottom cancels the \(\cancel{\text{hr}}\) on top, so what survives is feet over seconds, exactly the unit we want. $$\frac{45 \times 5{,}280}{3{,}600} \text{ ft/s} = \frac{237{,}600}{3{,}600} \text{ ft/s} = 66 \text{ ft/s}$$ A conversion factor is just a ratio doing a job, and chaining two of them lets us fix both halves of a rate at once. \(\boxed{66}\)
Key idea
Converting a rate requires two factors. A rate is not one quantity sitting alone, it is a ratio with one unit riding on top and a different unit underneath, so a single conversion factor only ever fixes one of them. To rebuild both, multiply by two factors at once, one aimed at the top unit and one aimed at the bottom, each flipped so its unwanted unit lands across from a matching copy and cancels. Whatever new-top-over-new-bottom is left standing is the converted rate.
A syrup has density \(1.5\,\text{g/mL}\). A tank holds 4 liters. Convert liters to mL, then apply the density. How many grams of syrup does the tank hold?
Show solution
Start with the volume and chain on two conversion factors, each oriented so the unwanted unit sits on the bottom and cancels. First send liters to milliliters, then let the density turn milliliters into grams. $$4 \text{ liters} \times \frac{1000 \text{ mL}}{1 \text{ liter}} \times \frac{1.5 \text{ g}}{1 \text{ mL}}$$ Watch the units fall away. The "liter" on top cancels the "liter" on the bottom, and then the "mL" cancels the "mL", leaving grams. $$4 \, \cancel{\text{liters}} \times \frac{1000 \, \cancel{\text{mL}}}{1 \, \cancel{\text{liter}}} \times \frac{1.5 \text{ g}}{1 \, \cancel{\text{mL}}}$$ With only grams remaining, multiply the numbers across the top. \(4 \times 1000 = 4000\) milliliters, and \(4000 \times 1.5 = 6000\) grams. The tank holds \(\boxed{6000}\) grams of syrup.
A sticker covers 12 square inches. Use \(1\,\text{in} = 2.5\,\text{cm}\). The factor must be applied twice since area is length \(\times\) length. How many square centimeters is the sticker?
Show solution
A square inch is \(1 \text{ in} \times 1 \text{ in}\), so to convert it you must convert each side from inches to centimeters. That means the conversion factor \(\frac{2.5 \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ in}}\) gets used twice, once for each inch. Start with the area and carry the units along. $$12 \text{ in}^2 \times \frac{2.5 \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ in}} \times \frac{2.5 \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ in}}$$ Write \(\text{in}^2\) as \(\text{in} \times \text{in}\) and watch the cancelling. One \(\text{in}\) on top cancels with the \(\text{in}\) in the first factor, and the other \(\text{in}\) cancels with the \(\text{in}\) in the second factor. $$12 \, \cancel{\text{in}} \, \cancel{\text{in}} \times \frac{2.5 \text{ cm}}{1 \, \cancel{\text{in}}} \times \frac{2.5 \text{ cm}}{1 \, \cancel{\text{in}}}$$ Both inches are gone and two centimeter units are left over, which multiply to \(\text{cm}^2\). The numbers are \(12 \times 2.5 \times 2.5\). The linear factor \(2.5\) appears twice, so it is squared, \(2.5 \times 2.5 = 6.25\). Then \(12 \times 6.25 = 75\). The sticker is \(\boxed{75}\) square centimeters.
Key idea
Area conversion squares the linear factor. An area is a length times a length, so when you convert it each length brings its own conversion factor, and using the factor twice is the same as squaring it. So if \(1\,\text{unit} = k\) of another, then \(1\,\text{square unit} = k^2\) square units, not just \(k\). The classic slip is squaring the number but forgetting the units do the same thing. Only multiplying the factor by itself cancels both copies of the unwanted unit and leaves a real square unit behind.
On a game island: \(1\,\text{brindle} = 5\,\text{floons}\), \(1\,\text{floon} = 4\,\text{ticks}\), \(1\,\text{tick} = 6\,\text{motes}\). A path is 3 brindles long. How many motes?
Show solution
Strange names, same machine. Lay the three given facts down as conversion factors, each a ratio equal to 1, and orient each one so the unwanted unit sits on the bottom and cancels. Brindle goes to floon, floon to tick, tick to mote. $$3 \text{ brindle} \times \frac{5 \text{ floons}}{1 \text{ brindle}} \times \frac{4 \text{ ticks}}{1 \text{ floon}} \times \frac{6 \text{ motes}}{1 \text{ tick}}$$ Watch the units fall away in order. The brindle on top cancels the brindle on the bottom, then floon cancels floon, then tick cancels tick. $$3 \cancel{\text{ brindle}} \times \frac{5 \cancel{\text{ floons}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ brindle}}} \times \frac{4 \cancel{\text{ ticks}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ floon}}} \times \frac{6 \text{ motes}}{1 \cancel{\text{ tick}}}$$ Only motes are left standing, exactly the unit the rulebook asked for. Now just multiply the numbers across the top, \(3 \times 5 \times 4 \times 6\). $$3 \times 5 = 15, \quad 15 \times 4 = 60, \quad 60 \times 6 = 360$$ The path is \(\boxed{360}\) motes long. You never needed to picture a single one of these units. Give the method true relationships and a target, and it does the rest.
Key idea
The method is unit-agnostic. Every conversion you have done, the single ones, the long chains, money and speeds, densities, areas, even invented units, is the exact same move. Multiply by a fraction that secretly equals \(1\), with its top and bottom the same real amount written two ways, aimed so the unwanted unit sits on the bottom and cancels. Because the fraction is really \(1\), the amount never changes, only its costume does, and the units are your safety check the whole way, since if they cancel down to the right label the chain is correct.

Review problems

Review 1. In the highlands, 1 league equals 3 miles. The Ridgeback Trail is 8 leagues long. How many miles long is the trail?
Show solution
Start with the amount you have and multiply by a conversion factor that cancels leagues. Since 1 league equals 3 miles, the factor \(\frac{3 \text{ miles}}{1 \text{ league}}\) is just a fancy way of writing 1, and it puts leagues on the bottom so it cancels. $$8 \text{ leagues} \times \frac{3 \text{ miles}}{1 \text{ league}} = 8 \times \frac{3 \text{ miles}}{1} = 24 \text{ miles}$$ The \(\text{leagues}\) on top and bottom cancel, leaving miles, so the trail is \(\boxed{24}\) miles.
Review 2. One gross equals 144 pencils. A warehouse has 360 loose pencils. How many gross is that?
Show solution
Start with the \(360\) pencils and choose the conversion factor that puts gross on top so pencils cancels. $$360 \text{ pencils} \times \frac{1 \text{ gross}}{144 \text{ pencils}} = \frac{360}{144} \text{ gross}$$ The word pencils sits on the top of one piece and the bottom of the other, so it cancels and leaves gross. Now reduce the leftover number. Both \(360\) and \(144\) share the factor \(72\), and \(360 \div 72 = 5\) while \(144 \div 72 = 2\). $$\frac{360}{144} = \frac{5}{2}$$ So the warehouse has \(\boxed{5/2}\) gross of pencils, which is two and a half gross.
Review 3. One ream equals 500 sheets. A dock has 3,500 sheets. Using the factor \(\frac{1\,\text{ream}}{500\,\text{sheets}}\), how many reams is that?
Show solution
Start with the amount you have and multiply by the conversion factor oriented so sheets cancels. $$3{,}500 \text{ sheets} \times \frac{1 \text{ ream}}{500 \text{ sheets}} = \frac{3{,}500}{500} \text{ reams}$$ The unit sheets appears on top and bottom, so it cancels and reams remains. Now divide. $$\frac{3{,}500}{500} = 7$$ So \(3{,}500\) sheets is \(\boxed{7}\) reams.
Review 4. Each brick is 9 inches long. A wall is 162 inches long. Using the factor \(\frac{1\,\text{brick}}{9\,\text{in}}\) so the inches cancel, how many bricks long is the wall?
Show solution
Start with the wall length and attach the conversion factor with inches on the bottom, so the inches cancel and bricks are left. $$162 \text{ in} \times \frac{1 \text{ brick}}{9 \text{ in}}$$ The inch units cancel, top and bottom. $$162 \cancel{\text{ in}} \times \frac{1 \text{ brick}}{9 \cancel{\text{ in}}} = \frac{162}{9} \text{ bricks}$$ Now divide. $$\frac{162}{9} = 18 \text{ bricks}$$ So the wall is \(\boxed{18}\) bricks long.
Review 5. A surveyor has \(1\,\text{mile} = 8\,\text{furlongs}\) and \(1\,\text{furlong} = 40\,\text{rods}\). A boundary is 2 miles. Chain two factors and convert to rods. How many rods long is the boundary?
Show solution
Start from the quantity you have, \(2\) miles, and multiply by conversion factors that each equal \(1\), choosing the orientation that cancels the unit you want to get rid of. First cancel miles using \(\frac{8 \text{ furlongs}}{1 \text{ mile}}\), then cancel furlongs using \(\frac{40 \text{ rods}}{1 \text{ furlong}}\). $$2 \text{ mile} \times \frac{8 \text{ furlong}}{1 \text{ mile}} \times \frac{40 \text{ rod}}{1 \text{ furlong}}$$ The mile on top cancels the mile on the bottom, and the furlong on top cancels the furlong on the bottom, so only rods survive. Now multiply the numbers, \(2 \times 8 \times 40 = 640\). The boundary line is \(\boxed{640}\) rods long.
Review 6. A recipe needs 5 gallons of water, measured in pints. Given \(1\,\text{gal} = 4\,\text{qt}\) and \(1\,\text{qt} = 2\,\text{pt}\), chain two factors. How many pints are in 5 gallons?
Show solution
Start with \(5\) gallons and chain two conversion factors so the unwanted units cancel. Orient each factor with the unit you want to remove on the bottom. $$5 \text{ gallon} \times \frac{4 \text{ quart}}{1 \text{ gallon}} \times \frac{2 \text{ pint}}{1 \text{ quart}}$$ The gallons cancel against the gallon on the bottom of the first factor, and the quarts cancel against the quart on the bottom of the second factor, leaving only pints. $$5 \times 4 \times 2 = 40 \text{ pint}$$ So \(5\) gallons is \(\boxed{40}\) pints.
Review 7. How many seconds are in 1 full day? Chain three factors using \(1\,\text{day} = 24\,\text{hr}\), \(1\,\text{hr} = 60\,\text{min}\), \(1\,\text{min} = 60\,\text{s}\).
Show solution
Write 1 day and multiply by three conversion factors, each turned so the unwanted unit is on the bottom and cancels. $$1 \text{ day} \times \frac{24 \text{ hours}}{1 \text{ day}} \times \frac{60 \text{ minutes}}{1 \text{ hour}} \times \frac{60 \text{ seconds}}{1 \text{ minute}}$$ The day cancels with the day in the first factor, the hour cancels with the hour in the second factor, and the minute cancels with the minute in the third factor, leaving only seconds. $$1 \times 24 \times 60 \times 60 \text{ seconds} = 86{,}400 \text{ seconds}$$ So one full day holds \(\boxed{86400}\) seconds.
Review 8. A collection is 3 stacks. Each stack holds 5 bundles, each bundle holds 4 packs, each pack holds 12 cards. Chain three factors and convert stacks to cards. How many cards is that?
Show solution
Chain three conversion factors, one per step, each oriented so the old unit cancels. Start from the 3 stacks and walk down to cards. $$3 \text{ stacks} \times \frac{5 \text{ bundles}}{1 \text{ stack}} \times \frac{4 \text{ packs}}{1 \text{ bundle}} \times \frac{12 \text{ cards}}{1 \text{ pack}}$$ Watch the units cancel down the line. The \(\text{stacks}\) cancel against \(\text{stack}\), the \(\text{bundles}\) cancel against \(\text{bundle}\), and the \(\text{packs}\) cancel against \(\text{pack}\), leaving only \(\text{cards}\). $$3 \times \cancel{\text{stacks}} \times \frac{5 \,\cancel{\text{bundles}}}{1 \,\cancel{\text{stack}}} \times \frac{4 \,\cancel{\text{packs}}}{1 \,\cancel{\text{bundle}}} \times \frac{12 \text{ cards}}{1 \,\cancel{\text{pack}}}$$ Now multiply the numbers. \(3 \times 5 = 15\), then \(15 \times 4 = 60\), then \(60 \times 12 = 720\). So the collection holds \(\boxed{720}\) cards.
Review 9. A festival booth exchanges 1 euro for 12 ride tokens. A visitor exchanges 15 euros. Using the factor \(\frac{12\,\text{tokens}}{1\,\text{euro}}\), how many tokens does the visitor get?
Show solution
Start with the amount you have and multiply by the conversion factor, lined up so the unwanted unit cancels. $$15 \text{ euros} \times \frac{12 \text{ tokens}}{1 \text{ euro}}$$ The word "euro" appears on top of the starting amount and on the bottom of the factor, so it cancels. $$15 \cancel{\text{ euros}} \times \frac{12 \text{ tokens}}{1 \cancel{\text{ euro}}} = 15 \times 12 \text{ tokens}$$ That leaves only tokens, which is the target unit. Multiplying gives \(15 \times 12 = 180\) ride tokens. \(\boxed{180}\)
Review 10. A summer fair: 1 dollar buys 20 game chips, and 1 chip earns 3 prize points. A guest exchanges 7 dollars. Chain two factors through chips. How many prize points is that worth?
Show solution
Treat each exchange as a conversion factor, a fraction equal to 1 because its top and bottom are the same value in two units. Orient each one so the unwanted unit sits on the bottom and cancels. Starting from \(7\) dollars, $$7 \text{ dollars} \times \frac{20 \text{ chips}}{1 \text{ dollar}} \times \frac{3 \text{ points}}{1 \text{ chip}}.$$ The dollars cancel against the first factor, and the chips cancel against the second, $$7 \cancel{\text{ dollars}} \times \frac{20 \cancel{\text{ chips}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ dollar}}} \times \frac{3 \text{ points}}{1 \cancel{\text{ chip}}} = 7 \times 20 \times 3 \text{ points}.$$ Multiplying gives \(7 \times 20 = 140\) chips along the way, then \(140 \times 3 = 420\) prize points. So \(7\) dollars is worth \(\boxed{420}\) prize points.
Review 11. A maglev train travels at 72 km/hr. Convert to m/s using \(1\,\text{km} = 1{,}000\,\text{m}\) and \(1\,\text{hr} = 3{,}600\,\text{s}\). What is the speed in meters per second?
Show solution
Build the chain so the unwanted units cancel one at a time. Start with the given speed and attach both conversion factors, each a ratio equal to 1. $$\frac{72 \text{ km}}{1 \text{ hr}} \times \frac{1{,}000 \text{ m}}{1 \text{ km}} \times \frac{1 \text{ hr}}{3{,}600 \text{ s}}$$ The \(\text{km}\) on top cancels the \(\text{km}\) on the bottom of the second factor, and the \(\text{hr}\) on the bottom cancels the \(\text{hr}\) on top of the third factor. $$\frac{72 \cancel{\text{ km}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ hr}}} \times \frac{1{,}000 \text{ m}}{1 \cancel{\text{ km}}} \times \frac{1 \cancel{\text{ hr}}}{3{,}600 \text{ s}}$$ What survives is meters on top and seconds on the bottom, which is meters per second. Now multiply the numbers. $$\frac{72 \times 1{,}000}{3{,}600} \text{ m/s} = \frac{72{,}000}{3{,}600} \text{ m/s} = 20 \text{ m/s}$$ So the train moves at \(\boxed{20}\) meters per second.
Review 12. A runner's pace is 15 mph. Convert to feet per minute using \(1\,\text{mi} = 5{,}280\,\text{ft}\) and \(1\,\text{hr} = 60\,\text{min}\). What is the speed in feet per minute?
Show solution
Start with the given rate and attach one conversion factor for each unit you want to change. To turn miles into feet, multiply by \(\frac{5{,}280 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ mi}}\), which puts miles on the bottom so it cancels the miles on top. To turn hours into minutes, multiply by \(\frac{1 \text{ hr}}{60 \text{ min}}\), which puts hours on top so it cancels the hours on the bottom. $$\frac{15 \text{ mi}}{1 \text{ hr}} \times \frac{5{,}280 \text{ ft}}{1 \text{ mi}} \times \frac{1 \text{ hr}}{60 \text{ min}}$$ Cancel the matching units. $$\frac{15 \cancel{\text{ mi}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ hr}}} \times \frac{5{,}280 \text{ ft}}{1 \cancel{\text{ mi}}} \times \frac{1 \cancel{\text{ hr}}}{60 \text{ min}}$$ What is left is feet over minutes, exactly the unit we want. Now do the arithmetic. $$\frac{15 \times 5{,}280}{60} \text{ ft/min} = \frac{79{,}200}{60} \text{ ft/min} = 1{,}320 \text{ ft/min}$$ The runner is moving at \(\boxed{1320}\) feet per minute.
Review 13. A jar holds 8 tablespoons of maple syrup. The density is \(\frac{21\,\text{g}}{1\,\text{tablespoon}}\). How many grams of syrup does the jar hold?
Show solution
Start with the amount you know and attach the density as a conversion factor, oriented so tablespoons cancels and grams stays. $$8 \text{ tablespoons} \times \frac{21 \text{ g}}{1 \text{ tablespoon}}$$ The \(\text{tablespoon}\) on top cancels with the \(\text{tablespoon}\) on the bottom. $$8 \, \cancel{\text{tablespoons}} \times \frac{21 \text{ g}}{1 \, \cancel{\text{tablespoon}}} = (8 \times 21) \text{ g}$$ Now finish the multiplication, \(8 \times 21 = 168\), and the only surviving unit is grams. So the jar holds \(\boxed{168}\) grams of syrup.
Review 14. A workshop floor covers 6 square feet. Convert to square inches using \(1\,\text{ft} = 12\,\text{in}\). Remember the linear factor gets applied twice for area. What is the area in square inches?
Show solution
Start with the area and multiply by the conversion factor \(\frac{12 \text{ in}}{1 \text{ ft}}\) twice, since area carries two lengths. $$6 \text{ ft}^2 \times \frac{12 \text{ in}}{1 \text{ ft}} \times \frac{12 \text{ in}}{1 \text{ ft}}$$ Notice the \(\text{ft}^2\) on top cancels with the two \(\text{ft}\) on the bottom, leaving \(\text{in}^2\). $$6 \times 12 \times 12 \text{ in}^2 = 6 \times 144 \text{ in}^2 = 864 \text{ in}^2$$ So the floor is \(\boxed{864}\) square inches.
