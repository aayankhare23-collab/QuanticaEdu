Prealgebra · Lesson 9.3

Percent Increase and Decrease

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

In 9.2 you took a percent of a fixed number. Now the number moves. A \(30\%\) rise means the new amount is \(130\%\) of the original, one multiply by \(1.30\), the multiplier. A drop of \(20\%\) leaves \(80\%\), a single multiply by \(0.80\).

A garden trail of \(240\) m was extended by \(35\%\). Find \(35\%\) of \(240\), then add it to \(240\). How many meters of trail are there now?
Show solution
Walk it the slow way first. The increase is \(35\%\) of the original \(240\) meters, so using part \(=\) percent \(\times\) whole gives $$0.35 \times 240 = 84 \text{ meters added.}$$ Now add that piece back onto where you started, \(240 + 84 = 324\). There is a faster route that lands in the same place. Keeping all of the original is \(100\%\) of it, and adding \(35\%\) more makes \(135\%\) in all, so the multiplier is \(1 + 0.35 = 1.35\). That gives \(240 \times 1.35 = 324\) in one step. Either way the trail is now \(\boxed{324}\) meters long.
Key idea
Increase adds onto the original. A \(p\%\) rise does not glue the percent on, it takes \(p\%\) of the amount you started with and adds it back onto that same start, so the new total is the original plus a slice of itself. The slice is always measured from where you began, never where you land. Since the original is already the full \(100\%\) and you piled \(p\%\) more on top, the new amount is \((100 + p)\%\) of the start.
A streaming channel had \(480\) subscribers and grew by \(35\%\). The new count is \(135\%\) of \(480\). How many subscribers does the channel have now?
Show solution
A \(35\%\) increase keeps the whole original and adds \(35\%\) more, so the new count is \(100\% + 35\% = 135\%\) of \(480\). Written as a multiplier that is \(1 + \tfrac{35}{100} = 1.35\). Multiply all at once. $$480 \times 1.35 = 648$$ Check it against the add-on path. \(35\%\) of \(480\) is \(0.35 \times 480 = 168\), and \(480 + 168 = 648\), the same result. The channel now has \(\boxed{648}\) subscribers.
Key idea
Increase multiplier. Growing by \(p\%\) keeps all \(100\%\) of the original and stacks \(p\%\) on top, which is \((100+p)\%\) of the start, so you can do it in one step by multiplying by \(1 + \frac{p}{100}\). The \(1\) hangs onto the whole original amount and the extra fraction adds the new slice. A \(30\%\) rise is a single multiply by \(1.30\).
A coastal marsh had \(350\) wading birds at dawn. By midday, \(12\%\) had flown off. The survivors are \(88\%\) of \(350\). How many birds remain at midday?
Show solution
A drop of \(12\%\) takes away \(12\%\) and leaves the other \(88\%\), since \(100\% - 12\% = 88\%\). So the multiplier for a \(12\%\) decrease is \(1 - 0.12 = 0.88\), and the midday count is $$350 \times 0.88 = 308.$$ The slower route agrees. First \(12\%\) of \(350\) is \(0.12 \times 350 = 42\) birds that flew off, and the survivors are \(350 - 42 = 308\). Both paths give the same flock, so \(308\) wading birds are still present at midday. \(\boxed{308}\)
A kayak rental costs \(\$90\) a day. A \(40\%\) discount removes \(40\%\) and leaves \(60\%\). What is the discounted price in dollars?
Show solution
A \(40\%\) decrease multiplies by \(1 - \tfrac{40}{100} = 1 - 0.40 = 0.60\), the share of the price that survives the discount. So the new price is $$90 \times 0.60 = 54.$$ You can check it the slow way. Forty percent of \(90\) is \(0.40 \times 90 = 36\), and subtracting gives \(90 - 36 = 54\), the same answer. The multiplier just folds the subtracting into one step. \(\boxed{54}\)
Key idea
Decrease multiplier. When you cut \(p\%\) off, what is left is the rest of the hundred, so removing \(12\%\) leaves \(88\%\) standing, a single multiply by \(0.88\). A \(p\%\) decrease multiplies by \(1 - \frac{p}{100}\), which is why a \(20\%\) discount is just \(80\%\) of the price. Increase lifts the multiplier above \(1\) and decrease pulls it below \(1\), but it is the same one-multiply move both ways.
A podcast went from \(250\) plays to \(320\). The change is \(70\) plays. Divide by the original \(250\), not the new \(320\). What percent increase was this? Give the number only.
Show solution
First find how much the plays grew, \(320 - 250 = 70\) more plays. A percent change is that change measured against the original amount, so divide by the \(250\) you started with, not the \(320\) you ended with. That gives the fraction \(\tfrac{70}{250}\). To read it as a percent, multiply by \(100\), so $$\frac{70}{250} \times 100 = 28.$$ You can check it with the multiplier idea. A \(28\%\) increase scales the original by \(1.28\), and \(250 \times 1.28 = 320\), which lands exactly on the second episode. The question names the direction, so the answer is the positive size of the increase, \(\boxed{28}\).
A library had \(640\) visitors on a sunny Saturday and \(480\) on a rainy one, a drop of \(160\). Divide by the original \(640\). What percent decrease was this? Give the number only.
Show solution
The drop in visitors is \(640 - 480 = 160\). A percent change always measures that change against the original amount, so divide by \(640\), not by \(480\).

$$\frac{640 - 480}{640} = \frac{160}{640} = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25 = 25\%$$

You can check it with the multiplier. A drop of \(25\%\) means the rainy Saturday kept \(1 - 0.25 = 0.75\) of the sunny crowd, and \(0.75 \times 640 = 480\), which matches. If you had divided by \(480\) instead you would get about \(33\%\), but that answers a different question, how much you would need to grow to climb back to \(640\). For the decrease from the original, the answer is \(\boxed{25}\).
Key idea
Percent change \(=\) change \(\div\) original. Find how much it changed, the new minus the old, then divide by the original amount and write the result as a percent. It is an increase when the number grew and a decrease when it shrank. The original is always the yardstick in the denominator, since the question is how big the change was compared to where you started. Dividing by the new amount would quietly answer a different question.
A bamboo shoot grew from \(14\) cm to \(35\) cm, a change of \(21\) cm. Since \(21 > 14\), the percent will sail past \(100\%\). What percent increase was this? Give the number only.
Show solution
First find the change, \(35 - 14 = 21\) centimeters. A percent change is measured against the original, so divide by \(14\), not by \(35\). $$\frac{35 - 14}{14} = \frac{21}{14} = \frac{3}{2} = 1.5$$ As a percent that is \(150\%\). You can sanity check it with the multiplier. Going from \(14\) to \(35\) means multiplying by \(\tfrac{35}{14} = 2.5\), and a multiplier of \(2.5\) is the same as \(1 + 1.5\), a \(150\%\) increase. That fits the warning in the problem. A plain doubling to \(28\) would have been a \(100\%\) increase, and this rose past double, so the percent climbs above \(100\%\). \(\boxed{150}\)
Key idea
Doubling is a \(100\%\) increase. Doubling adds one whole extra copy of the original, so that is \(100\%\) more, not \(200\%\), and tripling adds two copies for \(200\%\) more. A rise has no ceiling, since any amount can keep growing as far as you like. A decrease, by contrast, is capped at \(100\%\), because once everything is gone there is nothing left to remove.
Fun fact
"Percentage points" and "percent change" differ. A rate rising from \(5\%\) to \(7\%\) is \(2\) percentage points (subtraction) but a \(40\%\) increase (\(2\div5\)). Headlines often swap them.
After a \(20\%\) increase, a shelf holds 738 books. To recover last year's count, divide by \(1.20\), not subtract \(20\%\) of 738. How many books were there before?
Show solution
First see why subtracting fails. The wrong path gives \(738 - 0.20 \times 738 = 738 - 147.6 = 590.4\), and a shelf cannot hold \(590.4\) books. The reason is that the \(20\%\) was a slice of LAST year's count, not of this year's \(738\), so peeling \(20\%\) off the new total takes off too much. Set it up with the multiplier instead. A \(20\%\) increase means the new amount is \(1.20\) times the original, so $$1.20 \times \text{original} = 738.$$ Undo the multiplication by dividing both sides by \(1.20\), which gives \(\text{original} = 738 \div 1.20 = 615\). Check it by growing \(615\) by \(20\%\), since \(1.20 \times 615 = 615 + 123 = 738\), exactly the new count. Last year the shelf held \(\boxed{615}\) books.
Key idea
Undo a percent change by dividing by the multiplier. The new amount is the original times the multiplier, so original \(=\) new \(\div\) multiplier, since dividing undoes a multiply. Subtracting the same percent off the new amount is wrong, because that percent was a slice of the smaller original, and knocking it off the larger result peels off too much. After a \(20\%\) increase the new amount is \(1.20\) times the original, so divide by \(1.20\) to climb back.
A ferry pass now costs \(\$510\) after a \(15\%\) fare cut. A \(15\%\) decrease multiplied the old price by \(0.85\). What was the original season pass price in dollars?
Show solution
A \(15\%\) decrease leaves \(100\% - 15\% = 85\%\) of the original price, so the multiplier is \(0.85\). That multiplier acted on the old price to produce the current one, which means \(\text{original} \times 0.85 = 510\). To recover the original, divide the new amount by the multiplier. $$\text{original} = \frac{510}{0.85} = 600$$ Check it forward, \(600 \times 0.85 = 510\), which matches. Notice why the tempting shortcut fails. Adding \(15\%\) of \(510\) back gives \(510 + 76.50 = 586.50\), an overshoot, because \(15\%\) of the smaller current price is smaller than \(15\%\) of the original. The original season pass price was \(\boxed{600}\) dollars.
A bee colony of \(200\) grew by \(30\%\) one week, then shrank by \(30\%\) the next. Many guess it returns to \(200\). It does not. How many workers are in the colony at the end?
Show solution
Handle the weeks in order, each as a multiplier. A \(30\%\) increase multiplies by \(1.30\), and a \(30\%\) decrease multiplies by \(0.70\). After week one, \(200 \times 1.30 = 260\) workers. After week two, the cold snap takes \(30\%\) off that boosted \(260\), so \(260 \times 0.70 = 182\). Notice the two multipliers chain together as \(1.30 \times 0.70 = 0.91\), which is less than \(1\), so the colony lands below where it began. The \(30\%\) drop was taken from the larger number \(260\), so it stripped away more bees than the earlier \(30\%\) boost had added. The colony ends at \(\boxed{182}\) workers.
Key idea
Chain changes by multiplying multipliers. Sequential percent changes multiply, they do not add, because the second change acts on a different amount than the first. That is why a \(p\%\) rise then a \(p\%\) fall never returns you to the start, since \(\left(1+\frac{p}{100}\right)\!\left(1-\frac{p}{100}\right)\) is always a hair under \(1\), leaving you a touch below where you began. The same multiplying drives steady growth, rising \(10\%\) each period means multiplying by \(1.10\) over and over.
A recycling program collects \(5{,}000\) kg in Q1 and expects each later quarter to grow by \(10\%\). Each quarter multiplies by \(1.10\). How many kilograms are expected in Q4 (three quarters later)?
Show solution
A \(10\%\) increase means multiplying by \(1 + \tfrac{10}{100} = 1.10\). Since the same percent applies each quarter, I stack the same multiplier once for each step. Stepping through it, the second quarter is \(5{,}000 \times 1.10 = 5{,}500\), the third quarter is \(5{,}500 \times 1.10 = 6{,}050\), and the fourth quarter is \(6{,}050 \times 1.10 = 6{,}655\). Notice the jumps grow, \(500\) then \(550\) then \(605\), because each \(10\%\) is taken from a bigger amount than before. All three steps at once are $$5{,}000 \times 1.10^3 = 5{,}000 \times 1.331 = 6{,}655.$$ So three quarters later the program expects \(\boxed{6655}\) kilograms.

Every move in this lesson is one multiplier: above \(1\) for an increase, below \(1\) for a decrease. A chain of changes is just those multipliers multiplied together in order, no guessing whether they cancel.

An art class had \(400\) students. Enrollment rose \(50\%\) for summer, then fell \(40\%\) from that summer peak. A \(50\%\) rise and a \(40\%\) fall do not cancel. What is the fall enrollment?
Show solution
Take the two changes in order, each as a multiplier. The \(50\%\) increase multiplies by \(1 + 0.50 = 1.50\), so the summer peak is $$400 \times 1.50 = 600.$$ The fall drop of \(40\%\) is taken off that peak, and a \(40\%\) decrease multiplies by \(1 - 0.40 = 0.60\), so $$600 \times 0.60 = 360.$$ Here is the punchline. The two changes do not cancel. Chaining the multipliers gives \(1.50 \times 0.60 = 0.90\), which is a net \(90\%\) of the starting \(400\), a \(10\%\) drop overall. The reason is that the \(50\%\) rise was figured on \(400\) but the \(40\%\) fall was figured on the larger \(600\), so they sit on different bases and never quite undo each other. The fall enrollment is \(\boxed{360}\).

Review problems

Review 1. A bakery sold \(80\) loaves on Tuesday and \(15\%\) more on Wednesday. How many loaves did the bakery sell on Wednesday?
Show solution
A \(15\%\) increase multiplies the original by \(1 + \tfrac{15}{100} = 1.15\), since the new amount is \(100\%\) of Tuesday plus another \(15\%\). So Wednesday's loaves are $$80 \times 1.15 = 92.$$ You can check this the long way. \(15\%\) of \(80\) is \(0.15 \times 80 = 12\), and \(80 + 12 = 92\), which matches. The bakery sold \(\boxed{92}\) loaves on Wednesday.
Review 2. A winter jacket priced at \(\$250\) is marked \(30\%\) off. What is the sale price in dollars?
Show solution
A \(30\%\) decrease keeps \(70\%\) of the price, so the multiplier is \(1 - 0.30 = 0.70\). Apply it to the original price. $$250 \times 0.70 = 175$$ The sale price is \(\boxed{175}\) dollars.
Review 3. A stadium that held \(600\) fans expanded its seating by \(45\%\). How many seats are there now?
Show solution
A \(45\%\) increase means the new seating is the whole \(100\%\) plus another \(45\%\), which is \(145\%\) of the old amount. Written as a multiplier that is \(1 + 0.45 = 1.45\). Now multiply the original capacity by it. $$600 \times 1.45 = 870$$ So the stadium now holds \(\boxed{870}\) seats.
Review 4. A bamboo shoot rose from \(40\) cm to \(54\) cm, a change of \(14\) cm. Divide by the original. What percent increase is this? Give the number only.
Show solution
The shoot climbed from \(40\) up to \(54\), so the change is \(54 - 40 = 14\) centimeters. For a percent change you compare that change to the ORIGINAL height, so divide by \(40\). That gives \(\tfrac{14}{40} = 0.35\), and as a percent \(0.35 \times 100 = 35\%\). You can check it with the multiplier. A \(35\%\) increase multiplies by \(1 + \tfrac{35}{100} = 1.35\), and \(40 \times 1.35 = 54\), which matches the evening height. So the increase is \(\boxed{35}\).
Review 5. A reservoir dropped from \(900\) to \(720\) megalitres, a fall of \(180\). What percent decrease is this? Give the number only.
Show solution
The change is the drop, \(900 - 720 = 180\) megaliters. For a percent change you divide the change by the ORIGINAL amount, which is \(900\), not by the new \(720\). $$\frac{180}{900} = \frac{1}{5} = 0.20$$ So the level fell by \(20\%\). You can check this with the multiplier. The new amount over the original is \(\tfrac{720}{900} = 0.80\), meaning the reservoir holds \(80\%\) of what it did before, a \(20\%\) decrease. \(\boxed{20}\)
Review 6. A startup grew from \(25\) customers to \(75\), a jump of \(50\). Since \(50 > 25\), the percent lands above \(100\%\). What percent increase is this? Give the number only.
Show solution
First find the change in customers, which is \(75 - 25 = 50\). For a percent increase you compare this change to the ORIGINAL amount, so divide by \(25\), not by \(75\). That gives \(\tfrac{50}{25} = 2\), and \(2\) written as a percent is \(2 \times 100 = 200\%\). You can check this with the multiplier idea. The new count is \(75\), which is \(\tfrac{75}{25} = 3\) times the old count, a multiplier of \(3\). A multiplier of \(3\) means \(1 + 2\), and that extra \(2\) is the \(200\%\) increase sitting on top of the original \(100\%\). So the percent increase is \(\boxed{200}\).
Review 7. After a \(25\%\) increase, a road bike sells for \(\$500\). The \(25\%\) was on the OLD price. What was the original price in dollars?
Show solution
A \(25\%\) increase scales the original price by the multiplier \(1 + \tfrac{25}{100} = 1.25\). That means \(1.25 \times \text{original} = 500\). To find the original, divide the new price by the multiplier. $$\text{original} = \frac{500}{1.25} = 400$$ Quick check, \(1.25 \times 400 = 500\), which matches. Notice that subtracting \(25\%\) of \(500\) would give \(375\), the wrong answer, because the \(25\%\) was taken on the smaller original, not on \(500\). The original price was \(\boxed{400}\) dollars.
Review 8. After a \(20\%\) decrease, a sofa sells for \(\$480\). The multiplier was \(0.80\). What was the original price in dollars?
Show solution
A \(20\%\) decrease means the sofa keeps \(100\% - 20\% = 80\%\) of its price, so the multiplier is \(1 - 0.20 = 0.80\). That gives the relationship $$\text{original} \times 0.80 = 480.$$ To find the original, divide the new price by the multiplier rather than adding back \(20\%\). $$\text{original} = \frac{480}{0.80} = 600.$$ As a check, a \(20\%\) cut from \(600\) is \(600 \times 0.80 = 480\), which matches. The original price was \(\boxed{600}\) dollars.
Review 9. A vlogger started with \(200\) subscribers, gained \(40\%\), then lost \(25\%\) of that new total. How many subscribers does the channel have after both months?
Show solution
A \(40\%\) increase scales by \(1 + 0.40 = 1.40\), and a \(25\%\) decrease scales by \(1 - 0.25 = 0.75\). Apply them in order to the starting count of \(200\). After the first month the channel has \(200 \times 1.40 = 280\) subscribers. After the second month it has \(280 \times 0.75 = 210\) subscribers. Notice that gaining \(40\%\) then losing \(25\%\) did not land back at \(200\), because the \(25\%\) drop came off the larger number \(280\). The final count is \(\boxed{210}\).
Review 10. A koi pond holds \(2{,}000\) liters and gains \(20\%\) each day. How many liters does the pond hold after three full days of growth?
Show solution
A \(20\%\) increase multiplies by \(1 + \tfrac{20}{100} = 1.20\), and three days of growth means applying that multiplier three times. So the final volume is $$2{,}000 \times 1.20 \times 1.20 \times 1.20 = 2{,}000 \times 1.20^3.$$ Stepping through it, day one gives \(2{,}000 \times 1.20 = 2{,}400\), day two gives \(2{,}400 \times 1.20 = 2{,}880\), and day three gives \(2{,}880 \times 1.20 = 3{,}456\). The pond holds \(\boxed{3456}\) liters after three days.
Review 11. A factory used \(1{,}500\) kWh last month and cut usage by \(8\%\) this month. How many kWh did the factory use this month?
Show solution
An \(8\%\) decrease leaves \(100\% - 8\% = 92\%\) of the original, so the multiplier is \(0.92\). Multiply the original amount by it. $$1{,}500 \times 0.92 = 1{,}380$$ So this month the factory used \(\boxed{1380}\) kilowatt-hours.
Review 12. A scout troop grew from \(120\) to \(156\) members, a change of \(36\). Divide by the original. What percent increase is this? Give the number only.
Show solution
The change is \(156 - 120 = 36\) members. For a percent change you divide the change by the original amount, so $$\frac{36}{120} = 0.30.$$ A decimal of \(0.30\) is \(30\%\), which means the troop multiplied by \(1.30\). You can check it, since \(120 \times 1.30 = 156\). So this is a \(30\%\) increase, and the answer is \(\boxed{30}\).
Review 13. A camping tent is marked down \(40\%\) and now sells for \(\$90\). The sale price is \(60\%\) of the original. What was the original price in dollars?
Show solution
A \(40\%\) decrease uses the multiplier \(1 - 0.40 = 0.60\), so the sale price equals \(0.60\) times the original price. That gives \(0.60 \times \text{original} = 90\). To find the original, divide the new price by the multiplier, not by subtracting the percent back. So \(\text{original} = \tfrac{90}{0.60} = 150\). Check it by taking \(40\%\) off, \(150 - 0.40 \times 150 = 150 - 60 = 90\), which matches the sale price. The original price was \(\boxed{150}\) dollars.
Review 14. A club had \(\$500\). A fundraiser raised it by \(20\%\), then supply costs cut it by \(20\%\). How many dollars are left in the fund?
Show solution
The fundraiser is a \(20\%\) increase, so it multiplies by \(1 + 0.20 = 1.20\). The supply costs are a \(20\%\) decrease, so they multiply by \(1 - 0.20 = 0.80\). Stack the two changes by multiplying the multipliers. $$500 \times 1.20 \times 0.80 = 600 \times 0.80 = 480$$ Notice the fund lands below \(500\), not back at it, because the \(20\%\) drop is taken from the larger \(600\), so it removes more than the \(20\%\) that was added to the original \(500\). The fund has \(\boxed{480}\) dollars left.
