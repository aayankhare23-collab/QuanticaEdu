Prealgebra · Lesson 8.3

Simplifying Square Roots

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

In 8.2 you estimated \(\sqrt{12} \approx 3.5\). This lesson rewrites it exactly. The number \(12 = 4 \times 3\) hides a perfect square, and \(\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a}\sqrt{b}\) lets you pull \(\sqrt{4} = 2\) out front, giving \(2\sqrt{3}\).

Compute \(\sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{25}\) and compare it to \(\sqrt{4 \times 25}\). Do they agree? What is \(\sqrt{100}\)?
Show solution
Work the two sides and compare. Splitting first, \(\sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{25} = 2 \times 5 = 10\). Rooting the product, \(\sqrt{4 \times 25} = \sqrt{100} = 10\), because \(10 \times 10 = 100\). The two routes agree, which is exactly the rule we are after. A root of a product equals the product of the roots, so \(\sqrt{100} = \boxed{10}\).
Key idea
Product rule. The square root of a product is the product of the square roots, \(\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b}\) for nonnegative \(a\) and \(b\). So a root is allowed to split anywhere its radicand factors. That is the lever for simplifying: if one of those factors is a perfect square, its root pops out as a clean whole number while the rest stays under the radical.
Simplify \(\sqrt{12}\) using \(12 = 4 \times 3\). Split into \(\sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{3}\), then root the perfect-square piece. What whole number comes out in front of \(\sqrt{3}\)?
Show solution
Hunt for a perfect-square factor of \(12\). Since \(12 = 4 \times 3\) and \(4\) is a perfect square, split the root, $$\sqrt{12} = \sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{3}.$$ Now \(\sqrt{4} = 2\), and \(\sqrt{3}\) has no perfect square to pull out, so it stays put. That leaves \(2 \times \sqrt{3}\), which we write \(2\sqrt{3}\). A quick check, \((2\sqrt{3})^2 = 4 \times 3 = 12\), the number we started with. The whole number in front is \(\boxed{2}\).
Key idea
Simplifying. To simplify a square root, find the largest perfect-square factor of the radicand, take the root of that factor to pull a whole number out front, and leave whatever is left under the radical. The pulled-out number and the leftover root sit side by side, like \(\sqrt{12} = \sqrt{4}\sqrt{3} = 2\sqrt{3}\), which means \(2\) times \(\sqrt{3}\).
Simplify \(\sqrt{72}\). The largest perfect square dividing \(72\) is \(36\), since \(72 = 36 \times 2\). What whole number appears in front of \(\sqrt{2}\)?
Show solution
The largest perfect square dividing \(72\) is \(36\), and \(72 = 36 \times 2\). Split the root, $$\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36} \times \sqrt{2} = 6\sqrt{2}.$$ The \(\sqrt{2}\) cannot be reduced further, so \(6\sqrt{2}\) is simplest. Check, \((6\sqrt{2})^2 = 36 \times 2 = 72\). The whole number in front is \(\boxed{6}\).
Simplify \(\sqrt{48}\). The largest perfect square dividing \(48\) is \(16\), since \(48 = 16 \times 3\). What number remains under the radical?
Show solution
Split off the largest perfect-square factor. Since \(48 = 16 \times 3\), $$\sqrt{48} = \sqrt{16} \times \sqrt{3} = 4\sqrt{3}.$$ The \(4\) comes out front and the \(3\) stays under the radical, because \(3\) has no perfect-square factor bigger than \(1\). So the number left under the radical is \(\boxed{3}\).
Simplify \(\sqrt{98}\). Since \(98 = 49 \times 2\), split the root. What whole number is in front of \(\sqrt{2}\)?
Show solution
Search for a perfect-square factor of \(98\). Testing \(49\), we find \(98 = 49 \times 2\), so $$\sqrt{98} = \sqrt{49} \times \sqrt{2} = 7\sqrt{2}.$$ The leftover \(2\) is square-free, so \(7\sqrt{2}\) is simplest. Check, \((7\sqrt{2})^2 = 49 \times 2 = 98\). The whole number in front is \(\boxed{7}\).
Key idea
Simplest form. A square root is in simplest form when the number left under the radical has no perfect-square factor bigger than \(1\), since at that point nothing more can be pulled out. If you pull out a small square and the leftover still hides another square, you are not finished, so reaching for the largest perfect-square factor right away does the job in a single step.
Factor \(30 = 2 \times 3 \times 5\). Every prime appears just once, so no repeated factor forms a perfect square. What is the largest perfect square dividing \(30\)?
Show solution
Factor \(30\) completely, \(30 = 2 \times 3 \times 5\). A perfect-square factor needs a repeated prime, but here every prime shows up only once, so none of \(4\), \(9\), \(16\), or \(25\) divides \(30\). The largest perfect square dividing \(30\) is just \(1\). Since pulling out \(\sqrt{1} = 1\) changes nothing, \(\sqrt{30}\) is already in simplest form. The largest perfect-square factor is \(\boxed{1}\).
Simplify \(\sqrt{10} \times \sqrt{5}\). Combine under one radical: \(\sqrt{50}\). Then pull out the perfect-square factor. What whole number is in front of \(\sqrt{2}\)?
Show solution
Join the roots with the product rule, $$\sqrt{10} \times \sqrt{5} = \sqrt{10 \times 5} = \sqrt{50}.$$ Now simplify. The largest perfect square dividing \(50\) is \(25\), and \(50 = 25 \times 2\), so $$\sqrt{50} = \sqrt{25} \times \sqrt{2} = 5\sqrt{2}.$$ The whole number in front is \(\boxed{5}\).
Fun fact
An irrational root was called a surd, from the Latin surdus (deaf or mute), since it cannot be spoken as a clean ratio. The bar drawn over the radicand is the vinculum, Latin for a bond.
Simplify \(\sqrt{507}\). Since \(507 = 169 \times 3\) and \(\sqrt{169} = 13\), the result is a whole number times \(\sqrt{3}\). What is that whole number?
Show solution
Search \(507\) for a perfect-square factor. It is odd, so skip the even squares, and testing odd ones gives \(507 = 169 \times 3\), with \(169 = 13^2\). Split the root, $$\sqrt{507} = \sqrt{169} \times \sqrt{3} = 13\sqrt{3}.$$ The leftover \(3\) is square-free, so \(13\sqrt{3}\) is simplest. Check, \((13\sqrt{3})^2 = 169 \times 3 = 507\). The whole number in front is \(\boxed{13}\).

Review problems

Review 1. Simplify \(\sqrt{8}\). The largest perfect square dividing \(8\) is \(4\), since \(8 = 4 \times 2\). Written as a whole number times \(\sqrt{2}\), what is the whole number in front?
Show solution
Since \(8 = 4 \times 2\), $$\sqrt{8} = \sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{2} = 2\sqrt{2}.$$ The whole number in front is \(\boxed{2}\).
Review 2. Simplify \(\sqrt{75}\). Since \(75 = 25 \times 3\) and \(25\) is a perfect square, the root tidies into a whole number times \(\sqrt{3}\). What is the whole number in front?
Show solution
Since \(75 = 25 \times 3\), $$\sqrt{75} = \sqrt{25} \times \sqrt{3} = 5\sqrt{3}.$$ The whole number in front is \(\boxed{5}\).
Review 3. Simplify \(\sqrt{44}\). The largest perfect square dividing \(44\) is \(4\), since \(44 = 4 \times 11\). What number is left under the radical?
Show solution
Since \(44 = 4 \times 11\), $$\sqrt{44} = \sqrt{4} \times \sqrt{11} = 2\sqrt{11}.$$ The \(11\) is square-free and stays under the radical, so the number left under it is \(\boxed{11}\).
Review 4. Simplify \(\sqrt{128}\). The largest perfect square dividing \(128\) is \(64\), since \(128 = 64 \times 2\). Written as a whole number times \(\sqrt{2}\), what is the whole number in front?
Show solution
Since \(128 = 64 \times 2\), $$\sqrt{128} = \sqrt{64} \times \sqrt{2} = 8\sqrt{2}.$$ The whole number in front is \(\boxed{8}\).
Review 5. Combine \(\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{12}\) under one radical and evaluate. What whole number is the result?
Show solution
Join the roots, \(\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{12} = \sqrt{3 \times 12} = \sqrt{36}\). Since \(36\) is a perfect square, \(\sqrt{36} = 6\), a clean whole number. So the value is \(\boxed{6}\).
Review 6. Factor \(60 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5\). The repeated prime forms a perfect square. What is the largest perfect square dividing \(60\)?
Show solution
Factor \(60 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5\). The only repeated prime is \(2\), giving the perfect square \(2 \times 2 = 4\). No larger perfect square divides \(60\), since \(3\) and \(5\) appear only once. So the largest perfect-square factor is \(\boxed{4}\). (It would simplify \(\sqrt{60}\) to \(2\sqrt{15}\).)
Review 7. Decide whether \(\sqrt{33}\) can be simplified. Factor \(33\) completely, then find the largest perfect square that divides it. What is that largest perfect square?
Show solution
Factor \(33 = 3 \times 11\). Each prime appears once, so there is no repeated factor to form a perfect square. The largest perfect square dividing \(33\) is \(\boxed{1}\), which means \(\sqrt{33}\) is already in simplest form.
Review 8. Combine \(\sqrt{15} \times \sqrt{6}\) under one radical: \(\sqrt{90}\). Since \(90 = 9 \times 10\), simplify. What whole number appears in front of \(\sqrt{10}\)?
Show solution
Join the roots, \(\sqrt{15} \times \sqrt{6} = \sqrt{15 \times 6} = \sqrt{90}\). The largest perfect square dividing \(90\) is \(9\), and \(90 = 9 \times 10\), so $$\sqrt{90} = \sqrt{9} \times \sqrt{10} = 3\sqrt{10}.$$ The whole number in front is \(\boxed{3}\).
Review 9. Simplify \(\sqrt{675}\). Since \(675 = 225 \times 3\) and \(\sqrt{225} = 15\), what whole number is in front of \(\sqrt{3}\)?
Show solution
Search \(675\) for its largest perfect-square factor. It is odd, and \(675 = 225 \times 3\) with \(225 = 15^2\). Split the root, $$\sqrt{675} = \sqrt{225} \times \sqrt{3} = 15\sqrt{3}.$$ Check, \((15\sqrt{3})^2 = 225 \times 3 = 675\). The whole number in front is \(\boxed{15}\).
