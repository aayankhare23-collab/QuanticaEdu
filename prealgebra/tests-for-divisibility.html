Prealgebra · Lesson 3.2

Tests for Divisibility

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Place value hides a shortcut: given a big number like 6,351, must you divide to know whether 3 divides it, or can you glance and know? The secret lives in place value.

Five claim tags have numbers 2,470; 3,805; 6,118; 9,640; and 7,333. Without dividing, decide which are multiples of 5. Which single digit settles the question? How many of the five tags win a prize?
Show solution
Take any tag and cut it right after the tens, so the ones digit stands alone. Every tag then reads as a whole number of tens plus that single leftover, $$N = 10k + u,$$ where \(k\) is everything above the ones place and \(u\) is the ones digit itself. The first piece, \(10k\), is a whole number of tens, and since one ten is \(5\times 2\), that piece is always a multiple of \(5\) no matter how large \(k\) is. By closure, adding or removing a multiple of \(5\) cannot change whether the total is a multiple of \(5\), so the prize question slides entirely onto the leftover \(u\). A tag wins for \(5\) exactly when its ones digit is \(0\) or \(5\). Reading just the last digits, \(2{,}470\) ends in \(0\), \(3{,}805\) ends in \(5\), \(6{,}118\) ends in \(8\), \(9{,}640\) ends in \(0\), and \(7{,}333\) ends in \(3\). The winners are \(\boxed{2{,}470,\ 3{,}805,\ 9{,}640}\). The same split tells the \(10\) story for free, because \(10k\) is already a clean stack of tens, so a tag is a multiple of \(10\) only when the leftover \(u\) is \(0\), which singles out \(2{,}470\) and \(9{,}640\). Check \(3{,}805 = 5\times 761\), a genuine multiple of \(5\) but not of \(10\), just as its final \(5\) promised. The general idea is that the ones digit carries the whole verdict for \(5\) and \(10\), since everything above it is a pile of tens that closure lets us ignore.
Band sections have 84,317; 2,000,006; 55,550; and 9,999 players. Which counts are even and can line up in perfect pairs? How many of the four can pair up perfectly?
Show solution
Lining up two to a row with nobody alone is exactly asking whether each count is even, so I want to test evenness without wrestling those giant numbers. The trick is to break each count at the spot where everything to the left becomes a clean number of tens. Write any whole number as its tens part plus its single units digit, \(N = 10k + u\), where \(u\) is that last digit. The piece \(10k\) is ten times a whole number, and ten is even, so \(10k\) is even no matter how enormous \(k\) is. An even pile already splits perfectly into pairs by itself. That means the entire fate of \(N\) rests on the leftover \(u\), since adding an even chunk cannot change whether the total is even or odd. So \(N\) is even exactly when its units digit \(u\) is even. $$N = 10k + u, \qquad 10k \text{ is always even, so even-ness rides on } u$$ Now sweep the four bands, glancing only at the last digit. Brass \(84{,}317\) ends in \(7\), odd, so one player is always left without a partner. Percussion \(2{,}000{,}006\) ends in \(6\), and here is the surprise, the whole \(2{,}000{,}000\) in front is a flat number of tens and is already even all on its own, so every one of those zeros is silent and only the final \(6\) speaks, which is even, so it pairs. Woodwinds \(55{,}550\) ends in \(0\), and \(0\) is even, so it pairs cleanly too. Color guard \(9{,}999\) ends in \(9\), odd, so it cannot. The counts that line up in perfect pairs are $$\boxed{2{,}000{,}006 \text{ and } 55{,}550}.$$ Check, \(55{,}550 = 27{,}775 \times 2\) and \(2{,}000{,}006 = 1{,}000{,}003 \times 2\), both genuine doubles, while \(84{,}317\) and \(9{,}999\) each leave a remainder of \(1\) when you try. The general idea is that the tens part of any number is already a multiple of \(2\), so evenness is decided by the units digit alone, and all the other digits, however many millions they stretch across, simply do not matter.
Key idea
Last-digit tests for 10, 5, and 2. Split any whole number into its units digit and everything above it, \(N = 10k + u\), where \(k\) reads off the digits before the units. Since \(10\) already carries a factor of \(2\) and a factor of \(5\), the part \(10k\) is a multiple of \(2\), of \(5\), and of \(10\) all at once, so by closure it cannot change divisibility and the whole decision falls on \(u\). That gives three clean tests. Divisible by 10 exactly when \(u = 0\); by 5 exactly when \(u = 0\) or \(5\); by 2 exactly when \(u\) is even (\(0, 2, 4, 6, 8\)).

The last digit settled 2, 5, and 10 because every higher place is a multiple of ten. Now try 4. Ten is not a multiple of 4, so one digit is not enough. We need to cut earlier and keep a bigger tail. Where should the cut go?

A dealer splits stacks of 5,328; 7,914; 13,500; and 60,742 cards into complete hands of 4. Write each as hundreds plus its last two digits. How many stacks deal out evenly?
Show solution
The clever move is to cut each deck at the hundreds, since a hundred behaves beautifully here. One hundred is a whole number of hands, because $$100 = 4\times25,$$ so every full hundred cards deals out into exactly \(25\) complete hands of \(4\) with nothing left over. Now peel any stack apart at its last two digits, writing it as a pile of full hundreds plus the leftover. Take the first deck, \(5{,}328 = 53\times100 + 28\). The \(53\times100\) part is \(53\) hundreds, and each hundred is already a multiple of \(4\), so that whole chunk deals out evenly on its own. By closure, adding or removing such a clean chunk cannot change whether the total works, so the entire stack deals out evenly exactly when the leftover \(28\) does. That is the discovery, everything above the last two digits is full hundreds and can be ignored, so only the last two digits decide it. Run the four decks through this lens. $$5{,}328 = 53\times100 + 28,\qquad 7{,}914 = 79\times100 + 14$$ $$13{,}500 = 135\times100 + 00,\qquad 60{,}742 = 607\times100 + 42$$ The hundreds chunk is harmless every time, so check only the leftover. For \(5{,}328\) the leftover \(28 = 4\times7\) deals out, so it works. For \(7{,}914\) the leftover \(14\) is not a multiple of \(4\), since \(4\times3 = 12\) and \(4\times4 = 16\) skip right over it, so it fails. For \(13{,}500\) the leftover is \(00\), an empty leftover that needs no extra hand at all, so it works. For \(60{,}742\) the leftover \(42\) lands between \(40\) and \(44\), so it fails. The decks that deal out into full hands of \(4\) are $$\boxed{5{,}328 \text{ and } 13{,}500}.$$ Check the close one, \(5{,}328 = 4\times1332\), a tidy \(1332\) hands. The general idea is that since \(100\) is a multiple of \(4\), divisibility by \(4\) is decided entirely by the last two digits, because every higher place is a full bundle of hundreds that closure lets you throw away.
Key idea
Divisibility by 4. A whole number is divisible by \(4\) exactly when the number formed by its last two digits is. Split it into its last two digits plus everything from the hundreds up, like \(8{,}516 = 8{,}500 + 16\). The front chunk is always a whole number of hundreds, and since \(100 = 4 \times 25\) is a multiple of \(4\), a whole number of hundreds is a multiple of \(4\) too. So closure drops the front chunk without changing divisibility, leaving only the last two digits to check.
Arcade machines show totals 47,216; 9,360; and 128,500. Test only the last three digits of each for divisibility by 8. How many are divisible by 8?
Show solution
Dividing each whole total by \(8\) is the slow road, so cut the number where one chunk turns into complete strips. The key fact is that one thousand is already a stack of strips, since $$1000 = 8\times 125.$$ That means every full thousand tokens is a whole number of \(8\)-strips with nothing spare. Split each total right after its last three digits and watch the big chunk fall away by closure, because subtracting a pile of complete strips cannot change whether what remains is a whole number of strips. For the first machine, \(47{,}216 = 47\times 1000 + 216\); the \(47\times 1000\) is all complete strips, so only \(216\) is left, and \(216 = 8\times 27\), a clean fit, so \(47{,}216\) works. For the second, \(9{,}360 = 9\times 1000 + 360\), and the tail \(360 = 8\times 45\) fits too, so \(9{,}360\) works. For the third, \(128{,}500 = 128\times 1000 + 500\), and the tail \(500\) lands between \(8\times 62 = 496\) and \(8\times 64 = 512\), so it is not a multiple of \(8\) and \(128{,}500\) fails. The totals made of whole strips are \(\boxed{47{,}216 \text{ and } 9{,}360}\). Now the deeper why, the reason it took three digits here when \(4\) needed only two. You read exactly as many digits as the first power of ten your divisor divides into cleanly, because that power of ten is where the chunk above it becomes throwaway. Watch the powers of two march up the powers of ten, $$2 \mid 10, \qquad 4 \mid 100, \qquad 8 \mid 1000,$$ so \(2\) is decided by the last \(1\) digit, \(4\) by the last \(2\), and \(8\) by the last \(3\). Check, the winning tails really do fit, \(8\times 27 = 216\) and \(8\times 45 = 360\). The general idea is that you slice a number at the first place value the divisor swallows whole, let closure discard everything above the slice, and judge the whole number by the small tail that remains.
Key idea
Test by 8: last three digits. A number is divisible by 8 exactly when its last three digits form a multiple of 8, because everything to the left of those three digits is a count of thousands and \(1000 = 8 \times 125\), so that front part is already a multiple of 8 and closure peels it away. This is one rung of a taller ladder: testing by 2 reads the last 1 digit, by 4 the last 2, by 8 the last 3, since each power of two divides the matching power of ten, \(2 \mid 10\), \(4 \mid 100\), \(8 \mid 1000\).

Try the same tail trick on 9. For that, we'd need a power of ten that 9 divides, but \(10=9+1\), \(100=99+1\), \(1000=999+1\). Every power of ten misses being a multiple of 9 by exactly 1. That uniform miss is not a dead end; it is a clue.

The stamp number is 5,742. Break it by place value, write each power of ten as a multiple of 9 plus 1, and let the multiples fall away. What leftover decides whether 9 divides 5,742?
Show solution
The trick is to stop dividing 9 into the whole stamp number and divide it into each place-value piece instead. Spread \(5742\) out by place value, then write every power of ten as a multiple of 9 plus a leftover 1, since \(10 = 9+1\), \(100 = 99+1\), and \(1000 = 999+1\). $$5742 = 5(999+1) + 7(99+1) + 4(9+1) + 2$$ Now split each product into its big round part and its tiny leftover and gather them into two groups. $$5742 = \big(5\times 999 + 7\times 99 + 4\times 9\big) + \big(5 + 7 + 4 + 2\big)$$ The first group is built entirely from \(999\), \(99\), and \(9\), so it is a sum of multiples of 9, and by closure adding multiples of 9 keeps you on a multiple of 9. That whole chunk leaves no remainder, so it cannot change whether 9 divides the number. Everything now rides on the second group, which is exactly the digit sum \(5 + 7 + 4 + 2 = \boxed{18}\). Since \(18 = 9 \times 2\) is itself a multiple of 9, the stamp number \(5742\) is divisible by 9 too. Check by the brute road, \(5742 \div 9 = 638\) exactly, no remainder, just as the digit sum promised. The general idea is that each power of ten sits one step past a string of 9s, so the round part always vanishes as a multiple of 9 and a number leaves the same remainder on division by 9 as its digit sum does.
Key idea
A number is divisible by 9 exactly when its digit sum is, and the reason lives in place value. Every power of ten is one more than a multiple of \(9\), since \(10 = 9+1\), \(100 = 99+1\), \(1000 = 999+1\). So each place gives a digit times a string of nines plus that digit again, like \(7 \times 100 = 7 \times 99 + 7\). The strings-of-nines parts are all multiples of \(9\) and drop out together by closure, leaving only the digits added up, so the number and its digit sum share the same remainder mod \(9\). The same test works for 3 by chaining, since \(3\) is a factor of \(9\), so \(9, 99, 999, \ldots\) each carry a \(3\) and drop out as well.
A view counter reads 5,839,206. Sum its digits; if the result has more than one digit, sum again. What single digit do you land on, and what does it say about divisibility by 3 and by 9?
Show solution
Add the digits once, \(5+8+3+9+2+0+6 = 33\), and since that is still two digits, add again, \(3+3=6\). The view count collapses to the single digit \(6\), and that one digit answers everything. Here is why each pass is safe. Write the number in place value and split every power of ten as a multiple of nine plus one. $$5{,}839{,}206 = 5(999999) + 8(99999) + 3(9999) + 9(999) + 2(99) + 0(9) + 6 + (5+8+3+9+2+0+6)$$ Every chunk on the left, each a digit times a string of nines, is a multiple of \(9\), so by closure we may throw all of them away when we ask about remainders. What is left is exactly the digit sum, \(33\). So the original number and \(33\) leave the same remainder on division by \(9\). But \(33\) is itself a number whose digits we can sum the same way, \(33 = 3(9)+3+3\), leaving the same remainder as \(3+3=6\). Each pass peels off more nines and never touches the remainder, which is why summing again and again is allowed. The final single digit, \(\boxed{6}\), is that shared remainder. Since \(6\) is a multiple of \(3\) but not of \(9\), the count \(5{,}839{,}206\) is divisible by \(3\) and not by \(9\). Check the divide-by-three claim straight from \(6\), since \(6 = 2\times 3\), and indeed \(5{,}839{,}206 \div 3 = 1{,}946{,}402\) with nothing left over. The general idea is that a number and its digit sum always share the same remainder when divided by \(9\), so repeated digit summing reaches a single digit, the digital root, that reveals divisibility by \(3\) and by \(9\) at a glance.
Key idea
Digital root. Sum a number's digits, then sum the digits of that result, and keep going until one digit remains. That digit equals the remainder when the number is divided by 9, where a root of 9 stands for remainder 0. It works because each power of ten is one more than a multiple of 9, so a digit times its place is that digit times a multiple of 9 plus the digit itself, contributing nothing to the remainder mod 9. Each pass therefore keeps the remainder unchanged while shrinking the number. The root settles divisibility by 9 (root 9) and by 3 (root 3, 6, or 9). Check: \(7{,}284 \to 7+2+8+4 = 21 \to 3\), and \(7{,}284 = 809 \times 9 + 3\), remainder 3.
Fun fact
Bookkeepers used casting out nines: shrink each number to its digit sum and verify the small versions match. A mismatch flags an error; a match is no guarantee, but catches most slips.

Try 11 next. We have \(10=11-1\), so ten leaves remainder \(-1\). Then \(100=99+1\) leaves \(+1\). Then \(1000=1001-1\) leaves \(-1\). The remainders rock back and forth. Can you see what to do with digits whose place values alternate in sign?

Cards read 90,728 and 53,481; exactly one is a multiple of 11. Since \(10\equiv-1\pmod{11}\), combine digits with alternating signs. Which card is the multiple?
Show solution
The magician's whole trick lives inside the place values, so look at what each one leaves behind against \(11\). Start from his fact that \(10 = 11 - 1\), which means ten leaves remainder \(-1\). Climbing one place at a time, each power of ten lands just beside a multiple of \(11\), and the side it lands on keeps flipping. $$10 = 11 - 1,\qquad 100 = 99 + 1 = 9\times 11 + 1,\qquad 1000 = 1001 - 1 = 91\times 11 - 1$$ So the leftovers run \(-1, +1, -1, \ldots\) as you go up from the tens, which is the same as saying the place values, read from the units, carry signs \(+, -, +, -, \ldots\) once you throw the multiples of \(11\) away by closure. That means a number leaves exactly the same remainder against \(11\) as its digits added with those alternating signs, starting with a plus at the units. For the first card, \(90{,}728\), combine the digits from the units up. $$8 - 2 + 7 - 0 + 9 = 22$$ And \(22 = 2\times 11\) is a multiple of \(11\), so \(90{,}728\) is divisible by \(11\). For the second card, \(53{,}481\), do the same. $$1 - 8 + 4 - 3 + 5 = -1$$ That is not a multiple of \(11\), so \(53{,}481\) is not. The magician would point to \(\boxed{90{,}728}\). Check, \(90{,}728 = 11\times 8248\), exactly on the nose, while \(53{,}481 = 11\times 4862 + (-1)\) misses by that same \(-1\) the alternating sum predicted. The general idea is that since the place values alternate just under and just over multiples of \(11\), a number's remainder against \(11\) is decided entirely by its alternating digit sum.
Key idea
The by-11 test. A number is divisible by 11 exactly when its alternating digit sum is. Tag the digits from the right with alternating signs, plus the units, minus the next, plus the one after, and add them up. The signs flip because \(10\) is one below a multiple of \(11\) and leaves remainder \(-1\), then \(100 = 99 + 1\) leaves \(+1\), then \(1000 = 1001 - 1\) leaves \(-1\), the place values marching \(-1, +1, -1, +1\) as you climb. Closure strips the whole-multiple-of-\(11\) part of each place, leaving exactly the digits with their flipping signs, so the number and its alternating digit sum land on the same multiple of \(11\).

We can check 2, 3, 4, 5, 8, 9, 10, 11, but not 6, 12, or 15. Each of those is built from divisors we already test. Can we just run both piece-tests and combine the answers? Almost. There is a catch in which two pieces you pick.

A clerk thinks: if a pile is even and divisible by 6, it is divisible by 12. Find one pile divisible by 2 and by 6 that is NOT divisible by 12.
Show solution
Start by trusting the clerk and hunting for a count that breaks the promise. Walk the multiples of 6, which are \(6, 12, 18, 24, 30, \dots\), and notice they alternate. Half of them, the ones at \(12, 24, 36\), are even-times-6 and carry two factors of 2. The other half, at \(6, 18, 30\), are odd-times-6 and carry only one factor of 2. Take \(18\). It fills whole cartons since \(18 = 6\times 3\), and it is even since \(18 = 2\times 9\), so it passes both of the clerk's tests. But \(18 = 12 + 6\), and while \(12\) is one whole crate, the leftover \(6\) is only half a crate, so \(18\) is not divisible by 12. The clerk is wrong, and \(\boxed{18}\) proves it. Now the why, told through factors. Splitting a divisor into a product lets you test the pieces separately, but only when the pieces share no common factor. For 6 the split \(6 = 2\times 3\) is clean, since 2 and 3 share nothing, so passing the test for 2 and the test for 3 nails down one factor of 2 and one factor of 3, which together are exactly 6. The clerk copied this idea with \(12 = 2\times 6\), but here 2 and 6 are not coprime, since 6 already hides a 2 inside it. So the "even" test and the "divisible by 6" test both reach for the same single factor of 2 and double-count it, and \(12 = 2^2\times 3\) needs two factors of 2. The honest split is \(12 = 3\times 4\), where 3 and 4 share no common factor, so it really does deliver the full \(2^2\) and the 3. Check on 18. Its last two digits make 18, which is not a multiple of 4, so the \(3\times 4\) test correctly rejects it. The general idea is that you may glue divisibility tests together only along factors that are coprime, since shared factors get counted twice and a passed test can quietly lie.
Key idea
Combining rule. To test divisibility by a composite number, split it into two coprime factors (factors sharing no common factor), test each on its own, and the number is divisible by the whole exactly when it passes both. So \(6 = 2 \times 3\), \(12 = 3 \times 4\), \(15 = 3 \times 5\), \(20 = 4 \times 5\) all work. This is chaining: if a number's demand for a \(2\) and its demand for a \(3\) never lean on the same prime, they stack side by side and force a \(6\). The split must be coprime, since \(12 = 2 \times 6\) fails: both pieces ask for a \(2\), but those could be the same \(2\) counted twice, while \(12 = 2^2 \times 3\) demands two. That is why \(18\) passes the \(2\) and \(6\) tests yet is not a multiple of \(12\).
A print shop has 25,830 flyers. Using the matching shortcut for each of 2, 3, 4, 5, and 9, decide which stack sizes divide 25,830 evenly. How many of the five work?
Show solution
Write the run by place value so each test can grab only the part it needs, \(25{,}830 = 25{,}000 + 800 + 30\), or more fully \(2\times 10{,}000 + 5\times 1{,}000 + 8\times 100 + 3\times 10 + 0\). The last digit is \(0\), and a number ending in \(0\) is even and is a multiple of \(5\), so \(2\) and \(5\) both divide it. For \(4\), every power of ten from \(100\) up is already a multiple of \(4\), so closure lets us throw away \(25{,}800\) and only check the last two digits, \(30\). Since \(30 = 4\times 7 + 2\) is not a multiple of \(4\), the run is not divisible by \(4\), ending in \(0\) was not enough on its own. For \(3\) and \(9\), each power of ten is one more than a multiple of \(9\), like \(10 = 9 + 1\) and \(100 = 99 + 1\), so the whole number is its digit sum plus a pile of multiples of \(9\) that closure discards, leaving only the digit sum to test. $$2+5+8+3+0 = 18 = 9\times 2$$ Since \(18\) is a multiple of \(9\), and \(9\) is a multiple of \(3\), chaining gives both, so \(3\) and \(9\) divide the run. Collecting the winners, the stack sizes that split \(25{,}830\) evenly are \(\boxed{2,\ 3,\ 5,\ 9}\), all but \(4\). Check the trickiest one, \(9\times 2870 = 25{,}830\), so the \(9\) really does fit. The general idea is that each test cuts the number at the place where its chunk becomes a multiple of the divisor, then closure tosses that chunk and leaves a tiny piece to judge.

Run the tests backward. Instead of judging a finished number, suppose one digit is hidden and the number must be divisible by something. The same place-value thinking applies, but now you solve for the missing digit rather than just checking.

A boarding pass prints the flight number as 46,N12, but the hundreds digit N is smudged. The airline only issues numbers divisible by 9. Use the digit-sum test backward to find N.
Show solution
The digit-sum test for \(9\) comes straight from place value. Each digit rides a power of ten, and every power of ten is just one more than a multiple of \(9\), since \(10=9+1\), \(100=99+1\), \(1000=999+1\), and so on. When you peel the multiple-of-\(9\) part off each chunk and let closure throw it away, what survives is exactly the digit by itself. So the whole number is a multiple of \(9\) precisely when the leftover, the sum of its digits, is a multiple of \(9\). Run that test on \(46{,}N12\). Add the digits and keep \(N\) in the sum, $$4+6+N+1+2=13+N.$$ For the flight number to be divisible by \(9\), this digit sum has to be a multiple of \(9\). The multiples of \(9\) near \(13\) are \(9\) and \(18\), and \(13+N\) is already at least \(13\), so the only one it can reach with a single digit is \(18\). That forces \(13+N=18\), so \(N=\boxed{5}\) and the flight number is \(46{,}512\). Check, the digits of \(46{,}512\) sum to \(4+6+5+1+2=18\), which is \(2\times 9\), so the test passes. The general idea is that the digit-sum test runs both ways. Once you know a number is divisible by \(9\), the digit sum is pinned to a multiple of \(9\), and that single fact is often enough to back out a hidden digit.
A label reads 543N6 with the tens digit N smudged. It must pass both the sorter for multiples of 9 and the sorter for multiples of 4. What digit is N?
Show solution
Each machine reads the code its own way, so handle them one at a time and let the answers cross. Start with the first machine, which keeps multiples of \(9\). Write the code by place value, \(543N6 = 5\times 10{,}000 + 4\times 1{,}000 + 3\times 100 + N\times 10 + 6\). Every power of ten is one more than a multiple of \(9\), since \(10 = 9+1\), \(100 = 99+1\), and so on, so by closure each digit times its power of ten leaves behind just that digit when you toss out the multiples of \(9\). What is left is the digit sum, \(5+4+3+N+6 = 18+N\). The number is a multiple of \(9\) exactly when \(18+N\) is, and since \(18\) is already \(9\times 2\), we need \(N\) to push the sum to the next clean step. That gives two survivors, \(N=0\) with sum \(18\) and \(N=9\) with sum \(27\). The digit sum test narrowed five smudges down to two. Now the second machine, which keeps multiples of \(4\). Cut the code where one chunk turns into a multiple of \(4\). Since \(100\) is a multiple of \(4\), everything from the hundreds place up is already a multiple of \(4\), and closure throws that whole chunk away, leaving only the last two digits \(N6\) to check. Test the two survivors. With \(N=0\) the tail is \(06=6\), and \(6\) is not a multiple of \(4\). With \(N=9\) the tail is \(96\), and \(96 = 4\times 24\) is. So the second machine rejects \(0\) and keeps \(9\). Only one digit clears both machines, $$N = \boxed{9}.$$ Check the full code \(54{,}396\). Its digit sum is \(5+4+3+9+6 = 27 = 9\times 3\), so the \(9\) machine passes it, and its last two digits \(96 = 4\times 24\) pass the \(4\) machine. The general idea is that two divisibility tests look at different pieces of the same number, so stacking them traps a hidden digit where the candidate lists overlap.
Key idea
Running tests backward. The same place-value cuts that test a number can fill in a missing digit. For divisibility by 9, only the digit sum decides, so choose the hidden digit to land that sum on a multiple of 9; since each step in the digit gives one step in the sum, usually exactly one value works (the edge case: if the other digits already sum to a multiple of 9, both \(0\) and \(9\) qualify). For divisibility by 4, only the last two digits decide, a separate window blind to the rest. When both rules must hold, the two windows each rule out values, and where they overlap on the same hidden digit both must agree, pinning it to at most one value.

Every test cut where one chunk is a multiple of the divisor, let closure drop it, and read the small tail left. The cut fell at different places for different divisors, tools for the prime hunt ahead.

Review problems

Review 1. Five raffle tickets have numbers 3,160; 8,002; 47,225; 90,118; and 6,500. Which are divisible by 5? How many win a prize?
Show solution
Write any ticket number as its units digit plus ten times whatever sits above it, so \(3{,}160 = 316\times 10 + 0\) and \(47{,}225 = 4722\times 10 + 5\), and so on. The chunk \(\text{(rest)}\times 10\) is always a multiple of \(10\), and since \(10 = 2\times 5\) it is a multiple of \(5\) too. By closure, throwing that whole chunk away leaves the divisibility by \(5\) untouched, so the only thing that matters is the units digit, and a single digit is a multiple of \(5\) only when it is \(0\) or \(5\). $$3{,}160\to 0,\quad 8{,}002\to 2,\quad 47{,}225\to 5,\quad 90{,}118\to 8,\quad 6{,}500\to 0$$ The units digits \(0\), \(5\), and \(0\) pass, while \(2\) and \(8\) fail, so the winners are \(\boxed{3{,}160,\ 47{,}225,\ 6{,}500}\). Check the doubtful one, since \(47{,}225 = 9445\times 5\) it really is a multiple of \(5\), and \(90{,}118 = 18023\times 5 + 3\) lands \(3\) past a multiple, just as its last digit warned. The general idea is that ten is built from a \(5\), so place value sweeps every digit above the units into a multiple of \(5\) and leaves only the last digit to read.
Review 2. Five flight log codes are 1,316; 5,550; 23,128; 8,914; and 40,016. Which are divisible by 4? How many qualify?
Show solution
Write each code as its hundreds plus its last two digits, since that is exactly where the test wants us to cut. Take \(40{,}016 = 40{,}000 + 16\). The front piece is a pile of whole hundreds, and one hundred is \(100 = 4 \times 25\), a multiple of 4. So $$40{,}000 = 400 \times 100 = 400 \times 4 \times 25,$$ which is a multiple of 4, and closure lets us toss it away with nothing left over. The whole code is divisible by 4 exactly when the leftover last two digits are. Now read off the tail of each one. For \(1{,}316\) the tail is 16, and \(16 = 4 \times 4\), so it passes. For \(5{,}550\) the tail is 50, and \(50 = 48 + 2\) sits two past a multiple of 4, so it fails. For \(23{,}128\) the tail is 28, and \(28 = 4 \times 7\), so it passes. For \(8{,}914\) the tail is 14, which lands between \(12\) and \(16\) and is not a multiple of 4, so it fails. For \(40{,}016\) the tail is 16 again, which passes. The winners are \(\boxed{1{,}316,\ 23{,}128,\ 40{,}016}\). Check by dividing the tails straight, \(16 \div 4 = 4\), \(28 \div 4 = 7\), \(16 \div 4 = 4\), all clean, while \(50\) and \(14\) leave a remainder of 2. The general idea is that 100 is a multiple of 4, so all the hundreds vanish under closure and only the last two digits decide divisibility by 4.
Review 3. A track code reads 49,d06. To keep it divisible by 3, what is the largest digit d can be?
Show solution
The test for 3 comes from place value. Write the code as \(4\times 10000 + 9\times 1000 + d\times 100 + 0\times 10 + 6\). Every power of ten is one more than a multiple of 3, since \(10 = 9+1\), \(100 = 99+1\), \(1000 = 999+1\), and so on. So each digit's chunk is that digit times a multiple of 3 plus one copy of the digit itself. Closure lets us throw away all the multiples of 3 and keep only what is left over, which is the sum of the digits. That is why divisibility by 3 hangs entirely on the digit sum. Here the digit sum is $$4+9+d+0+6 = 19+d.$$ Now \(19\) sits one past \(18\), a multiple of 3, so \(19\) leaves a remainder of 1. To land back on a multiple of 3, the dial \(d\) must supply a remainder of 2, which happens for \(d = 2,\ 5,\ 8\). The largest single digit on that list is \(\boxed{8}\). Check, with \(d=8\) the sum is \(19+8 = 27\), and \(27 = 3\times 9\) is a clean multiple of 3, while \(d=9\) would give \(28\), which misses. The general idea is that powers of ten are each one more than a multiple of 3, so a number's divisibility by 3 is carried entirely by the sum of its digits.
Review 4. Batch codes are 5,431; 7,002; 12,345; 81,000; and 6,453. How many are divisible by 9?
Show solution
Write each code in place value to see why the digit sum is all that matters. A number like \(7{,}002\) is \(7\times 1000 + 0\times 100 + 0\times 10 + 2\), and each power of ten is one more than a multiple of 9, since \(10 = 9+1\), \(100 = 99+1\), \(1000 = 999+1\). So $$7{,}002 = 7(999+1) + 2 = (7\times 999) + (7+2).$$ Closure lets us throw away the \(7\times 999\) chunk, because it is already a multiple of 9, and we are left checking just the digit sum \(7+2=9\). The same move works for every code, so the whole test collapses to adding digits. The five digit sums are \(5+4+3+1=13\), \(7+0+0+2=9\), \(1+2+3+4+5=15\), \(8+1+0+0+0=9\), and \(6+4+5+3=18\). The multiples of 9 in that list are \(9\), \(9\), and \(18\), which belong to \(7{,}002\), \(81{,}000\), and \(6{,}453\); the sums \(13\) and \(15\) miss, so \(5{,}431\) and \(12{,}345\) are out. The codes she sets aside are \(\boxed{7{,}002,\ 81{,}000,\ 6{,}453}\). Check the largest sum, \(18 = 2\times 9\), and indeed \(6{,}453 = 9\times 717\). The general idea is that the digit sum carries a number's remainder under 9, because cutting at each power of ten lets closure discard a multiple of 9 and leave only the digits behind.
Review 5. Every divisibility test reads only the piece the tossed chunk cannot hide. Write a number as digits times powers of ten, cut where one chunk becomes a multiple of the divisor, and closure throws that chunk away. The last digit settles 2, 5, 10, since the whole upper part is a multiple of all three. The last two digits settle 4 and the last three settle 8, since \(100 = 4 \times 25\) and \(1000 = 8 \times 125\). The digit sum settles 3 and 9, since each power of ten is one above a multiple of 9. The alternating digit sum settles 11, since the powers of ten land just below then above a multiple of 11, flipping sign. A composite settles by splitting it into coprime factors and testing each.
Review 6. Five sticker orders are 4,128; 7,533; 90; 25,164; and 3,400. Which are divisible by 6? How many can the printer fill?
Show solution
Being divisible by \(6\) means being divisible by \(6 = 2 \times 3\), and since \(2\) and \(3\) share no factors, a number rides along by \(6\) exactly when it rides along by both \(2\) and \(3\). So we run two small tests instead of one big division. The test for \(2\) reads only the last digit, because every place above the units is a multiple of ten and ten is even, so closure lets all those higher chunks fall away and leaves just the units digit to check for evenness. The test for \(3\) adds the digits, because each power of ten is one more than a multiple of three, as in \(10 = 9+1\) and \(100 = 99+1\), so $$\text{number} = (\text{multiple of }3) + (\text{sum of digits}),$$ and closure throws the first piece away, leaving the digit sum to decide. Now sweep the five orders. \(4{,}128\) ends in \(8\) so it is even, and \(4+1+2+8 = 15\) is a multiple of \(3\), so it passes both. \(7{,}533\) ends in \(3\), already odd, so it fails the test for \(2\) and is out. \(90\) ends in \(0\) so it is even, and \(9+0 = 9\) is a multiple of \(3\), so it passes. \(25{,}164\) ends in \(4\) so it is even, and \(2+5+1+6+4 = 18\) is a multiple of \(3\), so it passes. \(3{,}400\) ends in \(0\) so it is even, but \(3+4+0+0 = 7\) is not a multiple of \(3\), so it slips through the first test and fails the second. The orders the printer can fill are \(\boxed{4{,}128,\ 90,\ 25{,}164}\). Check \(25{,}164 = 6 \times 4{,}194\) exactly, with no remainder. The general idea is that a divisibility test for a product of two factors that share nothing is just the two smaller tests run side by side, and a number must pass both to inherit the bigger factor.
Review 7. A bib number reads 34_7 with the tens digit hidden. Every bib is divisible by 9. Use the digit-sum test backward to find the hidden digit.
Show solution
The divisibility test for 9 comes straight from place value, since \(10\) leaves a remainder of \(1\) on division by 9, and so do \(100\) and \(1000\), which means each digit contributes just its own face value to the remainder and a number is a multiple of 9 exactly when its digit sum is a multiple of 9. For the bib \(34\_7\) with hidden tens digit \(d\), adding the digits gives \(3 + 4 + d + 7 = 14 + d\). Because \(d\) is a lone digit between 0 and 9, the quantity \(14 + d\) only ranges from 14 up to 23, and the single multiple of 9 sitting in that range is 18, so \(14 + d = 18\) forces \(d = \boxed{4}\). Check by filling the digit back in, which makes the bib \(3447\), and \(9 \times 383 = 3447\), so it really is a multiple of 9, and the digit sum reads \(3 + 4 + 4 + 7 = 18\) as it should. The general idea is that the digit sum is a fingerprint for divisibility by 9, and when one digit is missing you can run the test in reverse, writing the unknown as a letter and solving the little equation that says the digit total must be a multiple of 9.
Review 8. Daily krill counts are 3,128; 17,400; 9,990; 52,016; and 6,300. Which are divisible by 8? How many work?
Show solution
Splitting a five thousand pellet day pellet by pellet would be slow, so cut each number where one chunk turns friendly, right before the last three digits. Everything to the left of that cut is a whole number of thousands, and a thousand is a multiple of 8 because \(1000 = 8\times 125\). So the high chunk is always a multiple of 8 no matter what the leading digits are, and closure lets us subtract it away and forget it, leaving only the last three digits to judge. $$\text{number} = (\text{thousands, a multiple of }8) + (\text{last three digits})$$ Now test the tails. For 3,128 the tail is \(128 = 8\times 16\), a multiple of 8, so 3,128 splits evenly into 8 tanks. For 17,400 the tail is \(400 = 8\times 50\), so 17,400 works. For 9,990 the tail is \(990\), and \(8\times 123 = 984\) with \(990 - 984 = 6\) left over, so 9,990 fails. For 52,016 the tail is \(016 = 16 = 8\times 2\), so 52,016 works. For 6,300 the tail is \(300\), and \(8\times 37 = 296\) with \(4\) left over, so 6,300 fails. The day counts that share out evenly among 8 tanks are $$\boxed{3{,}128,\ 17{,}400,\ 52{,}016}.$$ Check the closest miss, \(990 = 984 + 6\), so it would leave 6 pellets with no full tank to land in, exactly the remainder we found. The general idea is that \(1000\) is a multiple of 8, so the thousands part always vanishes under closure and a number is divisible by 8 exactly when its last three digits are.
Review 9. Among house numbers 1 through 500, how many end in 6 and are divisible by 4?
Show solution
Divisibility by 4 only ever reads the last two digits, because every full hundred is \(100 = 4\times 25\), a clean multiple of 4 that closure lets us throw away, so a number ending in 6 is divisible by 4 exactly when its last two digits are. The endings that finish in 6 are the ten two-digit numbers \(06, 16, 26, 36, 46, 56, 66, 76, 86, 96\), and testing each against 4 the multiples are \(16 = 4\times 4\), \(36 = 4\times 9\), \(56 = 4\times 14\), \(76 = 4\times 19\), and \(96 = 4\times 24\), which are exactly the five with an odd tens digit. So inside every block of one hundred consecutive numbers there are 5 doors that both end in 6 and divide by 4. From 1 through 500 there are 5 full hundreds, each carrying those same 5 winners, so the total is \(5 \times 5 = \boxed{25}\). Check by listing the smallest few, \(16, 36, 56, 76, 96\) in the first hundred, then \(116, 136, 156, 176, 196\) in the second, the same five-per-hundred pattern marching up to \(416, 436, 456, 476, 496\) in the fifth hundred, which is 25 doors in all. The general idea is that a units digit fixes the ones place while divisibility by 4 is decided by the last two digits, so the tens digit does the deciding and gives a steady count per hundred that you simply scale up across the range.
Review 10. A count reads 8_,386 with the thousands digit missing. It is divisible by 9. Find the hidden digit, then decide whether the full number is also divisible by 4.
Show solution
The test for divisibility by \(9\) comes from place value. Every power of ten is one more than a multiple of \(9\), since \(10 = 9+1\), \(100 = 99+1\), \(1000 = 999+1\), and so on, so each digit's leftover when you strip away the \(9\)s is just the digit itself. Closure then lets us throw away all those multiples of \(9\) and check only what remains, the digit sum. Adding the digits we can read gives \(8+3+8+6 = 25\), so with the hidden thousands digit \(d\) the full digit sum is \(25 + d\). For \(d\) running from \(0\) to \(9\) that total ranges from \(25\) up to \(34\), and the only multiple of \(9\) inside that window is \(27\). $$25 + d = 27 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad d = 2$$ So the hidden digit is \(\boxed{2}\) and the bird count is \(82{,}386\). Now the surprise. Being forced onto a multiple of \(9\) says nothing about \(4\), because the two tests cut the number in completely different places. The test for \(4\) leans on the fact that \(100\) is a multiple of \(4\), so closure lets us discard everything from the hundreds place up and keep only the last two digits, \(86\). Since \(86 = 4 \times 21 + 2\), it leaves a remainder of \(2\), so \(82{,}386\) is not divisible by \(4\). Check, \(82{,}386 = 9 \times 9{,}154\) lands exactly, while \(82{,}386 \div 4 = 20{,}596.5\) does not. The general idea is that each divisibility test reads a different chunk of the number, the whole digit sum for \(9\) but only the final two digits for \(4\), so passing one test never drags the other along for free.
Review 11. A stamp serial reads 7_,320 with the thousands digit smudged. It must be divisible by both 3 and 8. How many digits could hide under the smudge?
Show solution
Two conditions have to hold together, divisible by 8 and divisible by 3, so I will test each one and then keep only the digits that survive both. Start with 8. The divisibility test for 8 looks only at the last three digits, because everything above the hundreds place is a whole number of thousands and \(1000 = 8 \times 125\), so by closure that upper chunk is a multiple of 8 and gets swept away. The last three digits here are \(320\), and \(320 = 8 \times 40\), a clean multiple of 8. The smudged digit lives all the way up in the thousands place, safely inside the part the 8 test ignores, so no matter which digit hides there the serial is divisible by 8. That means the 8 condition is satisfied for every choice and rules out nothing. Now the 3. Divisibility by 3 rides on the digit sum, since each place value is one more than a multiple of 3 and closure lets the multiples fall away, leaving only the sum of the digits to decide things. Adding the visible digits and the hidden one gives $$7 + \square + 3 + 2 + 0 = 12 + \square.$$ I need \(12 + \square\) to be a multiple of 3. Since \(12\) is already a multiple of 3, the sum stays a multiple of 3 exactly when \(\square\) is itself a multiple of 3, which among the digits \(0\) through \(9\) means \(\square = 0, 3, 6,\) or \(9\). Every one of those also passed the 8 test, so all four work, and the hidden digit can be \(\boxed{0, 3, 6, 9}\). Check, take \(\square = 6\), giving \(76{,}320 = 8 \times 9{,}540 = 3 \times 25{,}440\), divisible by both, while a non-multiple like \(\square = 4\) gives digit sum \(16\), which is not a multiple of 3, so it fails as expected. The general idea is that divisibility by 8 is settled by the last three digits and divisibility by 3 by the digit sum, and when a digit is hidden you simply find every value that makes both tests pass at the same time.
Review 12. Tickets read 2,051; 4,613; 5,000; 8,267; and 3,002. Unlike 2 or 5, the number 7 has no digit shortcut, so divide honestly. How many win a cookie?
Show solution
Since \(7\) offers no digit trick the way \(2\), \(5\), and \(10\) do, we divide each ticket by \(7\) and look at the remainder. $$2{,}051 = 7 \times 293$$ exactly, so \(2{,}051\) is divisible by \(7\). $$4{,}613 = 7 \times 659$$ exactly, so \(4{,}613\) is divisible by \(7\). $$5{,}000 = 7 \times 714 + 2$$ leaves a remainder of \(2\), so \(5{,}000\) is not divisible by \(7\). $$8{,}267 = 7 \times 1{,}181$$ exactly, so \(8{,}267\) is divisible by \(7\). $$3{,}002 = 7 \times 428 + 6$$ leaves a remainder of \(6\), so \(3{,}002\) is not divisible by \(7\). The winning tickets are \(\boxed{2{,}051,\ 4{,}613,\ 8{,}267}\). Check by multiplying back, \(7\times293=2{,}051\), \(7\times659=4{,}613\), and \(7\times1{,}181=8{,}267\), all landing right on the nose. The general idea is that not every divisor comes with a handy digit test, and for one like \(7\) you simply divide and read the remainder, where a remainder of \(0\) means the number is divisible.
Review 13. A wristband serial reads 41_9 with the tens digit hidden. Every serial is divisible by 11. Use the alternating-sum test backward to find the hidden digit.
Show solution
The divisibility test for 11 comes from place value, since \(10\) leaves a remainder of \(-1\) on division by 11, so \(100\) leaves \(+1\), \(1000\) leaves \(-1\), and the place values flip sign as you climb, which means a number is a multiple of 11 exactly when the digits taken with alternating signs from the right add to a multiple of 11. For the serial \(41\_9\) with hidden tens digit \(d\), reading from the right with the units plus, the tens minus, the hundreds plus, and the thousands minus gives \(9 - d + 1 - 4 = 6 - d\). Because \(d\) is a lone digit between 0 and 9, the quantity \(6 - d\) only ranges from 6 down to \(-3\), and the single multiple of 11 sitting in that range is 0, so \(6 - d = 0\) forces \(d = \boxed{6}\). Check by filling the digit back in, which makes the serial \(4169\), and \(11 \times 379 = 4169\), so it really is a multiple of 11, and the alternating sum reads \(9 - 6 + 1 - 4 = 0\) as it should. The general idea is that the alternating digit sum is a fingerprint for divisibility by 11, and when one digit is missing you can run the test in reverse, writing the unknown as a letter and solving the little equation that says the signed total must be a multiple of 11.
