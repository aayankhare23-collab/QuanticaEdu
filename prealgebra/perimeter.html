Prealgebra · Lesson 11.5

Perimeter

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

You already know the perimeter is the distance all the way around a figure, the total you get by adding up its sides. Now we turn that into real skill. We will build quick formulas for rectangles and squares, work with figures whose sides are not all labelled so you have to deduce the missing ones, and find a neat trick for what the boundary does when you cut a piece away.

Key idea
Perimeter of a rectangle and a square. A rectangle has two lengths and two widths, so instead of adding four numbers you can add one length and one width and double it, \(P = 2(l + w) = 2l + 2w\). A square is even simpler, since all four sides are equal, \(P = 4s\). The figure above shows a rectangle with its two \(l\)'s and two \(w\)'s.
In the diagram above is a rectangle 12 units wide and 7 units tall. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A rectangle has two equal widths and two equal heights, so instead of adding four sides you can add one of each and double the result. $$P = 2(l+w) = 2(12+7) = 2\times 19 = 38$$ The perimeter is \(\boxed{38}\) units.
In the diagram above is a square with side 9. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A square's four sides are all equal, so its perimeter is \(4s\). With a side of 9 that gives $$P = 4 \times 9 = 36.$$ The perimeter is \(\boxed{36}\) units.
Key idea
Working backward from the perimeter. The formulas run in reverse too. If you know a rectangle's perimeter and one side, you can undo the formula to find the other. Since \(2(l+w) = P\), one length plus one width is half the perimeter, so \(l + w = \frac{P}{2}\). Find that half, then subtract the side you already know and what's left is the side you want. A square is even quicker. All four sides are equal, so one side is a quarter of the perimeter, \(s = \frac{P}{4}\).
The figure above is a rectangle with length \(13\) and an unknown width. Its perimeter is \(40\). Find the width. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is \(2(l+w)\), two lengths and two widths together. That means half the perimeter is a single length plus a single width. Halving gives $$\frac{40}{2}=20,$$ and this \(20\) is one length plus one width. The length is \(13\), so the width is whatever is left after taking the length out of \(20\). We get \(20-13=7\), so the width is \(\boxed{7}\).
In the diagram above is a square whose perimeter is \(48\). Find the length of one side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A square's four sides are equal, so its perimeter is \(4s\). Here \(4s = 48\), so divide both sides by 4 to get one side. $$s = \frac{48}{4} = \boxed{12}$$ One side is 12 units.
Key idea
Figures with hidden sides. A composite figure is made of straight sides that step in and out, like an L. Its perimeter is still the sum of ALL its sides, but often one side is left unmarked. You find it from the sides you do know. The horizontal pieces across the top must span the same total width as the bottom, and the vertical pieces down one side must add to the same height as the other. So a missing length is just the matching run minus the pieces you already have. In the figure above the top spans \(8\) while the marked piece below it is \(5\), so the hidden piece is \(8 - 5 = 3\).
The diagram above shows an L-shaped figure with its six sides marked \(9\), \(3\), \(5\), \(3\), \(4\), and \(6\). Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Perimeter is the distance all the way around, so add the six sides. Since every side is marked, no deducing is needed here, just total them up. $$9 + 3 + 5 + 3 + 4 + 6 = \boxed{30}$$ The perimeter is \(30\) units.
In the diagram above is a staircase figure with all eight of its sides marked. The bottom and the left side are each \(6\), and the six little step sides are each \(2\). Add up every side to find the perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Add every side. Going around, the marked lengths are the \(6\) at the bottom, the \(6\) up the left, and the six step sides of \(2\) each. $$6 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 6 = 24.$$ Here is why the answer comes out so clean. The three horizontal steps span the same total width as the \(6\) across the bottom, and the three vertical steps rise the same total height as the \(6\) on the left. So the staircase wraps exactly as far as the \(6\)-by-\(6\) box around it, giving \(2(6+6)=\boxed{24}\).
Key idea
A staircase and its box. Draw the smallest rectangle that a staircase fits inside, its bounding box. As you walk the steps, every little horizontal piece slides over to cover exactly the width of the box twice, once along the top and once along the bottom, and every vertical piece covers the height twice. So a figure that only steps outward has the very same perimeter as its bounding box, \(2(W+H)\), no matter how many steps. The figure above shows the box drawn around the steps.
In the diagram above is a staircase figure. Only its overall width \(12\) along the bottom and its overall height \(8\) up the left side are marked; every other side is left blank. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The steps look like they should add extra length, but a staircase only ever steps outward. Slide every horizontal rung up to the top and every vertical riser out to the right, and the outline collapses onto its bounding box with no length lost. So the perimeter equals that of a \(12\) by \(8\) rectangle. $$P = 2(W+H) = 2(12+8) = \boxed{40}$$
Key idea
Notches and slots. When you cut a rectangular piece out of a figure, the perimeter may or may not change, and which one depends entirely on where you cut. Take a bite out of a corner and the two new edges you open up run exactly as far as the two edge-pieces you just removed, so nothing is gained or lost and the perimeter stays the same. Cut a slot straight into the middle of an edge and it is a different story. You still lose the little stretch of edge where the slot opens, but now you also gain two walls that go down into the figure and back up out of it. Those two walls are the extra length, and each one is as deep as the slot, so the perimeter grows by twice the depth. In the figure above those two new walls are the pieces highlighted going down and back up.
The diagram above is a rectangle 14 by 9 with a small rectangular piece cut out of one corner. Its six sides, going around, are \(14\), \(7\), \(3\), \(2\), \(11\), and \(9\). Find the perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Cutting a corner notch never changes the perimeter. The two short sides of the notch step inward and then back out, and together they cover the same distance as the piece of the original edge they replaced. So the boundary is still as long as the full \(14\) by \(9\) rectangle, $$2(14+9) = 46.$$ Adding the six marked sides confirms it, \(14+7+3+2+11+9 = 46\), so the perimeter is \(\boxed{46}\).
In the diagram above is a rectangle 12 units wide and 8 units tall, with a slot cut into its top edge 3 units wide and 2 units deep. Find the perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Begin with the whole rectangle, ignoring the slot. Its perimeter is $$2(12+8) = 40.$$ Now cut the slot. Its 3-unit width was already sitting on the top edge, so removing that strip and replacing it takes away 3 and puts back the same 3 along the slot's floor. The top edge length does not change. What is genuinely new are the two side walls of the slot, each 2 units deep, so the slot adds \(2\times 2 = 4\). The perimeter is \(40 + 4 = \boxed{44}\).
Key idea
Perimeter as an equation. When the sides are written as expressions in \(x\), the perimeter is still their sum. Add the expressions to get the perimeter in terms of \(x\). If a side is \(x+3\) and another is \(2x\), they combine just like numbers, so the perimeter might come out to something like \(6x+10\). And if you are told what the perimeter equals, set that expression equal to the given total and solve for \(x\). It is the same adding and doubling you already do, now carried out with letters.
The rectangle in the diagram above has length \((x+3)\) and width \(2x\), and its perimeter is \(30\). Find \(x\). Give the value of \(x\).
Show solution
Perimeter is \(2(l+w)\), so \(2((x+3)+2x)=30\). Divide both sides by \(2\) to get \((x+3)+2x=15\), which is \(3x+3=15\). Then \(3x=12\), so \(x=\boxed{4}\).
In the diagram above is a triangle whose three sides are \(x\), \((x+2)\), and \((x+4)\), and its perimeter is \(27\). Find the LONGEST side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of the three sides, so add the labels and set the total to \(27\).$$x + (x+2) + (x+4) = 27$$Combine like terms to get \(3x + 6 = 27\), so \(3x = 21\) and \(x = 7\). The longest side is \(x+4\), which is \(7+4 = \boxed{11}\).
Fun fact
Legend says that when Queen Dido founded the city of Carthage, she was allowed only as much land as a single oxhide could surround. So she cut the hide into one long, thin strip to make the longest boundary she could, then laid it in a great arc against the sea to enclose the most land possible. It is an ancient echo of a real idea, that for a fixed perimeter a rounded shape holds the most area.
The figure above is an L-shaped room. Its six walls measure \(16\), \(10\), \(7\), \(4\), \(9\), and \(6\). A builder wants to run baseboard trim all the way around the floor, along every wall. Find the total length of baseboard needed, which is the perimeter of the room. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The baseboard hugs every wall with no breaks, so the length you need is exactly the perimeter, the sum of all the sides. Here all six walls are already marked, so there is nothing hidden to work out. Just add them. $$16 + 10 + 7 + 4 + 9 + 6 = \boxed{52}$$ So the builder needs \(52\) units of baseboard.
In the diagram above is a U-shaped figure with a deep slot cut down from the top. Its eight sides, going around, are \(12\), \(8\), \(4\), \(5\), \(4\), \(5\), \(4\), and \(8\). Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the length of the full trip around the outside, so add every side. The slot is the key trap here. When you cut a slot into an edge you carve two new walls going in and out of it, and you walk along both, so both \(5\)-unit walls count. Add all eight sides $$12 + 8 + 4 + 5 + 4 + 5 + 4 + 8 = 50.$$ The perimeter is \(\boxed{50}\) units.
Key idea
The perimeter toolkit. A rectangle's perimeter is \(2(l+w)\) and a square's is \(4s\), and both run backward just as well: if you know the perimeter, divide back out to recover a missing side. For a figure that is not a plain rectangle, there is no shortcut to memorize. Add up every side. The one move you need is deducing an unmarked side from the run across from it, since the pieces along the top span the same width as the bottom and the pieces up one side span the same height as the other. Two shapes are worth recognizing on sight. A staircase that only ever steps outward has the same perimeter as the bounding box it fits inside, \(2(W+H)\), because sliding each step out to the edge changes nothing in the total. Reshaping tells a similar story: cutting a corner notch leaves the perimeter untouched, since the two new edges replace exactly the two you removed, but cutting a slot into an edge adds twice its depth, because you travel down into it and back out. And when the sides are given as expressions, the perimeter is still just their sum. Set that sum equal to the total you were given and solve for \(x\).

Review problems

Review 1.
In the diagram above is a rectangle 15 units wide and 8 units tall. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A rectangle's perimeter is the whole trip around the outside, and since opposite sides match it equals \(2(l+w)\). Reading the diagram above, the width is \(15\) and the height is \(8\), so $$2(15+8) = 2 \times 23 = \boxed{46}.$$ The perimeter is \(46\) units.
Review 2.
The diagram above shows a square with each side 11 units long. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A square is four copies of the same side, so its perimeter is \(4s\). With \(s = 11\), $$4 \times 11 = 44.$$ The perimeter is \(\boxed{44}\) units.
Review 3.
The rectangle in the diagram above is 16 units long, and its perimeter is 50. Find its width. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A rectangle's perimeter is \(2(l+w)\), so half of it is exactly one length plus one width. Half of \(50\) is \(25\), and that equals the length plus the width. The length is \(16\), so the width is $$25 - 16 = \boxed{9}.$$
Review 4.
The diagram above shows a square whose perimeter is \(52\). Find the length of one side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
All four sides of a square are equal, so the perimeter is \(4s\). Setting that equal to \(52\) and dividing by \(4\) gives the side.$$s = \frac{52}{4} = \boxed{13}$$
Review 5.
In the diagram above is an L-shape with its six sides marked \(8\), \(4\), \(5\), \(3\), \(3\), and \(7\). Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter of any figure is the total distance around it, so add up all six sides marked in the diagram. $$8+4+5+3+3+7=\boxed{30}$$ Walking once around the L and adding each side as you pass it gives \(30\) units.
Review 6.
In the diagram above is a staircase figure with its six sides marked \(8\), \(3\), \(4\), \(3\), \(4\), and \(6\). Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Perimeter is the total distance around the figure, so add all six marked sides. $$8+3+4+3+4+6 = \boxed{28}$$ The perimeter is \(28\) units.
Review 7.
The staircase in the diagram above steps outward from bottom to top, and only two lengths are marked: the total width \(10\) along the bottom and the total height \(7\) up the left side. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
A staircase that only steps outward has the same perimeter as its bounding box, the smallest rectangle it fits inside. Every horizontal piece of the top edge, added up, spans the same \(10\) as the bottom, and every vertical piece down the right side, added up, climbs the same \(7\) as the left. So the total path around the staircase matches the path around the \(10\) by \(7\) rectangle. $$P = 2(10 + 7) = 2(17) = \boxed{34}$$ You never needed the individual step sizes. As long as the figure only steps out, they rearrange into the sides of the bounding box.
Review 8.
The figure above is a 12-by-8 rectangle with a small notch cut out of one corner. Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Cutting a notch out of a corner does not change the perimeter. The two short segments removed from the outer edges are exactly replaced by the two segments of the notch, so the trip around the boundary is the same length as around the full rectangle. That means you can ignore the notch and use \(2(l+w)\) on the original 12-by-8 rectangle. $$2(12+8) = \boxed{40}$$
Review 9.
The figure above is an 11-by-7 rectangle with a slot 2 units wide and 3 units deep cut straight into its top edge. Find the perimeter of this figure. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Cutting a slot into an edge adds twice its depth, since you now trace both walls going down and back up. The full rectangle is \(2(11+7) = 36\), and the slot adds \(2\times 3 = 6\). $$36 + 6 = \boxed{42}$$ The width of the slot changes nothing here, because the top edge you removed is exactly replaced by the slot's floor. The perimeter is \(42\) units.
Review 10.
In the diagram above is a rectangle whose length is \((x+5)\) and whose width is \(3x\). Its perimeter is \(42\). Find the value of \(x\). Give the value of \(x\).
Show solution
The perimeter is \(2(l+w)\), so one length plus one width is half of it, \(21\). That gives $$(x+5)+3x = 21.$$ Combine the \(x\) terms to get \(4x + 5 = 21\), so \(4x = 16\) and \(x = \boxed{4}\).
Review 11.
In the diagram above is an isosceles triangle whose sides are labelled \(x\), \(2x\), and \(2x\), and its perimeter is \(40\). Find the length of the longest side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter is the sum of all three sides, so \(x + 2x + 2x = 5x\). Setting that equal to the given perimeter gives $$5x = 40,\qquad x = 8.$$ The longest side is \(2x = 2(8) = \boxed{16}\).
Review 12.
In the diagram above is an L-shaped patio. Its walls, going around, measure \(15, 9, 6, 4, 9,\) and \(5\) units. Find the perimeter of the patio. Give the number of units.
Show solution
Perimeter is the total distance around the figure, so add all six walls in order. $$15+9+6+4+9+5 = \boxed{48}$$ The patio's perimeter is \(48\) units.
Review 13.
The diagram above shows a U-shape with its sides marked \(14, 9, 5, 6, 4, 6, 5, 9\). Find its perimeter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The perimeter of any figure is the sum of all its sides, so read the eight marked lengths off the diagram and add them up. $$14+9+5+6+4+6+5+9 = \boxed{58}$$ The perimeter is \(58\) units.
