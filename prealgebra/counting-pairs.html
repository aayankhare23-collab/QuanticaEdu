Prealgebra · Lesson 10.4

Counting Pairs

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

When order matters, multiply straight across. But when picking a pair, like two people shaking hands or two teammates, order doesn't matter, and a straight multiply counts every pair twice.

Six friends each shake hands once with every other friend. How many handshakes happen in all?
Show solution
A handshake is really just an unordered pair of friends, where Ana shaking Ben's hand is the same single handshake as Ben shaking Ana's hand. So this is a pair count, and everyone comes from the one group of six friends. \nLet us count the easy way first and fix the trouble after. Each of the \(6\) friends shakes hands with the other \(5\) friends, and multiplying those gives $$6 \times 5 = 30.$$ \nBut look closely, because that \(30\) counts every handshake twice. When Ana reaches out to Ben we count it once, and when Ben reaches out to Ana we count the same handshake again. Each pair got tallied from both sides, so the count is doubled. \nThe honest answer is half as big, so we divide by \(2\) to undo the overcount, which gives $$30 / 2 = 15.$$ \nSo the six friends share \(\boxed{15}\) handshakes.
Key idea
Counting pairs. The number of unordered pairs from \(n\) items is \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\). First count the ordered way you trust from the Multiplication Principle: \(n\) choices for the first pick, then \(n-1\) left for the second, giving \(n(n-1)\). But every pair got counted twice, once as A then B and once as B then A, the same handshake both times, so divide by \(2\) to undo the double count. Count ordered, then halve.
In an 8-player round-robin tournament, every pair of players meets exactly once. How many matches are played?
Show solution
Every match is just a pair of players, so we are choosing \(2\) players from the \(8\), where order does not matter since Ana playing Ben is the same match as Ben playing Ana. Start with the multiplying habit. Each of the \(8\) players can face any of the other \(7\), which gives \(8 \times 7 = 56\). But that counts each match twice, once from each player's side, so we undo the overcount by dividing by \(2\). That gives $$\frac{8 \times 7}{2} = \frac{56}{2} = 28.$$ Since both players come from the same group of \(8\), the divide-by-\(2\) is exactly what keeps us honest, and we land on \(\boxed{28}\) matches.
Key idea
The pair formula. Choosing \(2\) things from \(n\) is the workhorse of this chapter, and it always counts the same way: \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\). A handshake in a room, a match in a round-robin, a team of two, a pair of co-presidents, every one of them is just a pair pulled from \(n\). When you meet a new pair problem, draw two boxes for the two picks, multiply to fill them, then halve, because the two boxes are really the same pair seen from each side.
An ice cream shop has \(11\) flavors. A double scoop uses \(2\) different flavors, order doesn't matter. How many different double scoops are possible?
Show solution
A double scoop is just an unordered pair of flavors, because a scoop of mint on top of strawberry is the exact same dessert as strawberry on top of mint. So this is the handshake idea wearing a new costume, and we count pairs from one group of \(11\). Start the way we multiply in 10.2. The first flavor can be any of the \(11\), and once it is chosen the second flavor can be any of the \(10\) that are left, and you get $$11 \times 10 = 110.$$ But watch the same trap as the handshakes. Choosing chocolate first and vanilla second gives the same double scoop as choosing vanilla first and chocolate second, so every scoop has been counted twice. To undo that overcount we take half, which gives $$\frac{11 \times 10}{2} = \frac{110}{2} = 55.$$ So there are \(\boxed{55}\) different double scoops.
Key idea
Same group vs. two groups. When the two things you pick come from the same group, like two people in one room, every pair gets counted twice, so you divide by \(2\). But when you pick one thing from each of two groups you can tell apart, like a leader and a follower or a team from each of two schools, the two picks can never swap places, so nothing is ever counted twice. There you just multiply the group sizes and do not divide at all.
A dance class has \(7\) leaders and \(6\) followers. Every leader dances once with every follower. How many dances take place?
Show solution
Each dance pairs one leader with one follower, and those two picks come from TWO different groups, the leaders and the followers. When the picks come from groups you can tell apart, you simply multiply the sizes, so $$7\times6=42.$$ Notice there is no dividing by \(2\) this time. We divide by \(2\) only when both picks come from the same group, because then choosing Ana then Ben is the same pair as choosing Ben then Ana. Here a leader and a follower can never swap roles, so no dance is ever counted twice and nothing needs undoing. The number of dances is \(\boxed{42}\).
At a party, \(5\) women shake hands with every other person, but the \(4\) men never shake hands with each other. How many handshakes take place?
Show solution
A handshake is a pair of people, and order does not matter, so all we are really doing is counting the allowed pairs. The trick is that the pairs come in different kinds, so let us split them up and count each kind on its own. \nFirst the woman-with-woman handshakes. Both people in such a pair come from the same group of \(5\) women. Each woman could reach out to the other \(4\), and you get \(5 \times 4\), but that counts Ana-with-Bea and Bea-with-Ana as two different things when they are the very same handshake. So we divide by \(2\) to undo that double count, which gives \(\frac{5 \times 4}{2} = 10\). \nNext the woman-with-man handshakes. Now the two people come from groups you can tell apart, one woman and one man, so nothing gets counted twice and there is no dividing by \(2\). You simply multiply the sizes, which gives \(5 \times 4 = 20\). \nLast the man-with-man handshakes. The rule says no two men shake hands, so this kind adds nothing at all, a flat \(0\). \nNow add the kinds together, and you get \(10 + 20 = 30\). $$\boxed{30}$$
Key idea
Pairs with restrictions. When some pairs are off limits, you do not count them one by one. Count every pair with \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\), then take away the forbidden ones, the way the diagonals of a polygon are all the pairs of corners with the \(n\) sides removed. Or split the pairs into kinds, like same-group versus cross-group, count each kind on its own, and add. That is the casework from 10.3 wearing a new hat, only now the things you split and subtract are pairs.
A hexagon has \(6\) corners and \(6\) sides. How many diagonals does it have?
Show solution
Start with a friendly idea. Every pair of corners in the hexagon is joined by a line, and that line is either a side or a diagonal, never both. So if you count all the pairs of corners first, the diagonals are just what is left after you take the sides away. Count the pairs of corners. Each of the \(6\) corners can reach out to the other \(5\) corners, and that gives \(6 \times 5\). But joining corner A to corner B is the same line as joining corner B to A, so every pair got counted twice. Divide by \(2\) to undo that double count, which gives $$\frac{6 \times 5}{2} = 15.$$ So there are \(15\) pairs of corners in all. Of these, \(6\) of the lines are the sides of the hexagon, and a side is not a diagonal. Take those away and the diagonals are $$15 - 6 = 9.$$ So the hexagon has \(\boxed{9}\) diagonals.
Key idea
Diagonals of a polygon. A polygon with \(n\) corners has \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2} - n\) diagonals. Pick any two corners and the line joining them is either a side or a diagonal, never both. There are \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\) pairs of corners in all, and exactly \(n\) of them are sides, one per edge, which leaves \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2} - n\) diagonals. You did not hunt for diagonals one by one, you counted every pair the easy way and subtracted the few you did not want.
A decagon has \(10\) corners and \(10\) sides. How many diagonals does it have?
Show solution
Start by counting every pair of corners, since each pair gives one straight segment joining them. There are \(10\) corners, and each one reaches out to the other \(9\), which gives \(10 \times 9\). But Ana to Ben and Ben to Ana are the same segment counted twice, so you divide by \(2\) and you get $$\frac{10 \times 9}{2} = 45.$$ Those \(45\) segments include the \(10\) sides of the decagon, and sides are not diagonals. So take them back out, which gives \(45 - 10 = 35\). The decagon has \(\boxed{35}\) diagonals.
Six players each play every other player exactly \(3\) games. How many games are played in all?
Show solution
Start by counting the matchups, since each game is played by a pair of players. Each of the \(6\) people reaches out to the other \(5\), which gives \(6 \times 5 = 30\). But that counts every matchup twice, because Ana playing Ben is the very same pairing as Ben playing Ana, so divide by \(2\) to undo the overcount and you get $$\frac{6 \times 5}{2} = 15$$ pairs of players. Now bring in the part that makes this more than a plain pair count. Each pair does not play once, it plays \(3\) games, so multiply the number of pairs by the games per pair, which gives $$15 \times 3 = 45.$$ So the tournament has \(\boxed{45}\) games in all.
Key idea
Pairs played \(k\) times. When every pair does the same thing several times, like a team that plays each other team \(k\) games, you do not start over. Count the pairs first with \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\), then multiply that by \(k\), giving \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2} \times k\). So pairs first, then the Multiplication Principle from 10.2 sitting right on top: choosing the pair handles the order-does-not-matter part, and the \(\times k\) just stacks the repeats on after.
A league has \(12\) teams in two divisions of \(6\). Every team plays every division-mate twice and every team in the other division once. How many games are in the full season?
Show solution
Split the season into two kinds of games, the ones inside a division and the ones across the divisions. Start inside one division. A game there is a pair of teams where order does not matter, so the number of pairs is \(\frac{6 \times 5}{2} = 15\). Each of those pairs plays twice, which gives \(15 \times 2 = 30\) games in that one division. There are two divisions just like it, so that part is \(30 \times 2 = 60\). Now the across games. Here you pick one team from one division and one team from the other, and those are two groups you can tell apart, so nothing gets double counted and you never divide by \(2\). Each of the \(6\) teams plays each of the other \(6\) once, which gives \(6 \times 6 = 36\). Add the two kinds together and you get \(60 + 36 = 96\). $$\boxed{96}$$
\(4\) students volunteer and exactly \(2\) work together each day. No pair repeats. How many days at most can be scheduled?
Show solution
Each day puts a different pair of students together, and no pair ever repeats, so the most days you can schedule is simply the number of different pairs you can make. To count the pairs, think of each of the \(4\) students reaching out to the other \(3\), which gives \(4 \times 3 = 12\). But that counts every pair twice, once from each person's side, since Ana with Ben is the same team as Ben with Ana. So the honest count is half as big and you divide by \(2\), which gives $$\frac{4 \times 3}{2} = \frac{12}{2} = 6.$$ There are \(6\) different pairs, so the greatest number of days that can be scheduled is \(\boxed{6}\).
Key idea
Pairs are triangular numbers. Counting pairs is secretly adding up \(1 + 2 + \cdots + (n-1)\). Bring people in one at a time and the second person makes \(1\) new pair, the third makes \(2\), the fourth makes \(3\), up to \(n-1\) for the last, so the running total climbs the staircase \(1, 2, 3, \ldots\) That is why \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\) and \(1 + 2 + \cdots + (n-1)\) are the same number in two outfits. Add a long climbing sum in a flash with the Gauss trick: pair the ends, first plus last, second plus second-to-last, since every such pair sums to the same thing.
A 17-player round-robin has \(\frac{17 \times 16}{2} = 136\) matches. Verify this by computing the sum \(1+2+3+\cdots+16\) using the Gauss pairing trick. What is the value of this sum?
Show solution
This is the triangular-number way of counting pairs. Instead of adding \(1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + 16\) term by term, fold the staircase in half the way Gauss did and pair the terms from the ends. The smallest plus the largest gives \(1 + 16 = 17\), the next pair gives \(2 + 15 = 17\), and you keep going inward down to \(8 + 9 = 17\). Every pair sums to the same \(17\). There are \(8\) of these pairs, since \(16\) terms split into \(8\) cozy couples, which gives $$8 \times 17 = 136.$$ This matches the pair-counting formula straight away, because choosing a pair from \(17\) players is \(\frac{17 \times 16}{2} = 136\). Two roads, one answer, and the adding road shows you that counting pairs is really just stacking up \(1 + 2 + \cdots + (n-1)\). So the sum is \(\boxed{136}\).
An icosahedron has \(12\) vertices and \(30\) edges. Each face is a triangle. How many interior diagonals does it have?
Show solution
A diagonal is a segment, and a segment is just two corners chosen together, where it does not matter which one you name first. So this is a pair count from the one group of \(12\) vertices. Start by counting all the pairs of vertices you could possibly join. Each of the \(12\) vertices can reach out to the other \(11\), and then you divide by \(2\) so that joining Ana to Ben and joining Ben to Ana count as one segment, which gives $$\frac{12 \times 11}{2} = 66.$$ That \(66\) counts every segment, but we only want the interior ones. So we remove the pairs that lie on a common face. Here is the lovely part. Every face is a triangle, and a triangle has no diagonals of its own, so the only vertex-pairs that share a face are the ones that are actual edges of the solid. There are \(30\) edges, so there are \(30\) pairs to take out. Taking those out leaves $$66 - 30 = 36.$$ So an icosahedron has \(\boxed{36}\) interior diagonals.
Fun fact
The pair counts \(1, 3, 6, 10, 15, \ldots\) are called triangular numbers because they equal the number of dots that tile a perfect triangle, \(1\) on top, then a row of \(2\), then \(3\), and so on.
Key idea
Pairs summary. To count pairs from one group, multiply then divide by \(2\), giving \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\), since each thing reaches out to the other \(n-1\) and then you undo the double count. Across two groups you can tell apart, just multiply the sizes and never divide. When some pairs are not allowed, subtract the forbidden ones or split into cases, and for the diagonals of a polygon take all the corner pairs and subtract the \(n\) sides, giving \(\frac{n(n-1)}{2} - n\). In 10.5 these very counts become the denominators of probabilities.

Review problems

Review 1. A 13-player round-robin has every pair playing exactly once. How many matches are played?
Show solution
A match is a pair of players, so we are counting pairs from one group of \(13\). Start the way we always do, by letting each player reach out to everyone else. Each of the \(13\) players faces the other \(12\), and multiplying gives \(13 \times 12 = 156\). But here is the trap, because the match where Ana plays Ben is the very same match as the one where Ben plays Ana, yet we just counted it twice, once from each side. To undo that double count we cut the total in half, so we divide by \(2\) and get $$\frac{13 \times 12}{2} = 78.$$ So the tournament has \(\boxed{78}\) matches.
Review 2. Fourteen people each shake hands once with every other person. How many handshakes happen?
Show solution
A handshake joins two people, and the order does not matter, since Ana shaking Ben's hand and Ben shaking Ana's hand are the very same handshake. So we are counting pairs, and everyone comes from one group of \(14\). Give each person a hand to reach out. Each of the \(14\) people reaches the other \(13\), which gives \(14\times13\). But this counts every handshake twice, once from each person's side, so the honest count is half as big and we divide by \(2\), and you get $$\frac{14\times13}{2}=91.$$ So the number of handshakes is \(\boxed{91}\).
Review 3. A team of \(16\) picks \(2\) players as co-captains (they share the same role). How many pairs of co-captains are possible?
Show solution
Both co-captains come from the same team, and the two of them share the job, so naming one first and the other second does not make a new choice. That tells us we are counting pairs, where order does not matter. \nThink about it from one player's view first. Any single player could team up with any of the other \(15\) players, and there are \(16\) players who could do that reaching out, which gives \(16 \times 15 = 240\). But here is the trap. When Maya pairs with Leo and when Leo pairs with Maya, that is the very same pair of co-captains counted twice. So \(240\) is exactly double the honest answer. \nWe undo that double count by cutting it in half, and you get \(\frac{16 \times 15}{2} = 120\). So there are $$\boxed{120}$$ different pairs of co-captains.
Review 4. Eighteen points lie on a circle. A chord connects each pair of points. How many chords are drawn?
Show solution
A chord just connects two of the points, so drawing a chord is the same as choosing a pair of points. The order does not matter, because the chord joining point \(A\) to point \(B\) is the exact same chord as the one joining \(B\) to \(A\), and all the points sit in one single group on the circle. \nLet us count the reaching-out way first. Each of the \(18\) points can connect to any of the other \(17\) points, which gives \(18 \times 17 = 306\). But watch the trap. When point \(A\) reaches over to \(B\) and when \(B\) reaches back to \(A\), that is one and the same chord counted twice. So \(306\) is exactly double the real number of chords. \nWe undo the overcount by dividing by \(2\), which gives $$\frac{18 \times 17}{2} = \frac{306}{2} = 153.$$ \nSo the number of chords drawn is \(\boxed{153}\).
Review 5. A pentagon has \(5\) corners and \(5\) sides. How many diagonals does it have?
Show solution
Start by counting every pair of corners, because each segment of the pentagon, side or diagonal, joins two corners. There are \(5\) corners, and each one can pair with the other \(4\), which gives \(5 \times 4\). But that counts every pair twice, once from each corner, like corner Ana to corner Ben and corner Ben to corner Ana being the same segment, so you divide by \(2\) and you get $$\frac{5 \times 4}{2} = 10$$ corner pairs in all. Now \(10\) is too many, because some of those pairs are the sides of the pentagon, not diagonals. A pentagon has \(5\) sides, so take those out, which gives \(10 - 5 = 5\). So the pentagon has \(\boxed{5}\) diagonals.
Review 6. An octagon has \(8\) corners and \(8\) sides. How many diagonals does it have?
Show solution
Every segment between two corners is decided by a pair of corners, and the pair from A to B is the very same pair as from B to A, so order does not matter and all the corners come from one group of \(8\). First count how many pairs of corners there are in all. Each of the \(8\) corners reaches out to the other \(7\), which gives \(8 \times 7\), but that counts each pair twice, once from each end, so you divide by \(2\) and get $$\frac{8 \times 7}{2} = 28.$$ That \(28\) is every segment you could draw between two corners. But \(8\) of those segments are the sides of the octagon, and a side is not a diagonal, so take those away, which gives \(28 - 8 = 20\). So an octagon has \(\boxed{20}\) diagonals.
Review 7. School A has \(7\) players and School B has \(8\). Every player from A plays every player from B once. How many games are played?
Show solution
Every game pairs one player from the first school with one player from the second school. Take any one of the \(7\) players on the first team, and that player has \(8\) opponents to face on the other team, which gives \(8\) games. The same is true for each of the \(7\) players, so you multiply, \(7 \times 8 = 56\). Now check whether you have overcounted. When two players come from the same group you would count each handshake twice and need to divide by \(2\), but here the two players come from different schools that you can tell apart, so no game is ever counted twice and you never divide by \(2\). The honest total is $$7 \times 8 = 56,$$ so the number of games is \(\boxed{56}\).
Review 8. At a gathering of \(6\) women and \(3\) men, every woman shakes hands with every other person, but the men never shake hands with each other. How many handshakes happen?
Show solution
Let us sort the handshakes into the kinds that happen. First the women among themselves. A handshake here is a pair of women, where order does not matter, since Ana shaking Bea is the very same handshake as Bea shaking Ana. Each of the \(6\) women reaches out to the other \(5\), which gives \(6 \times 5\), but that counts every handshake twice, so we halve it and get \(\frac{6 \times 5}{2} = 15\). Next the women with the men. Here the two people come from groups we can tell apart, a woman from one side and a man from the other, so nothing is double counted and we simply multiply. That is \(6 \times 3 = 18\), with no dividing by \(2\). The men never shake each other, so there is nothing more to add. Putting the two counts together gives \(15 + 18 = 33\). So the number of handshakes is \(\boxed{33}\).
Review 9. A club has \(6\) players and every pair plays \(4\) games. How many games are played in all?
Show solution
First figure out how many different pairs of players there are, since each pair is one matchup. Each of the \(6\) players reaches out to the other \(5\), and you get \(6 \times 5\). But when Ana faces Ben and when Ben faces Ana that is the very same pair counted twice, so the honest count is half as big, which means we divide by \(2\) and you get $$\frac{6 \times 5}{2} = 15$$ pairs of players. Now each of those \(15\) pairs plays \(4\) games, not just one. So we multiply the number of pairs by the games each pair plays, which gives \(15 \times 4 = 60\). So the total number of games is \(\boxed{60}\).
Review 10. A league has \(8\) teams in two divisions of \(4\). Every team plays each division-mate twice and each team in the other division once. How many games are in the season?
Show solution
There are really two different kinds of games here, so let us count each kind on its own and add them at the end. \nFirst the games inside a division. A game between two teams in the same division is just a pair of teams, and the order does not matter, since playing team A against team B is the very same game as team B against team A. With \(4\) teams in a division, the number of pairs is $$\frac{4 \times 3}{2} = 6.$$\nEach of those pairs meets twice, so one division has \(6 \times 2 = 12\) games. There are two divisions built the same way, so the inside games total $$12 + 12 = 24.$$ \nNow the games across the divisions. Here you pick one team from the first division and one team from the second division. These come from two groups you can tell apart, so nothing gets double counted and you do not divide by \(2\), you simply multiply the sizes and you get $$4 \times 4 = 16.$$ \nThe inside games and the across games never overlap, so we add them and you get $$24 + 16 = 40.$$ \nSo the season has \(\boxed{40}\) games.
Review 11. Find the sum \(2 + 4 + 6 + \cdots + 28\) (the first 14 even numbers) using the Gauss pairing trick.
Show solution
Instead of adding the numbers one slow step at a time, let us pair them from the ends, the same folding move that made counting pairs so friendly. Walk in from both sides at once. The smallest and the largest team up first, and you get \(2 + 28 = 30\). The next ones in are \(4\) and \(26\), and you get \(4 + 26 = 30\) again. Keep going, \(6 + 24 = 30\), and the same total keeps coming up.\nEvery pair lands on \(30\), which is the nice part. There are \(14\) numbers in the list, and folding them in half makes \(7\) pairs. So you have \(7\) pairs that each add to \(30\), which gives $$7 \times 30 = 210.$$\nThat is exactly the Gauss staircase trick. You can also see it as \(14 \times 15 = 210\), since the average of the two ends times the count gives the same total. So the sum is \(\boxed{210}\).
Review 12. A cube has \(8\) vertices, \(12\) edges, and \(6\) square faces (each with \(2\) face diagonals). How many interior diagonals does it have?
Show solution
Start by joining corners with no rules at all. Any line that joins two of the cube's corners comes from choosing a pair of vertices, and since starting at one corner or the other gives the same line, order does not matter. With \(8\) vertices the number of pairs is $$\frac{8 \times 7}{2} = 28.$$ So there are \(28\) lines joining corners in total. Now we sift out the ones that are not interior. Some of those pairs are edges of the cube, and a cube has \(12\) edges, so we take those away. Some other pairs run straight across a face, and each square face has \(2\) face diagonals, so across the \(6\) faces that is \(6 \times 2 = 12\) face diagonals to take away as well. What is left after removing the edges and the face diagonals must be the lines that pierce the inside, which gives $$28 - 12 - 12 = 4.$$ So a cube has \(\boxed{4}\) interior diagonals.
Review 13. A pizza shop offers \(20\) toppings. A 2-topping pizza uses two different toppings, order doesn't matter. How many different 2-topping pizzas are possible?
Show solution
Picking two different toppings from one list of \(20\), where order does not matter, is exactly a count of pairs. Think of it the way we counted handshakes. Each of the \(20\) toppings can join with any of the other \(19\), which gives \(20 \times 19\) ordered picks. But choosing mushroom then onion makes the very same pizza as onion then mushroom, so every pizza has been counted twice. To honestly count each pizza once you divide by \(2\), and you get $$\frac{20 \times 19}{2} = \frac{380}{2} = 190.$$ So there are \(\boxed{190}\) different 2-topping pizzas.
Review 14. A heptagon has \(7\) corners and \(7\) sides. How many diagonals does it have?
Show solution
A diagonal joins two corners, and the line from one corner to another is the same line no matter which corner you name first, so every diagonal is really a pair of corners where order does not matter. That is exactly a pair-counting job. With \(7\) corners, the number of ways to choose a pair is \(\frac{7 \times 6}{2} = 21\), where you multiply because each of the \(7\) corners reaches out to the other \(6\), and you divide by \(2\) because that count sees each pair twice. So there are \(21\) lines joining corners in all. But not every such line is a diagonal. The \(7\) sides of the heptagon also join two corners, and we do not want to count those. So we take all the corner pairs and subtract the sides, which gives \(21 - 7 = 14\). The heptagon has \(\boxed{14}\) diagonals.
