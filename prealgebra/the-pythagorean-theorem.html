Prealgebra · Lesson 11.8

The Pythagorean Theorem

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

The most famous relationship in all of geometry ties the three sides of a right triangle together, and it holds exactly, not just close enough. This lesson does more than hand you the formula. You will see why it has to be true using nothing but areas you already know, and then turn it loose to measure distances no ruler could ever reach.

Key idea
Legs and the hypotenuse. In a right triangle, the two sides that meet at the right angle are the legs, and the third side, the one directly across from the right angle, is the hypotenuse. The hypotenuse is always the longest side, because it stretches across from the widest corner. Getting these names straight matters, because the theorem treats the hypotenuse differently from the legs. The figure above labels all three.
Key idea
The Pythagorean Theorem. Build a square on each side of a right triangle. Here is the astonishing fact. The square on the hypotenuse has exactly the same area as the two squares on the legs put together. A square on a side of length \(s\) has area \(s^2\), so that fact is saying \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the legs and \(c\) is the hypotenuse. The figure above shows it for a 3-4-5 triangle. The small squares have areas \(9\) and \(16\), the big square has area \(25\), and sure enough \(9 + 16 = 25\).
Key idea
Why the theorem works. Here is a proof you can see. Take four copies of the right triangle and fit them into a big square whose side is \(a + b\), leaving a tilted square hole in the middle whose side is the hypotenuse \(c\). The big square's area is \((a+b)^2\). But that same area is also the four triangles plus the hole, \(4\times \frac{1}{2}ab + c^2\). Setting the two equal, $$(a+b)^2 = 2ab + c^2.$$ Now \((a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2\), so the \(2ab\) sits on both sides and cancels, leaving \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\). The figure above shows the arrangement.
In the diagram above is a right triangle with legs \(3\) and \(4\). Find the hypotenuse. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The two legs meet at the right angle, so their squares add to the square of the hypotenuse. By the theorem, \(c^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25\). Take the square root, \(c = \sqrt{25} = 5\). The hypotenuse is \(\boxed{5}\).
In the diagram above is a right triangle with legs \(6\) and \(8\). Find the hypotenuse. Give the number of units.
Show solution
By the Pythagorean Theorem, the square of the hypotenuse is the sum of the squares of the legs. $$c^2 = 6^2 + 8^2 = 36 + 64 = 100$$ Take the square root, so \(c = \sqrt{100} = 10\). The hypotenuse is \(\boxed{10}\).
In the diagram above is a right triangle with legs \(5\) and \(12\). Find the hypotenuse. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The hypotenuse is opposite the right angle, so it is the \(c\) in the Pythagorean Theorem. Add the squares of the two legs. $$c^2 = 5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169.$$ Now take the square root. Since \(13^2 = 169\), we get \(c = \sqrt{169} = 13\). This is the 5-12-13 right triangle, one worth remembering. The hypotenuse is \(\boxed{13}\).
Key idea
Working out a leg. The theorem does not only find the hypotenuse. It also finds a leg when you already know the hypotenuse and the other leg. Start from \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\) and move the known leg to the other side, which gives \(b^2 = c^2 - a^2\). So now you SUBTRACT the known leg's square from the hypotenuse's square, then take the square root. Watch the order carefully. The hypotenuse is the longest side, so \(c^2\) is the biggest square, and everything gets subtracted from it. If you ever find yourself adding when you are hunting for a leg, or subtracting a bigger number from a smaller one, you have the roles mixed up. Ask which side is opposite the right angle, call that \(c\), and take it from there.
In the diagram above is a right triangle with hypotenuse \(13\) and one leg \(5\). The other leg is the question mark. Find the missing leg. Give the number of units.
Show solution
This time we know the hypotenuse and one leg, so we subtract instead of add. Put the missing leg on its own side of \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\). $$b^2 = 13^2 - 5^2 = 169 - 25 = 144$$ Now take the square root, \(b = \sqrt{144} = 12\). This is the 5-12-13 triple, so the missing leg is \(\boxed{12}\).
In the diagram above is a right triangle with hypotenuse \(17\) and one leg \(8\). Find the missing leg. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The hypotenuse is given and one leg is missing, so subtract instead of add. Square the hypotenuse and pull out the known leg. $$b^2 = 17^2 - 8^2 = 289 - 64 = 225$$ Take the square root, \(b = \sqrt{225} = 15\). The missing leg is \(\boxed{15}\).
Key idea
Whole-number right triangles. A few right triangles have whole numbers on all three sides, and they turn up so often it pays to know them: 3-4-5, 5-12-13, and 8-15-17. Any multiple of one works too, since scaling all three sides keeps the relationship, so 6-8-10 and 9-12-15 are just the 3-4-5 triangle grown larger. Spotting a triple lets you skip the arithmetic and read the answer straight off. The figure above shows two of them.
In the diagram above is a right triangle with legs \(12\) and \(16\). Find the hypotenuse. Give the number of units.
Show solution
By the Pythagorean Theorem, add the squares of the legs to get the square of the hypotenuse. $$12^2 + 16^2 = 144 + 256 = 400$$ So \(c = \sqrt{400} = 20\). You could also spot that \(12\) and \(16\) are \(4\) times \(3\) and \(4\). This is a 3-4-5 triangle scaled by \(4\), so the hypotenuse is \(4 \times 5 = 20\). The hypotenuse is \(\boxed{20}\).
In the diagram above is a right triangle with legs \(8\) and \(15\). Find the hypotenuse. Give the number of units.
Show solution
By the Pythagorean Theorem, the square of the hypotenuse is the sum of the squares of the legs. $$c^2 = 8^2 + 15^2 = 64 + 225 = 289$$ Take the square root, \(c = \sqrt{289} = 17\). This is the 8-15-17 triple. The hypotenuse is \(\boxed{17}\).
Key idea
Reaching distances you cannot measure. The real power of the theorem is that it hands you a length you could never stretch a tape across. A ladder leans against a wall, a wire runs between the tops of two poles, a straight path cuts across a park from one corner to the opposite one. In every case the slanted distance is the hypotenuse of a right triangle, and its two legs are things you can actually measure. Walk along the ground to get one leg, look up the wall or the pole to get the other, and the theorem does the rest. You never climb the ladder or string the wire to find out how long it is. You measure the two easy sides on the ground and up the side, square them, add, and take the square root. \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\) turns two short measurements you can reach into one long one you cannot.
In the diagram above, a ladder leans against a wall. Its foot sits \(7\) feet from the wall, and the top reaches \(24\) feet up the wall. How long is the ladder? Give the number of units.
Show solution
The ladder is the hypotenuse, the side reaching across from the right angle where the wall meets the ground. The legs are \(7\) and \(24\), so the Pythagorean Theorem gives $$c^2 = 7^2 + 24^2 = 49 + 576 = 625.$$ Taking the square root, \(c = \sqrt{625} = 25\). This is the \(7\)-\(24\)-\(25\) right triangle, another whole-number set worth recognizing on sight. The ladder is \(\boxed{25}\) feet long.
In the diagram above, the tops of two poles are joined by a straight wire. The tops differ in height by \(20\) feet, and the poles stand \(21\) feet apart. Find the length of the wire. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The wire is the hypotenuse of a right triangle whose legs are the height difference \(20\) and the horizontal gap \(21\). By the Pythagorean Theorem, $$c^2 = 20^2 + 21^2 = 400 + 441 = 841.$$ Taking the square root, \(c = \sqrt{841} = \boxed{29}\). The wire is \(29\) feet long.
In the diagram above, two points sit \(9\) units apart horizontally and \(40\) units apart vertically. The straight line between them is the hypotenuse of a right triangle whose legs are those two gaps. Find the straight distance between the points. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The straight distance is the hypotenuse, with the horizontal and vertical gaps as the legs. By the Pythagorean Theorem, $$c^2 = 9^2 + 40^2 = 81 + 1600 = 1681.$$ Taking the square root, \(c = \sqrt{1681} = 41\). So the distance is \(\boxed{41}\).
In the diagram above, a rectangle has a diagonal of length \(26\) and one side of length \(10\). Find the length of the other side. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The diagonal is the hypotenuse of a right triangle whose legs are the two sides of the rectangle. To find a missing leg you subtract the squares and take the square root, $$\sqrt{26^2 - 10^2} = \sqrt{676 - 100} = \sqrt{576} = 24.$$ So the other side is \(\boxed{24}\).
Fun fact
Though it carries Pythagoras's name, this relationship was known to the Babylonians more than a thousand years before he was born. Clay tablets show they were already working with whole-number right triangles. Today there are over four hundred different proofs of the theorem, and one of them was worked out by James Garfield, a future president of the United States, while he was serving in Congress.
In the diagram above is a right triangle with legs \(35\) and \(12\). Find the hypotenuse. Give the number of units.
Show solution
By the Pythagorean Theorem, the squares of the legs add to the square of the hypotenuse. $$c^2 = 35^2 + 12^2 = 1225 + 144 = 1369$$ Take the square root, and \(c = \sqrt{1369} = 37\). This is the 12-35-37 right triangle. The hypotenuse is \(\boxed{37}\).
In the diagram above is a right triangle with hypotenuse \(25\) and one leg \(20\). The other leg is unknown. Find the area of the triangle. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
This is two steps. First find the missing leg with the Pythagorean Theorem. The hypotenuse is \(25\) and one leg is \(20\), so $$b = \sqrt{25^2 - 20^2} = \sqrt{625 - 400} = \sqrt{225} = 15.$$ Now the two legs are \(20\) and \(15\), and in a right triangle the legs are the base and height. So the area is $$\frac{1}{2}\times 20 \times 15 = \boxed{150}.$$
Key idea
The Pythagorean toolkit. Pull the lesson together. In a right triangle the legs meet at the right angle and the hypotenuse lies opposite it, the longest side. The theorem \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\) is exact, and it holds because four copies of the triangle fill an \((a+b)\) square around a \(c\) square, forcing \(a^2 + b^2\) to equal \(c^2\). To find the hypotenuse, add the squares of the legs and take the root; to find a missing leg, subtract from the hypotenuse's square and take the root. Learn the triples 3-4-5, 5-12-13, and 8-15-17 and their multiples so you can spot them on sight. And whenever a right angle turns up in the world, a ladder, a wire between two poles, a distance across a map, the theorem hands you the slanted length you could not measure directly.

In the diagram above is a right triangle with hypotenuse \(25\) and one leg \(24\). Find the other leg. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The hypotenuse is the longest side, \(25\), and one leg is \(24\), so the missing leg \(b\) satisfies \(24^2 + b^2 = 25^2\). Subtracting to isolate \(b^2\),$$b^2 = 25^2 - 24^2 = 625 - 576 = 49.$$Take the square root, \(b = \sqrt{49} = \boxed{7}\). This is the 7-24-25 right triangle, one more of the whole-number triples worth recognizing on sight.

Review 13.
In the diagram above is a right triangle with hypotenuse \(17\) and one leg \(15\). Find the area of the triangle. Give the number of square units.
Show solution
You can't find the area yet, because you only have one leg. Get the other one from the theorem, subtracting to find a missing leg.$$b^2 = 17^2 - 15^2 = 289 - 225 = 64,\quad b = \sqrt{64} = 8$$Now the two legs, \(15\) and \(8\), are the base and height of the right triangle, so the area of a triangle is half of base times height.$$\tfrac{1}{2}\times 15 \times 8 = \boxed{60}$$The area is \(60\) square units.
