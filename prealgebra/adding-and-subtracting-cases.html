Prealgebra · Lesson 10.1

Adding and Subtracting Cases

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Counting is the backbone of all combinatorics. The tricky part is never the counting itself. It is deciding what to count and making sure you include everything exactly once. Every move in this chapter is careful addition and subtraction.

Key idea
Count small, find the pattern, trust it. Counting has almost no formulas worth memorizing. When a problem looks tricky, count a tiny version of it by hand, watch the pattern that shows up, then trust that same pattern on the big problem. Every question in this chapter is just careful addition and subtraction, so the whole job is to add the cases and subtract the overlap, and to understand why each part is there instead of swallowing a trick.
An apartment has residents on every floor from the \(3\)rd to the \(11\)th, both included. How many floors have people on them?
Show solution
The floors with people are the whole numbers from \(3\) up to \(11\), so write them out and count. They are \(3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11\), which is \(9\) floors. Now look at why the quick guess misses. Subtracting gives \(11 - 3 = 8\), but that \(8\) counts the gaps you climb between floors, not the floors themselves. The \(3\)rd floor, where you started, never got counted by any of those gaps, so you have to add it back. That gives \(11 - 3 + 1 = 9\), which matches the list exactly. This little add one is the heart of counting a run of whole numbers including both ends. $$\boxed{9}$$
Key idea
Fencepost rule. The count of whole numbers from \(a\) to \(b\), with both ends included, is \(b - a + 1\). The subtraction \(b - a\) counts the gaps between the numbers, the way a fence \(10\) feet long with a post every foot has \(10\) gaps. But a fence always has one more post than gap, since both ends get a post, so you add \(1\) to put the starting post back. Subtraction finds the gaps, the \(+1\) catches the last fencepost.
A rocket countdown calls every whole number from \(30\) down to \(0\), both included. How many numbers are called?
Show solution
The announcer says every whole number from \(0\) all the way up to \(30\), and both ends are spoken. If we just did \(30 - 0\) we would get \(30\), but that counts the steps between the numbers, not the numbers themselves. It quietly leaves one number out. Think of a tiny version first. Counting from \(0\) to \(2\) gives \(0, 1, 2\), which is \(3\) numbers, yet \(2 - 0 = 2\). We are always short by one, because both endpoints need to be counted. So we add one back. For whole numbers from \(0\) to \(30\), the count is \(30 - 0 + 1\), which is \(30 + 1\), giving \(31\). The \(0\) at liftoff is a real number the announcer calls, so it must be in the count. $$\boxed{31}$$
How many whole numbers are strictly between \(1\) and \(50\), not including \(1\) or \(50\)?
Show solution
The word between leaves out both ends, so \(1\) and \(50\) are not choices. The smallest number you may pick is \(2\) and the largest is \(49\). Now you are just counting the whole numbers from \(2\) to \(49\) with both ends counted. Subtracting gives \(49 - 2 = 47\), and then you add \(1\) so you do not miss the starting number, giving \(47 + 1 = 48\). So there are \(48\) whole numbers you could pick. $$\boxed{48}$$
Key idea
Evenly spaced count. For multiples or any evenly spaced run, the count is \(\frac{\text{last}-\text{first}}{\text{step}}+1\). It is the fencepost idea with bigger gaps: the span from the first number in range to the last, divided by the step, gives the number of gaps, and you add \(1\) for the post you started on. To count the multiples of \(7\) from \(21\) to \(56\), the span \(56-21=35\) holds \(35\div 7=5\) gaps, so \(\tfrac{56-21}{7}+1=6\) numbers.
Lockers \(1\) through \(200\) line a hallway. Every locker whose number is a multiple of \(5\) gets painted. How many lockers get painted?
Show solution
The painted lockers are the multiples of \(5\) that sit between \(1\) and \(200\). The first one is \(5\) and the last one is \(200\), since \(200\) is itself a multiple of \(5\). These numbers march up in even steps of \(5\), like \(5, 10, 15, \dots, 200\). To count them, first see how far we travel from the front locker to the back one. That distance is \(200 - 5 = 195\). Each step is \(5\), so the number of steps is \(195 \div 5 = 39\). But the steps count the gaps between lockers, not the lockers themselves. The very first painted locker takes no step to reach, so we add one more for it. That gives \(39 + 1 = 40\) painted lockers. $$\boxed{40}$$
On Maple Street the even-numbered houses \(24, 26, \ldots, 150\) sit on the sunny side. How many houses are on the sunny side?
Show solution
The sunny-side houses go \(24, 26, 28\), and so on up to \(150\). They are evenly spaced because each one is \(2\) more than the one before. So the first house in our list is \(24\) and the last is \(150\), with a step of \(2\). First we count the gaps between houses. From \(24\) up to \(150\) is \(150 - 24 = 126\), and each gap is \(2\), so there are \(126 \div 2 = 63\) gaps. But gaps are the spaces between houses, not the houses themselves. Picture \(63\) jumps of \(2\) starting at house \(24\). After \(63\) jumps you land on the last house, and you also have the house you started on. So we add \(1\) to count both ends, giving \(63 + 1 = 64\). $$\boxed{64}$$
A radio station plays every 4th rank from \(96\) down to \(4\). How many songs does it play?
Show solution
The station plays rank \(96\), then \(92\), then \(88\), dropping by \(4\) each time down to \(4\). Counting a falling list works exactly like counting a rising one, so first measure the gap from the smallest to the largest rank, which is \(96 - 4 = 92\). Each step drops the rank by \(4\), so the number of steps is \(92 \div 4 = 23\). Steps are the spaces between the songs, not the songs themselves, so there is one more song than there are steps. Add \(1\) to catch both ends.$$23 + 1 = 24$$So the station plays \(\boxed{24}\) songs.
Key idea
Width does not decide the count. Two ranges of the same width can hold different numbers of multiples. The windows \(10\) to \(20\) and \(11\) to \(21\) span the same distance, yet \(10, 15, 20\) gives three multiples of \(5\) while \(15, 20\) gives only two. What matters is which multiples actually land inside, so always pin down the real first multiple and the real last multiple in your range before you count.
You are 9th from the front and 15th from the back of a line. How many people are in the line altogether?
Show solution
The 9 people counted from the front include you, all the way back to you. The 15 people counted from the back also include you, all the way up to you. So when you add \(9+15=24\), you have counted yourself twice, once from each side. You are only one person, so take away that one extra count, giving \(9+15-1=23\). $$\boxed{23}$$
Key idea
Line counting. If you are \(f\)th from the front and \(b\)th from the back, the line holds \(f + b - 1\) people. Adding \(f\) and \(b\) counts you once from each side, so you are counted twice. Whenever two counts share one thing, that shared thing is counted twice, so subtract it once to fix the total. Being 6th from the front and 4th from the back means \(6 + 4 - 1 = 9\) people, not \(10\).
Key idea
Inclusion-exclusion. The number in A or B is \(|A| + |B| - |A \cap B|\). When you add the size of one group to the size of the other, anyone in both gets counted twice, once in each total, so you subtract the overlap once to fix the double count. Once you know how many are in A or B, the number in neither is just the total minus that. The same adding and subtracting works whether you count people or percents.
\(15\) kids play soccer, \(12\) play chess, and \(7\) play both. How many kids play at least one?
Show solution
If we just add the two groups, \(15 + 12 = 27\). But that counts the \(7\) kids who play both twice, once inside the soccer count and once inside the chess count. To fix the double count we subtract the overlap one time, so \(27 - 7 = 20\). That gives the number who play at least one game, soccer or chess. $$\boxed{20}$$
\(22\) kids signed up for archery, \(19\) for swimming, and \(33\) signed up for at least one. How many signed up for both?
Show solution
When you add the two lists you get \(22+19=41\). But that is more than the \(33\) kids who actually signed up. Why? Every kid who picked both archery and swimming sits on both lists, so adding the lists counts each of them twice. The total for archery or swimming is the two lists added, then the overlap subtracted once to fix the double count. So \(33 = 22 + 19 - \text{both}\). That means the both group is the amount we overcounted, which is \(41-33=8\). So \(8\) kids signed up for both archery and swimming. $$\boxed{8}$$
At a 50-person reunion, \(32\) drink coffee, \(19\) drink tea, and \(8\) drink both. How many drink neither?
Show solution
First figure out how many people drink coffee or tea. If you simply add the two groups you get \(32 + 19 = 51\), but that is already more than the whole reunion, which is a clue that something got counted twice. The \(8\) people who drink both were counted once among the coffee drinkers and again among the tea drinkers, so you must remove that overlap one time. That gives \(32 + 19 - 8 = 43\) people who drink at least one of the two. Everyone else drinks neither, so subtract from the total of \(50\), giving \(50 - 43 = 7\). $$\boxed{7}$$
Key idea
Two-way table. When every item is sorted two ways at once, a \(2\) by \(2\) grid holds all four groups in one picture. Each row adds across to a total on the side and each column adds down to a total along the bottom, and those edge totals must agree with the grand total in the corner. So if one inside cell is missing, you never have to guess: subtract the cells you know from the row or column total they share, and the missing count appears.
A garden has \(70\) plants. \(40\) are flowering, \(30\) are in the sunny corner, and \(22\) are both. How many plants are non-flowering and in the shade?
Show solution
Picture four boxes that sort every plant two ways, flowering or not down the side, sunny or shady across the top. We are told the flowering and sunny corner holds \(22\) plants, so write \(22\) there. There are \(40\) flowering plants in all. Of those, \(22\) are sunny, so the flowering and shady box holds \(40-22=18\). The sunny corner has \(30\) plants in all. Of those, \(22\) are flowering, so the non-flowering and sunny box holds \(30-22=8\). Now we can find the last box two ways. Every plant is somewhere, and the four boxes must add to \(70\). The three boxes we have add to \(22+18+8=48\), so the non-flowering and shady box holds \(70-48=22\). The same answer falls out of inclusion and exclusion. The plants that are flowering or sunny number \(40+30-22=48\), and we subtracted \(22\) once so we do not count the both group twice. The rest, the plants that are neither flowering nor sunny, number \(70-48=22\). $$\boxed{22}$$
Fun fact
The word tally comes from a notched stick. Medieval shepherds carved one notch per sheep, and some split the stick so each party kept half, the notches had to match up to prevent cheating.
How many two-digit numbers are divisible by \(3\) or by \(5\)?
Show solution
We want the two-digit numbers divisible by \(3\) or by \(5\), so we count each group and then fix the double counting. First the multiples of \(3\). The smallest two-digit multiple of \(3\) is \(12\) and the largest is \(99\). They step by \(3\), so the count is \((99-12)\div 3 + 1 = 87\div 3 + 1 = 29+1 = 30\). Next the multiples of \(5\). The smallest is \(10\) and the largest is \(95\). They step by \(5\), so the count is \((95-10)\div 5 + 1 = 85\div 5 + 1 = 17+1 = 18\). If we just add, we get \(30 + 18 = 48\). But a number divisible by both \(3\) and \(5\) is a multiple of \(15\), and we counted each of those twice, once in each list. The two-digit multiples of \(15\) are \(15, 30, 45, 60, 75, 90\), which is \(6\) numbers. So we subtract the overlap once and the answer is \(30 + 18 - 6 = 42\). $$\boxed{42}$$

Review problems

Review 1. Seats \(5\) through \(23\) are all filled, both ends included. How many people are sitting in the row?
Show solution
Picture a tiny row to see the idea. Seats \(1\) to \(3\) hold \(3\) people, yet \(3 - 1 = 2\), so plain subtraction comes up one short. The reason is that \(23 - 5\) counts the steps from the first seat to the last, not the seats themselves, and it skips one of the two ends. So we subtract to get the gap and then add \(1\) to put that missing end back. Here \(23 - 5 = 18\), and \(18 + 1 = 19\). So there are \(19\) people in the row. $$\boxed{19}$$
Review 2. A timeline poster marks one tick per year from \(1990\) to \(2024\), both ends included. How many ticks are on the poster?
Show solution
It is tempting to just do \(2024-1990=34\), but watch a small case first. From \(1990\) to \(1992\) the ticks are \(1990\), \(1991\), \(1992\), which is \(3\) ticks, yet \(1992-1990=2\). The subtraction came up one short. That happens because subtracting measures the gaps between the ticks, and a row of ticks always has one more tick than it has gaps. The first year never gets counted by the subtraction, so we add it back. Here \(2024-1990=34\) gives the gaps, and adding \(1\) for the starting year gives \(34+1=35\) ticks. $$\boxed{35}$$
Review 3. A scientist records the temperature at every whole degree from \(12\) down to \(-4\), both included. How many readings does she take?
Show solution
We are counting every whole degree from \(12\) down to \(-4\), with both ends included. First find the gap from the top reading to the bottom reading. That gap is \(12 - (-4)\), and subtracting a negative adds, so \(12 - (-4) = 12 + 4 = 16\). Now the gap is not the same as the number of readings. A short list shows why. From \(2\) down to \(0\) the readings are \(2, 1, 0\), which is \(3\) readings, one more than the gap of \(2\). The same fencepost idea happens here, so we take the gap and add \(1\), giving \(16 + 1 = 17\). So the scientist takes $$\boxed{17}$$ readings.
Review 4. A vending machine restocks every slot whose number is a multiple of \(6\), from slot \(1\) to slot \(100\). How many slots are restocked?
Show solution
The restocked slots are the multiples of \(6\) sitting between \(1\) and \(100\). The smallest multiple of \(6\) in that range is \(6\) itself, and the largest is \(96\), because \(96 = 6 \times 16\) while the next multiple \(102\) shoots past \(100\). So the list runs \(6, 12, 18, \dots, 96\), stepping up by \(6\) each time. To count an evenly spaced list, find how many steps separate the first and last terms, then add one for the starting term. The gap from \(6\) to \(96\) is \(96 - 6 = 90\), and each step covers \(6\), so there are \(90 \div 6 = 15\) steps. Add the \(1\) for the first slot so you do not leave it out, giving \(15 + 1 = 16\). $$\boxed{16}$$
Review 5. Odd-numbered jerseys \(15, 17, 19, \ldots, 67\) hang on a wall. How many jerseys are there?
Show solution
The hung numbers go up by \(2\) each time, so this is an evenly spaced list, not a solid run. The trick is to count the steps first and then remember the jerseys. The whole climb from the first number to the last covers \(67-15=52\). Each step is a jump of \(2\), so the number of steps is \(52\div 2=26\). But steps are the gaps between jerseys, and there is always one more jersey than gap, since you start standing on the first jersey before taking any step at all. So add that starting jersey back in, giving \(26+1=27\). You can sanity check it with the formula for an evenly spaced count, which is \(\frac{\text{last}-\text{first}}{\text{step}}+1\), and that gives \(\frac{67-15}{2}+1=26+1=27\). $$\boxed{27}$$
Review 6. Bins \(50, 100, 150, \ldots, 900\) are audited. How many bins are audited?
Show solution
The audited bins are the multiples of \(50\), evenly spaced \(50\) apart. The first one in range is \(50\) and the last is \(900\). To count evenly spaced numbers, first see how far you travel from the first to the last, which is \(900 - 50 = 850\). Each step is \(50\), so the number of steps is \(850 \div 50 = 17\). But the steps count the gaps between bins, not the bins themselves, and there is always one more bin than there are gaps. So we add \(1\) and get \(17 + 1 = 18\). $$\boxed{18}$$
Review 7. A class calls \(100, 96, 92, \ldots, 20\), dropping by \(4\) each time. How many numbers are called?
Show solution
The numbers are evenly spaced. They start at \(100\), end at \(20\), and drop by \(4\) at each step. First figure out how far the list travels. From \(100\) down to \(20\) is a gap of \(100-20=80\). Each step is \(4\), so the number of steps is \(80 \div 4 = 20\). But \(20\) steps means \(20\) gaps, and the gaps sit between the numbers. The very first number, \(100\), starts the list before any step is taken, so we add one more to count it. That gives \(20+1=21\) numbers. You can write this all at once as \(\frac{100-20}{4}+1 = \frac{80}{4}+1 = 20+1\), giving $$\boxed{21}$$
Review 8. The first multiple of \(7\) that is at least \(20\) is \(21\), and the last no more than \(95\) is \(91\). How many multiples of \(7\) lie in the range \(20\) to \(95\)?
Show solution
The numbers \(20\) and \(95\) are not multiples of \(7\), so they cannot be counted. The smallest multiple of \(7\) that fits is \(21\), and the largest is \(91\). So we really want to count the evenly spaced list \(21, 28, 35, \dots, 91\), where each number is \(7\) more than the one before. \nTo count an evenly spaced list, find how far it is from the first term to the last, then see how many steps of \(7\) that takes. The gap from \(21\) to \(91\) is \(91 - 21 = 70\), and \(70 \div 7 = 10\), so there are \(10\) jumps. \nBut \(10\) jumps land on \(10\) new numbers after the starting one, so we must add \(1\) for \(21\) itself. That gives \(10 + 1 = 11\) numbers in all. $$\boxed{11}$$
Review 9. A marching band is \(15\)th from the start of the parade and \(12\)th from the end. How many groups are in the parade?
Show solution
The band is the \(15\)th group from the start, so there are \(15\) groups from the front up to and including the band. The same band is the \(12\)th group from the end, so there are \(12\) groups from the back up to and including the band. If you add those two numbers you get \(15 + 12 = 27\), but the band itself sits in both of those counts, so it was counted twice. To fix the double count you subtract that one overlap, giving \(15 + 12 - 1 = 26\) groups. $$\boxed{26}$$
Review 10. You are \(7\)th from the front and \(7\)th from the back of a line. How many people are in the line?
Show solution
Think of the line as two overlapping groups. Counting from the front, you are \(7\)th, so the front group is \(7\) people. Counting from the back, you are \(7\)th, so the back group is also \(7\) people. Adding them gives \(7+7=14\). But you are in BOTH groups, so you got counted twice. Subtract the one extra count of yourself, giving \(14-1=13\). You can check it another way. With \(6\) people ahead of you and \(6\) people behind you, the line is \(6+1+6=13\). $$\boxed{13}$$
Review 11. \(22\) students ate pizza, \(18\) ate burgers, and \(11\) ate both. How many ate at least one?
Show solution
Start by adding the two groups. The pizza eaters and the burger eaters give \(22+18=40\). But the \(11\) students who ate both are inside the pizza count and inside the burger count, so they were counted twice. We need to take away one of those copies. That gives \(40-11=29\). So the number who eat at least one food is $$\boxed{29}$$
Review 12. \(28\) customers bought a taco, \(21\) bought a burrito, and \(35\) bought at least one. How many bought both?
Show solution
When we add the \(28\) taco buyers and the \(21\) burrito buyers we get \(28 + 21 = 49\). But anyone who bought both shows up in both of those groups, so they got counted twice in that \(49\). The true number of customers who bought a taco or a burrito is \(35\), and that counts each person just once. So the sum is bigger than \(35\) by exactly the size of the overlap. That overlap is \(49 - 35 = 14\). So the number who bought both is $$\boxed{14}$$
Review 13. A pet show has \(30\) cats and \(20\) dogs. Of all entries, \(28\) are short-haired and \(16\) cats are short-haired. How many dogs are short-haired?
Show solution
Think about the whole short-haired group. Every short-haired entry is either a cat or a dog, so the short-haired cats and the short-haired dogs together make up all \(28\) short-haired entries. We already know \(16\) of those are cats. So the rest must be dogs, giving \(28 - 16 = 12\). The \(50\) total, the \(30\) cats, and the \(20\) dogs were extra facts we did not need for this question. The number of short-haired dogs is $$\boxed{12}$$
Review 14. How many two-digit numbers are divisible by \(4\) or by \(9\)?
Show solution
We count three things and combine them carefully. First the multiples of \(4\). The smallest two-digit one is \(12\) and the largest is \(96\), stepping by \(4\). The count is \((96-12)\div 4+1=84\div 4+1=21+1=22\). Next the multiples of \(9\). The smallest two-digit one is \(18\) and the largest is \(99\), stepping by \(9\). The count is \((99-18)\div 9+1=81\div 9+1=9+1=10\). Now the overlap. A number divisible by both \(4\) and \(9\) is divisible by \(36\). The two-digit multiples of \(36\) are \(36\) and \(72\), so there are \(2\) of them. These got counted once in the multiples of \(4\) and again in the multiples of \(9\). Using the overlap idea, the count of numbers divisible by \(4\) or \(9\) is \(22+10-2=30\). $$\boxed{30}$$
