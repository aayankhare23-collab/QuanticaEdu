Prealgebra · Lesson 8.4

Arithmetic with Roots

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

In 8.3 you simplified a single root. This lesson combines roots: multiply, divide, add, and subtract them, all staying exact, no rounding. Adding uses the like-terms rule from 6.1, with the root playing the role of the variable.

Multiply \(\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{12}\). Use the product rule to combine under one radical, then evaluate. What whole number is the result?
Show solution
Use the product rule to slide both numbers under one radical. $$\sqrt{3}\times\sqrt{12}=\sqrt{3\times12}=\sqrt{36}.$$ Now \(36\) is a perfect square because \(6\times6=36\), so \(\sqrt{36}=6\). The radical vanishes and you are left with a clean whole number. $$\sqrt{3}\times\sqrt{12}=\boxed{6}.$$
Key idea
Multiplying roots. The product rule from 8.3 split one root into two; read left to right it joins two roots into one, \(\sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b} = \sqrt{ab}\). So to multiply roots, tuck both numbers under a single radical, then simplify whatever lands there the 8.3 way. Every so often the product \(ab\) is a perfect square, the radical vanishes, and a plain whole number is left, the way \(\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{27} = \sqrt{81} = 9\). That clean exact landing is the whole reason to keep the root instead of cashing it in for a messy decimal.
Multiply \(\sqrt{6} \times \sqrt{10}\). Combine into \(\sqrt{60}\), then simplify. What whole number appears in front of the remaining root?
Show solution
Start by joining the two roots with the product rule, so the numbers underneath multiply together. That gives \(\sqrt{6}\times\sqrt{10}=\sqrt{6\times10}=\sqrt{60}\). Since \(60\) is not a perfect square, switch into the simplifying move from 8.3 and look for the largest perfect square that divides it. Checking \(4, 9, 16, 25\), and \(36\), the winner is \(4\), because \(60=4\times15\) and \(15\) has no perfect-square factor left. Now split the radical, $$\sqrt{60}=\sqrt{4}\times\sqrt{15}=2\sqrt{15}.$$ In simplest form the result is \(2\sqrt{15}\), so the whole number sitting in front is \(\boxed{2}\).
Fun fact
A-series paper (A4, A3, etc.) has sides in ratio \(\sqrt{2}\:1\). Fold any sheet in half and the result has the same ratio, so A3 folded is A4. The reason: \(1 \div \tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = \sqrt{2}\).
Multiply \(4\sqrt{3} \times 2\sqrt{6}\). The coefficients multiply and the radicands multiply. After simplifying the result, what whole number is in front of \(\sqrt{2}\)?
Show solution
Keep the coefficients and the radicands in their own lanes. The whole numbers out front multiply, \(4\times 2=8\), and the numbers under the radicals multiply by the product rule, \(\sqrt{3}\times\sqrt{6}=\sqrt{18}\). So far we have \(4\sqrt{3}\times 2\sqrt{6}=8\sqrt{18}\). This is correct but not yet simplest, because \(\sqrt{18}\) still hides a perfect square. Pull it out the 8.3 way, since \(18=9\times 2\), so \(\sqrt{18}=\sqrt{9}\times\sqrt{2}=3\sqrt{2}\). That frees a \(3\), which folds into the \(8\) already out front, \(8\times 3=24\). The simplest form is \(24\sqrt{2}\), and the whole number in front is \(\boxed{24}\).
Key idea
Multiplying with coefficients. A root with a number out front, like \(4\sqrt{3}\), is just that number times the root, so to multiply \(a\sqrt{m} \times b\sqrt{n}\) you run two channels at once: the coefficients multiply together and the radicands multiply together, giving \((ab)\sqrt{mn}\). Only then do you simplify, pulling any perfect square out from under the root and folding it into the coefficient, which is how \(4\sqrt{3} \times 2\sqrt{6}\) tidies to \(24\sqrt{2}\). A bare root carries a hidden coefficient of \(1\), just as a lonely \(x\) meant \(1x\) in 6.1.
Evaluate \(\left(5\sqrt{2}\right)^2\). The coefficient squares to \(25\) and \(\sqrt{2} \times \sqrt{2} = 2\). What whole number is the result?
Show solution
To square \(5\sqrt{2}\), write it as a product of itself. $$\left(5\sqrt{2}\right)^2=5\sqrt{2}\times 5\sqrt{2}$$ Now collect the coefficients on one side and the roots on the other. The coefficients give \(5\times 5=25\), and the roots give \(\sqrt{2}\times\sqrt{2}=\sqrt{2\times 2}=\sqrt{4}=2\). So the square sends the radical away and leaves $$25\times 2=50.$$ This is the heart of why a square root and a square cancel. Squaring \(k\sqrt{n}\) always gives \(k^2\times n\), a whole number whenever \(k\) and \(n\) are. \(\boxed{50}\)
Key idea
Squaring a root expression. Squaring undoes a square root, so \(\sqrt{a}\) times itself hands back the plain number \(a\). When you square \(k\sqrt{a}\), the coefficient gets squared and the number under the root steps out bare, giving \((k\sqrt{a})^2 = k^2 \times a\) with no radical left. So \((5\sqrt{2})^2 = 25 \times 2 = 50\), a whole number every time the radicand starts as a whole number.
Simplify \(6\sqrt{7} + 2\sqrt{7}\). Both terms carry \(\sqrt{7}\), so treat \(\sqrt{7}\) like a variable and add the coefficients. What whole number is in front of \(\sqrt{7}\)?
Show solution
Both terms are built on the same radical \(\sqrt{7}\), so they are like radicals, just like \(6x\) and \(2x\) are like terms. That means you keep the shared \(\sqrt{7}\) and add only the coefficients in front. $$6\sqrt{7} + 2\sqrt{7} = (6 + 2)\sqrt{7} = 8\sqrt{7}$$ The combined root is \(8\sqrt{7}\), so the whole number in front is \(\boxed{8}\).
Key idea
Like radicals. This links back to collecting like terms in 6.1. When two radicals carry the exact same number under the root, the root plays the part of the variable, so they are like terms and you add or subtract only the coefficients out front while the root stays untouched, which is why \(6\sqrt{7} + 2\sqrt{7} = 8\sqrt{7}\) the way \(6x + 2x = 8x\). A lonely \(\sqrt{7}\) is secretly \(1\sqrt{7}\). If the numbers under the roots differ, the radicals are different kinds of thing and nothing fuses them, exactly as an \(x\) and a \(y\) refuse to merge.
Tempting but wrong: \(\sqrt{36} + \sqrt{64} \overset{?}{=} \sqrt{100}\). Test it directly instead. What is the actual value of \(\sqrt{36} + \sqrt{64}\)?
Show solution
Handle each radical by itself, because that is the only thing the root sign actually applies to. Since \(6\times 6=36\), we get \(\sqrt{36}=6\), and since \(8\times 8=64\), we get \(\sqrt{64}=8\). So the sum is the honest addition $$\sqrt{36}+\sqrt{64}=6+8=14.$$ Now look at the trap. The shortcut \(\sqrt{36+64}=\sqrt{100}=10\) gives a different number, \(10\), not \(14\). They are not equal, which proves the rule. A square root does not distribute over a plus sign, so \(\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{b}\) is NOT \(\sqrt{a+b}\). You may only combine roots when they are like radicals sitting under the same number, and even then you add their coefficients, never the numbers under the root. The true value is \(\boxed{14}\).
Key idea
No addition inside a root. There is no rule that lets a root slide across a plus or minus sign: \(\sqrt{a} + \sqrt{b} \ne \sqrt{a+b}\). The counter-example settles it, since \(\sqrt{36} + \sqrt{64} = 6 + 8 = 14\) while \(\sqrt{36+64} = \sqrt{100} = 10\), and \(14\) is simply not \(10\). A root only splits across multiplication, never across addition or subtraction, so a \(+\) or \(-\) between two roots is a wall the numbers underneath can never cross. That is exactly why adding works only on like radicals, by their coefficients.
Simplify \(3\sqrt{8} - \sqrt{72}\). First simplify each radical, then combine. What whole number does the expression equal?
Show solution
Simplify each radical first so the disguise comes off. For the first term, \(\sqrt{8}=\sqrt{4}\times\sqrt{2}=2\sqrt{2}\), and the \(3\) out front multiplies in, so $$3\sqrt{8}=3\times 2\sqrt{2}=6\sqrt{2}.$$ For the second term, the largest perfect square inside \(72\) is \(36\), since \(72=36\times 2\), so $$\sqrt{72}=\sqrt{36}\times\sqrt{2}=6\sqrt{2}.$$ Both terms are now like radicals, each a multiple of \(\sqrt{2}\). Subtracting is just collecting like terms, with \(\sqrt{2}\) acting like a variable, so combine the coefficients \(6-6\), which gives $$3\sqrt{8}-\sqrt{72}=6\sqrt{2}-6\sqrt{2}=(6-6)\sqrt{2}=0\sqrt{2}.$$ Zero of anything is zero, so the whole expression collapses all the way down. The two radicals were secretly the same number the entire time. \(3\sqrt{8}-\sqrt{72}=\boxed{0}\).
Key idea
Simplify before combining. Never decide whether two roots combine by how they look at the start, because \(3\sqrt{8}\) and \(\sqrt{72}\) look like strangers, yet simplifying each one turns both into \(6\sqrt{2}\). So before adding or subtracting, pull the largest perfect square out of every term, then check for like radicals to collect, the way you collected like terms in 6.1 with the root as the variable. The reward can be big: if two simplified terms match and one is subtracted from the other, they cancel to a clean \(0\), as in \(3\sqrt{8} - \sqrt{72} = 6\sqrt{2} - 6\sqrt{2} = 0\).
Simplify \(\dfrac{\sqrt{252}}{\sqrt{7}}\). Use the quotient rule to write it as \(\sqrt{252 \div 7}\), then evaluate. What whole number is the result?
Show solution
The quotient rule lets you join two roots into one. \(\dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}\), so the division slides underneath a single radical. $$\frac{\sqrt{252}}{\sqrt{7}}=\sqrt{\frac{252}{7}}$$ Now divide the numbers under the root. \(252\div 7=36\), which leaves $$\sqrt{36}.$$ And \(36\) is a perfect square, since \(6\times 6=36\), so the whole quotient collapses to a single whole number. $$\frac{\sqrt{252}}{\sqrt{7}}=\sqrt{36}=\boxed{6}$$ Neither \(\sqrt{252}\) nor \(\sqrt{7}\) is a whole number on its own, yet their quotient is. That is the quotient rule doing the same favor for division that the product rule does for multiplication, finding the clean number hiding inside.
Key idea
Quotient rule. Division has its own rule, a twin of the multiplying one: \(\dfrac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}\). A quotient of two roots collapses into a single root of the quotient, and when that quotient is a perfect square the radical just vanishes, the way \(\dfrac{\sqrt{252}}{\sqrt{7}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{252}{7}} = \sqrt{36} = 6\). The rule runs the other way too, letting you take the root of a tidy fraction or terminating decimal exactly, since the root of a quotient is the root of the top over the root of the bottom.
Simplify \(\sqrt{\dfrac{25}{49}}\). Split the root across the fraction and evaluate each piece. Write your answer as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
Use the quotient rule to send the root across the fraction. $$\sqrt{\frac{25}{49}}=\frac{\sqrt{25}}{\sqrt{49}}$$ Now take each root by itself. The top is \(\sqrt{25}=5\) because \(5\times 5=25\), and the bottom is \(\sqrt{49}=7\) because \(7\times 7=49\). That leaves \(\dfrac{5}{7}\). Since \(5\) and \(7\) share no common factor, the fraction is already in lowest terms. You can check it the other way too, since \(\left(\dfrac{5}{7}\right)^2=\dfrac{25}{49}\), so \(\dfrac{5}{7}\) really is the root. $$\sqrt{\frac{25}{49}}=\boxed{5/7}$$
Find \(\sqrt{0.64}\) exactly. Rewrite as \(\sqrt{\dfrac{64}{100}}\), apply the quotient rule, and write the result as a decimal.
Show solution
Read the decimal as a fraction over one hundred, since two digits after the point means hundredths. That gives $$\sqrt{0.64}=\sqrt{\frac{64}{100}}.$$ Now use the quotient rule to split the root across the top and the bottom, $$\sqrt{\frac{64}{100}}=\frac{\sqrt{64}}{\sqrt{100}}.$$ Both numbers are perfect squares, with \(\sqrt{64}=8\) and \(\sqrt{100}=10\), so the value is \(\frac{8}{10}\). Writing that back as a decimal gives a clean, terminating result. So \(\sqrt{0.64} = \boxed{0.8}\). You can check it by squaring, since \(0.8\times 0.8=0.64\).
Simplify all four terms: \(\sqrt{12} + \sqrt{50} + \sqrt{63} + \sqrt{8}\). After combining like radicals, how many distinct radical terms remain?
Show solution
Simplify each radical by pulling out its largest perfect-square factor. Since \(12=4\times3\), we get \(\sqrt{12}=2\sqrt{3}\). Since \(50=25\times2\), we get \(\sqrt{50}=5\sqrt{2}\). Since \(63=9\times7\), we get \(\sqrt{63}=3\sqrt{7}\). And since \(8=4\times2\), we get \(\sqrt{8}=2\sqrt{2}\). So the sum becomes $$2\sqrt{3}+5\sqrt{2}+3\sqrt{7}+2\sqrt{2}.$$ Now collect like radicals. The two terms with \(\sqrt{2}\) combine, since \(5\sqrt{2}+2\sqrt{2}=7\sqrt{2}\), while \(2\sqrt{3}\) and \(3\sqrt{7}\) have no partner and stand alone. The cleanest form is $$7\sqrt{2}+2\sqrt{3}+3\sqrt{7}.$$ Three different square-free numbers, namely \(2\), \(3\), and \(7\), remain under the roots, so there are three distinct radical terms left. The count is \(\boxed{3}\).
Evaluate each piece, then combine. $$\left(2\sqrt{5}\right)^2+\sqrt{72}\times\sqrt{2}-\frac{\sqrt{180}}{\sqrt{5}}$$ What single whole number does this equal?
Show solution
Work through the three pieces one at a time. Squaring. Squaring \(2\sqrt{5}\) squares the coefficient and the root separately, so \(\left(2\sqrt{5}\right)^2=2^2\times\left(\sqrt{5}\right)^2=4\times 5=20\). Multiplying. By the product rule \(\sqrt{72}\times\sqrt{2}=\sqrt{72\times 2}=\sqrt{144}=12\). Dividing. By the quotient rule \(\dfrac{\sqrt{180}}{\sqrt{5}}=\sqrt{\dfrac{180}{5}}=\sqrt{36}=6\). Now the whole expression is just three plain numbers, \(20+12-6\). Adding and subtracting gives \(32-6=26\). A string of square roots collapses to one clean whole number. $$\left(2\sqrt{5}\right)^2+\sqrt{72}\times\sqrt{2}-\frac{\sqrt{180}}{\sqrt{5}}=20+12-6=\boxed{26}$$

Review problems

Review 1. Simplify \(\sqrt{5} \times \sqrt{45}\). Combine under one radical and evaluate. What whole number is the result?
Show solution
Use the product rule to merge the two roots into one. Since \(\sqrt{a}\times\sqrt{b}=\sqrt{a\times b}\), you get $$\sqrt{5}\times\sqrt{45}=\sqrt{5\times 45}=\sqrt{225}.$$ Now check whether \(225\) is a perfect square. It is, because \(15\times 15=225\), so \(\sqrt{225}=15\). The two roots looked irrational on their own, yet their product is a clean whole number. \(\boxed{15}\)
Review 2. Find the value of \(\sqrt{2} \times \sqrt{32}\). What whole number does it equal?
Show solution
Use the product rule to pull both numbers under a single root. That gives \(\sqrt{2}\times\sqrt{32}=\sqrt{2\times32}=\sqrt{64}\). Now \(64\) is a perfect square because \(8\times8=64\), so \(\sqrt{64}=8\). The two roots looked irrational on their own, but their product lands right on a whole number, \(\boxed{8}\).
Review 3. Multiply \(\sqrt{6} \times \sqrt{14}\) and simplify fully. The answer has the form (whole number)\(\sqrt{21}\). What whole number is in front?
Show solution
Multiply the two roots into one. The product rule says the numbers under the radicals just multiply, so $$\sqrt{6}\times\sqrt{14}=\sqrt{6\times 14}=\sqrt{84}.$$ Now simplify \(\sqrt{84}\) the way you did in 8.3, by finding the largest perfect-square factor. Here \(84=4\times 21\), and \(4\) is a perfect square while \(21\) has no perfect-square factor left. So $$\sqrt{84}=\sqrt{4\times 21}=\sqrt{4}\times\sqrt{21}=2\sqrt{21}.$$ The result is \(2\sqrt{21}\), and the whole number out in front is \(\boxed{2}\).
Review 4. Evaluate \(3\sqrt{2} \times 5\sqrt{2}\). The roots multiply to a perfect square, so the result is a whole number. What is it?
Show solution
Split the product into the whole numbers and the roots. The coefficients multiply to \(3\times 5=15\), and the roots multiply to \(\sqrt{2}\times\sqrt{2}=\sqrt{4}=2\). So the expression becomes \(15\times 2\). That gives \(15\times 2=30\), which is a plain whole number because the matching roots squared away to a perfect square. The result is \(\boxed{30}\).
Review 5. Multiply \(2\sqrt{3} \times 5\sqrt{6}\) and simplify. The answer has the form (whole number)\(\sqrt{2}\). What whole number is in front?
Show solution
Handle the whole numbers and the roots on their own. The coefficients multiply to give \(2\times 5=10\), and the radicands multiply to give \(\sqrt{3}\times\sqrt{6}=\sqrt{18}\). So far the product is \(10\sqrt{18}\). Now \(\sqrt{18}\) is not in simplest form, since \(18=9\times 2\) and \(9\) is a perfect square, so \(\sqrt{18}=\sqrt{9}\times\sqrt{2}=3\sqrt{2}\). Putting it back together gives \(10\times 3\sqrt{2}=30\sqrt{2}\). The whole number sitting in front of \(\sqrt{2}\) is \(\boxed{30}\).
Review 6. Evaluate \(\left(4\sqrt{5}\right)^2\). The coefficient squares and the root cancels. What whole number is the result?
Show solution
To square \(4\sqrt{5}\), write it as a product with itself, \(\left(4\sqrt{5}\right)^2 = 4\sqrt{5}\times 4\sqrt{5}\). Regroup so the whole-number coefficients multiply together and the roots multiply together, giving \(\left(4\times 4\right)\times\left(\sqrt{5}\times\sqrt{5}\right)\). The coefficients give \(16\), and a square root times itself returns the number underneath, so \(\sqrt{5}\times\sqrt{5}=5\). That leaves \(16\times 5 = 80\). So \(\left(4\sqrt{5}\right)^2 = \boxed{80}\).
Review 7. Evaluate \(\left(6\sqrt{3}\right)^2\). The coefficient squares and the root cancels. What whole number is the result?
Show solution
Squaring an expression means multiplying it by itself, so $$\left(6\sqrt{3}\right)^2=6\sqrt{3}\times 6\sqrt{3}.$$ Multiply the coefficients together and the roots together, \(\left(6\times 6\right)\times\left(\sqrt{3}\times\sqrt{3}\right)=36\times\left(\sqrt{3}\times\sqrt{3}\right)\). A square root times itself gives back the number underneath, so \(\sqrt{3}\times\sqrt{3}=3\), and the irrational part disappears completely. That leaves \(36\times 3=108\), a clean whole number. So \(\left(6\sqrt{3}\right)^2=\boxed{108}\).
Review 8. Collect like radicals: \(10\sqrt{3} - 4\sqrt{3} + \sqrt{3}\). What whole number \(k\) gives the combined term \(k\sqrt{3}\)?
Show solution
Each term has the same root, \(\sqrt{3}\), so these are like radicals and they collect together exactly like \(10x-4x+x\). First make the lonely root show its coefficient, so \(\sqrt{3}=1\sqrt{3}\). Now combine the numbers in front. \(10-4+1=7\). The root stays put, giving \(7\sqrt{3}\), and the whole number in front is \(\boxed{7}\).
Review 9. Evaluate \(\sqrt{144} + \sqrt{25}\) by taking each root separately, then adding. What is the result?
Show solution
Work each radical on its own. Since \(12\times 12=144\), we get \(\sqrt{144}=12\). Since \(5\times 5=25\), we get \(\sqrt{25}=5\). Now add the two whole numbers, \(12+5=17\). Watch the trap. The root of a sum is not the sum of the roots, so \(\sqrt{144}+\sqrt{25}\) is not \(\sqrt{144+25}=\sqrt{169}=13\). The roots stay separate across the plus sign, and \(13\neq 17\). The correct value is \(\boxed{17}\).
Review 10. Simplify \(\sqrt{27} + \sqrt{48}\). Each hides a factor of \(\sqrt{3}\). After simplifying, combine into one term \(k\sqrt{3}\). What is \(k\)?
Show solution
Simplify each radical first. Since \(27=9\times 3\), we get \(\sqrt{27}=\sqrt{9}\times\sqrt{3}=3\sqrt{3}\). Since \(48=16\times 3\), we get \(\sqrt{48}=\sqrt{16}\times\sqrt{3}=4\sqrt{3}\). Now both terms carry the same \(\sqrt{3}\), so they are like radicals. Add the coefficients just like collecting like terms, \(3\sqrt{3}+4\sqrt{3}=(3+4)\sqrt{3}=7\sqrt{3}\). The whole number in front of \(\sqrt{3}\) is \(\boxed{7}\).
Review 11. Simplify \(5\sqrt{2} - \sqrt{50}\). First simplify \(\sqrt{50}\), then subtract. What whole number does the expression equal?
Show solution
Simplify the second radical before doing anything else. The number \(50\) factors as \(25\times 2\), and \(25\) is a perfect square, so $$\sqrt{50}=\sqrt{25\times 2}=\sqrt{25}\times\sqrt{2}=5\sqrt{2}.$$ Rewrite the expression with that simplified form. Now the subtraction reads $$5\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{50}=5\sqrt{2}-5\sqrt{2}.$$ Both terms are like radicals, the same \(\sqrt{2}\) with the same coefficient, so think of \(\sqrt{2}\) as a single object and collect. You have \(5\) of it minus \(5\) of it, which leaves \(0\) of it. The radical disappears completely, so \(5\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{50}=\boxed{0}\).
Review 12. Simplify \(\dfrac{\sqrt{300}}{\sqrt{3}}\) using the quotient rule. What whole number is the result?
Show solution
Pull the two roots together with the quotient rule, since a root divided by a root is the root of the quotient. $$\frac{\sqrt{300}}{\sqrt{3}}=\sqrt{\frac{300}{3}}$$ Now divide inside the radical. \(300\div 3=100\), so the expression becomes \(\sqrt{100}\). That is a perfect square, and \(10\times 10=100\), so \(\sqrt{100}=10\). The two roots that looked messy collapse into a clean whole number. \(\boxed{10}\)
Review 13. Evaluate \(\sqrt{\tfrac{9}{64}}\) using the quotient rule. Write your answer as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
By the quotient rule the root of a fraction is the root of the top over the root of the bottom, so $$\sqrt{\frac{9}{64}}=\frac{\sqrt{9}}{\sqrt{64}}.$$ Both pieces are perfect squares. Since \(3\times 3=9\) we get \(\sqrt{9}=3\), and since \(8\times 8=64\) we get \(\sqrt{64}=8\). That leaves \(\dfrac{3}{8}\), which is already in lowest terms because \(3\) and \(8\) share no common factor. You can check it by squaring, since \(\left(\tfrac{3}{8}\right)^2=\tfrac{9}{64}\). So \(\sqrt{\tfrac{9}{64}}=\boxed{3/8}\).
Review 14. Find \(\sqrt{0.81}\) exactly. Rewrite as \(\sqrt{\tfrac{81}{100}}\) and apply the quotient rule. What is the decimal value?
Show solution
Start by turning the decimal into a fraction. The 81 lands in the hundredths place, so \(0.81=\dfrac{81}{100}\). Now apply the quotient rule, which lets the root split across the fraction, giving \(\sqrt{0.81}=\sqrt{\dfrac{81}{100}}=\dfrac{\sqrt{81}}{\sqrt{100}}\). Both pieces are perfect squares, since \(\sqrt{81}=9\) and \(\sqrt{100}=10\), so the value is \(\dfrac{9}{10}\). Written back as a decimal that is \(0.9\). You can check it by squaring, since \(0.9\times0.9=0.81\). So \(\boxed{0.9}\).
