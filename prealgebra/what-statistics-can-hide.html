Prealgebra · Lesson 10.8

What Statistics Can Hide

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

You can now find the mean, the median, and the mode of any list of numbers. This lesson turns that skill around and asks a harder question, what those single numbers do not tell you. One statistic is a summary, and a summary always leaves something out. Sometimes what it hides is small. Sometimes it is the whole story.

Two basketball teams both average 20 points a game. The Hawks scored 19, 20, and 21 points in their three games. The Falcons scored 5, 20, and 35. Find the range of the Falcons' scores, meaning their highest score minus their lowest score. Give the number.
Show solution
The mean is a center, and it says nothing about spread. Both teams average 20 points, yet the Hawks stayed near 19, 20, 21 while the Falcons swung from a cold 5 to a hot 35. To measure that swing, take the range, the highest score minus the lowest, giving $$35 - 5 = \boxed{30}.$$ The Falcons' range is 30 while the Hawks' is only 2, so two teams with the exact same average can be worlds apart in consistency. The mean hid the whole story of how wildly the Falcons' games varied.
Key idea
The center hides the spread. Two lists can share the exact same mean and still be worlds apart, one bunched tightly together and one flung wide. Picture the quiz scores \(79, 80, 81\) next to \(30, 80, 130\). Both average to $$\frac{79+80+81}{3}=\frac{30+80+130}{3}=80,$$ yet the first group barely moved off center while the second swung from near zero to well past a hundred. The mean marks a center, but it says nothing about how far the values scatter around that center. Spread, how consistent or how varied the data is, is a separate fact the average quietly leaves out.
Anna took seven quizzes and scored 94, 93, 90, 93, 92, and 91. She missed the last quiz and scored a 0. Because of that single 0, her mean sits at about 79, which makes her look like a middling student. Find the median of her seven scores, the summary number that shrugs off the lone 0. Give the number.
Show solution
The mean is fragile because it lets one extreme value, an outlier, pull the whole average toward it. The 0 does exactly that here, dropping the mean to about 79. The median is sturdier because it only cares about position, not size. \nOrder the seven scores from lowest to highest, giving $$0,\ 90,\ 91,\ 92,\ 93,\ 93,\ 94.$$\nWith seven values the median is the 4th one, the value with three scores on each side, so $$\boxed{92}.$$ \nThe median is 92, while the mean is only about 79. Notice how differently they describe Anna. The mean says "average student" because the 0 got baked into the total, but the median says "strong student" because it steps right past that single missed quiz. The answer is 92, and the lesson is that a mean can hide a story that one outlier is quietly rewriting, while the median tells you what a typical quiz really looked like.
Key idea
Outliers wreck the mean, not the median. The mean adds up every value, so one number far from the rest drags the whole average toward itself. Slip a single wild value into a data set and the mean lurches after it, while the median barely notices, because the median only cares about which value lands in the middle. Take the ages of four kids and their teacher, \(10\), \(11\), \(12\), \(13\), and \(45\). The median age is $$\text{median} = 12,$$ a fair picture of the group. The mean tells a different story, giving $$\text{mean} = \frac{10+11+12+13+45}{5} = \frac{91}{5} = 18.2,$$ an age older than every student in the room. So when one value is dramatically different from the others, trust the median. It reports what is typical, while the mean reports what one outlier dragged it toward.
A basketball player's five games scored 40, 45, 50, 55, and 110 points. Find the mean and the median of these five scores, then give the mean minus the median. Give the number.
Show solution
The gap between the mean and the median is a skew detector. The median is the middle of the sorted scores 40, 45, 50, 55, 110, which is 50. The mean adds all five and divides by five, giving $$\frac{40+45+50+55+110}{5}=\frac{300}{5}=60.$$ So the mean minus the median is $$60-50=\boxed{10}.$$ The single huge game of 110 barely moves the median, since the middle spot stays at 50, but it pulls the mean all the way up to 60. A positive gap like this is a warning sign that a few unusually large values are stretching the average. The mean alone would make every game look like a 60-point night, hiding the fact that four of the five were far below that.
Key idea
Compare the mean and median. Line the two numbers up next to each other and the gap between them tells a story. When the mean sits above the median, a few unusually large values are stretching a long tail out to the right. Incomes work this way, where a handful of enormous salaries drag the mean up while most people earn far less. When the mean sits below the median, a few unusually small values pull a tail out to the left. When the two are close, nothing is pulling hard in either direction and the data is roughly balanced. So the size and direction of the gap is a quick detector for lopsided data and hidden outliers, $$\text{mean} - \text{median} > 0 \Rightarrow \text{tail to the right},$$ and a gap near zero means the summary number is safe to trust.
A basketball team of 11 players has a median height of 76 inches. Someone claims the tallest player must therefore be well above 76 inches. What is the shortest the tallest player could possibly be? Give the number in inches.
Show solution
The median pins down the middle value and says nothing definite about how far the top stretches. Line the 11 heights up from shortest to tallest. The median is the 6th height, which is 76 inches, so the five players above it are each at least 76 inches. That makes the tallest at least 76 inches as well. But nothing forces those top players to be strictly taller, because ties are fine. Picture the whole upper half sitting at exactly 76. $$76,\ 76,\ 76,\ 76,\ 76,\ \underbrace{76}_{\text{median}},\ 76,\ 76,\ 76,\ 76,\ 76$$ The median is still 76, and now the tallest player is 76 too, so the shortest the tallest player could possibly be is $$\boxed{76}\text{ inches.}$$ The lesson is that the median hides the tails. It reports the middle and leaves the extremes free, so a median of 76 is fully consistent with a tallest player of exactly 76 or of 90. A single middle number tells you nothing certain about how tall the tallest player really is.
Key idea
The median is silent about the extremes. The median pins down the middle value and nothing more. It cannot tell you how high the highest reaches or how low the lowest sinks. Say a basketball team has a median height of 76 inches, so the middle player stands at 76 inches. That is all you know for certain. The tallest player could also be exactly 76 inches, with everyone bunched near the middle, or the tallest could tower at 90 inches. Both worlds have the same median, because everything above the middle is free to be anything at or beyond it. The median guards the center and lets the edges roam.
Five houses on a block are worth 10, 14, 20, 26, and 30 in ten-thousands of dollars, so their median value is 20. A developer builds a sixth house on the block, a mansion worth 5000 in the same units (fifty million dollars). Find the new median of all six houses, in ten-thousands of dollars. Give the number.
Show solution
This shows how the median resists an extreme value. Sort the six house values: 10, 14, 20, 26, 30, 5000. With an even count, the median is the average of the two middle numbers, which are 20 and 26, giving $$\frac{20 + 26}{2} = \boxed{23}.$$ The mansion is worth fifty million dollars, yet the median only nudged from 20 to 23. The mean, by contrast, would rocket past 800, dragged up by that one huge number. So the median barely felt the mansion, while a single summary number like the mean would hide the fact that five ordinary houses share the block with one giant. The answer is 23.
A class of 20 students took a test scored out of 100, and their mean was exactly 75. Nothing else about the individual scores is known. Consider these four statements. (1) At least one student scored 75 or higher. (2) The twenty scores add up to 1500. (3) At least one student scored below 75. (4) No one scored a perfect 100. How many of the four statements must be true? Give the number.
Show solution
The trap is thinking the mean tells you more than it does. A mean fixes the total and nothing else, so most claims about individual scores are not forced. Start with the one fact the mean guarantees. Total equals mean times count, giving $$75 \times 20 = 1500.$$ So statement (2) is locked in. It must be true. Now statement (1). If every student scored below 75, the mean would land below 75 too, which contradicts a mean of exactly 75. So someone has to be at 75 or above. It must be true. Statement (3) sounds symmetric, but it is not forced. Imagine all twenty students scored exactly 75. The total is still 1500 and the mean is still 75, yet nobody scored below 75. So (3) can fail. Statement (4) is also not forced. One student could score a perfect 100, with the rest arranged to keep the total at 1500. So a perfect score is allowed, and (4) can fail. Two statements are chained to the mean and two are free, so $$\boxed{2}$$ of the four must be true. The lesson is that a mean hands you the total and one guaranteed value near it, then goes silent. Everything about the spread, the extremes, and the individual scores stays hidden.
Key idea
A summary throws information away. A single statistic squeezes a whole list into one number, and that squeeze runs one way. You cannot rebuild the data from its mean, because countless different lists share the very same mean. The scores \(4, 5, 6\) and the scores \(1, 5, 9\) both average to $$\frac{4+5+6}{3} = \frac{1+5+9}{3} = 5,$$ yet one list barely moves while the other swings wide. Knowing an average pins down the total and nothing more. It stays silent about the individual values, how high they climb, how low they fall, and how far apart they sit.
Richville has an average wealth of 150 thousand dollars per person, and Poorville has an average of 20 thousand dollars per person. The two towns merge into one. If Richville and Poorville have exactly the same number of people, what is the merged town's average wealth, in thousands? Give the number.
Show solution
The idea here is that you can only average the averages when the groups are the same size. Because Richville and Poorville have the same number of people, each town contributes an equal share to the merged total, so the two averages get equal weight. That lets you just average them, $$\frac{150 + 20}{2} = \boxed{85}$$ so the merged town's average wealth is 85 thousand dollars per person. The thing to notice is that this shortcut is fragile. It works only because the two towns are the same size. If Poorville had ten times as many people as Richville, the merged average would be pulled far below 85, closer to Poorville's 20. A blended average hides the sizes of the groups behind it, and without those sizes the halfway point can be badly wrong.
Two towns are merging into one. Richville has 3 residents with an average wealth of 150 thousand dollars each. Poorville has 7 residents with an average wealth of 20 thousand dollars each. After the merge the new town has all 10 people. Find the average wealth of the merged town, in thousands. Give the number.
Show solution
This is a weighted mean, where each town counts as much as it has people. Averaging the two numbers straight would give the halfway point of 85, but that treats the towns as equal in size when they are not. Richville holds \(3 \times 150 = 450\) thousand in total wealth, and Poorville holds \(7 \times 20 = 140\) thousand. Divide the combined wealth by all 10 residents, giving $$\frac{450 + 140}{10} = \boxed{59}$$ The merged average is 59, which sits far below the midpoint of 85. Poorville has more than twice as many people, so its lower number pulls the average toward itself. A plain average of the averages hides the sizes of the groups, and size is exactly what decides where the true mean lands.
Key idea
You cannot average averages. To combine two group means into one, you need to know how big each group is. Suppose one class of 30 students averages 70 on a test and another class of 10 students averages 90. It is tempting to say the combined average is \(\frac{70+90}{2}=80\), but that is wrong. Averaging the two means treats the classes as if they were the same size, and here they are not. The right way is to weight each mean by how many values it stands for, so the 30 scores near 70 pull harder than the 10 scores near 90, giving $$\text{combined mean}=\frac{30\times 70 + 10\times 90}{30+10}=\frac{3000}{40}=75.$$ The true average is 75, not 80. It sits closer to 70 because the larger group has more pull. Averaging the averages is only correct in the special case where the groups are equally sized. Whenever they differ, the combined mean drifts toward the average of whichever group is larger, and you have to weight by size to find it.
A company advertises that its "average salary" is 80 thousand dollars, and technically that is true. But that mean is pulled up by a handful of executives who earn far more than everyone else. The median salary is 45 thousand, and nearly every worker earns close to that. Which number honestly describes what a typical worker at this company earns, in thousands? Give the number (in thousands).
Show solution
The idea is that a few very large values can inflate the mean, so the mean drifts up above where most people actually sit, while the median stays put at the middle of the pack. Line everyone up from lowest paid to highest paid. The median is whoever stands in the middle, so it reports what a genuinely typical worker earns, which is 45 thousand. The mean, on the other hand, adds every salary and divides, so the enormous executive salaries get spread across the whole total and lift it. That is how the mean lands at $$\text{mean} = 80 \text{ thousand}, \qquad \text{median} = \boxed{45} \text{ thousand}.$$ Since almost everyone earns close to 45 thousand, the median is the honest "typical" here. The company quoted the mean because it is the flattering number, not the accurate one. When a small group at the top holds unusually large values, the mean gets pulled toward them and can make pay look better than it really is, so the number that honestly describes a typical worker is 45.
Nine students turned in a project, each scored out of 10. Their scores were 3, 3, 3, 3, 7, 8, 9, 10, and 10. The mode is 3, which makes it sound like the class struggled. Find the median instead, since it better reflects how the class actually did. Give the number.
Show solution
The mode reports the most common score, and nothing more. Four students scored 3, so 3 is the mode, but that count of four is the whole story it tells. It says nothing about the other five students. The median finds the middle of the ordered list. The scores are already in order, and with nine of them the middle is the 5th value, counting in from either end. $$3,\ 3,\ 3,\ 3,\ \boxed{7},\ 8,\ 9,\ 10,\ 10$$ The median is 7. Look at the top half, 7, 8, 9, 10, 10, and you see most of the class actually scored high. The mode of 3 hid that by rewarding the one cluster of low scores, while the median 7 tells the truer story of how the class did.
Key idea
Pick the statistic that answers the question. The same data set has a mean, a median, and a mode, and they can disagree sharply, so whoever reports just one of them gets to choose the impression it gives. A mean dragged upward by a few huge values can make a typical outcome look far better than it is, and a mode parked on some unrepresentative clump can do the same. Before you trust a summary, ask what the number is actually for. When a handful of extremes pull the mean away from the crowd, the median usually tells the fairer story, since it sits where the middle person really lands. And treat the word "average" as a warning sign whenever nobody says which one it means, because that silence is often where the misleading happens.
Five families have a median income of 40 thousand dollars. The two lowest earners make 10 thousand and 25 thousand. What is the smallest the total income of all five families could be? Give the number (in thousands).
Show solution
The median fixes only the middle value, not the total. Sort the five incomes. The median of 40 means the third one is 40, and the two given low earners, 10 and 25, sit below it. The two remaining families are above the median, so each can be anything at or above 40. To shrink the total, we let both of them sink all the way to 40, the smallest they are allowed to be. That gives $$10 + 25 + 40 + 40 + 40 = \boxed{155}$$ so the smallest possible total is 155 thousand dollars. The lesson is that a median tells you where the middle sits but says almost nothing about the total. The families above it could be at 40 or at 4000, and the median never changes.
A company has 10 people. Their mean salary is 100 thousand dollars and their median salary is 40 thousand dollars. The nine non-executive workers all earn exactly the same amount, and the tenth person is the CEO. How much does the CEO earn? Give the number (in thousands).
Show solution
Both summaries are clues, and together they let us rebuild a number nobody told us. Work each statistic backward. The mean unlocks the total. Ten people averaging 100 means the payroll adds up to $$100 \times 10 = 1000 \text{ thousand}.$$ The median unlocks the workers. With ten salaries the median is the average of the 5th and 6th, and both of those are ordinary workers. Since all nine workers earn the same, that shared salary is 40. Nine of them take $$9 \times 40 = 360 \text{ thousand},$$ which leaves the CEO with $$1000 - 360 = \boxed{640} \text{ thousand}.$$ The CEO earns 640 thousand. Notice what tipped us off. The mean was 100 but the median was only 40, and that huge gap between them is the fingerprint of a single enormous outlier pulling the average up while the middle stays put. We did not just report the gap, we used it to reconstruct the exact number the two summaries were hiding.
Fun fact
In 1954 the journalist Darrell Huff published a slim book called "How to Lie with Statistics", showing how averages, graphs, and percentages get quietly bent to mislead. It went on to become one of the best selling statistics books ever written. That is a strange kind of fame for a math book, and it proves something worth remembering. Knowing what a number hides turns out to be just as valuable as knowing how to compute it.
Key idea
Always ask what the number hides. A statistic is a summary, and every summary leaves something out. The mean is easily swayed by a single outlier and blind to how spread out the data is. The median ignores the extremes and tells you nothing about the total. The mode can sit on a lump of values that are not typical at all. And none of the three can be run backward into the data they came from. So keep one habit. Set the mean beside the median to sense skew and outliers, take a look at how far the values scatter, ask which summary the question actually needs, and never forget that one number cannot carry the whole story.

Review problems

Review 1. Two chess clubs each average 50 points per match. Club A scored 50, 50, and 50. Club B scored 10, 50, and 90. Find the range of Club B's scores. Give the number.
Show solution
The mean hides spread. Club A and Club B both average 50, so on paper they look the same. But Club A never strayed from 50, while Club B swung from a low of 10 to a high of 90. The range captures that swing, giving $$90 - 10 = \boxed{80}$$ So Club B's range is 80. The lesson is that two data sets can share the exact same mean yet behave completely differently. The average told us nothing about how consistent or wild the scores were, and the spread is what it kept hidden.
Review 2. Ben played five basketball games and scored 88, 90, 85, 92, and 0 points. He fouled out early in one game, which is why one of his scores is 0. Find the median of his five scores. Give the number.
Show solution
The median is the middle value of the ordered list, not the average, so start by sorting the five scores from smallest to largest. $$0,\ 85,\ \boxed{88},\ 90,\ 92$$ With five values, the middle one is the third, which is \(88\). So Ben's median is \(88\) points. Here is what this hides. That 0 came from fouling out and is a true outlier, and it drags the mean down to \(\frac{0+85+88+90+92}{5}=71\) points. The median barely notices it and stays at \(88\), right where his typical game lives. The mean says his scoring collapsed, the median says one game was a fluke and the rest were strong. The single number you pick decides the whole story.
Review 3. Five houses on a street sold for 20, 22, 24, 26, and 58 ten-thousands of dollars. Find the mean and the median of these five prices, then give the mean minus the median. Give the number.
Show solution
The mean is sensitive to outliers, the median is not. Add all five prices and divide by five, giving $$\frac{20+22+24+26+58}{5}=\frac{150}{5}=30.$$ The median is the middle value once they are in order, which is the third one, so the median is \(24\). The gap between them is $$30-24=\boxed{6}.$$ Four of the five houses sold near 20 to 26 ten-thousands, yet the mean says 30. That single expensive house dragged the mean six ten-thousands above the median. The mean minus median is \(6\), and the lesson is that when the two summaries disagree this much, one high value is quietly hiding inside the average.
Review 4. A team of 9 players has a median height of 70 inches. Someone reads that and pictures nine tall people. But the median only pins down the middle player once the heights are lined up in order. Four players are shorter than the middle one and four are taller, and nothing says how tall those four at the top can climb. What is the smallest possible height, in inches, of the tallest player on the team? Give the number in inches.
Show solution
The median tells you about the middle, not the extremes. Put the 9 heights in order from shortest to tallest. With 9 players the median is the 5th value, so the 5th player is exactly 70 inches. Every player after the 5th is at least as tall as the median, so the four tallest players are each at least 70. To make the tallest player as short as possible, let all of them tie the median, giving a lineup where the top values are $$70,\ 70,\ 70,\ 70.$$ The tallest can be no shorter than that, so the answer is \(\boxed{70}\) inches. This is what the median hides. It fixes the middle but says nothing about how the tails behave, so a team with a median of 70 could be all the same height or could stretch far above it, and one summary number cannot tell you which.
Review 5. A server's five weekly tips, in dollars, are 4, 8, 10, 14, and 20, so the median is 10. One night a grateful customer leaves a huge 9000 dollar tip, added as a sixth value. Find the median of all six tips. Give the number.
Show solution
The idea here is that the median barely notices the giant tip. Sort the six values in order, giving 4, 8, 10, 14, 20, 9000. With an even count of numbers, the median is the average of the two middle ones, which are 10 and 14, so $$\frac{10 + 14}{2} = \boxed{12}.$$ Adding a value 9000 dollars larger than everything else moved the median only from 10 to 12. That is what the median hides and protects against. A single wild value can swing the mean enormously, but the median just shifts to the next spot in the sorted list. The new median is 12.
Review 6. Five numbers have a mean of 12. Consider these four statements about them. (1) The five numbers add to 60. (2) At least one of them is 12 or more. (3) They are not all equal. (4) At least one of them is 12 or less. Some of these are forced by the mean and some are not. How many of the four statements must be true?
Show solution
A mean pins down the total and the balance point, and nothing else. Since the mean is 12, the five numbers must add to $$5 \times 12 = 60,$$ so statement (1) is forced. For (2) and (4), if every value were below 12 the total would fall short of 60, and if every value were above 12 the total would overshoot, so there has to be at least one value that is 12 or more and at least one that is 12 or less. Both are forced. Statement (3) is the trap. Five 12's average to 12 and are all equal, so the data does not have to be spread out at all. That leaves (1), (2), and (4) true and (3) not forced, giving $$\boxed{3}$$ statements that must be true. The lesson is that the mean tells you the total and the center, but it hides the spread. Equal data and wildly scattered data can share the exact same mean.
Review 7. Team A has 20 players who average 90 points each. Team B has 20 players who average 30 points each. The two teams merge into one group of 40 players. Find the average of the merged group. Give the number.
Show solution
When two groups are the same size, you can average their averages, because each side carries equal weight. Halfway between 90 and 30 is $$\frac{90+30}{2}=\boxed{60}.$$ The merged group averages 60. Notice what the single number hides. A player who scores exactly 60 does not exist on either team. The average lands between the two clusters, in an empty gap where nobody actually is.
Review 8. A gym advertises that its members' average number of monthly visits is 70. But a few members come almost every day, and their high counts pull that average up. The median number of visits is 40, which sits much closer to what most members actually do. Which number honestly describes a typical member, the average of 70 or the median of 40? Give the number.
Show solution
The mean gets pulled toward extreme values, while the median reports the middle no matter how large the extremes are. A few daily regulars log huge visit counts, and those big numbers lift the mean to 70 even though most members are nowhere near that. The median ignores how far out the extremes stretch and simply marks the middle of the group, giving $$\text{median} = \boxed{40}$$ So 40 is the honest description of a typical member. The lesson is that a single average can hide a lopsided group. When a few values sit far above the rest, the mean drifts toward them and stops describing anyone real, so the median is the number that tells the truth here.
Review 9. A tutoring center has two study groups. Group A has 4 students who averaged 100 on a test, and Group B has 6 students who averaged 55 on the same test. All 10 students are combined into one list. Find the average score of the combined group. Give the number.
Show solution
An average times the count gives back the total, so recover each group's point total and add them. Group A holds \(4 \times 100 = 400\) points and Group B holds \(6 \times 55 = 330\) points. Divide the combined total by all 10 students, giving $$\frac{400 + 330}{10} = \frac{730}{10} = \boxed{73}.$$ \nThe combined average is \(73\). Notice it lands closer to \(55\) than to \(100\), because the larger group pulls the average toward its own value. That is the whole point of a weighted average, and it is exactly what a plain midpoint of \(77.5\) between \(55\) and \(100\) would hide.
Review 10. Five stores report their daily sales in hundreds of dollars. The median of the five values is 30, and the two lowest stores sold 5 and 12. What is the smallest possible total for all five stores? Give the number.
Show solution
The trap here is thinking the median controls the total. It does not. The median just pins the middle value, leaving the top two stores free to be anything from 30 upward. To shrink the total as much as possible, push those top two stores all the way down to 30, the lowest they can be while 30 still sits in the middle. That gives the values 5, 12, 30, 30, 30, so the smallest total is $$5 + 12 + 30 + 30 + 30 = \boxed{107}.$$ The smallest possible total is 107 (hundreds of dollars). Notice what the median hid. A median of 30 puts no ceiling on the total at all. The real total could be far larger, since the top two stores might have sold thousands. One middle number tells you where the center sits, never how much sits above it.
Review 11. Nine people rated a movie on a scale from 1 to 13. Their ratings were 2, 2, 2, 2, 9, 10, 11, 12, and 13. Someone reports the mode and says the typical rating was low. Find the median instead. Give the number.
Show solution
The mode picks whatever value appears most, and four ratings of 2 give it the win. But that ignores that most people rated the movie high, so the mode paints the wrong picture here. The median fixes this by finding the true middle. The nine ratings are already in order, and with nine values the middle one is the 5th, giving $$2,\ 2,\ 2,\ 2,\ \boxed{9},\ 10,\ 11,\ 12,\ 13$$ The median is 9. That single number sits far above the mode of 2, and it tells the honest story. The mode hid the fact that most people liked the movie, while the median showed the middle of the crowd.
Review 12. Ten test scores have a mean of 60. Nine of the ten scores are each 50. Find the tenth score. Give the number.
Show solution
A mean is a promise about the total, not about any one number. If ten scores average 60, their sum is locked at $$10 \times 60 = 600.$$ Nine of them are each 50, contributing \(9 \times 50 = 450\). The tenth score has to make up the difference, giving $$600 - 450 = \boxed{150}.$$ So the tenth score is 150. Notice what the summary hid. A mean of 60 sounds like a room full of ordinary scores near 60, but here nine scores sit far below it and one score towers far above. The average alone never warns you that one value is doing all the heavy lifting.
