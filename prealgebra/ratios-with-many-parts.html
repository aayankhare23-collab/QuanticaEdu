Prealgebra · Lesson 7.2

Ratios with Many Parts

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A kombucha brewer keeps the same blend of tea, juice, and ginger every batch, writing it \(6:4:1\). Three quantities marching together work exactly like a two-way ratio, same idea, just one more seat at the table.

A garden potting mix follows the ratio \(5:3:2\), compost to peat to sand. Using exactly one unscaled batch, how many scoops of potting mix is that in total?
Show solution
A three-way ratio works the same way a two-way ratio does, it just tracks more parts at once. The ratio \(5:3:2\) tells you that one batch is 5 parts compost, 3 parts peat, and 2 parts sand. Since you are using one unscaled batch, each part is exactly 1 scoop, so the amounts are 5 scoops, 3 scoops, and 2 scoops. Everything goes into the same pile, so the total is the sum of all the parts, \(5+3+2=10\). The whole batch is \(\boxed{10}\) scoops.
A marching band lines up in the ratio \(5:3:1\), brass to woodwinds to percussion. Reading the ratio in that order, how many parts belong to the woodwinds?
Show solution
In a multi-way ratio the order is the meaning. Each number is glued to the label in the matching position of the reading. Reading \(5:3:1\) as brass to woodwinds to percussion lines them up cleanly. Brass takes the first part, \(5\). Woodwinds takes the second part, \(3\). Percussion takes the third part, \(1\). The new member only needs the woodwinds spot, and that is the middle number, so the woodwinds share is \(\boxed{3}\) parts.
Key idea
Multi-way ratios. A ratio does not have to stop at two things. Writing \(a:b:c\) compares three quantities at once, read "a to b to c", where the order is the whole point since it tells you which number belongs to which quantity. Like any ratio it pins down only the relative sizes and never the total on its own. Everything you already know carries straight over, equivalent ratios, simplifying, and the parts method, with simply more parts now and every one of them kept in step.
A florist arranges roses to lilies to ferns in the ratio \(3:2:4\). What is the smallest total number of stems that fits this comparison exactly?
Show solution
A multi-way ratio hides a total, just like a two-way ratio does. The comparison \(3:2:4\) splits the window into \(3+2+4=9\) equal shares, so one full set is \(3\) roses, \(2\) lilies, and \(4\) ferns, which is \(9\) stems. The greatest common factor of \(3\), \(2\), and \(4\) is \(1\), so the comparison is already in lowest terms and you cannot shrink it any further. Every window that fits the comparison is some whole number of these sets, so every valid total is a multiple of \(9\), like \(9\), \(18\), \(27\), and so on. Since \(8\) is less than one full set, there is no way to keep at least one of each kind and still land on \(8\), which is exactly why the florist rules it out. The smallest total that works is a single full set, \(\boxed{9}\).
Key idea
A multi-way ratio hides a total. When a mix follows \(a:b:c\), the real amounts could be \(a\), \(b\), \(c\), or twice each, or any matched scale-up \(na:nb:nc\), and every one of those is the same comparison. The trick is that the whole triple grows by one shared multiplier, never one part on its own. That is why the total is always a multiple of \(a+b+c\), and why one ratio can show up at many different total sizes.
A muralist mixes 18 oz blue, 12 oz white, and 30 oz black. Find the GCF of all three parts and write the simplified ratio of blue to white to black in the form a:b:c.
Show solution
To simplify a multi-way ratio, divide every part by the greatest common factor that all the parts share. So first find the largest whole number that divides \(18\), \(12\), and \(30\) evenly. Checking the biggest candidates, \(6\) works for all three since \(18 = 6\times 3\), \(12 = 6\times 2\), and \(30 = 6\times 5\), and nothing larger divides all three. So the greatest common factor is \(6\). Now divide each part by \(6\): blue becomes \(18\div 6 = 3\), white becomes \(12\div 6 = 2\), and black becomes \(30\div 6 = 5\). That gives \(3:2:5\), and since \(3\), \(2\), and \(5\) share no common factor beyond \(1\), it is fully reduced. The cleanest form of the recipe is \(\boxed{3:2:5}\).
Key idea
Reducing a multi-way ratio. Divide every part by the greatest common factor that all the parts share together, not a factor that only fits two of them. It is the same move as reducing a fraction, just spread across more numbers. Watch the trap of staring at one small part and deciding you must be finished, since a part being tiny means nothing on its own. You are done only when no whole number larger than \(1\) divides all the parts at the same time.
A board game ships action, coin, and gem tokens in the ratio \(6:10:15\). Write the ratio in lowest terms as a:b:c.
Show solution
To simplify a multi-way ratio, you divide every part by the greatest common factor of all the parts at once, not just one pair. So break each number into its factors. \(6 = 2\times 3\), \(10 = 2\times 5\), and \(15 = 3\times 5\). Now look for a factor shared by all three. The number \(2\) divides \(6\) and \(10\) but not \(15\). The number \(3\) divides \(6\) and \(15\) but not \(10\). The number \(5\) divides \(10\) and \(15\) but not \(6\). Each pair shares something, yet no single number bigger than \(1\) divides all three. That makes the greatest common factor of \(6\), \(10\), and \(15\) equal to \(1\). Dividing every part by \(1\) leaves the comparison exactly as it was, so the ratio is already in lowest terms. The player's instinct was a trap. A shared factor inside a pair does not mean the whole thing shrinks. The simplified ratio is \(\boxed{6:10:15}\).
A baker\'s blend uses \(1\tfrac{1}{3}\) cups oat flour, \(2\) cups rice flour, and \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) cup almond meal. Clear the fractions and reduce. Write the ratio of oat to rice to almond as a:b:c.
Show solution
First rewrite the awkward part as an improper fraction so the whole blend is in thirds. Oat flour is \(1\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{4}{3}\), rice flour is \(2=\tfrac{6}{3}\), and almond meal is \(\tfrac{2}{3}\). Every part is now a number of thirds, so multiplying all three parts by \(3\) clears the denominators at the same time. That gives \(\tfrac{4}{3}\times 3=4\), \(2\times 3=6\), and \(\tfrac{2}{3}\times 3=2\), so the ratio becomes \(4:6:2\). Scaling every part by the same number keeps the comparison the same, it just dresses it in whole numbers. Now simplify. The greatest common factor of \(4\), \(6\), and \(2\) is \(2\), so divide every part by \(2\) to get \(2:3:1\). Since \(2\), \(3\), and \(1\) share no common factor bigger than \(1\), this is fully reduced. The blend compares oat flour to rice flour to almond meal as \(\boxed{2:3:1}\).
Key idea
Clear fractions in a multi-way ratio by multiplying every part by a common denominator, so the fractions and mixed numbers all turn into whole numbers at once. Stretching every part by the same factor never changes what they are comparing, so the ratio you get is the same comparison wearing whole numbers. Then finish the usual way, dividing every part by the greatest common factor they all share to reach lowest terms.
A terrarium is layered sand to gravel to soil in the ratio \(3:5:7\), filling 60 scoops total. How many scoops of gravel does the terrarium use?
Show solution
The ratio \(3:5:7\) tells you the jar is split into \(3+5+7=15\) equal parts. Those \(15\) parts together make up all \(60\) scoops, so one part is worth $$\frac{60}{15}=4 \text{ scoops}.$$ Gravel is the middle number in the ratio, so it takes up \(5\) parts. Five parts at \(4\) scoops each gives \(5\times 4=20\) scoops. (As a check, sand is \(3\times 4=12\) and soil is \(7\times 4=28\), and \(12+20+28=60\), the full jar.) So the gravel uses \(\boxed{20}\) scoops.
Key idea
Parts method with many shares. Read \(a:b:c\) as one pile of \(a+b+c\) equal parts. Add every number to get the part count, divide the real total by that count to find what one part is worth, then each quantity is its own part count times that one-part value. It is the exact same engine whether there are two, three, or more shares, and the only thing that changes is how many numbers you add up at the start.
A choir keeps sopranos to altos to tenors in the ratio \(6:5:4\). Tonight 18 sopranos arrived. How many tenors are at the rehearsal?
Show solution
The comparison \(6:5:4\) splits the choir into \(6+5+4 = 15\) equal parts in all, but you only need the part size to answer this. The sopranos own the first number, so they fill \(6\) of those equal parts. Tonight 18 sopranos walk in, which means those 6 parts together hold 18 singers. One part is therefore \(18 \div 6 = 3\) singers. Every section is measured in these same parts, and the tenors own the \(4\), so the tenors hold \(4\) parts of \(3\) singers each, giving \(4 \times 3 = 12\). A quick sanity check fits the comparison nicely, since \(18\) sopranos, \(15\) altos, and \(12\) tenors reduce straight back to \(6:5:4\). So the number of tenors is \(\boxed{12}\).
An orchard plants apple to pear to plum in the ratio \(4:3:2\). This season there are 24 plum trees. How many trees are in the whole orchard?
Show solution
The ratio \(4:3:2\) compares apple to pear to plum, so the plum trees sit in the \(2\) part. The grower counted \(24\) plum trees, and those \(24\) trees fill exactly \(2\) equal parts. To find the value of a single part, share the plum count across its parts, \(24 \div 2 = 12\) trees per part. Every part of this ratio is worth that same \(12\) trees. The whole orchard is built from all the parts together, \(4+3+2 = 9\) parts. Multiply the number of parts by the value of one part, \(9 \times 12 = 108\). As a check, apple is \(4 \times 12 = 48\), pear is \(3 \times 12 = 36\), and plum is \(2 \times 12 = 24\), and \(48 + 36 + 24 = 108\). So the whole orchard holds \(\boxed{108}\) trees.

Sometimes you get two overlapping ratios that share one quantity, one tying together this pair of things and a second tying together that pair, with a single quantity sitting in both. You want all three in one clean \(a:b:c\). The trouble is the shared quantity almost never shows up as the same number in the two ratios, so they refuse to line up on sight. The next problems uncover the one move that fixes exactly that and snaps the two ratios into one.

A gear train: front to middle is \(4:3\) and middle to rear is \(2:5\). Rescale so the middle numbers match, then write front to rear as a:b in lowest terms.
Show solution
The two ratios share the middle gear, but they count it differently. Front to middle is \(4:3\), so the middle shows up as \(3\). Middle to rear is \(2:5\), so the middle shows up as \(2\). Before I can chain them into one front to middle to rear ratio, those middle numbers have to agree. I need a common multiple of \(3\) and \(2\), and the smallest is \(6\). Scale the first ratio up so its middle becomes \(6\). Multiply \(4:3\) by \(2\) to get \(8:6\). Scale the second ratio up so its middle becomes \(6\) too. Multiply \(2:5\) by \(3\) to get \(6:15\). Now both ratios call the middle gear \(6\), so they snap together into one chain, \(8:6:15\), reading front to middle to rear. The front gear is \(8\) and the rear gear is \(15\). Their comparison is \(8:15\), and since \(8\) and \(15\) share no common factor bigger than \(1\), it is already in lowest terms. So the front gear to rear gear ratio is \(\boxed{8:15}\).
Key idea
Linking two ratios through a shared part. When \(A:B\) and \(B:C\) both talk about the same middle quantity \(B\) but print different numbers for it, scale each ratio until both show the same \(B\), usually a common multiple of the two \(B\) values. The moment those numbers line up the three quantities click together into one longer ratio \(A:B:C\). The shared quantity is the hinge, and once everything hangs on it you can read off any comparison you want, even \(A:C\), which neither ratio showed you directly.
Yellow to blue is \(3:2\) and blue to white is \(4:7\). Rescale so the blue numbers agree, then write the ratio of yellow to white as a:b in lowest terms.
Show solution
The shared color is blue, but it carries different numbers in the two recipes, so first make those numbers agree. Blue is \(2\) in the yellow to blue ratio and \(4\) in the blue to white ratio. Doubling the first ratio turns its blue into \(4\) without changing the comparison, since multiplying both parts by the same number gives an equivalent ratio. So \(3:2\) becomes \(6:4\). The second ratio already has blue as \(4\), so leave it as \(4:7\). Now both recipes agree that blue is \(4\), and you can line all three colors up in one chain as yellow to blue to white, \(6:4:7\). Reading off only the first and last parts gives yellow to white as \(6:7\). The greatest common factor of \(6\) and \(7\) is \(1\), so this is already in lowest terms. The ratio of yellow to white is \(\boxed{6:7}\).
Fun fact
A major chord\'s notes vibrate near \(4:5:6\). The root-to-fifth gives \(4:6=3:2\), the "perfect fifth." Since 4, 5, 6 share no common factor, \(4:5:6\) is already in lowest terms.
A sourdough recipe: flour to water is \(5:3\), water to starter is \(6:5\). Water appears as 3 in one and 6 in the other. Rescale and combine. How many grams of flour are in a 630-gram batch?
Show solution
The two ratios share water, but they disagree on its number. Flour to water is \(5:3\) and water to starter is \(6:5\). To link them, rescale the first ratio so its water matches the \(6\) in the second. Multiplying \(5:3\) by \(2\) gives \(10:6\), so flour to water is \(10:6\) and water to starter is \(6:5\). Now both call water \(6\), and the three line up into one ratio, \(10:6:5\). The greatest common factor of \(10\), \(6\), and \(5\) is \(1\), so it is already in lowest terms. Add the parts to get \(10+6+5=21\) equal shares. The batch weighs \(630\) grams, so one share weighs \(\tfrac{630}{21}=30\) grams. Flour is \(10\) shares, which is \(10\times 30 = 300\) grams. As a check, water is \(6\times 30 = 180\) and starter is \(5\times 30 = 150\), and \(300+180+150 = 630\). So the batch contains \(\boxed{300}\) grams of flour.

Review problems

Review 1. A birdseed recipe follows the ratio \(7:4:2\), millet to sunflower to cracked corn. Using exactly one unscaled batch, how many parts of birdseed are there in total?
Show solution
A multi-way ratio carries a hidden total, the sum of all its parts. Here the blend is \(7\) parts millet, \(4\) parts sunflower, and \(2\) parts cracked corn. One full unscaled batch uses those numbers exactly as written, so no rescaling is needed and each part keeps its given size. Add every part to find the whole, \(7 + 4 + 2 = 13\). So one batch is \(\boxed{13}\) parts of birdseed in total.
Review 2. A printmaker logs a blend as \(20:50:35\), cyan to magenta to yellow. Divide every part by the GCF of all three. Write the simplified ratio in the form a:b:c.
Show solution
First find the greatest common factor of all three parts. The factors of \(20\) are \(1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20\), the factors of \(50\) are \(1, 2, 5, 10, 25, 50\), and the factors of \(35\) are \(1, 5, 7, 35\). The largest number on all three lists is \(5\), so the greatest common factor is \(5\). Now divide every part by \(5\). You get \(20 \div 5 = 4\), then \(50 \div 5 = 10\), then \(35 \div 5 = 7\). That gives \(4:10:7\). Check that this is fully reduced. The parts are \(4\), \(10\), and \(7\), and \(7\) is prime and does not divide \(4\) or \(10\), so the three parts share no common factor larger than \(1\). The blend in lowest terms is \(\boxed{4:10:7}\).
Review 3. A trail mix uses oats, raisins, and seeds in the ratio \(10:9:25\). Reduce to lowest terms, if nothing divides all three, the original is the answer. Write your answer as a:b:c.
Show solution
To reduce a multi-way ratio you divide every part by the greatest common factor of all three numbers together, so the real task is to find what \(10\), \(9\), and \(25\) share. Look at \(9\) first, since it is the most restrictive. It is odd, so \(2\) cannot divide it, which already rules out any even common factor. Its only factors are \(1\), \(3\), and \(9\). Now test \(3\) against the others. It does not divide \(10\), since \(10=3\times 3+1\), so \(3\) is not a common factor. The same goes for \(9\). That leaves only \(1\) as a number dividing all three parts, so the greatest common factor of \(10\), \(9\), and \(25\) is \(1\). When the greatest common factor is \(1\), there is nothing to divide out, and the ratio is already as small as it can get. Dividing each part by \(1\) just gives back \(10:9:25\), so the comparison was already in lowest terms from the start. \(\boxed{10:9:25}\)
Review 4. A flower farmer counts 42 marigolds, 14 basil plants, and 28 thyme plants. Simplify the ratio \(42:14:28\) to lowest terms. Write as a:b:c.
Show solution
To reduce a multi-way ratio I divide every part by the greatest common factor of all the parts, not just two of them. The parts are \(42\), \(14\), and \(28\). The factors of \(14\) are \(1, 2, 7, 14\), and the largest of these that also divides \(42\) and \(28\) is \(14\) itself, since \(42=14\times 3\) and \(28=14\times 2\). So the greatest common factor of all three is \(14\). Now I divide each part by \(14\), keeping the order marigolds, basil, thyme. That gives \(42\div 14=3\), then \(14\div 14=1\), then \(28\div 14=2\), so the ratio becomes \(3:1:2\). I can check by scaling back up, since \(3\times 14:1\times 14:2\times 14=42:14:28\), the original. The new parts \(3\), \(1\), and \(2\) share no common factor bigger than \(1\), so this is fully reduced. \(\boxed{3:1:2}\)
Review 5. A smoothie uses \(\tfrac{1}{3}\) cup mango, \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) cup spinach, and \(\tfrac{1}{2}\) cup yogurt. Clear fractions with a common denominator and reduce. Write the ratio as a:b:c.
Show solution
To compare these fairly, the cup amounts need to be whole numbers. The denominators are 3, 6, and 2, and the smallest number all three divide into evenly is 6. Multiply every part by 6. Mango becomes \(\tfrac{1}{3}\times 6 = 2\), spinach becomes \(\tfrac{1}{6}\times 6 = 1\), and yogurt becomes \(\tfrac{1}{2}\times 6 = 3\). Multiplying all three parts by the same number keeps the ratio equivalent, so \(\tfrac{1}{3} : \tfrac{1}{6} : \tfrac{1}{2}\) is the same as \(2:1:3\). Now check for a common factor across all three parts. The greatest common factor of 2, 1, and 3 is 1, so the ratio is already in lowest terms. The simplified ratio of mango to spinach to yogurt is \(\boxed{2:1:3}\).
Review 6. A candle recipe uses \(2\tfrac{1}{2}\) oz soy wax, \(1\tfrac{1}{4}\) oz beeswax, and \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) oz fragrance oil. Clear fractions, then reduce. Write the ratio as a:b:c in lowest terms.
Show solution
Start with the three amounts \(2\tfrac{1}{2}\), \(1\tfrac{1}{4}\), and \(\tfrac{5}{8}\). The smallest denominator that fits all of them is 8, so scaling every part up by 8 will clear the fractions while keeping the ratio the same. Soy wax becomes \(2\tfrac{1}{2}\times 8 = 20\), beeswax becomes \(1\tfrac{1}{4}\times 8 = 10\), and fragrance oil becomes \(\tfrac{5}{8}\times 8 = 5\). That gives the whole-number ratio \(20:10:5\). Now divide out the common factor. The greatest common factor of \(20\), \(10\), and \(5\) is \(5\), so divide each part by \(5\) to get \(4:2:1\). Since the parts \(4\), \(2\), and \(1\) share no factor bigger than \(1\), this is fully reduced. The ratio of soy wax to beeswax to fragrance oil is \(\boxed{4:2:1}\).
Review 7. A mosaic border of 96 tiles uses gold, copper, and slate in the ratio \(1:2:5\). How many copper tiles are in the border?
Show solution
The ratio \(1:2:5\) splits the border into \(1+2+5=8\) equal parts, and all \(96\) tiles fill those \(8\) parts evenly. One part is worth $$96\div 8=12\text{ tiles}.$$ Copper matches the middle number in the ratio, which is \(2\) parts, so the copper count is \(2\times 12=24\). As a quick check, gold takes \(1\times 12=12\) tiles and slate takes \(5\times 12=60\) tiles, and \(12+24+60=96\), which matches the border. So the number of copper tiles is \(\boxed{24}\).
Review 8. A 75-bead bracelet uses jade, amber, onyx, and pearl in the ratio \(2:4:3:6\). How many pearl beads are on the bracelet?
Show solution
The comparison \(2:4:3:6\) tells you how the \(75\) beads divide into equal shares. Add every part to count the shares, \(2+4+3+6 = 15\), so the whole bracelet is built from \(15\) equal shares. Find what one share is worth by splitting the total evenly, \(75 \div 15 = 5\), so each single share is \(5\) beads. Now scale up to the kind the question asks about. Pearl holds \(6\) shares, so the pearl count is \(6 \times 5 = 30\). As a check, jade gives \(2 \times 5 = 10\), amber gives \(4 \times 5 = 20\), and onyx gives \(3 \times 5 = 15\), and \(10 + 20 + 15 + 30 = 75\), which matches the bracelet exactly. So the bracelet carries \(\boxed{30}\) pearl beads.
Review 9. A robotics club has builders, coders, and designers in the ratio \(8:5:3\). Tonight 35 coders showed up. How many builders does the club have?
Show solution
The comparison \(8:5:3\) means builders take \(8\) parts, coders take \(5\) parts, and designers take \(3\) parts, all the same size. The coders are the link here, so start there. There are \(35\) coders sitting in \(5\) parts, so one part is worth \(35 \div 5 = 7\) members. Now rescale to builders. Builders take \(8\) parts, and each part is worth \(7\), so the club has \(8 \times 7 = 56\) builders. As a quick check, designers would be \(3 \times 7 = 21\), and \(56 + 35 + 21 = 112\) members in total, which is \(16\) equal parts of \(7\). The number of builders is \(\boxed{56}\).
Review 10. A produce crate is packed plums to apples to pears in the ratio \(2:9:4\). The crate holds 28 pears. How many apples are in the crate?
Show solution
In the comparison \(2:9:4\) the pears are the third number, so they are worth 4 parts. The crate holds 28 pears, and those 28 pears fill 4 equal parts, so one part is \(28 \div 4 = 7\) pieces of fruit. Now look at the apples. They are the middle number, worth 9 parts. Each part is 7, so the apples come to \(9 \times 7 = 63\). That gives \(\boxed{63}\).
Review 11. A relay team splits training as run to bike to swim to row in the ratio \(6:5:4:3\). The bike leg is 20 km. How many kilometers is the whole training distance?
Show solution
The comparison \(6:5:4:3\) is already in lowest terms, since the only number that divides all four parts is \(1\). The bike leg sits at the \(5\) in that comparison, so it is worth \(5\) parts, and you are told those \(5\) parts equal \(20\) kilometers. Rescale to find one part. One part is \(20 \div 5 = 4\) kilometers. The whole distance covers every part, so add them up, \(6+5+4+3 = 18\) parts. Multiply to get the total, \(18 \times 4 = 72\) kilometers. So the whole training distance is \(\boxed{72}\) kilometers.
Review 12. A spice jar blends cumin, paprika, and chili in the ratio \(7:2:1\). The jar has 18 grams of paprika. How many grams of spice are in the jar altogether?
Show solution
Paprika is the middle number in \(7:2:1\), so it stands for 2 parts. Those 2 parts weigh 18 grams, so one part is \(18\div 2 = 9\) grams. The whole jar is \(7+2+1 = 10\) parts. Multiply the value of one part by the total number of parts to rescale up to the full jar, \(10\times 9 = 90\) grams. As a check, cumin is \(7\times 9 = 63\) grams, paprika is \(2\times 9 = 18\) grams, and chili is \(1\times 9 = 9\) grams, and \(63+18+9 = 90\). \(\boxed{90}\)
Review 13. A clockwork toy has three gears. Gear 1 to gear 2 turns at \(5:6\); gear 2 to gear 3 turns at \(4:3\). Rescale so the gear-2 numbers match, then write gear 1 to gear 3 as a:b in lowest terms.
Show solution
The second gear is the shared quantity, so the first job is to make its number match in both ratios. It appears as \(6\) in \(5:6\) and as \(4\) in \(4:3\). The smallest number that both \(6\) and \(4\) divide into is \(12\). Rescale the first ratio by multiplying both parts by \(2\), which gives \(5:6 = 10:12\). Rescale the second ratio by multiplying both parts by \(3\), which gives \(4:3 = 12:9\). Now the second gear reads \(12\) in each, so the three gears line up as \(10:12:9\). The first gear is \(10\) and the third gear is \(9\), so their comparison is \(10:9\). The greatest common factor of \(10\) and \(9\) is \(1\), so it is already in lowest terms. \(\boxed{10:9}\)
Review 14. Nitrogen to phosphorus is \(2:3\) and phosphorus to potash is \(9:8\). Rescale the first ratio so its phosphorus matches 9, combine, then write nitrogen to potash as a:b in lowest terms.
Show solution
The two ratios share phosphorus, but they measure it with different numbers, so the first job is to make those numbers agree. In \(2:3\) the phosphorus part is \(3\), and in \(9:8\) the phosphorus part is \(9\). Multiply every part of the first ratio by \(3\) to lift that \(3\) up to \(9\). That turns nitrogen to phosphorus from \(2:3\) into \(6:9\). Now both ratios speak about phosphorus as \(9\), so they snap together into a single chain, nitrogen to phosphorus to potash equals \(6:9:8\). The question only asks how nitrogen compares to potash, so pull out those two parts, \(6:8\). The greatest common factor of \(6\) and \(8\) is \(2\), and dividing both by \(2\) gives \(\boxed{3:4}\).
