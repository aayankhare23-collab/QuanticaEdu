Prealgebra · Lesson 10.7

Reading Tables and Graphs

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

In 10.6 you squeezed a whole list down to one number with the mean, median, and mode. Now we go the other way and read data that someone has already arranged for you. A good display shows at a glance what a bare list hides, the biggest category, a trend over time, how a whole splits into parts. In this lesson you will read tables, bar and line graphs, pie charts, stem-and-leaf plots, and histograms.

The table above shows how many boxes of cookies three scout troops sold in October and November. Birch's Total is highlighted with a ? because it is missing. Complete the table by finding Birch's Total. Give the number.
Show solution
Read across Birch's row and pick out its October and November boxes. The Total for a troop is the two months added, so add them together, giving $$120 + 65 = \boxed{185}.$$ Birch sold 185 boxes in all.
Key idea
Tables. A table sorts data into rows and columns so any single value is easy to find. To read one, run your finger along a row and down a column until they meet, and there sits the exact number you want, pulled clean out of a pile of others without hunting. Many tables add a Total row or a Total column that sums a whole line at once. When you need a total, read straight across the row and add every entry, or read straight down the column and add, and if the total is already given you just read it off. That is the power of a table, exact lookup. Every value has one home, so you always know where to look.
The bar graph above shows how many marbles of each color are in a jar. Which color is most common, and how many marbles of that color are there? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the height of each bar and look for the tallest one. The blue bar rises higher than red, green, yellow, and purple, so blue is the most common color. Tracing the top of the blue bar across to the scale gives its count, so $$\boxed{22}$$ The jar holds more blue marbles than any other color, and there are 22 of them.
The bar graph above shows how many goals five players scored. How many more goals did the top scorer make than the lowest scorer? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the tallest bar to find the top scorer and the shortest bar to find the lowest scorer. The tallest bar reaches 8 and the shortest reaches 3. Subtract to find how many more the top scorer made, giving $$8 - 3 = \boxed{5}.$$ The top scorer made 5 more goals than the lowest scorer.
Key idea
Bar graphs. A bar graph turns amounts into heights, so a taller bar means more and you can compare categories at a glance without reading a single number. To pull out an actual value, read the top of a bar straight across to the axis. Once every height is a number, the usual arithmetic works. The tallest bar is the most, the gap between two bars is a difference, and all the bars added together give a total.
The line graph above tracks a plant's height in centimeters across six weeks. During which week did the plant climb the most from the week before, meaning the week with the steepest rise on the graph? Give the week number.
Show solution
Read the height at each week off the line graph and find the biggest jump from one week to the next. Subtracting each height from the one after it gives the weekly gains, and the largest of these is the climb into week 4, $$16 - 9 = 7 \text{ centimeters}.$$ No other week rises by more than 7, so the plant grew the most going into week \(\boxed{4}\).
The line graph above tracks two food trucks over five days. On exactly one day the two trucks sold the same amount, which is where the lines meet. How many did each truck sell on that day? Give the number.
Show solution
Look for the day where the two lines cross, since that is the only day both trucks match. The lines meet on day 3. Slide across from that crossing point to the vertical axis to read its height, giving $$\boxed{45}.$$ Both trucks sold 45 on the day their lines meet.
Key idea
Line graphs. A line graph is built for change over time. The line's steepness is the rate, so a steep climb is fast growth and a flat stretch is no change at all. That means the biggest jump lives in the steepest segment, not the highest point. When two lines are drawn together, the place where they cross is the moment the two quantities are equal, and the vertical gap between them is how far apart they are.
The pie chart above shows the favorite season of 400 surveyed students. How many of them chose summer? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the summer slice on the pie chart above. It is labeled \(40\%\), so 40 percent of the 400 students chose summer. Turn the percent into a decimal and multiply by the total, giving $$0.40 \times 400 = \boxed{160}.$$ So 160 students chose summer.
The pie chart above shows the favorite season of 400 students. How many more students chose summer than winter? Give the number.
Show solution
Read summer and winter off the pie chart above. Summer is 40 percent of 400 and winter is 15 percent of 400. One quick way is to subtract the percents first, then take that share of 400, giving $$(0.40 - 0.15)\times 400 = 0.25 \times 400 = 100.$$ Counting each group instead, summer is \(0.40\times 400 = 160\) students and winter is \(0.15\times 400 = 60\), so \(160 - 60 = \boxed{100}\). Either way, 100 more students chose summer than winter.
The pie chart above shows students' favorite pets, but the reptile slice is left blank with a question mark. What percent is the reptile slice? Give the number.
Show solution
The whole pie is 100 percent, so the five slices must add to 100. Add the four labeled slices and subtract from 100 to find the reptile slice, giving $$100-(35+25+15+12)=100-87=\boxed{13}.$$ The reptile slice is 13 percent.
Key idea
Pie charts. A pie chart shows how a whole splits into parts, and because the circle is the whole, all the slices must add to 100%. That gives you two moves. To turn a slice into a count, take its percent of the total, so a slice worth \(25\%\) of a group of \(80\) is $$0.25 \times 80 = 20.$$ To find a missing slice, subtract the known slices from 100%. A pie is the right display when you care about shares of a whole, not raw amounts.
The stem-and-leaf plot above shows how many push-ups each of thirteen students did. What is the median number of push-ups? Give the number.
Show solution
The median is the middle value, and a stem-and-leaf plot already lists the leaves in order. With thirteen push-up counts, the middle one is the 7th value counting from the smallest. Counting through the leaves from the top, the 7th value lands in the stem-2 row, on the leaf 7, giving $$\text{median} = \boxed{27}$$ So the median number of push-ups is 27.
The stem-and-leaf plot above shows eleven quiz scores. Find the mode of the scores. Give the number.
Show solution
The mode is the value that appears most often, so look for the leaf that repeats the most on one stem. On stem 6, the leaf 8 is written three times, more than any other leaf on any stem. Reading that stem and leaf together the way the key does gives $$6\,|\,8 = \boxed{68}.$$ The score that appears most often is 68.
Key idea
Stem-and-leaf plots. A stem-and-leaf plot splits each number into a stem, the leading digits, and a leaf, the last digit. So the value \(47\) becomes a stem of \(4\) and a leaf of \(7\). All the numbers with the same stem share one row, and their leaves get listed out beside it. That gives you the shape of the data like a sideways bar graph, since a longer row of leaves means more values landed in that range, while still keeping every exact number. And because the leaves in each row are written in order from smallest to largest, the plot does double duty. You can find the median by counting leaves inward to the middle one, and the mode by spotting the leaf value that repeats most.
The histogram above shows how many households own each number of pets. How many households were surveyed in all? Give the number.
Show solution
Every household lands in exactly one bar, so the total surveyed is the sum of all the bar heights. Reading each bar's height off the vertical axis and adding them, $$5+12+8+3+2=\boxed{30}.$$ So 30 households were surveyed in all.
The histogram above shows how many households own each number of pets. What is the mean number of pets per household? Give your answer as a decimal.
Show solution
Read each bar's height to see how many households own that many pets, then build a weighted total. Multiply each pet count by its number of households, add those up, and divide by the total households. There are \(5+12+8+3+2=30\) households, so $$\frac{0\cdot5+1\cdot12+2\cdot8+3\cdot3+4\cdot2}{30}=\frac{45}{30}=\boxed{1.5}$$ The mean is 1.5 pets per household.
Key idea
Histograms. A histogram is a bar graph of frequency, how OFTEN each value or range shows up. That is why its bars touch, with no gaps, since the values sweep across a continuous number line. The tallest bar marks the value that happens most, so it is the mode. Add up all the bar heights and you get the total count. And the mean is the same combined-mean move from 10.6, weighting each value by its frequency, add those products, and divide by the total, $$\text{mean}=\frac{\text{sum of (value}\times\text{frequency)}}{\text{total count}}.$$
The bar graph above shows the daily high temperature for six days, Monday through Saturday. What was the median daily high over those six days? Give the number.
Show solution
Read all six bar heights and put them in order from lowest to highest, giving \(12, 15, 16, 18, 20, 22\). Six values is an even count, so the median is the average of the two middle ones, the 3rd and 4th in the list. Those are \(16\) and \(18\), so average them, $$\frac{16+18}{2}=\frac{34}{2}=\boxed{17}$$ The median daily high temperature over the six days is 17 degrees.
Fun fact
Almost every graph you have read in this lesson was dreamed up by one restless Scottish engineer. William Playfair published the first bar chart and the first line graphs in his 1786 Commercial and Political Atlas, and in 1801 he drew the very first pie chart. Before him, numbers lived only in long tables and columns, and the idea of turning quantity into a picture you could take in at a glance was brand new. So when you find the tallest bar or trace a line to its peak, you are using tools that trace back to Playfair, who figured out that the eye can often see a pattern the eye would never spot in a wall of digits.
Key idea
Match the display to the question. Every display in this lesson is a tool built for one kind of question, and the whole skill is choosing the one that makes your question easy to answer. Reach for a table when you need an exact value or a total. You look up one cell or read across a row to the Total column, and the number is right there, no eyeballing. Reach for a bar graph to compare amounts across categories. The tallest bar wins and the shortest loses, and you can see the gap between any two at a glance. Reach for a line graph to track change over time, or to find where two lines cross. A rising line means going up, a falling line means going down, and a crossing point is the moment two things are equal. Reach for a pie chart for shares of a whole. Every slice is a percent, all the slices add to $$100\%,$$ and to turn a slice into a count you take that percent of the total. Reach for a stem-and-leaf plot when you want to keep every single value and still see the shape. The middle leaf gives the median and the most repeated leaf gives the mode, both just by reading. Reach for a histogram to see how often values occur. The tallest bar is the mode, and to find the mean you weight each value by how many times it happens. So the move is always the same. Decide what the question is really asking, pick the display that answers it cleanly, then read it carefully.

Review problems

Review 1.
The table above shows the points each team scored in two rounds. Add up every entry to find the grand total of all points scored across both rounds. Give the number.
Show solution
Add every number in the table. Total each round column first, then combine. Round 1 gives \(30+41+28=99\), and Round 2 gives \(22+19+35=76\). Now add the two column totals, so $$99+76=\boxed{175}.$$ The grand total of all points scored is 175.
Review 2.
The bar graph above shows how many votes each music genre received in a class poll. How many votes did rock get? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the height of the rock bar against the vote scale. Its top lines up with the 18 mark, giving $$\boxed{18}$$ votes. So rock received 18 votes.
Review 3.
The bar graph above shows how many new members the club gained each quarter. How many new members did the club gain over the whole year? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the height of each bar and add them, since the year is all four quarters together, giving $$9+13+7+11=\boxed{40}.$$ The club gained 40 new members over the whole year.
Review 4.
The line graph above tracks a seedling's height in centimeters over six weeks. How tall was the seedling at week 4? Give the number.
Show solution
Go to week 4 on the horizontal axis and look at the point plotted there. Reading straight across to the height axis lands you at $$\boxed{14}$$ so the seedling was 14 centimeters tall at week 4.
Review 5.
The line graph above tracks two runners' weekly miles over five weeks. On how many of the five weeks did Runner A run more miles than Runner B? Give the number.
Show solution
Walk across the graph one week at a time and mark each week where Runner A's point sits above Runner B's. That happens in week 2, week 3, and week 5, and B is higher or tied on the other weeks. Counting the weeks where A is on top, $$3 \text{ weeks} = \boxed{3}$$ So Runner A ran more miles than Runner B on 3 of the five weeks.
Review 6.
The pie chart above shows how 300 students get to school. How many of them take the bus? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the bus slice off the pie chart above, then take that percent of the 300 students. Writing the percent as a decimal and multiplying gives $$0.45 \times 300 = \boxed{135}.$$ So 135 students take the bus.
Review 7.
The pie chart above shows how the 300 students get to school. How many students either walk or bike? Add those two slices, then find that percent of 300. Give the number.
Show solution
Read the walk and bike slices from the pie chart and add them, so the combined share is \(20\% + 10\% = 30\%\). Now take \(30\%\) of the 300 students, giving $$0.30 \times 300 = \boxed{90}.$$ So 90 students walk or bike.
Review 8.
The pie chart above shows the favorite colors in a class. One slice, orange, is marked with a question mark instead of its percent. What percent of the class picked orange? What percent?
Show solution
A whole pie chart is 100 percent. Add the four labeled slices, then take that away from 100 to find what orange must be. Adding the known slices gives $$28 + 22 + 19 + 16 = 85,$$ so the orange slice is the leftover, $$100 - 85 = \boxed{15}.$$ Orange is 15 percent of the class.
Review 9.
The stem-and-leaf plot above shows the eight scores from a bowling match. What is the median of these scores? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the leaves off each stem in order, which already gives the eight scores from smallest to largest. With eight values the median is the mean of the fourth and fifth scores, which are 31 and 33. Average them, giving $$\frac{31+33}{2}=\boxed{32}.$$ The median score is 32.
Review 10.
The stem-and-leaf plot above shows ten step-counts, in hundreds. How many days had 40 hundred steps or more? How many days?
Show solution
You want values of at least 40 hundred, so read the stems that start those values, which are stem 4 and stem 5. Stem 4 carries three leaves and stem 5 carries one, giving $$3 + 1 = \boxed{4}$$ So 4 days had 40 hundred steps or more.
Review 11.
The histogram above shows how many students have each number of siblings. What is the most common number of siblings among these students? Give the number.
Show solution
Look for the tallest bar in the histogram, since its height tells you how many students share that number of siblings. The bar over 2 siblings rises higher than every other bar, so more students have 2 siblings than any other amount. That makes the most common number of siblings $$\boxed{2}.$$ The mode is 2 siblings.
Review 12.
The histogram above shows how many books each student read last month. How many students were surveyed in all? Give the number.
Show solution
Every student counted lands in exactly one bar, so add up all the bar heights to get the total surveyed. Reading the bars from left to right and summing their heights, $$3+7+8+5+1=\boxed{24}.$$ So 24 students were surveyed in all.
Review 13.
The pie chart above shows how 500 smoothie orders split across four flavors. How many more mango smoothies were ordered than kiwi? Give the number.
Show solution
Read the mango slice and the kiwi slice off the pie chart above, then turn each percent into a count out of 500. Mango is \(40\%\) of 500, so \(0.40 \times 500 = 200\) orders. Kiwi is \(15\%\) of 500, so \(0.15 \times 500 = 75\) orders. Now subtract to compare, giving $$200 - 75 = \boxed{125}.$$ So 125 more mango smoothies were ordered than kiwi.
