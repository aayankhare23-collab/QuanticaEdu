Prealgebra · Lesson 4.2

Equivalent Fractions

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

One ranger cuts a trail into \(4\) equal stretches and marks \(\tfrac{3}{4}\) of the way out. Another cuts the same trail into \(12\) stretches and marks \(\tfrac{9}{12}\). They walk out and find their ribbons on the same patch of dirt. How can one fixed point answer to two different fractions at once?

Trail cut into \(4\) stretches, then into \(12\). Every third stake of the second ranger lands on a stake of the first. Which stake sits on \(\tfrac{3}{4}\)? Write as \(a/b\).
Show solution
The point on the trail never moves. Only the name changes, because the two rangers are counting in different sized pieces. Her ribbon sits at \(\tfrac{3}{4}\), which means \(3\) of her \(4\) equal stretches lie behind it. Each of those stretches was sliced into \(3\) of his little pieces, so the distance behind the ribbon is \(3\) stretches times \(3\) pieces each, which is \(3 \times 3 = 9\) of his pieces. That puts the ribbon exactly \(9\) of his pieces out of \(12\) along the trail, so his ninth stake stands on the same patch of dirt. You can see it as multiplying the name by a clever form of \(1\), namely \(\tfrac{3}{4} \times \tfrac{3}{3} = \tfrac{9}{12}\). Multiplying top and bottom by the same number does not move the point, it just renames the address with finer marks. His stake sits at \(\boxed{9/12}\).
A fudge tray has \(3\) strips; \(2\) go to an order (\(\tfrac{2}{3}\)). Each strip is re-scored into \(5\) mini strips. Write the new fraction name for the order as \(a/b\).
Show solution
Nothing about the fudge itself changed when the baker ran the scoring tool a second time. The same blocks of fudge are sitting there, just sliced finer. So the order is still the exact same amount it always was, only its name is about to change because we are counting in smaller pieces. First the whole tray. It had 3 strips, and each strip got cut into 5 mini strips, so the tray now holds \(3 \times 5 = 15\) mini strips in all. That 15 is the new denominator, the number of equal pieces the whole is split into. Now the order. It was 2 strips, and each of those 2 strips also got cut into 5 mini strips, so the order is made of \(2 \times 5 = 10\) mini strips. That 10 is the new numerator. The order is \(10\) out of \(15\), which we write as \(\tfrac{10}{15}\). Notice what really happened. We multiplied the top and the bottom by the same number 5, because every single piece, whether it was in the order or not, got re-cut the same way. That is why \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) and \(\tfrac{10}{15}\) point at the identical pile of fudge. The re-cut is a move you can make on purpose with any fraction, slice each of the \(b\) pieces into \(k\) smaller pieces and you are holding \(a \times k\) of \(b \times k\), the same amount with a fresh name. \(\boxed{10/15}\)
A coach multiplies top and bottom of \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) by \(4\). The extra \(4\) appears on both floors and cancels, leaving the same value. Write the bigger name as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
Read \(\frac{5}{6}\) as a single division, \(5\) divided by \(6\). Multiplying the top by \(4\) and the bottom by \(4\) rewrites that division as \((5\times 4)\) divided by \((6\times 4)\). Now do each multiplication. The top becomes \(5\times 4 = 20\) and the bottom becomes \(6\times 4 = 24\), so the new name is \(\frac{20}{24}\). Why is this still the same split? You can pull the factor of \(4\) out of both pieces of the division, and a \(4\) in the top of a division paired with a \(4\) in the bottom undoes itself, leaving \(5\) divided by \(6\) again. The quotient never moved, only its label did. So the bigger numbered name for the same split is \(\boxed{20/24}\).
Key idea
Equivalent fractions. For any nonzero \(k\), $$\frac{a}{b}=\frac{a\times k}{b\times k}.$$ A fraction is one number, \(a\) divided by \(b\), so sticking the same \(k\) on top and bottom means \(k\) divides and is divided in the same breath. It cancels and the number never moves. The picture agrees. Cut each of your \(b\) pieces into \(k\) smaller pieces and you have \(k\) times as many of them, each \(k\) times thinner, so the total amount stays on the same spot. Choosing the bigger numbers on purpose is called building the fraction up.
A counter must display fractions over \(21\). A runner is \(\tfrac{2}{3}\) done. Find \(k\) where \(3k=21\), scale the top. How many of \(21\) lights should show?
Show solution
The counter can only show a bottom number of 21, so the job is to rewrite \(\frac{2}{3}\) as something over 21 while keeping the value exactly the same. The safe move is to multiply the top and bottom by the same whole number, because that just re-cuts the same amount into more, smaller pieces. Nothing about the actual size changes.

So start with the bottom, since that is the number you are aiming at. You want \(3 \times k = 21\). Solving gives \(k = 21 \div 3 = 7\). That is the instruction hidden in the story, slice each of the 3 thirds into 7 equal little pieces, and you get \(3 \times 7 = 21\) pieces filling the whole warmup.

Whatever you do to the bottom you must do to the top, so multiply the top by that same \(k = 7\). The 2 thirds that were already filled become \(2 \times 7 = 14\) of the small pieces. That gives $$\frac{2}{3} = \frac{2 \times 7}{3 \times 7} = \frac{14}{21}.$$ A quick sanity check, \(\frac{14}{21}\) reduces back to \(\frac{2}{3}\) since both 14 and 21 share a factor of 7, so the value really is unchanged. The counter should light up \(\boxed{14}\) of its 21 lights.
Scoreboard shows \(\tfrac{18}{24}\). Divide by \(2\) to get \(\tfrac{9}{12}\), then divide by \(3\). Stop when top and bottom share no factor above \(1\). Write the result as \(a/b\).
Show solution
Start with what the scoreboard shows, \(\tfrac{18}{24}\). Reducing is the build-up move played in reverse, so instead of multiplying top and bottom by the same number you divide them by the same number, and the only numbers you may use are ones that divide both cleanly. Both 18 and 24 are even, so 2 is a fair choice. Dividing gives \(\tfrac{18\div 2}{24\div 2}=\tfrac{9}{12}\). Nothing about the score changed, the player still made the same share of her shots, the name just got smaller because the shared 2 cancelled out of the division. Now 9 and 12 are both multiples of 3, so 3 is the next number that divides both. Dividing again gives \(\tfrac{9\div 3}{12\div 3}=\tfrac{3}{4}\). Here is the moment that tells you to stop. The new top is 3 and the new bottom is 4, and the only whole number that divides both of them is 1. There is no shared factor left bigger than 1, so there is nothing left to cancel and the fraction can shrink no further. The scoreboard's old record, written in its smallest name, is \(\boxed{3/4}\).
Key idea
One move, two directions. There is really one move and it points both ways. Multiply top and bottom by the same nonzero \(k\) to build up, \(\tfrac{a}{b}=\tfrac{a\times k}{b\times k}\), giving a bigger name with more, smaller pieces. Run it backward, and when some \(d\) divides both top and bottom evenly, divide them both by \(d\) to shrink, \(\tfrac{a}{b}=\tfrac{a\div d}{b\div d}\), peeling the name back toward fewer, fatter pieces. Either way you are only sliding a shared factor into or out of one division, so the point never budges on the line.
A coach writes a split as \(\tfrac{84}{120}\). Find \(\gcd(84,120)\), divide both top and bottom by it in one stroke, and write \(\tfrac{84}{120}\) in lowest terms as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
The slow road is to cancel one shared factor at a time, and it works, but it takes several rounds. The faster move is to find the one number that carries away every shared factor in a single division, the gcd. Factor each number into primes. We have \(84 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 7\) and \(120 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5\). The factors they hold in common are two 2s and one 3, so \(\gcd(84,120) = 2 \times 2 \times 3 = 12\). Now divide top and bottom by that single number. \(84 \div 12 = 7\) and \(120 \div 12 = 10\), which gives \(\tfrac{7}{10}\). Why is one pass enough? The gcd is built from exactly the primes 84 and 120 share, so once you divide it out, nothing shared is left behind. The new top 7 and the new bottom 10 have no common factor above 1, and that is precisely what lowest terms means. \(\boxed{7/10}\)
Key idea
Lowest terms. A fraction is in lowest terms exactly when the top and bottom share nothing bigger than \(1\), that is when \(\gcd(\text{top},\text{bottom})=1\). If the gcd is anything larger, a shared factor is still hiding in there waiting to cancel, so you are not done yet. The shortcut is that you never have to peel off one factor at a time. Find the gcd of the top and bottom and divide both by it, and that single division strips out every shared factor at once and lands you straight in lowest terms.
Shared primes of \(126\) and \(350\) are \(2\) and \(7\). Multiply them for the common factor, divide both by it. Write \(\tfrac{126}{350}\) in lowest terms as \(a/b\).
Show solution
Start from the prime factorizations the machinist found. The top is \(126 = 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 7\) and the bottom is \(350 = 2 \times 5 \times 5 \times 7\). Now ask which primes live in both lists, because only a shared prime can be divided out of the numerator and denominator together. The \(2\) is in both and the \(7\) is in both. The \(3\)s sit only on top and the \(5\)s sit only on the bottom, so those cannot cancel. The shared primes are \(2\) and \(7\), and their product is the greatest common divisor, \(2 \times 7 = 14\). That is the whole block of factors leaving at once. Divide the top by it, \(126 \div 14 = 9\), and divide the bottom by it, \(350 \div 14 = 25\). What is left has no prime in common, since \(9 = 3 \times 3\) and \(25 = 5 \times 5\), so the fraction is fully reduced. The gears in lowest terms are \(\boxed{9/25}\).
Key idea
Unique simplest form. Pick any fraction and you can spin out endless copies, multiplying top and bottom by \(2\), by \(7\), by anything you like, so one single number wears infinitely many fraction outfits. But strip every shared factor away and you always land on exactly one of them, the lowest-terms version whose top and bottom share nothing above \(1\). That one acts like a name tag. Each number has its own and no two numbers share, so two fractions are secretly the same number precisely when they reduce to the same lowest-terms name.
An app stores \(\tfrac{2}{5}\); display only allows bottom \(\leq 40\). Allowed bottoms are \(5k\leq 40\). How many different names fit?
Show solution
Each allowed name is \(\tfrac{2k}{5k}\) for some whole number \(k\), and the rule is that the bottom \(5k\) must be at most \(40\). The top never causes trouble, so the whole question collapses into a single condition on the bottom, \(5k \le 40\). That is exactly a count of the multiples of \(5\) that land at or below \(40\), the move you learned in Chapter 3. Dividing the bound by the step gives \(40 \div 5 = 8\), so \(k\) can be \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8\) and no further, since \(k = 9\) would push the bottom to \(45\) and overflow the label. Those eight values of \(k\) produce the bottoms \(5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40\), one legal name apiece. The neat idea here is that the whole family of names for a fraction is nothing more than a list of multiples, so counting names is counting multiples. There are \(\boxed{8}\) names.

When two fractions appear side by side, the fastest test gives both the same denominator: pieces of one size can be counted against each other. This shared-denominator move also powers fraction addition, so it pays to meet it now.

Slopes \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{8}\) over shared bottom \(24\): sixths \(\times 4\), eighths \(\times 3\). Compare tops. Write the steeper slope as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
While the two slopes wear different bottoms, the numbers on top live on different scales, so \(5\) and \(7\) cannot be raced against each other yet. The fix is to give them a shared bottom. A bottom of \(24\) works for both, since \(6\times 4 = 24\) and \(8\times 3 = 24\). Renaming a fraction by multiplying its top and bottom by the same number keeps its value untouched, it only changes the costume. Build the east ramp up by \(\frac{4}{4}\): $$\frac{5}{6} = \frac{5\times 4}{6\times 4} = \frac{20}{24}.$$ Build the west ramp up by \(\frac{3}{3}\): $$\frac{7}{8} = \frac{7\times 3}{8\times 3} = \frac{21}{24}.$$ Now both ramps measure their rise in twenty-fourths, so the comparison is just counting. We have \(20\) twenty-fourths against \(21\) twenty-fourths, and \(21 > 20\), so \(\frac{21}{24}\) is the larger slope. That bigger slope is the renamed west ramp, whose original fraction is \(\frac{7}{8}\). The steeper ramp is the west ramp, with slope \(\boxed{7/8}\).
Is \(\tfrac{4}{6}=\tfrac{6}{9}\)? Compute \(4\times 9\) and \(6\times 6\). Equal products mean equal fractions. Enter the common product if equal, else \(0\).
Show solution
Follow the judge's move and clear the bars. He multiplies the two equal-or-not numbers \(\tfrac{4}{6}\) and \(\tfrac{6}{9}\) by the same thing, \(6\times 9\), which keeps any true equality true. On the left, \(\tfrac{4}{6}\times 6\times 9\) cancels the \(6\) on the bottom and leaves \(4\times 9\). On the right, \(\tfrac{6}{9}\times 6\times 9\) cancels the \(9\) on the bottom and leaves \(6\times 6\). Notice what survived. The top of the first fraction got paired with the bottom of the second, and the bottom of the first got paired with the top of the second. The fractions are equal exactly when these two crossed products match. Now just compute. \(4\times 9 = 36\) and \(6\times 6 = 36\). They agree, so \(\tfrac{4}{6}\) and \(\tfrac{6}{9}\) really are the same number, and the common value is \(36\). That crossing trick is no accident. For any \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) and \(\tfrac{c}{d}\), clearing the bars the same way leaves \(a\times d\) against \(b\times c\), so the fractions are equal exactly when \(a\times d = b\times c\). The judge found his reduce-free test, and the answer here is \(\boxed{36}\).
Key idea
Cross-multiplication test. To check whether \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) and \(\tfrac{c}{d}\) are the same number, you do not have to reduce anything. Re-cut both to the shared bottom \(b\times d\). The first turns into \(a\times d\) of those tiny pieces and the second into \(c\times b\) of them, and since the pieces are now identical in size, the two fractions match exactly when those counts match. So just cross-multiply, top of each times the bottom of the other, and check whether \(ad=bc\). If they are not equal, the bigger product belongs to the bigger fraction, so this also tells you which name points further along the line.
Fun fact
Reducing shrinks the numbers written, not the amount. The gcd from Chapter 3 does the whole job in one step.
Master fraction \(\tfrac{8}{14}\). Three locks: \(\tfrac{12}{21}\), \(\tfrac{20}{35}\), \(\tfrac{16}{30}\). Cross-product test each. How many equal \(\tfrac{8}{14}\)?
Show solution
The cross product test says \(\tfrac{c}{d}\) equals \(\tfrac{8}{14}\) exactly when \(8 \times d = 14 \times c\). So for each lock she builds two products and checks whether they match. No reducing required, which is the whole point of the trick. First lock, \(\tfrac{12}{21}\). She computes \(8 \times 21 = 168\) and \(14 \times 12 = 168\). The two products match, so this lock truly equals \(\tfrac{8}{14}\). It stays. Second lock, \(\tfrac{20}{35}\). She computes \(8 \times 35 = 280\) and \(14 \times 20 = 280\). Match again, so this lock belongs too. Third lock, \(\tfrac{16}{30}\). She computes \(8 \times 30 = 240\) and \(14 \times 16 = 224\). These are close, only \(16\) apart, but close does not count. The products differ, so \(\tfrac{16}{30}\) is a near miss and does not equal \(\tfrac{8}{14}\). That lock gets tossed. Two of the three pairs matched, so two of the stamped fractions are genuinely equal to the master number. The cross product test caught the impostor without her ever simplifying a thing. \(\boxed{2}\)
Ticks \(\tfrac{n}{36}\) for \(n=1\ldots 36\). Gold plate only where \(\gcd(n,36)=1\). Since \(36=2^2\times 3^2\), \(n\) must miss multiples of \(2\) and \(3\). How many ticks are gold plated?
Show solution
A tick gets gold plated when its top number \(n\) shares no factor with 36 except 1. Since \(36 = 2\times 2\times 3\times 3\), the only prime factors lurking in 36 are 2 and 3. So \(n\) is safe exactly when it is not a multiple of 2 and not a multiple of 3. Instead of testing all 36 numbers, count the ones that fail and subtract. Among 1 to 36 there are \(\tfrac{36}{2}=18\) multiples of 2 and \(\tfrac{36}{3}=12\) multiples of 3. Adding those gives 30, but numbers like 6, 12, 18 got counted twice, once as a multiple of 2 and once as a multiple of 3. Those double counted numbers are exactly the multiples of 6, and there are \(\tfrac{36}{6}=6\) of them. So the count of numbers that have at least one bad factor is \(18 + 12 - 6 = 24\). The survivors are \(36 - 24 = 12\). You can sanity check by listing them, since the safe top numbers are \(1, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23, 25, 29, 31, 35\), and that is indeed twelve of them. The clockmaker gold plates \(\boxed{12}\) ticks.

Review problems

Review 1. A window box is split into \(3\) equal sections; flowers fill the first section (\(\tfrac{1}{3}\)). Re-scoring into \(12\) cells: how many cells do the flowers cover?
Show solution
Scoring each of the 3 sections into 4 cells multiplies the total count by \(4\), turning 3 parts into \(3 \times 4 = 12\) parts. The flowers never moved, so the same patch of box is still covered. To keep the fraction equal while the bottom grows by a factor of \(4\), the top has to grow by the same factor of \(4\). $$\frac{1}{3} = \frac{1 \times 4}{3 \times 4} = \frac{4}{12}$$ So the filled section is now described as \(4\) of the \(12\) cells. You can check it the other way too, since \(\tfrac{4}{12}\) reduces back to \(\tfrac{1}{3}\). The flowers cover \(\boxed{4}\) cells.
Review 2. Five sections report harmony fractions: \(\tfrac{8}{12}\), \(\tfrac{6}{10}\), \(\tfrac{10}{15}\), \(\tfrac{14}{21}\), \(\tfrac{9}{12}\). How many equal \(\tfrac{2}{3}\)?
Show solution
Two fractions name the same point exactly when their cross products are equal, so \(\tfrac{a}{b} = \tfrac{2}{3}\) precisely when \(3a = 2b\). Check each section. \(\tfrac{8}{12}\): \(3 \times 8 = 24\) and \(2 \times 12 = 24\). Equal, so this matches. \(\tfrac{6}{10}\): \(3 \times 6 = 18\) and \(2 \times 10 = 20\). Not equal, so this does not match. \(\tfrac{10}{15}\): \(3 \times 10 = 30\) and \(2 \times 15 = 30\). Equal, so this matches. \(\tfrac{14}{21}\): \(3 \times 14 = 42\) and \(2 \times 21 = 42\). Equal, so this matches. \(\tfrac{9}{12}\): \(3 \times 9 = 27\) and \(2 \times 12 = 24\). Not equal, so this does not match. Three of the five sections report a fraction equal to \(\tfrac{2}{3}\). \(\boxed{3}\)
Review 3. A hose covers \(\tfrac{2}{7}\) of a garden row (\(2\) of \(7\) beds). Every bed is re-cut into \(4\) patches. After the re-cut, how many equal patches make up the whole row?
Show solution
Cutting every bed into smaller pieces does not move the hose, so the watered share is still \(\tfrac{2}{7}\). We are just renaming that same amount with finer patches. Each of the 7 beds splits into 4 patches, so the whole row now holds \(7 \times 4 = 28\) equal patches. That \(28\) is the new bottom number. To be sure the hose still covers the same strip, count its patches too. The 2 watered beds each split into 4, giving \(2 \times 4 = 8\) patches, so the watered share is \(\tfrac{8}{28}\). And \(\tfrac{8}{28}\) reduces right back to \(\tfrac{2}{7}\), which confirms nothing about the coverage changed, only the names of the pieces. The whole row is made of \(\boxed{28}\) equal patches.
Review 4. A jar is \(\tfrac{10}{25}\) full. Reduce to lowest terms by dividing by the common divisor. Write the simplest name as \(a/b\).
Show solution
A fraction stays the same value when you divide the top and bottom by the same number, so I look for a factor they share. Both \(10\) and \(25\) are multiples of \(5\), and \(5\) is the largest number that divides both, so \(\gcd(10,25)=5\). Dividing each part by \(5\) gives \(10\div 5 = 2\) on top and \(25\div 5 = 5\) on the bottom. That leaves \(\tfrac{2}{5}\), and since \(2\) and \(5\) share no factor bigger than \(1\), it cannot shrink any further. The robot's shortest label is \(\boxed{2/5}\).
Review 5. \(30\) yes votes out of \(42\) total. Find \(\gcd(30,42)\), divide top and bottom by it. Write \(\tfrac{30}{42}\) in lowest terms as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
The fastest way to lowest terms is to peel off the whole common factor at once instead of chipping away at it. So start by finding \(\gcd(30,42)\). Both numbers are even, and looking closer, \(6\) divides \(30\) since \(30 = 6 \times 5\), and \(6\) divides \(42\) since \(42 = 6 \times 7\). Nothing larger divides both, so \(\gcd(30,42) = 6\). Now divide the top and bottom by that \(6\) in one step. $$\frac{30}{42} = \frac{30 \div 6}{42 \div 6} = \frac{5}{7}$$ Since \(5\) and \(7\) share no common factor bigger than \(1\), the fraction is fully reduced. \(\boxed{5/7}\)
Review 6. A label reads \(\tfrac{15}{28}\). Before reducing, check: is it already in lowest terms? A fraction is simplest when \(\gcd(\text{top},\text{bottom})=1\). Find \(\gcd(15,28)\).
Show solution
Break each number into its prime factors. The top, \(15\), is \(3 \times 5\). The bottom, \(28\), is \(2 \times 2 \times 7\). Now look at the two lists of primes side by side. One uses \(3\) and \(5\), the other uses \(2\) and \(7\). They share no prime at all. When two numbers have no prime factor in common, the only positive whole number dividing both of them is \(1\), so \(\gcd(15, 28) = 1\). That tells you the label \(\tfrac{15}{28}\) is already in lowest terms, and there is nothing left to cancel. The gcd you were asked for is \(\boxed{1}\).
Review 7. A reading of \(\tfrac{7}{12}\) must be rewritten over \(48\). Find \(k\) where \(12\times k=48\), then scale the top by \(k\). What top number is stored over \(48\)?
Show solution
The bottom has to grow from \(12\) to \(48\), so first find the scaling number \(k\). Since \(12 \times k = 48\), we get \(k = 48 \div 12 = 4\). The trick with equivalent fractions is that the value only stays the same if you scale the top and the bottom by the exact same number, so you multiply the top by \(4\) too. That gives a top of \(7 \times 4 = 28\), which means \(\tfrac{7}{12} = \tfrac{28}{48}\). You can check it by reducing \(\tfrac{28}{48}\), dividing both parts by \(4\) lands you right back at \(\tfrac{7}{12}\). The number stored over \(48\) is \(\boxed{28}\).
Review 8. A battery shows \(\tfrac{56}{64}\) charged. Divide top and bottom by their gcd. What is \(\tfrac{56}{64}\) in lowest terms as \(a/b\)?
Show solution
A fraction is in lowest terms when the top and bottom share no common divisor larger than \(1\). So the job is to find the greatest common divisor of \(56\) and \(64\), then divide both by it. Both numbers are even, and in fact both are built from a lot of \(2\)s. We have \(56 = 8 \times 7\) and \(64 = 8 \times 8\), so their greatest common divisor is \(8\). Dividing both parts by \(8\) gives $$\frac{56}{64} = \frac{56 \div 8}{64 \div 8} = \frac{7}{8}.$$ Now \(7\) is prime and \(8\) is not a multiple of \(7\), so they share no factor beyond \(1\). The battery is \(\boxed{7/8}\) charged.
Review 9. Sensor 1: \(\tfrac{8}{14}\). Sensor 2: \(\tfrac{12}{21}\). Cross-multiply: compare \(8\times 21\) against \(14\times 12\). Do they name the same point? Enter yes or no.
Show solution
The cross-multiply test takes the top of one fraction times the bottom of the other and checks both diagonals against each other. Run the first diagonal, \(8 \times 21 = 168\). Now run the second diagonal, \(14 \times 12 = 168\). The two products are the same, \(168 = 168\), so the test says the readings are equivalent. You can feel why by reducing each one. Dividing \(\tfrac{8}{14}\) by \(2\) gives \(\tfrac{4}{7}\), and dividing \(\tfrac{12}{21}\) by \(3\) also gives \(\tfrac{4}{7}\). Both sensors are really reporting the same fraction in disguise, so they do name the same point on the beam. \(\boxed{yes}\)
Review 10. A dial stores \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) as \(\tfrac{x}{40}\). Cross-multiplying gives \(5\times 40=8\times x\). What is \(x\)?
Show solution
Equivalent fractions point to the same spot, so \(\dfrac{x}{40}\) and \(\dfrac{5}{8}\) have to be equal. Cross-multiplying lines up the two diagonals, giving \(8 \times x = 5 \times 40\). The right side is \(5 \times 40 = 200\), so \(8 \times x = 200\). Dividing both sides by \(8\) leaves \(x = \dfrac{200}{8} = 25\). A quick check confirms it, since \(\dfrac{25}{40}\) reduces by \(5\) to \(\dfrac{5}{8}\), the value we started with. So the stored top number is \(\boxed{25}\).
Review 11. A scanner counted \(162\) clean jars out of \(252\) total. Reduce \(\tfrac{162}{252}\) all the way to simplest form \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
Start by breaking each number all the way into primes. $$162 = 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 2 \times 3^4$$ $$252 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 \times 7 = 2^2 \times 3^2 \times 7$$ Now line up the primes the two numbers share. Both have a single \(2\) to spare, and both carry at least two \(3\)s. So the shared primes are one \(2\) and two \(3\)s. Multiply those together to get the greatest common divisor (gcd). $$\gcd(162, 252) = 2 \times 3 \times 3 = 18$$ Divide the numerator and denominator by \(18\). $$\frac{162}{252} = \frac{162 \div 18}{252 \div 18} = \frac{9}{14}$$ Since \(9 = 3^2\) and \(14 = 2 \times 7\) share no primes, the fraction is fully reduced. The clean-jar share is \(\boxed{9/14}\).
Review 12. A machine stamps fractions equal to \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) with bottom \(\leq 80\). Allowed bottoms are multiples of \(8\). How many such fractions fit?
Show solution
A fraction equal to \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) is built by multiplying the top and the bottom by the same whole number \(k\), giving \(\tfrac{3k}{8k}\). Since \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) is already in lowest terms, the bottom of any equal fraction is exactly a multiple of \(8\), namely \(8k\). We need \(8k\) to be a whole number no larger than \(80\), so \(8k \le 80\), which means \(k \le 10\). The allowed values are \(k = 1, 2, 3, \dots, 10\), giving the bottoms \(8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, 56, 64, 72, 80\). That is \(10\) values of \(k\), so there are \(\boxed{10}\) such fractions.
Review 13. Prize wheel: \(30\) slots labelled \(\tfrac{n}{30}\). Sticker when \(\gcd(n,30)=1\). \(30=2\times 3\times 5\), so \(n\) must miss multiples of \(2\), \(3\), \(5\). How many slots get stickers?
Show solution
A slot wins when \(\gcd(n, 30) = 1\), and because \(30 = 2 \times 3 \times 5\), the winning \(n\) cannot be a multiple of 2, of 3, or of 5. The cleanest way is to just hunt for the survivors. Any even number is out, so I only look at odd numbers. Any multiple of 5 is out, which removes 5, 15, 25. Any multiple of 3 is out, which removes 3, 9, 15, 21, 27. What is left from 1 to 30 is \(1, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23, 29\). Counting those gives 8 winners. There is also a slick shortcut once you notice the factors. Of the 30 numbers, exactly half are even, so 15 survive the "no 2" rule. Of those that survive, one third would be multiples of 3 and one fifth would be multiples of 5, and the pattern of crossing-out leaves a fraction \(\left(1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\left(1 - \tfrac{1}{3}\right)\left(1 - \tfrac{1}{5}\right) = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \tfrac{2}{3} \cdot \tfrac{4}{5} = \tfrac{8}{30}\) of the whole, and \(\tfrac{8}{30}\) of 30 is \(8\). Both roads land in the same place. $$\boxed{8}$$
