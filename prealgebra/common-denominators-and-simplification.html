Common Denominators and Simplification

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

A researcher writes a fox count as \(\tfrac{84}{120}\), honest but bulky, while a teammate records his night over a denominator of \(45\). Two troubles are tangled here. One single fraction wants its smallest, cleanest name, and two fractions sliced into different-sized pieces need a shared unit before they can be compared. Both are the same Chapter 3 idea aimed in opposite directions.

The fuel reading is \(\tfrac{84}{120}\). Find \(\gcd(84,120)\), divide top and bottom by it in one move. Write \(\tfrac{84}{120}\) in simplest form as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
First find the biggest factor the top and bottom share. Break each number into primes. \(84 = 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 7\) and \(120 = 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 5\). The primes they both carry are two \(2\)s and one \(3\), so the \(\gcd\) is \(2 \times 2 \times 3 = 12\). That \(12\) is the single biggest chunk hiding in both numbers. Now divide top and bottom by it in one clean move. \(84 \div 12 = 7\) and \(120 \div 12 = 10\), which gives \(\tfrac{7}{10}\). Check that we are truly done. The new top is \(7\), a prime, and \(10 = 2 \times 5\) shares none of its factors, so the only thing they have in common is \(1\). The fraction is in simplest form, and we got there in one step instead of three. $$\boxed{7/10}$$
Key idea
Simplest form via the gcd. A fraction is in simplest form when the only number its top and bottom still share is \(1\), since there is nothing bigger left to divide out. Dividing out a shared factor is always fair, because that factor sits on the top and the bottom at once, so cutting it from both scales them by the same amount and leaves the point untouched. The quickest path is to find the single biggest thing the top and bottom share, their \(\gcd\), and divide both by it one time. That one cut sweeps out every shared factor at once, because the biggest already carried all the smaller ones along with it.
Divide \(\tfrac{9}{20}\div\tfrac{3}{8}=\tfrac{9}{20}\times\tfrac{8}{3}\). Cancel before multiplying. Write the result as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
Start by turning the division into a multiplication. Dividing by \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) is the same as multiplying by its reciprocal \(\tfrac{8}{3}\), so \(\tfrac{9}{20}\div\tfrac{3}{8}=\tfrac{9}{20}\times\tfrac{8}{3}\). The flip is what makes the simplifying easy, because in a multiplication any top factor is free to cancel with any bottom factor. Look across the two fractions. The \(9\) on top and the \(3\) on a bottom both carry a factor of \(3\), so divide each by \(3\) to get \(3\) and \(1\). The \(8\) on top and the \(20\) on a bottom both carry a factor of \(4\), so divide each by \(4\) to get \(2\) and \(5\). The product is now \(\tfrac{3}{5}\times\tfrac{2}{1}\). Multiply straight across, \(3\times2=6\) on top and \(5\times1=5\) on the bottom, which gives \(\tfrac{6}{5}\). Since \(6\) and \(5\) share no common factor, it is already in simplest form. So a little over one small batch fits, \(\tfrac{6}{5}\) of a batch to be exact. \(\boxed{6/5}\)
Evaluate \(\tfrac{3}{4}\times\tfrac{4}{5}\times\cdots\times\tfrac{8}{9}\). Every interior bottom is the next top and cancels. Write the collapsed result as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
Stack all the tops over all the bottoms in one fraction instead of multiplying step by step. That gives $$\frac{3\times4\times5\times6\times7\times8}{4\times5\times6\times7\times8\times9}.$$ Now compare the two rows. The top runs \(3,4,5,6,7,8\). The bottom runs \(4,5,6,7,8,9\). Every number from \(4\) up to \(8\) appears once on top and once on the bottom, and any number divided by itself is \(1\). So each of those pairs quietly cancels, the \(4\) with the \(4\), the \(5\) with the \(5\), and so on through the \(8\). The cancellation cascades through the whole chain from the inside out. When the dust settles, the only top with no partner is the \(3\) that started the chain, and the only bottom with no partner is the \(9\) that ended it. Everything between them has folded shut. What remains is \(\tfrac{3}{9}\), and since \(3\) and \(9\) share a factor of \(3\), that simplifies to \(\tfrac{1}{3}\). The relay covers exactly a third of the first runner's reference leg. \(\boxed{\dfrac{1}{3}}\)
Key idea
Cancel first, multiply second. Whenever fractions get multiplied, or divided once you have flipped the second one, every top is about to pile onto one big top and every bottom onto one big bottom. So the smart move is never to multiply across and then clean up the mess. Scan each top against every bottom, find every shared factor no matter which fraction it came from, and lift it off both floors before you multiply a single thing. Do that and the answer is born already in simplest form, with no reducing step waiting at the end and no giant numbers to wrestle in the middle.
Fun fact
Egyptians wrote every fraction as distinct unit fractions: \(\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{1}{4}\). The Rhind Papyrus (c. 1550 BCE) tabulates these splits.
\(1{,}485=3^3\times 5\times 11\) and \(1{,}890=2\times 3^3\times 5\times 7\). Cancel shared primes, read what remains. Write \(\tfrac{1{,}485}{1{,}890}\) in simplest form as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
Stack the prime factorizations so matching primes sit above each other. The top is \(1{,}485 = 3\times 3\times 3\times 5\times 11\) and the bottom is \(1{,}890 = 2\times 3\times 3\times 3\times 5\times 7\). The shared primes are the ones in both lists, namely \(3\times 3\times 3\) and a single \(5\). Cancel each shared prime from the top against its twin on the bottom. $$\frac{1{,}485}{1{,}890}=\frac{\cancel{3}\times \cancel{3}\times \cancel{3}\times \cancel{5}\times 11}{2\times \cancel{3}\times \cancel{3}\times \cancel{3}\times \cancel{5}\times 7}=\frac{11}{2\times 7}$$ Whatever survives on top is the new numerator and whatever survives on the bottom is the new denominator. On top only \(11\) is left, and on the bottom \(2\times 7 = 14\) is left. Since \(11\) is prime and shares no factor with \(14\), the fraction cannot shrink any further. The slow part, spotting that the hidden \(\gcd\) was \(3\times 3\times 3\times 5 = 135\), became automatic once the prime trees laid every common factor side by side. $$\boxed{\dfrac{11}{14}}$$
Simplify \(\dfrac{k^4\times 10}{k^2\times 15}\): cancel two \(k\)s and the shared factor \(5\). Result is \(\dfrac{2k^2}{3}\). Now set \(k=3\). What single whole number does the fraction equal?
Show solution
Start by counting copies of each factor instead of multiplying everything out. The top holds four \(k\)s and the bottom holds two \(k\)s. Every \(k\) on the bottom can pair off with a \(k\) on the top and cancel, since a factor over the same factor is just \(1\). Two pairs cancel, which uses up both bottom \(k\)s and two of the top \(k\)s, leaving \(k\times k\) upstairs and a clean \(1\) downstairs from those letters. Next handle the plain numbers. \(\frac{10}{15}\) shares a factor of \(5\), and dividing both by \(5\) gives \(\frac{2}{3}\). Put the two pieces together and the fraction is \(\frac{2\times k\times k}{3}\), that is \(\frac{2k^2}{3}\). All of that was done without knowing \(k\) at all. Now plug in \(k=3\). Then \(k^2=3\times 3=9\), the top becomes \(2\times 9=18\), and \(\frac{18}{3}=6\). $$\boxed{6}$$
Simplify \(\dfrac{a^4 b^2}{a^2 b^5}\): cancel \(a\)s and \(b\)s in pairs. Then set \(a=3\) and \(b=2\). What single fraction in simplest form does the whole expression equal?
Show solution
Treat each letter as its own little pile and never let them mix. Start with the \(a\)s. There are four of them on top and two on the bottom. Two of those top \(a\)s pair off with the two bottom \(a\)s and cancel, leaving \(4-2=2\) extra \(a\)s upstairs. Now the \(b\)s. There are two on top and five on the bottom. The two top \(b\)s cancel with two of the bottom ones, leaving \(5-2=3\) extra \(b\)s downstairs. Nothing else is left to cancel, so the simplified fraction is $$\frac{a\times a}{b\times b\times b}=\frac{a^2}{b^3}.$$ This is already in simplest form as far as the letters go, and notice it stayed a fraction, with the \(a\)s on top and the \(b\)s on the bottom. Now make it a number. With \(a=3\), the top is \(a^2=3\times 3=9\). With \(b=2\), the bottom is \(b^3=2\times 2\times 2=8\). So the value is \(\dfrac{9}{8}\). Since \(9\) and \(8\) share no common factor, this is already simplest form, and it really is a fraction, not a whole number. $$\boxed{9/8}$$
Key idea
Cancelling powers. A power is just a tally of copies. For each kind of factor, count how many copies sit on top and how many on the bottom, then strike them off in pairs, one from upstairs against one from downstairs, until one side runs out. Whatever copies are left stay on the side that started with more, and that is the simplified answer. A letter plays by the exact same rules, since it is only a factor you have not put a number to yet, so cancel the matching copies first and fill in its value at the very end.
Panels generated \(168\) kWh; building used \(480\) kWh. Divide \(\gcd(168,480)\) into both top and bottom once. Write \(\tfrac{168}{480}\) in simplest form as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
The whole job is to write \(\tfrac{168}{480}\) so that nothing more cancels. The fastest honest way is to find the largest number that divides both, the \(\gcd\), and divide by it once.

Factor both numbers into primes. We get \(168 = 2^3 \cdot 3 \cdot 7\) and \(480 = 2^5 \cdot 3 \cdot 5\). The factors they have in common are three twos and one three, so \(\gcd(168, 480) = 2^3 \cdot 3 = 24\).

Now divide top and bottom by \(24\) in one move. \(168 \div 24 = 7\) and \(480 \div 24 = 20\), which gives \(\tfrac{7}{20}\). Since \(7\) is prime and does not divide \(20\), nothing is left to cancel, so this really is simplest form.

The caretaker can post that the panels supplied \(\tfrac{7}{20}\) of the building's electricity, a little over a third of the day's power. \(\boxed{\tfrac{7}{20}}\)
Key idea
Simplest form is the smallest private denominator. Every reducing problem is secretly the same move. You take one fraction and squeeze its denominator down as small as it can go, and the tool that does the squeezing is always the \(\gcd\), because pulling out the biggest factor the top and bottom share is exactly what drops the denominator as low as it will drop. What you land on is that fraction's smallest private name, the tidiest denominator it can wear all on its own. Hold onto that word, private, because a simplest form belongs to that one fraction and nobody else, which is why it acts like a name tag.

Simplest form gives one fraction its private name. But \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{8}\) are both already simplest, and you still cannot say which is larger, because sixths and eighths are different-sized cuts and you cannot count one kind against the other. Here the clever move flips. Instead of shrinking each denominator toward its private name, we grow them until they agree on one shared size of piece.

The lcd of \(6\) and \(8\) is \(24\). Eighths \(\times 3\): \(\tfrac{7}{8}=\tfrac{21}{24}\). Do the same for \(\tfrac{5}{6}\). What does \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) become over \(24\)?
Show solution
The goal is to keep the same amount of ribbon but describe it in twenty fourths instead of sixths. First figure out the multiplier that turns the denominator \(6\) into \(24\). Since \(6\times 4=24\), the multiplier is \(4\). The equivalent fraction move says whatever you do to the bottom you must do to the top, so multiply the numerator by \(4\) too. That gives \(5\times 4=20\) on top, and the rebuilt fraction is \(\tfrac{20}{24}\). A quick gut check confirms it is the same amount, because \(\tfrac{20}{24}\) just means twenty of the little pieces while \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) meant five of the big pieces, and each big piece was split into \(4\) little ones. Both ribbons are now measured in matching twenty fourths, \(\tfrac{20}{24}\) and \(\tfrac{21}{24}\), so they are finally ready to count side by side. $$\tfrac{5}{6}=\boxed{20/24}$$
To add \(\tfrac{1}{6}+\tfrac{4}{9}\) find a shared denominator. \(6\times 9=54\) works but overshoots; \(6\) and \(9\) share \(3\). What is \(\operatorname{lcm}(6,9)\)?
Show solution
The product \(6\times 9=54\) works, but it overshoots because \(6\) and \(9\) overlap. Both carry a factor of \(3\), so multiplying them counts that \(3\) twice. The lcm fixes this by counting each prime only as many times as it is truly needed. Break each denominator into primes, \(6=2\times 3\) and \(9=3\times 3\). To be a multiple of \(6\) you need one \(2\) and one \(3\). To be a multiple of \(9\) you need two \(3\)s. So the smallest number that covers both needs one \(2\) and two \(3\)s, which is \(2\times 3\times 3=18\). You can check it the plain way too. The multiples of \(9\) are \(9, 18, 27, \dots\), and the very first one that \(6\) also divides is \(18\), since \(18=6\times 3\). Notice \(18\) is exactly \(\tfrac{54}{3}\), smaller than the product by the shared factor of \(3\). The least common denominator of \(\tfrac{1}{6}\) and \(\tfrac{4}{9}\) is \(\operatorname{lcm}(6,9)=\boxed{18}\).
Key idea
Least common denominator (lcd). To give two fractions a common denominator, find a number that every denominator divides into evenly, a shared multiple, and rewrite each fraction over it as an equivalent fraction. Any shared multiple works, and just multiplying the denominators together always hands you one, but it is usually bigger and clumsier than it needs to be. The smallest shared multiple, the \(\operatorname{lcm}\) of the denominators, keeps the numbers as gentle as possible, and that one earns its own name, the least common denominator. Where simplest form reached for the \(\gcd\), a common denominator reaches for the \(\operatorname{lcm}\).
\(\operatorname{lcm}(4,6,9)\): primes are \(2^2\) and \(3^2\). Multiply the highest powers. What is the lcd of \(\tfrac{1}{4}\), \(\tfrac{5}{6}\), \(\tfrac{7}{9}\)?
Show solution
Write each denominator as primes so nothing stays hidden, \(4 = 2^2\), \(6 = 2^1 \times 3^1\), and \(9 = 3^2\). The least common denominator must be a number that \(4\), \(6\), and \(9\) all divide into evenly, so it must carry every prime that shows up, and carry enough copies to cover the greediest denominator for that prime. For the prime \(2\), the greediest is the \(4\) with two of them, so we need \(2^2\). For the prime \(3\), the greediest is the \(9\) with two of them, so we need \(3^2\). Take the highest power of each and multiply, \(2^2 \times 3^2 = 4 \times 9 = 36\). Check that all three fit, \(36 \div 4 = 9\), \(36 \div 6 = 6\), and \(36 \div 9 = 4\), all whole. And \(36\) really is the smallest such number, because dropping even one factor would leave some denominator unable to divide in. The least common denominator is \(\boxed{36}\).
lcd is \(36\). \(\tfrac{1}{4}\to\tfrac{9}{36}\). \(\tfrac{7}{9}\to\tfrac{28}{36}\). What does \(\tfrac{5}{6}\) become over \(36\)?
Show solution
Start with the bottom, since that is what decides the multiplier. You want \(6\) to become \(36\), and \(6\times 6 = 36\), so the scaling factor here is \(6\). The whole point of building up a fraction is that the top must climb by the same factor, otherwise you would be changing the value instead of just renaming it. So multiply the top by \(6\) as well, \(5\times 6 = 30\). Putting them together, \(\tfrac{5}{6} = \tfrac{5\times 6}{6\times 6} = \tfrac{30}{36}\). Notice this is the exact same rhythm you used for \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) and \(\tfrac{7}{9}\), look at what the bottom needs, then copy that move onto the top. Three fractions now share the floor \(36\), reading \(\tfrac{9}{36}\), \(\tfrac{30}{36}\), and \(\tfrac{28}{36}\), all ready to be compared or combined. The one you were asked for is \(\boxed{30/36}\).
\(\tfrac{5}{9}\) vs \(\tfrac{7}{12}\). lcd is \(36\). Build each to \(36\) and compare tops. Which is larger? Write it as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
As long as the denominators differ, the numerators are counting pieces of different sizes, so \(5\) ninths against \(7\) twelfths is not a fair race. Make the pieces match first. The least common denominator of \(9\) and \(12\) is \(36\). Grow \(\tfrac{5}{9}\) by multiplying top and bottom by \(4\), which gives \(\tfrac{5 \times 4}{9 \times 4} = \tfrac{20}{36}\). Grow \(\tfrac{7}{12}\) by multiplying top and bottom by \(3\), which gives \(\tfrac{7 \times 3}{12 \times 3} = \tfrac{21}{36}\). Now both are stacks of thirty-sixths, identical in size, so whichever has more pieces is the bigger share. Since \(21 > 20\), the second claim wins, and the comparison that was invisible at the start is now obvious. The larger fraction is \(\boxed{\tfrac{7}{12}}\).
Key idea
Growing to a shared denominator. To set two fractions side by side, pick a number that every bottom divides into, the smallest being their \(\operatorname{lcm}\), and rebuild each fraction over it by scaling the top by exactly the amount its own bottom was stretched. Once that is done, every fraction is sliced into pieces of one identical size, and pieces of the same size can finally be counted straight against each other, so the larger share jumps out. Growing the denominators never moves a single amount, the same way shrinking them never did. It only hands the fractions a shared set of names so they can speak to each other at last.
Key idea
Shrink versus grow. The right denominator was never a single number, it was a direction. When a fraction works alone and just wants its cleanest name, you shrink it, dividing top and bottom by their \(\gcd\) until the denominator drops to the smallest private name it can carry. When fractions need to meet, to be compared or added, you grow them instead, lifting each to the \(\operatorname{lcm}\) of the denominators where they settle on the smallest shared name. Two tools, the \(\gcd\) and the \(\operatorname{lcm}\), pulling in opposite directions, both from Chapter 3, yet both reaching for the easiest denominator the moment asks for.

Review problems

Review 1. A contact sheet has \(24\) thumbnails; \(18\) are keepers. Reduce \(\tfrac{18}{24}\) by dividing by \(\gcd(18,24)\). Write the answer as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
You could chip away at this fraction in stages, dividing by \(2\) and then by \(3\), but the cleanest path is to find the biggest factor the numerator and denominator share and remove it all at once. That factor is the \(\gcd\). The numbers that divide \(18\) are \(1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18\), and the numbers that divide \(24\) are \(1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24\). The largest one in both lists is \(6\), so \(\gcd(18,24)=6\). Now divide the top and the bottom by \(6\) in a single step. $$\frac{18}{24}=\frac{18\div 6}{24\div 6}=\frac{3}{4}$$ Since \(3\) and \(4\) share no common factor larger than \(1\), the fraction cannot shrink any further. So \(18\) keepers out of \(24\) thumbnails is exactly \(\boxed{3/4}\) of the sheet, which means three of every four pictures made the cut.
Review 2. A big batch uses \(360\) g of one ingredient per \(504\) g of another. Simplify \(\tfrac{360}{504}\) to simplest form and write as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
The slow road would be to peel off factors of \(2\) and \(3\) over and over. The fast road is to find the greatest common divisor once and divide both numbers by it together. To get \(\gcd(360,504)\), I chase the difference. Since \(504-360=144\), the gcd of \(360\) and \(504\) equals the gcd of \(360\) and \(144\). Then \(360-2\cdot 144 = 72\), so it equals the gcd of \(144\) and \(72\), and \(72\) divides \(144\) evenly, so \(\gcd(360,504)=72\). Now divide top and bottom by \(72\). We get \(360\div 72 = 5\) and \(504\div 72 = 7\). Since \(5\) and \(7\) share no common factor, the fraction is fully reduced. \(\tfrac{360}{504}=\tfrac{5}{7}\), so the answer is \(\boxed{5/7}\).
Review 3. Compute \(\tfrac{8}{21}\times\tfrac{7}{12}\). Cancel shared factors across tops and bottoms before multiplying. Write the product as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
Write the product as one big fraction, \(\tfrac{8 \times 7}{21 \times 12}\), and hunt for shared factors before you multiply anything out. The \(7\) on top and the \(21\) on the bottom share a factor of \(7\), since \(21 = 3 \times 7\). Cancelling that turns the \(7\) into \(1\) and the \(21\) into \(3\). Next, the \(8\) on top and the \(12\) on the bottom share a factor of \(4\), since \(8 = 4 \times 2\) and \(12 = 4 \times 3\). Cancelling that leaves a \(2\) on top and a \(3\) on the bottom. Now the leftovers are \(\tfrac{2 \times 1}{3 \times 3} = \tfrac{2}{9}\). Because every common factor was already removed, the fraction is in lowest terms with nothing left to simplify, so the product is \(\boxed{2/9}\).
Review 4. Divide \(\tfrac{15}{16}\div\tfrac{25}{8}\). Flip the divisor, cancel, then multiply. Write the quotient as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
Dividing by a fraction means multiplying by its reciprocal, so flip the divisor. \(\tfrac{15}{16}\div\tfrac{25}{8}=\tfrac{15}{16}\times\tfrac{8}{25}\). Now cancel across before multiplying. The \(8\) on top and the \(16\) on the bottom share a factor of \(8\), leaving \(1\) on top and \(2\) on the bottom. The \(15\) on top and the \(25\) on the bottom share a factor of \(5\), leaving \(3\) on top and \(5\) on the bottom. That turns the product into \(\tfrac{3}{2}\times\tfrac{1}{5}=\tfrac{3\times 1}{2\times 5}=\tfrac{3}{10}\). Since \(3\) and \(10\) share no common factor other than \(1\), this is already in simplest form. So \(\tfrac{a}{b}=\boxed{\tfrac{3}{10}}\).
Review 5. \(\tfrac{2310}{2730}\): top is \(2\times 3\times 5\times 7\times 11\), bottom is \(2\times 3\times 5\times 7\times 13\). Cancel shared primes. Write the reduced fraction as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
Stacking the two factorizations makes the shared primes easy to spot. The top is \(2\times 3\times 5\times 7\times 11\) and the bottom is \(2\times 3\times 5\times 7\times 13\). Every prime in the bottom except \(13\) also shows up in the top, so the part the two numbers have in common is \(2\times 3\times 5\times 7=210\). That common chunk is exactly the \(\gcd\), and dividing it out of both numbers cancels it cleanly. On top, \(2\times 3\times 5\times 7\) cancels and only \(11\) survives. On the bottom, the same four primes cancel and only \(13\) survives. So the whole fraction collapses to \(\tfrac{11}{13}\). Since \(11\) and \(13\) are both prime and different, they share no factors, which tells us this is already in lowest terms. $$\frac{2310}{2730}=\frac{2\times 3\times 5\times 7\times 11}{2\times 3\times 5\times 7\times 13}=\boxed{\frac{11}{13}}$$
Review 6. \(\tfrac{2}{3}\times\tfrac{3}{4}\times\tfrac{4}{5}\times\tfrac{5}{6}\times\tfrac{6}{7}\). Cancel matching pairs. Write the result as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
Multiply across the tops and across the bottoms, but keep the factors lined up so the pattern shows. $$\frac{2\times 3\times 4\times 5\times 6}{3\times 4\times 5\times 6\times 7}$$ The bottom carries a \(3,4,5,6\), and so does the top. Each of those pairs cancels to \(1\), since a number over itself is \(1\). What never gets a partner is the \(2\) at the very front of the top and the \(7\) at the very end of the bottom. Everything between them folds away, leaving $$\frac{2}{7}.$$ The fraction \(\tfrac{2}{7}\) is already in simplest form, because \(2\) and \(7\) share no common factor bigger than \(1\). So the entire chain collapses to \(\boxed{2/7}\).
Review 7. Simplify \(\dfrac{m^5}{m^2\times 4}\): cancel \(m\)s, leaving \(\dfrac{m^3}{4}\). Set \(m=2\). What single number does the expression equal?
Show solution
Cancelling shared factors works for letters exactly like it works for numbers. The top is five copies of \(m\) multiplied together, and the bottom is two copies of \(m\) times \(4\). Each \(m\) on the bottom cancels one \(m\) on top. Two of the five top copies pair off and vanish, so three copies of \(m\) survive on top and only the \(4\) is left underneath. That gives \(\dfrac{m^3}{4}\). Now set \(m=2\). The top becomes \(2\times 2\times 2=8\), so the fraction is \(\dfrac{8}{4}=2\). The whole expression equals \(\boxed{2}\).
Review 8. A measuring strip must mark both \(\tfrac{3}{8}\) in and \(\tfrac{1}{10}\) in tick marks. Find the smallest shared bottom: \(\operatorname{lcm}(8,10)\). What is that least common denominator?
Show solution
The shared bottom has to be a number that both \(8\) and \(10\) divide into cleanly, and we want the smallest one so the strip is not cut finer than it needs to be. That is the \(\operatorname{lcm}\) of \(8\) and \(10\). Break each into primes, \(8 = 2\times 2\times 2\) and \(10 = 2\times 5\). The least common multiple takes the most of each prime that appears in either number, which is three \(2\)s and one \(5\), so $$\operatorname{lcm}(8,10) = 2\times 2\times 2\times 5 = 40.$$ Check that both bottoms fit, \(40 \div 8 = 5\) and \(40 \div 10 = 4\), both whole, so a strip ruled into \(40\)ths shows \(\tfrac{3}{8} = \tfrac{15}{40}\) and \(\tfrac{1}{10} = \tfrac{4}{40}\) exactly. The least common denominator is \(\boxed{40}\).
Review 9. Three rhythm loops land every \(4\), \(10\), and \(15\) beats. The next beat where all three align is \(\operatorname{lcm}(4,10,15)\). What is that number?
Show solution
Start by writing each number as a product of primes. \(4=2^2\), \(10=2\cdot 5\), and \(15=3\cdot 5\). The least common multiple has to be divisible by all three, so it must include enough copies of each prime to cover whichever number uses it most. The \(4\) demands two \(2\)s, the \(15\) brings in a \(3\), and the \(5\) appears in both \(10\) and \(15\) so we need one \(5\). Putting those together gives \(2^2\cdot 3\cdot 5 = 4\cdot 3\cdot 5 = 60\). A quick check confirms it. \(60\div 4=15\), \(60\div 10=6\), and \(60\div 15=4\), all whole numbers, and nothing smaller works for all three. So the loops realign, and the common denominator is \(\boxed{60}\).
Review 10. Rewrite \(\tfrac{7}{12}\) as an equivalent fraction over \(60\). Find what \(12\) is multiplied by to reach \(60\), then scale the top. What is the new numerator?
Show solution
An equivalent fraction comes from multiplying the top and bottom by the same number, so the fraction keeps its value while wearing a new outfit. Start with the denominators. To turn \(12\) into \(60\), solve \(12 \times ? = 60\), which gives \(60 \div 12 = 5\). So the scaling factor is \(5\). Now apply that same factor to the numerator so the fraction stays equal. \(7 \times 5 = 35\). That means \(\tfrac{7}{12} = \tfrac{35}{60}\), and you can check it since both reduce back to \(\tfrac{7}{12}\). The missing top is \(\boxed{35}\).
Review 11. A track is \(\tfrac{4}{9}\) done. Rewrite over the common denominator \(45\). Find what \(9\) is multiplied by to reach \(45\), then scale the top. What is the new numerator over \(45\)?
Show solution
The denominator changed from \(9\) to \(45\). To find the scaling factor, see what \(9\) was multiplied by. Since \(9 \times 5 = 45\), the bottom was scaled by \(5\). To keep the fraction equal to \(\tfrac{4}{9}\), the top has to grow by the same factor. So \(4 \times 5 = 20\), which gives \(\tfrac{20}{45}\). You can check it. \(\tfrac{20}{45}\) divides back down by \(5\) to \(\tfrac{4}{9}\), so the value never changed. The new numerator is \(\boxed{20}\).
Review 12. Maya finishes \(\tfrac{4}{7}\) of a loop; Theo finishes \(\tfrac{5}{9}\). Grow both to the lcd of \(7\) and \(9\), compare the tops. Which is larger? Write the answer as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
Both fractions have different bottoms, so they cannot be compared at a glance. The fix is to give them a shared denominator. Since \(7\) and \(9\) have no common factor, the least common denominator is \(7 \times 9 = 63\). Now grow each fraction to that denominator. For \(\tfrac{4}{7}\), multiply top and bottom by \(9\), which gives \(\tfrac{4 \times 9}{7 \times 9} = \tfrac{36}{63}\). For \(\tfrac{5}{9}\), multiply top and bottom by \(7\), which gives \(\tfrac{5 \times 7}{9 \times 7} = \tfrac{35}{63}\). With equal denominators the comparison is just the numerators, and \(36 > 35\), so \(\tfrac{36}{63}\) is the larger amount. That came from \(\tfrac{4}{7}\), which means Maya covered more of the loop. Reported in its original form, the larger fraction is \(\boxed{\tfrac{4}{7}}\).
Review 13. A turbine produced \(280\) MWh out of \(630\) regional MWh. Break both into primes, cancel shared factors, and write \(\tfrac{280}{630}\) in simplest form.
Show solution
The share is \(\tfrac{280}{630}\), and the fast road to simplest form is the \(\gcd\). Break both into primes, \(280 = 2\times 2\times 2\times 5\times 7\) and \(630 = 2\times 3\times 3\times 5\times 7\). The primes they both carry are one \(2\), one \(5\), and one \(7\), so $$\gcd(280,630) = 2\times 5\times 7 = 70.$$ Divide top and bottom by that single number, \(280 \div 70 = 4\) and \(630 \div 70 = 9\), which gives \(\tfrac{4}{9}\). Check we are done, \(4 = 2\times 2\) and \(9 = 3\times 3\) share no prime, so the only common factor left is \(1\). The turbine's share in simplest form is \(\boxed{4/9}\).
Review 14. Rope 1 reaches \(\tfrac{11}{15}\) of a wall; Rope 2 reaches \(\tfrac{7}{9}\). The lcd is \(45\). Rename both over \(45\) and compare. Which fraction is larger? Write it as \(\tfrac{a}{b}\).
Show solution
The two fractions have different denominators, so rename them over a shared one before comparing. The least common multiple of \(15\) and \(9\) is \(45\), since \(45 = 15 \times 3 = 9 \times 5\). Grow each fraction to forty-fifths. For the first rope, \(\tfrac{11}{15} = \tfrac{11 \times 3}{15 \times 3} = \tfrac{33}{45}\). For the second, \(\tfrac{7}{9} = \tfrac{7 \times 5}{9 \times 5} = \tfrac{35}{45}\). Now the pieces are the same size, so I just compare numerators. Since \(35 > 33\), the second rope reaches farther, which means \(\tfrac{7}{9}\) is the larger fraction. The larger fraction is \(\boxed{7/9}\).
Review 15. Evaluate \(\tfrac{12}{25}\times\tfrac{35}{18}\div\tfrac{7}{15}\). Flip the last fraction, then cancel every shared factor across all tops and bottoms. What single number unlocks the box?
Show solution
First turn the division into multiplication by flipping the last fraction. Dividing by \(\tfrac{7}{15}\) means multiplying by \(\tfrac{15}{7}\), so the whole expression becomes \[\frac{12}{25}\times\frac{35}{18}\times\frac{15}{7}.\] Now cancel shared factors across all the tops and bottoms before multiplying anything. The \(35\) on top and the \(7\) on the bottom share a \(7\), leaving \(5\) up top. That \(5\) pairs with the \(25\) on the bottom, which becomes \(5\). Next, \(15\) on top and that remaining \(5\) on the bottom leave \(3\) up top. Now look at \(12\) and \(18\), which share a \(6\), becoming \(2\) and \(3\). The \(3\) on the bottom cancels the \(3\) we still had on top. After all the cancelling, the top holds a single \(2\) and the bottom holds a single \(1\). So the product is \(\tfrac{2}{1}\), and the box opens with \(\boxed{2}\).
