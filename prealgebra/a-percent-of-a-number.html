Prealgebra · Lesson 9.2

A Percent of a Number

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Part = percent × whole. The word "of" between a percent and a number means multiply. A jacket at \(25\%\) off, a tip at \(18\%\), a poll where \(60\%\) agree, each is just that one relationship read from a different corner.

A wildlife reserve counts \(200\) nesting birds, and \(25\%\) are herons. How many herons are there?
Show solution
A percent of a number is a multiplication, with "of" as the times sign. First convert the percent, so \(25\% = \tfrac{1}{4} = 0.25\). Then multiply by the whole, $$25\% \text{ of } 200 = \tfrac{1}{4} \times 200 = 50.$$ The same answer comes from \(0.25 \times 200 = 50\). So there are \(\boxed{50}\) herons.
Key idea
"Of" means multiply. To find a percent of a number, turn the percent into a fraction or a decimal and multiply, because the word "of" sitting between a percent and a number always means multiply. This is just part \(=\) percent \(\times\) whole, with the percent and the whole known and the part waiting to be found.
A bakery sold \(35\) loaves, and \(60\%\) were sourdough. How many sourdough loaves were sold?
Show solution
Convert and multiply. Since \(60\% = \tfrac{3}{5} = 0.6\), $$60\% \text{ of } 35 = \tfrac{3}{5} \times 35 = 3 \times 7 = 21.$$ So \(\boxed{21}\) sourdough loaves were sold.
A startup had \(15\) employees and grew to \(300\%\) of that size. How many employees are there now?
Show solution
Convert the percent, \(300\% = \tfrac{300}{100} = 3\). Then $$300\% \text{ of } 15 = 3 \times 15 = 45.$$ Notice the answer is larger than the original \(15\), which makes sense because a percent above \(100\) gives more than the whole. So there are \(\boxed{45}\) employees.
Key idea
Size of the percent matters. Taking \(100\%\) of a number leaves it unchanged, taking less than \(100\%\) gives less, and taking more than \(100\%\) gives more, since you are multiplying by exactly \(1\), by less than \(1\), or by more than \(1\). The multiplier's size is a built-in check on whether your answer should grow or shrink.
A theater holds \(64\) seats, and \(12\tfrac{1}{2}\%\) are reserved for staff. Since \(12\tfrac{1}{2}\% = \tfrac{1}{8}\), how many seats are reserved?
Show solution
Convert the fractional percent to a clean fraction, \(12\tfrac{1}{2}\% = \tfrac{12.5}{100} = \tfrac{1}{8}\). Then $$12\tfrac{1}{2}\% \text{ of } 64 = \tfrac{1}{8} \times 64 = 8.$$ So \(\boxed{8}\) seats are reserved.
A water sample weighs \(80\) g, and \(2.5\%\) is dissolved minerals. How many grams of minerals are there?
Show solution
Convert the percent to a decimal by sliding the point two places left, \(2.5\% = 0.025\). Then $$2.5\% \text{ of } 80 = 0.025 \times 80 = 2.$$ So there are \(\boxed{2}\) grams of dissolved minerals.
A class read \(30\) books out of a goal of \(120\). What percent of the goal did they reach? Give just the number.
Show solution
To find what percent one number is of another, divide the part by the whole, then write it as a percent. $$\tfrac{30}{120} = \tfrac{1}{4} = 0.25 = 25\%.$$ So \(30\) is \(\boxed{25}\) percent of \(120\).
Key idea
Percent \(=\) part \(\div\) whole. To find what percent one number is of another, divide the part by the whole and write the answer as a percent. This is the same relationship read from a different corner, since a percent is just the part measured against the whole and then scaled to one hundred.
A phone battery holds \(80\) units full and \(60\) units now. What percent of \(80\) is \(60\)? Give just the number.
Show solution
Divide the part by the whole, then convert to a percent. $$\tfrac{60}{80} = \tfrac{3}{4} = 0.75 = 75\%.$$ So \(60\) is \(\boxed{75}\) percent of \(80\).
A shop bought a desk for \(\$17\) and resold it for \(\$51\). What percent is \(51\) of \(17\)? Give just the number.
Show solution
Divide the part by the whole, \(\tfrac{51}{17} = 3\). As a percent that is \(3 = 300\%\). The resale price is three times the cost, so \(51\) is \(\boxed{300}\) percent of \(17\).
A scholarship covers \(\$80\) of a fee, and that is exactly \(20\%\) of the full fee. \(80\) is \(20\%\) of what number?
Show solution
Since the part is the percent times the whole, the whole is the part divided by the percent. With \(20\% = 0.2\), $$\text{whole} = \frac{80}{0.2} = 400.$$ Check it, \(20\%\) of \(400\) is \(0.2 \times 400 = 80\), which matches. So \(80\) is \(20\%\) of \(\boxed{400}\).
Key idea
Whole \(=\) part \(\div\) percent. When you know the part and the percent but not the whole, divide the part by the percent, written as a fraction or decimal, to recover the whole. This is the reverse of multiplying, so always check by taking that percent of your answer to see if it lands back on the part.
A hiker has walked \(27\) km, which is \(75\%\) of the full trail. \(27\) is \(75\%\) of what number?
Show solution
Divide the part by the percent. With \(75\% = \tfrac{3}{4}\), $$\text{whole} = 27 \div \tfrac{3}{4} = 27 \times \tfrac{4}{3} = 36.$$ Check, \(\tfrac{3}{4} \times 36 = 27\), which matches. The trail is \(\boxed{36}\) kilometers long.
A startup's revenue this month is \(\$18\)k, which is \(150\%\) of last month's. \(18\) is \(150\%\) of what number?
Show solution
Divide the part by the percent, with \(150\% = 1.5\). $$\text{whole} = \frac{18}{1.5} = 12.$$ Because \(150\%\) is more than \(100\%\), the answer \(12\) is smaller than the part \(18\), which fits. Check, \(1.5 \times 12 = 18\). So last month's revenue was \(\boxed{12}\) thousand dollars.
Fun fact
Ancient Rome funded its army with a \(1\%\) levy on public auctions. Counting in hundredths made tax rates easy to compare and collect, the same reason a percent is handy today.
A museum finds \(30\%\) of \(80\) visitors came by bus. What percent of \(120\) school visitors is that same bus count? Give just the number.
Show solution
Do it in two steps. First the part, \(30\%\) of \(80 = 0.3 \times 80 = 24\). Then ask what percent \(24\) is of \(120\), $$\tfrac{24}{120} = \tfrac{1}{5} = 0.2 = 20\%.$$ So \(30\%\) of \(80\) is \(\boxed{20}\) percent of \(120\).

Review problems

Review 1. A farmers market had \(90\) stalls, and \(40\%\) sold vegetables. How many stalls sold vegetables?
Show solution
\(40\% = 0.4\), so \(40\% \text{ of } 90 = 0.4 \times 90 = 36\). So \(\boxed{36}\) stalls sold vegetables.
Review 2. A song had \(12\) thousand listeners last week and climbed to \(250\%\) of that this week. How many thousand listeners this week?
Show solution
\(250\% = \tfrac{250}{100} = 2.5\), so \(250\% \text{ of } 12 = 2.5 \times 12 = 30\). So there are \(\boxed{30}\) thousand listeners.
Review 3. A puzzle has \(16\) pieces, and \(37\tfrac{1}{2}\%\) are edge pieces. How many edge pieces are there?
Show solution
\(37\tfrac{1}{2}\% = \tfrac{37.5}{100} = \tfrac{3}{8}\), so \(\tfrac{3}{8} \times 16 = 6\). So there are \(\boxed{6}\) edge pieces.
Review 4. A reservoir holds \(150\) million liters, and \(8\%\) is in reserve. How many million liters are in reserve?
Show solution
\(8\% = 0.08\), so \(8\% \text{ of } 150 = 0.08 \times 150 = 12\). So \(\boxed{12}\) million liters are in reserve.
Review 5. A team won \(18\) of its \(72\) games. What percent did the team win? Give just the number.
Show solution
\(\tfrac{18}{72} = \tfrac{1}{4} = 0.25 = 25\%\). So the team won \(\boxed{25}\) percent of its games.
Review 6. A jar holds \(40\) marbles and \(30\) are blue. What percent of \(40\) is \(30\)? Give just the number.
Show solution
\(\tfrac{30}{40} = \tfrac{3}{4} = 0.75 = 75\%\). So \(30\) is \(\boxed{75}\) percent of \(40\).
Review 7. A sequel sold \(84\) thousand copies; the original sold \(56\) thousand. What percent is \(84\) of \(56\)? Give just the number.
Show solution
\(\tfrac{84}{56} = \tfrac{3}{2} = 1.5 = 150\%\). So \(84\) is \(\boxed{150}\) percent of \(56\).
Review 8. A deposit of \(\$21\) is \(30\%\) of a bike's total price. \(21\) is \(30\%\) of what number?
Show solution
Whole equals part divided by percent, with \(30\% = 0.3\), so \(\tfrac{21}{0.3} = 70\). Check, \(0.3 \times 70 = 21\). So the price is \(\boxed{70}\) dollars.
Review 9. A runner has finished \(45\) laps, which is \(90\%\) of the race. \(45\) is \(90\%\) of what number?
Show solution
Whole equals part divided by percent, with \(90\% = 0.9\), so \(\tfrac{45}{0.9} = 50\). Check, \(0.9 \times 50 = 45\). So the race is \(\boxed{50}\) laps.
Review 10. This year a town recorded \(120\) building permits, which is \(150\%\) of last year's. \(120\) is \(150\%\) of what number?
Show solution
Whole equals part divided by percent, with \(150\% = 1.5\), so \(\tfrac{120}{1.5} = 80\). Check, \(1.5 \times 80 = 120\). So last year had \(\boxed{80}\) permits.
Review 11. A tasting portion is \(5\) g, which is \(12\tfrac{1}{2}\%\) of a full serving. \(5\) is \(12\tfrac{1}{2}\%\) of what number?
Show solution
Convert the percent, \(12\tfrac{1}{2}\% = \tfrac{1}{8}\). Whole equals part divided by percent, so \(5 \div \tfrac{1}{8} = 5 \times 8 = 40\). Check, \(\tfrac{1}{8} \times 40 = 5\). So a full serving is \(\boxed{40}\) grams.
Review 12. Chain two steps: take \(60\%\) of \(500\), then take \(20\%\) of that result. What is \(20\%\) of \(60\%\) of \(500\)?
Show solution
Work from the inside out. First \(60\%\) of \(500 = 0.6 \times 500 = 300\). Then \(20\%\) of \(300 = 0.2 \times 300 = 60\). So \(20\%\) of \(60\%\) of \(500\) is \(\boxed{60}\).
