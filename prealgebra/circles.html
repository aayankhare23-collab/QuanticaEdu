Prealgebra · Lesson 11.7

Circles

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Every shape so far has had straight sides you could count and add. A circle has none. Yet it still has a length running all the way around it and a flat space filling its inside, a perimeter and an area, just like the others. Measuring them turns up one famous number, \(\pi\), that hides inside every circle ever drawn. Once you have hold of it, you can find the distance around and the space within for any circle at all.

Key idea
Radius and diameter. Every point of a circle sits the same distance from the center, and that distance is the radius. A straight line all the way across through the center is the diameter, and since it is just two radii laid end to end, the diameter is twice the radius, \(d = 2r\) (so \(r = \frac{d}{2}\)). The figure above marks both.
In the diagram above is a circle whose radius is \(8\). Find its diameter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The diameter is twice the radius, \(d = 2r\). The diagram above marks the radius as \(8\), so $$d = 2 \times 8 = 16.$$ The diameter is \(\boxed{16}\).
Key idea
Circumference and \(\pi\). Roll any circle along a line and let it make one full turn. The distance it covers is its circumference, and that distance is always the same multiple of the diameter, no matter how big or small the circle is. That multiple is the number \(\pi\) (pi), about \(3.14\), and it is the same for every circle in the world. So the circumference is \(C = \pi d\), and since the diameter is twice the radius, \(d = 2r\), we can also write \(C = 2\pi r\). When \(r\) is a whole number the circumference comes out as a whole number times \(\pi\), like \(14\pi\) or \(20\pi\). In this course we leave the answer in that form and just give the whole number in front of \(\pi\). So a circumference of \(14\pi\) is answered \(14\). Do not multiply out by \(3.14\). The figure above unrolls one circle into a straight line of length \(\pi d\).
In the diagram above is a circle with diameter \(10\). Its circumference is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The circumference is pi times the diameter. Read the diameter straight off the diagram above, it is \(10\), and plug it in. $$C = \pi d = \pi \times 10 = 10\pi$$ So the circumference is \(10\pi\), and the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{10}\).
In the diagram above is a circle with radius \(7\). Its circumference is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The circumference of a circle is \(C = 2\pi r\). The radius in the diagram above is \(7\), so $$C = 2 \times 7 \times \pi = 14\pi.$$ That is a whole number times \(\pi\), and the whole number in front is \(\boxed{14}\).
Key idea
Area of a circle. Slice a circle into many thin wedges and lay them out in a row, alternating point up and point down. They line up into a shape very close to a parallelogram whose base is half the way around the circle, \(\pi r\), and whose height is the radius \(r\). Its area is base times height, \(\pi r \times r\), so the area of a circle is \(A = \pi r^2\). Just like the circumference, this comes out as a whole number times \(\pi\), and you give that whole number, so an area of \(36\pi\) is answered \(36\). The figure above shows the wedges rearranged.
In the diagram above is a circle with radius \(6\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The area of a circle is \(A = \pi r^2\). Read the radius \(6\) off the diagram above and square it. $$A = \pi \times 6^2 = 36\pi$$ The area is \(36\pi\), so the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{36}\).
In the diagram above is a circle with diameter \(14\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The area formula needs the radius, so start from the diameter. Since \(d = 2r\), the radius is \(r = \frac{14}{2} = 7\). Now apply the area of a circle, \(A = \pi r^2\). $$A = \pi \cdot 7^2 = 49\pi.$$ The area is \(49\pi\), so the whole number is \(\boxed{49}\).
Key idea
Working backward. The circle formulas run in reverse too. If you know a circumference or an area written as a whole number times \(\pi\), you can recover the radius. Set the formula equal to what you are given, then cancel the \(\pi\) that sits on both sides. For a circumference, \(2\pi r = 20\pi\) becomes \(2r = 20\), so \(r = 10\). For an area, \(\pi r^2 = 49\pi\) becomes \(r^2 = 49\), so \(r = 7\). Undo the doubling for a circumference, or take the square root for an area.
In the diagram above is a circle whose circumference is \(26\pi\), with the radius marked by a question mark. Find the radius. Give the number of units.
Show solution
This is a reverse problem, so we work backward from the circumference. The rule is circumference equals \(2\pi r\). We know the circumference is \(26\pi\), so $$2\pi r = 26\pi.$$ Cancel the \(\pi\) on both sides to get \(2r = 26\), then divide by \(2\) to find \(r = 13\). The radius is \(\boxed{13}\).
In the diagram above is a circle whose area is \(64\pi\). Find the radius. Give the number of units.
Show solution
This is a reverse problem, so we start from the area formula and work back to the radius. The area is \(A = \pi r^2\), and here it equals \(64\pi\), so $$\pi r^2 = 64\pi.$$ Cancel the \(\pi\) on both sides to get \(r^2 = 64\). The number that squares to \(64\) is \(8\), so \(r = \boxed{8}\).
Key idea
Half circles, quarter circles, and rings. You do not need a new formula for a slice of a circle. Whatever piece you take keeps the same \(\pi r^2\), scaled by how much of the whole circle it is. A semicircle is half a circle, so its area is \(\frac{1}{2}\pi r^2\). A quarter circle is a fourth, so its area is \(\frac{1}{4}\pi r^2\). The figure above shows one of each, a half and a quarter, cut from circles of the same radius. A ring is different. It is the flat band left between an outer circle of radius \(R\) and an inner circle of radius \(r\), and you get it by taking the big circle and removing the small one, \(\pi R^2 - \pi r^2\). In every case the whole number in front of \(\pi\) is still your answer, so a semicircle of area \(8\pi\) is answered \(8\) and a ring of area \(21\pi\) is answered \(21\).
In the diagram above is a semicircle with radius \(8\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
A semicircle is half a circle, so its area is half of the full circle's \(\pi r^2\). With radius \(8\), $$A = \frac{1}{2}\pi r^2 = \frac{1}{2}\times \pi \times 64 = 32\pi.$$ We leave the answer in terms of \(\pi\), so the area is \(32\pi\) and the whole number is \(\boxed{32}\).
In the diagram above is a quarter circle with radius \(10\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
A quarter circle is one fourth of a full circle, so its area is \(\frac{1}{4}\pi r^2\). With \(r = 10\), $$A = \frac{1}{4}\pi r^2 = \frac{1}{4}\times \pi \times 100 = 25\pi.$$ The area is \(25\pi\), so the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{25}\).
In the diagram above is a ring, the shaded space between an outer circle of radius \(10\) and an inner circle of radius \(6\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
A ring is the big circle with the small circle removed, so its area is the difference of the two areas. The big circle has area \(\pi \times 10^2 = 100\pi\) and the small circle has area \(\pi \times 6^2 = 36\pi\). Subtract to get what's left in the ring. $$100\pi - 36\pi = 64\pi$$ The area is \(64\pi\), so the whole number is \(\boxed{64}\).
Key idea
When the radius grows. Area depends on the radius squared, \(A = \pi r^2\), so when you grow the radius by some factor, the area grows by the square of that factor. Double the radius and the area does not double, it becomes \(2^2 = 4\) times as big. Triple the radius and the area becomes \(3^2 = 9\) times as big. Scale the radius by \(10\) and the area jumps by \(100\). This is why a pizza with twice the radius is four times as much pizza, not twice. The squaring is doing the heavy lifting.
In the diagram above, the small circular garden has radius \(10\) and needs \(3000\) seeds to fill it. The larger garden has three times that radius. How many seeds does it need to fill? Give the number of seeds.
Show solution
Seeds needed match the area, and the area of a circle depends on the radius squared. Tripling the radius multiplies the area by $$3^2 = 9.$$ So the larger garden needs \(9\) times as many seeds as the small one, $$9 \times 3000 = 27000.$$ The larger garden needs \(\boxed{27000}\) seeds.
In the diagram above are two pizzas, a small one of radius \(6\) and a large one of radius \(12\). How many times as much pizza (area) is the large one? Give the number.
Show solution
The area of a circle is \(A = \pi r^2\), so area depends on the radius squared. Going from radius \(6\) to radius \(12\), the radius doubles, and squaring that scale factor gives $$2^2 = 4.$$ You can check it directly. The small pizza has area \(\pi \times 6^2 = 36\pi\) and the large one has \(\pi \times 12^2 = 144\pi\), and \(144\pi\) is \(4\) times \(36\pi\). So the large pizza is \(\boxed{4}\) times as much.
Fun fact
The number \(\pi\) never ends and never falls into a repeating pattern. Its digits run on forever with no rhythm, which makes it an irrational number, and computers have now chased it out to trillions of places past the decimal point. It is famous enough to have its own holiday. Every March 14, written 3/14 in the United States, is celebrated as Pi Day, and a few devoted fans memorize thousands of its digits just for the fun of it.
In the diagram above is a dartboard of three circles with radii \(4\), \(6\), and \(9\). Find the area of the outer ring, the shaded space between the radius-\(6\) circle and the radius-\(9\) circle. That area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The outer ring is the space left when you take the radius-\(9\) circle and remove the radius-\(6\) circle from inside it. So its area is outer area minus inner area, and the radius-\(4\) circle plays no part. The big circle has area \(\pi \times 9^2 = 81\pi\), and the circle we remove has area \(\pi \times 6^2 = 36\pi\). Subtracting, $$81\pi - 36\pi = 45\pi.$$ The ring's area is \(45\pi\), so the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{45}\).
Key idea
The circle toolkit. Everything about a circle grows out of one length. A diameter is twice the radius, \(d = 2r\), so if you know one you know the other. From the radius come the two big formulas. The circumference, the distance around, is \(C = \pi d = 2\pi r\), and the area, the space inside, is \(A = \pi r^2\), where \(\pi\) is about \(3.14\) and is the same number for every circle. Both of these land as a whole number times \(\pi\), and that is exactly what we leave in place. A circumference of \(14\pi\) is answered \(14\), an area of \(36\pi\) is answered \(36\). No multiplying out by \(3.14\). Run the same formulas backward, matching the whole number sitting in front of \(\pi\), and you can recover a radius from a circumference or from an area. The pieces work the same way. A semicircle is half a circle, \(\frac{1}{2}\pi r^2\), a quarter circle is a fourth, \(\frac{1}{4}\pi r^2\), and a ring between an outer radius \(R\) and an inner radius \(r\) is the big area minus the small one, \(\pi R^2 - \pi r^2\). One last thing worth remembering. Because the area uses the radius squared, doubling the radius does not double the area, it quadruples it.

Review problems

Review 1.
In the diagram above is a circle with radius \(11\). Its diameter is a length you can read off. Find the diameter. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The diameter runs all the way across a circle, and it is exactly twice the radius, so \(d = 2r\). Here the radius is \(11\), so $$d = 2 \times 11 = 22.$$ The diameter is \(\boxed{22}\).
Review 2.
In the diagram above is a circle with diameter \(8\). Its circumference is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The circumference is the diameter times \(\pi\). Read the diameter off the diagram above, \(d = 8\), and use $$C = \pi d = 8\pi.$$ So the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{8}\).
Review 3.
In the diagram above is a circle with radius \(9\). Its circumference is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The circumference of a circle is \(C = 2\pi r\). Here the radius is \(9\), so $$C = 2\pi \times 9 = 18\pi.$$ Leaving the answer in terms of \(\pi\), the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{18}\).
Review 4.
In the diagram above is a circle with radius \(5\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The area of a circle is \(A = \pi r^2\). Read the radius \(5\) off the diagram above and square it. $$A = \pi \times 5^2 = 25\pi$$ The area is \(25\pi\), so the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{25}\).
Review 5.
In the diagram above is a circle with diameter \(20\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
The diameter is \(20\), and the radius is half of that, so \(r = 10\). Area is \(\pi r^2\), so $$A = \pi \times 10^2 = 100\pi.$$ We leave the answer in terms of \(\pi\) and give just the whole number in front, so the answer is \(\boxed{100}\).
Review 6.
In the diagram above is a circle whose circumference is \(30\pi\). Find its radius. Give the number of units.
Show solution
This runs the circumference formula backward. The circumference is \(2\pi r\), and here it equals \(30\pi\), so $$2\pi r = 30\pi.$$ Divide both sides by \(2\pi\) to get \(r = 15\). The radius is \(\boxed{15}\).
Review 7.
In the diagram above is a circle whose area is \(49\pi\). Find its radius. Give the number of units.
Show solution
The area of a circle is \(\pi r^2\). Here that equals \(49\pi\), so $$\pi r^2 = 49\pi.$$ Divide both sides by \(\pi\) to get \(r^2 = 49\). The number that squares to \(49\) is \(7\), so the radius is \(\boxed{7}\).
Review 8.
In the diagram above is a semicircle with radius \(10\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
A semicircle is half a full circle, so its area is half of \(\pi r^2\). With \(r = 10\) the radius squared is \(r^2 = 100\), so $$A = \frac{1}{2}\pi r^2 = \frac{1}{2}\times 100\pi = 50\pi.$$ The area is \(50\pi\), so the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{50}\).
Review 9.
In the diagram above is a quarter circle with radius \(8\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
A quarter circle covers a fourth of the whole circle, so its area is \(\frac{1}{4}\pi r^2\). Reading the radius off the diagram above, \(r = 8\), so \(r^2 = 64\) and $$A = \frac{1}{4}\pi r^2 = \frac{1}{4}\times 64\pi = 16\pi.$$ We leave the answer in terms of \(\pi\), so the whole number in front of \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{16}\).
Review 10.
In the diagram above is a ring between an outer circle of radius \(13\) and an inner circle of radius \(5\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
A ring is the big disk with the small disk punched out, so its area is the outer area minus the inner area. Using \(A = \pi r^2\) for each, $$\pi \times 13^2 - \pi \times 5^2 = 169\pi - 25\pi = 144\pi.$$ The area is \(144\pi\), so the whole number multiplying \(\pi\) is \(\boxed{144}\).
Review 11.
In the diagram above are two circles, one with radius \(5\) and one with radius \(10\). The radius of the larger circle is twice the radius of the smaller. How many times as big as the smaller circle's area is the larger circle's area? Give the number.
Show solution
Area depends on the radius squared, \(A = \pi r^2\). The small circle has area \(\pi \cdot 5^2 = 25\pi\) and the large one has area \(\pi \cdot 10^2 = 100\pi\), and \(100\pi\) is \(4\) times \(25\pi\). Doubling the radius doesn't double the area, it multiplies it by \(2^2\).$$2^2 = \boxed{4}$$
Review 12.
In the diagram above, a small circular flower bed of radius \(7\) needs \(150\) bags of soil to fill it. The larger bed beside it has twice the radius. How many bags of soil does the larger bed need? Give the number of bags.
Show solution
The area of a circle is \(\pi r^2\), so it grows with the square of the radius. Doubling the radius multiplies the area by \(2^2 = 4\). The larger bed covers \(4\) times the area, so it needs \(4\) times the soil. $$4 \times 150 = 600$$ The larger bed needs \(\boxed{600}\) bags.
Review 13.
In the diagram above is a dartboard of three circles with radii \(5\), \(8\), and \(12\). Find the area of the outer ring, the region between the circle of radius \(8\) and the circle of radius \(12\). Its area is a whole number times \(\pi\). Find that whole number. Give the whole number multiplying \(\pi\).
Show solution
A ring is one circle with a smaller one taken out of it, so its area is the big area minus the small area, \(\pi R^2 - \pi r^2\). The outer edge is at radius \(12\) and the inner edge is at radius \(8\), so $$\pi \times 12^2 - \pi \times 8^2 = 144\pi - 64\pi = 80\pi.$$ The area is \(80\pi\), so the whole number is \(\boxed{80}\).
