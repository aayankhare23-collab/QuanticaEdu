Prealgebra · Lesson 5.3

Switching Between Fractions and Decimals

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

\(\tfrac{3}{4}\) and \(0.75\) are two names for one number. This lesson covers switching between them in both directions, and reveals what decides whether a fraction produces a stopping decimal or one that runs forever.

A trail sign reads \(0.7\) km to the next lookout. The ranger's logbook only accepts fractions over a power of ten. Write \(0.7\) as a fraction.
Show solution
Read the decimal out loud the way you would say it and the fraction almost writes itself. You would say "seven tenths," and "tenths" is already the denominator while "seven" is already the numerator. Here is why the place does all the work for you. The point splits whole units from pieces of a unit, and each step to the right cuts the piece into ten times as many parts. The very first slot past the point holds tenths, so a digit sitting there counts how many tenths you have. In \(0.7\) that digit is \(7\), which means you have \(7\) copies of one tenth. That is exactly $$0.7 = \frac{7}{10}.$$ Notice there was no division and no whole part to carry along, since the sign showed less than one kilometer. The single filled place named the denominator, \(10\), and the digit after the point became the numerator, \(7\). So the lookout is \(\boxed{7/10}\) of a kilometer ahead.
A ribbon measures \(0.45\) m. Start with \(\frac{45}{100}\), find \(\gcd(45,100)\), and reduce. Write \(0.45\) as a fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Two digits live past the decimal point, so the last place is hundredths and the denominator is \(100\). Reading the digits as a whole number puts \(45\) on top, giving the honest first version \(\frac{45}{100}\). It is true, just loose. \nTo tighten it, find what \(45\) and \(100\) have in common. Break each one into its prime pieces. \(45 = 3 \times 3 \times 5\) and \(100 = 2 \times 2 \times 5 \times 5\). Lining those up, the only piece showing in both lists is a single \(5\), so the greatest common divisor is \(\gcd(45,100) = 5\). That \(5\) is the biggest cut you can make in one step. \nDivide top and bottom by it together. $$\frac{45}{100} = \frac{45 \div 5}{100 \div 5} = \frac{9}{20}$$ Now check that nothing is left to remove. The top is \(9 = 3 \times 3\) and the bottom is \(20 = 2 \times 2 \times 5\). They share no prime, so the fraction is pulled all the way tight. If you want proof it is still the same length of ribbon, notice \(\frac{9}{20} = \frac{45}{100} = 0.45\), right back where you started. \(\boxed{9/20}\)
Key idea
Decimal-to-fraction shortcut. A terminating decimal is already a fraction, so you never have to divide to turn it into one. The place of the last digit hands you the denominator (tenths→\(10\), hundredths→\(100\), thousandths→\(1000\)), and every digit after the point, read straight across as one number, becomes the numerator. That just sets the fraction down, and then you tidy it the Chapter 4 way by dividing top and bottom by their gcd. So \(0.7\) is already \(\tfrac{7}{10}\), while \(0.45=\tfrac{45}{100}=\tfrac{9}{20}\).
Write \(2.5\) as a single improper fraction in simplest form. Read all digits as \(\tfrac{25}{10}\), then reduce.
Show solution
The cleanest route is to read every figure in \(2.5\) at once and forget that one of them lives in front of the point. The digits are \(25\), and exactly one digit sits to the right of the decimal point, so the denominator is \(10^1 = 10\). That gives \(2.5 = \tfrac{25}{10}\). The whole part out front did not change the rule, the count of digits past the point is still what sets the power of ten underneath. If you prefer the other reading, two and five tenths is \(2 + \tfrac{5}{10} = \tfrac{20}{10} + \tfrac{5}{10} = \tfrac{25}{10}\), the very same fraction. Now simplify. The greatest common factor of \(25\) and \(10\) is \(5\), so \(\tfrac{25}{10} = \tfrac{25 \div 5}{10 \div 5} = \tfrac{5}{2}\). Since \(5\) is larger than \(2\), this is already an improper fraction, exactly what was asked for. A quick sanity check, \(5 \div 2 = 2.5\), back to where we started. $$2.5 = \frac{25}{10} = \boxed{5/2}$$
Write \(-1.72\) as a single improper fraction in simplest form, keeping the sign. Convert the size \(1.72\) to \(\tfrac{172}{100}\), reduce using \(\gcd(172,100)=4\), then reattach the minus sign.
Show solution
Handle the sign and the size separately. The negative sign is not a number to be simplified, it is just a label saying which direction the temperature moved, so park it and convert the size \(1.72\) by itself. \nThe digit \(2\) is the last digit and it lives in the hundredths place, so \(1.72\) is one hundred seventy-two hundredths. As a fraction that is \(\tfrac{172}{100}\). \nNow reduce. The greatest common divisor of \(172\) and \(100\) is \(4\), so divide top and bottom by \(4\): $$\frac{172}{100}=\frac{172\div 4}{100\div 4}=\frac{43}{25}.$$ Check that this is fully reduced. The numerator \(43\) is prime, and \(25=5\cdot 5\) shares no factor with it, so \(\tfrac{43}{25}\) cannot shrink any further. \nFinally, put the sign back exactly where it was. The size came out to \(\tfrac{43}{25}\), and the temperature dropped, so the answer is negative. \(\boxed{-43/25}\)
A depth sensor reports \(\frac{47}{100}\) m. The denominator is already \(100\). Drop the digits \(47\) into the tenths and hundredths places. Write \(\frac{47}{100}\) as a decimal.
Show solution
Remember from earlier that \(\frac{47}{100}\) just means \(47\) copies of one hundredth, and one hundredth is exactly what the second place after the decimal point measures. So the whole job is to write \(47\) hundredths in place-value form. The numerator \(47\) has two digits, and the denominator \(100\) carries two zeros. That match is the whole trick. The two zeros tell you to reserve two slots to the right of the decimal point, the tenths and the hundredths, and the numerator fills them right to left. The \(7\) drops into the hundredths place and the \(4\) sits to its left in the tenths place. Nothing is left over for the ones place, since \(47\) is less than \(100\), so the number is smaller than one whole. We park a \(0\) in the ones place as a guard, just so the decimal point is not floating alone at the front. That gives \(0.47\). This is exactly the chart you built before, read backward. Going from a decimal to a fraction, you saw \(0.47\) and named it \(\frac{47}{100}\). Going from a fraction to a decimal, you see \(\frac{47}{100}\) and name it \(0.47\). Same chart, opposite direction. $$\frac{47}{100} = \boxed{0.47}$$
Write \(\tfrac{3}{5}\) as a decimal by multiplying numerator and denominator by \(2\) so the bottom becomes \(10\). Read off the decimal.
Show solution
The whole game is getting a power of ten under the fraction so the decimal can be read directly. Right now the denominator is \(5\), and \(5\) is one short multiplication away from \(10\), since \(5 \times 2 = 10\). So multiply the bottom by \(2\). The catch is that touching only the bottom would change the value, so multiply the top by \(2\) as well. Doing the same thing top and bottom is just multiplying by \(\tfrac{2}{2}\), and \(\tfrac{2}{2} = 1\), so the value is untouched. This is the equivalent-fraction move from Chapter 4. $$\frac{3}{5} = \frac{3 \times 2}{5 \times 2} = \frac{6}{10}$$ Now the denominator is a power of ten. Six tenths is exactly what the first place after the decimal point holds, so you read it straight off as \(0.6\). The gear turned through \(0.6\) of a revolution. \(\boxed{0.6}\)
Write \(\tfrac{11}{20}\) as a decimal. The denominator \(20\) does not reach \(10\), so aim for the next power of ten it divides into evenly.
Show solution
The goal is to give the denominator a value of \(10\), \(100\), \(1{,}000\), or some other power of ten, because those are exactly the denominators that read off cleanly as decimals. Twenty is not one of them, so the question becomes which power of ten it divides into evenly. It will not fit into \(10\), since \(10\) is smaller than \(20\). The next one up is \(100\), and \(100 \div 20 = 5\) with nothing left over. That is how we knew to reach for \(5\). The bottom has to grow from \(20\) to \(100\), and \(5\) is the number that carries it there. Whatever we do to the bottom we must also do to the top, so multiply both by \(5\). $$\frac{11}{20} = \frac{11 \times 5}{20 \times 5} = \frac{55}{100}$$ Now the denominator is \(100\), so the fraction is \(55\) hundredths, which is \(0.55\). A quick check makes sure that fits. We know \(\tfrac{10}{20} = \tfrac{1}{2} = 0.5\), and \(\tfrac{11}{20}\) is one twentieth more than that, so a value just past \(0.5\) is exactly what we should land on. A little over half the scoop is raisins. \(\boxed{0.55}\)
Key idea
Fraction-to-decimal by scaling. Scale the fraction up until the bottom is a power of ten. Ask what the denominator must be multiplied by to land on \(10\), \(100\), or \(1{,}000\), then multiply the top by that same number so the value never changes. Once the bottom is a power of ten, the decimal is just the numerator read into its matching places, like \(\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{75}{100}=0.75\). Sometimes the bottom is already a power of ten, sometimes it is one short step away.
A wrench is \(\tfrac{7}{8}\) inch wide. Since \(8=2^3\), supply three matching \(5\)s to reach \(2^3 \times 5^3=1{,}000\), then read off the decimal. Write \(\tfrac{7}{8}\) as a decimal.
Show solution
The trick to turning a fraction into a decimal is to get a power of ten on the bottom, because \(\tfrac{875}{1000}\) is just another way of writing \(0.875\). So the whole job is reshaping the denominator into a power of ten without changing the value of the fraction. First break the bottom into primes. Here \(8 = 2\times 2\times 2 = 2^3\), which is three 2s and zero 5s. Now recall how powers of ten are built. Since \(10 = 2\times 5\), every power of ten needs its 2s and 5s in equal counts. For example \(1{,}000 = 10^3 = 2^3\times 5^3\), three of each. Compare that to what you have. Your denominator already supplies all three 2s, so it is short exactly three 5s, and \(5^3 = 125\). Supply the missing 5s by multiplying top and bottom by \(125\), which is the same as multiplying by \(1\), so the value does not change. $$\frac{7}{8} = \frac{7\times 125}{8\times 125} = \frac{875}{1000}.$$ A denominator of \(1{,}000\) means three decimal places, so \(\tfrac{875}{1000}\) reads as \(0.875\). The big idea is that you only ever have to top up whichever prime the bottom is short of. An all-2s denominator like \(8\) cleanly becomes a decimal precisely because you can fill in the matching 5s. \(\boxed{0.875}\)
Key idea
Scaling uses only the missing prime partners. A power of ten is \(2^n \times 5^n\), a stack of \(2\)s matched one for one with the same number of \(5\)s. So scaling the bottom up never invents anything, it only supplies the partners that are missing: \(8\) with its three \(2\)s needs three \(5\)s, while \(40\) already owns one \(5\) and needs two more. The multiplier you reach for is exactly whatever \(2\)s and \(5\)s finish the matched set. A denominator carrying any other prime like \(3\) or \(7\) can never be completed, so that fraction cannot terminate.
Factor each denominator: \(\frac{7}{16}\), \(\frac{9}{40}\), \(\frac{5}{12}\), \(\frac{3}{25}\). Only \(2\)s and \(5\)s allow termination. Which fraction cannot terminate? Give its denominator.
Show solution
Factor every denominator into primes and the answer almost reveals itself. \(16 = 2^4\), so it is pure 2s. \(40 = 2^3 \times 5\), made of 2s and a 5. \(25 = 5^2\), pure 5s. And \(12 = 2^2 \times 3\), which carries a 3. The question is whether each denominator can be scaled up to a power of ten, because a power of ten in the denominator is exactly what a terminating decimal looks like. Since \(10^n = 2^n \times 5^n\), a power of ten is built from 2s and 5s and nothing else. For \(\dfrac{7}{16}\), multiply top and bottom by \(5^4\) to fill in the missing 5s and you reach \(10^4\), so it terminates. For \(\dfrac{9}{40}\), one extra factor of \(5^2\) balances the 2s and 5s into \(1{,}000\). For \(\dfrac{3}{25}\), multiply by \(2^2\) and you land on \(100\). But \(\dfrac{5}{12}\) is stuck. Its denominator holds a 3, and no matter how many 2s or 5s you multiply on, that 3 never disappears, so the denominator can never become a power of ten. The fraction that cannot terminate is \(\dfrac{5}{12}\), and its denominator is \(\boxed{12}\).
Key idea
The termination test. Put the fraction in lowest terms and factor the denominator. If the only primes there are \(2\)s and \(5\)s, you can always feed it the missing \(2\)s or \(5\)s to grow it into a power of ten, and a power of ten is exactly what a terminating decimal is, so those fractions stop. But the moment any other prime sneaks in, a \(3\) or a \(7\) or an \(11\), no amount of \(2\)s and \(5\)s can clear it, no power of ten will ever fit, and the decimal is forced to run forever. 2 and 5 are the gatekeepers of a clean decimal name.
Key idea
One number, many coats. A fraction and its decimal are one number wearing two coats, and the coat that turns one into the other is plain division, since the decimal of \(\tfrac{a}{b}\) is just \(a\) divided by \(b\). Once the fraction is in lowest terms with a bottom built of nothing but \(2\)s and \(5\)s, you can scale it up to a power of ten, which is the exact same reason the division runs out of remainder and stops clean. So scales to a power of ten, bottom is only \(2\)s and \(5\)s, and the division ends are three descriptions of one situation. The two names always point at the identical spot on the number line.
Fun fact
Babylonians counted in base \(60 = 2^2 \times 3 \times 5\), so \(\tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{20}{60}\) was a clean finite number for them. Whether a decimal terminates depends on the base, not the fraction.
Find the reciprocal of \(2.5\) and write it as a decimal. Convert \(2.5\) to a fraction, flip it, then convert back to a decimal.
Show solution
Take the round trip one step at a time. Step 1, decimal to fraction. The number \(2.5\) has one digit after the point, so it is twenty five tenths, \(2.5 = \frac{25}{10}\). Both \(25\) and \(10\) share a factor of \(5\), so divide them out to get the lowest terms version, \(\frac{25}{10} = \frac{5}{2}\). Step 2, flip for the reciprocal. Taking a reciprocal just swaps the top and bottom of a fraction, exactly like Chapter 4. So the reciprocal of \(\frac{5}{2}\) is \(\frac{2}{5}\). Notice how much friendlier this flip is on the fraction than it would be on the messy decimal. Step 3, fraction to decimal. Turn \(\frac{2}{5}\) into tenths by multiplying the top and bottom by \(2\), which gives \(\frac{2}{5} = \frac{4}{10}\), and four tenths written as a decimal is \(0.4\). Look back at the whole journey. You walked in carrying a decimal, slipped into a fraction so the flip was clean, then walked back out as a decimal, a full round trip through both names. As a quick check, \(2.5 \times 0.4 = 1\), which is exactly what a number and its reciprocal must multiply to. \(\boxed{0.4}\)
A board is \(5.75\) feet. The carpenter marks off eighths of a foot. Convert \(5.75\) to a fraction with denominator \(8\). How many eighths of a foot are in \(5.75\)?
Show solution
Asking how many eighths are in a number is just choosing a coat whose bottom is \(8\) and then reading off the top. So the whole job is to dress \(5.75\) as a fraction over \(8\). Start by writing the decimal as a fraction. The \(75\) sits in the hundredths place, so \(5.75 = \frac{575}{100}\). That fraction is bulky, so reduce it. Both \(575\) and \(100\) share a factor of \(25\), and \(575 \div 25 = 23\) while \(100 \div 25 = 4\), which gives \(\frac{23}{4}\). Now rename \(\frac{23}{4}\) so its bottom is \(8\). Since \(4 \times 2 = 8\), multiply the top and bottom by the same \(2\), $$\frac{23}{4} = \frac{23 \times 2}{4 \times 2} = \frac{46}{8}.$$ The bottom is now \(8\), so each piece is one eighth, and the top simply counts how many of them there are. The marks really do land exactly, with \(46\) of them filling the board with nothing left over. There are \(\boxed{46}\) eighths in \(5.75\).
Key idea
Counting units by renaming. When a question asks how many of some unit fit inside a number, like how many eighths or how many fifths, it is really asking you to rewrite that number as a fraction whose bottom is the unit, and then the numerator on top is the count. To hunt for eighths, steer the bottom to \(8\); to hunt for fifths, steer it to \(5\). So knowing both names of a number is not only for converting back and forth, it is a way to count and compare, since you pick the name with the bottom you want and read the answer straight off the top.
A calculator shows exactly \(0.84\). The divisor was less than \(30\) and the division was already in simplest form. Reduce \(\tfrac{84}{100}\) and read off the dividend.
Show solution
Start with what the screen actually says. A reading of \(0.84\) means eighty-four hundredths, so \(0.84 = \tfrac{84}{100}\). That is not the original division yet, because \(84\) and \(100\) still share a factor. Their \(\gcd\) is \(4\), so divide top and bottom by \(4\): $$\frac{84}{100} = \frac{84 \div 4}{100 \div 4} = \frac{21}{25}.$$ Now \(21\) and \(25\) have nothing left in common, so \(\tfrac{21}{25}\) is in lowest terms. The student said their numbers were already in simplest shape before dividing, so the divisor they typed had to reduce to this \(25\). The options would be \(25\) itself, or a multiple like \(50\) or \(75\). But the clue pins the divisor under \(30\), and the only one of those below \(30\) is \(25\). That nails the divisor to \(25\) and the dividend on top to \(21\). One last check explains why the screen could show a clean ending decimal at all. The lowest-terms denominator is \(25 = 5 \times 5\), built from nothing but \(5\)s, so the decimal terminates instead of running forever. Everything lines up. The number that was sitting on top is \(\boxed{21}\).

Review problems

Review 1. A sprint timer showed \(0.3\) of a second. Write \(0.3\) as a fraction over a power of ten.
Show solution
Read the decimal by its last place. The \(3\) sits one spot to the right of the point, which is the tenths place, so \(0.3\) means three tenths. Writing that over a power of ten gives \(0.3 = \frac{3}{10}\). Now ask whether it can be reduced. The only factors of \(3\) are \(1\) and \(3\), and \(10 = 2 \times 5\) shares neither, so \(\gcd(3,10) = 1\) and the fraction is already in lowest terms. The answer is \(\boxed{3/10}\).
Review 2. A bottle is labeled \(0.6\) full. Write \(0.6\) as a fraction in simplest form.
Show solution
Start by reading the decimal place by place. The single digit after the point is in the tenths place, so \(0.6\) means six tenths, which is \(\frac{6}{10}\). That is already a fraction, but the machine wants simplest form, so check whether the top and bottom share a factor. Both 6 and 10 are even, and in fact \(\gcd(6,10)=2\). Divide top and bottom by 2: $$\frac{6}{10}=\frac{6\div 2}{10\div 2}=\frac{3}{5}.$$ Now 3 and 5 share no common factor larger than 1, so this is as simple as it gets. The bottle is \(\boxed{3/5}\) full.
Review 3. \(0.85\) of students voted to keep the lunch menu. Write \(0.85\) as a fraction in lowest terms.
Show solution
The last digit of \(0.85\) lands in the hundredths place, so the decimal reads as 85 hundredths. That gives the starting fraction \(\frac{85}{100}\). Next, simplify. Look for the largest number that divides both 85 and 100. Since \(85 = 5 \times 17\) and \(100 = 5 \times 20\), the greatest common divisor is \(5\). Divide the top and bottom by \(5\): $$\frac{85}{100} = \frac{85 \div 5}{100 \div 5} = \frac{17}{20}.$$ Now 17 is prime and does not divide 20, so the fraction cannot shrink any further. The poll result in simplest form is \(\boxed{17/20}\).
Review 4. A drill bit is stamped \(0.375\) of an inch. Start from \(\tfrac{375}{1{,}000}\) and reduce. Write \(0.375\) in simplest form as a fraction.
Show solution
The last digit of \(0.375\) sits in the thousandths place, so the place value tells you the denominator. That gives \(0.375 = \tfrac{375}{1{,}000}\). Now reduce. The greatest common divisor of \(375\) and \(1{,}000\) is \(125\), since \(375 = 125 \times 3\) and \(1{,}000 = 125 \times 8\). Dividing both parts by \(125\) gives \(\tfrac{375 \div 125}{1{,}000 \div 125} = \tfrac{3}{8}\). A quick check confirms it, \(3 \div 8 = 0.375\), right back where you started. So the drawer gets labeled \(\boxed{3/8}\).
Review 5. Convert \(-2.6\) to a single improper fraction in simplest form, keeping the sign. Write your answer as one fraction.
Show solution
The minus sign just rides along, so first handle the size \(2.6\) and reattach the sign at the end. The last digit of \(2.6\) is in the tenths place, which tells you the denominator is \(10\). That gives \(2.6 = \frac{26}{10}\). Now simplify. Both \(26\) and \(10\) share a factor of \(2\), since \(\gcd(26,10)=2\), so \(\frac{26}{10} = \frac{26 \div 2}{10 \div 2} = \frac{13}{5}\). That fraction is already improper and in lowest terms, because \(13\) is prime and does not divide \(5\). Finally, bring the negative sign back to the front. The elevation change as a single improper fraction is \(\boxed{-13/5}\). A quick sanity check, \(\frac{13}{5} = 2.6\), and the sign matches the original reading.
Review 6. A rain gauge reads \(\tfrac{73}{100}\) of a liter. The denominator is already a power of ten. Write \(\tfrac{73}{100}\) as a decimal.
Show solution
When the denominator is a power of ten, you do not have to divide at all. You just read the numerator into the right decimal places. The denominator here is \(100\), which counts hundredths, so the value goes out to the second place after the point. Drop the \(73\) into the tenths and hundredths spots, and put a \(0\) in front since there are no whole liters. That gives \(\tfrac{73}{100} = 0.73\). You can sanity check it the other way too, since \(0.73\) read aloud is "seventy-three hundredths", which is exactly \(\tfrac{73}{100}\). So the rain gauge holds \(\boxed{0.73}\) of a liter.
Review 7. A crew has paved \(\tfrac{4}{5}\) of a trail. Double the numerator and denominator to reach a denominator of \(10\). Write \(\tfrac{4}{5}\) as a decimal.
Show solution
The fastest route to a decimal here is to land on a denominator of \(10\), because a number of tenths is exactly what the first decimal place records. Since \(5 \times 2 = 10\), multiply the top and bottom by the same \(2\) so the fraction keeps its value. \(\frac{4}{5} = \frac{4 \times 2}{5 \times 2} = \frac{8}{10}\). Now \(\frac{8}{10}\) means eight tenths, and eight tenths sits in the first place after the decimal point, so it is \(0.8\). You can sanity check it the other way too, since \(0.8 = \frac{8}{10} = \frac{4}{5}\). The conversion terminates after a single digit, which makes sense because the denominator \(5\) divides evenly into a power of ten. \(\boxed{0.8}\)
Review 8. A jar is \(\tfrac{13}{20}\) full. Multiply top and bottom by \(5\) to get a denominator of \(100\), then read the decimal. Write \(\tfrac{13}{20}\) as a decimal.
Show solution
Decimals are secretly fractions whose denominators are powers of ten, so the move is to rename \(\tfrac{13}{20}\) with a denominator that is a power of ten. Since \(20 \times 5 = 100\), multiply the top and bottom by the same \(5\) so the value stays the same. $$\frac{13}{20} = \frac{13 \times 5}{20 \times 5} = \frac{65}{100}$$ A fraction over \(100\) is read as hundredths, and \(\tfrac{65}{100}\) is sixty-five hundredths, which is written \(0.65\). You can sanity check it the long-division way too, since \(13 \div 20 = 0.65\), a terminating decimal because \(20 = 2^2 \times 5\) has only \(2\)s and \(5\)s in its factorization. The jar is \(\boxed{0.65}\) full.
Review 9. A gauge block is \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) inch thick. Since \(8=2^3\), supply \(5^3=125\) to reach \(1{,}000\). Write \(\tfrac{5}{8}\) as a decimal.
Show solution
The trick with the scaling method is to ask what the denominator is missing. Here \(8 = 2^3\), so it carries three \(2\)s and no \(5\)s. A power of ten always pairs its \(2\)s and \(5\)s evenly, and the smallest one that uses three \(2\)s is \(10^3 = 2^3 \times 5^3 = 1{,}000\). So the only thing standing between \(8\) and \(1{,}000\) is three \(5\)s, and \(5^3 = 125\). Multiply the top and bottom by that same \(125\) so the fraction keeps its value. $$\frac{5}{8} = \frac{5 \times 125}{8 \times 125} = \frac{625}{1{,}000}.$$ Six hundred twenty-five thousandths slides straight onto the readout as \(0.625\). You can sanity check it the long way too. Start from \(\tfrac{4}{8} = 0.5\) and add one more eighth, \(\tfrac{1}{8} = 0.125\), and \(0.5 + 0.125 = 0.625\), the same answer. \(\boxed{0.625}\)
Review 10. Three fractions in lowest terms: \(\dfrac{7}{8}\), \(\dfrac{4}{15}\), \(\dfrac{9}{40}\). Factor each denominator. Which one has a prime other than \(2\) or \(5\)? Give that denominator.
Show solution
Factor each denominator and check whether it is built only from \(2\)s and \(5\)s. For \(\dfrac{7}{8}\), the denominator is \(8 = 2^3\), all \(2\)s, so it terminates. In fact \(\dfrac{7}{8} = 0.875\). For \(\dfrac{9}{40}\), the denominator is \(40 = 2^3 \times 5\), only \(2\)s and a \(5\), so it terminates too, giving \(\dfrac{9}{40} = 0.225\). For \(\dfrac{4}{15}\), the denominator is \(15 = 3 \times 5\). That hidden \(3\) is not a \(2\) or a \(5\), and since the fraction is already in lowest terms nothing can cancel it away. The \(3\) blocks the decimal from ever stopping, so \(\dfrac{4}{15}\) is the card that runs forever. Its denominator is \(\boxed{15}\).
Review 11. The full-power brightness factor is \(1.25\). The low-power factor is its reciprocal. Convert \(1.25\) to a fraction, flip it, then write the reciprocal as a decimal.
Show solution
Start by writing \(1.25\) as a fraction. The last digit lands in the hundredths place, so \(1.25 = \frac{125}{100}\). Both numbers share a factor of \(25\), and \(\frac{125}{100} = \frac{5}{4}\) in lowest terms. The reciprocal just swaps top and bottom, so the reciprocal of \(\frac{5}{4}\) is \(\frac{4}{5}\). To read \(\frac{4}{5}\) as a decimal, rescale the bottom to a power of ten. Multiply top and bottom by \(2\) to get \(\frac{4 \times 2}{5 \times 2} = \frac{8}{10}\), and \(\frac{8}{10} = 0.8\). A quick check, \(1.25 \times 0.8 = 1\), which is exactly what a reciprocal pair should do. \(\boxed{0.8}\)
Review 12. A glaze recipe is \(\tfrac{17}{50}\) cobalt. Since \(50 \times 2 = 100\), multiply top and bottom by \(2\). Write \(\tfrac{17}{50}\) as a decimal.
Show solution
The denominator \(50\) factors as \(2 \times 5^2\), only \(2\)s and \(5\)s, so a clean decimal is waiting. To reach a power of ten, climb from \(50\) to \(100\) by multiplying by \(2\). Multiply the top by the same \(2\) so the value does not change, $$\frac{17}{50} = \frac{17 \times 2}{50 \times 2} = \frac{34}{100}.$$ A denominator of \(100\) means hundredths, so the digits \(34\) sit two places past the point. The glaze is \(\boxed{0.34}\) cobalt.
Review 13. A shelf is \(3.25\) feet. Convert to a fraction, reduce to \(\tfrac{13}{4}\), then rename with denominator \(8\). How many eighths of a foot are in \(3.25\)?
Show solution
The shelf length is \(3.25\) feet, and we want to know how many eighths of a foot that is. The trick is that one number can wear two outfits, a decimal outfit and a fraction outfit, and switching to the fraction makes the counting easy. First write the decimal as a fraction. Two digits sit after the decimal point, so the natural denominator is \(100\): $$3.25 = \frac{325}{100}.$$ Now reduce it. Both \(325\) and \(100\) share a factor of \(25\), so divide top and bottom by \(25\): $$\frac{325}{100} = \frac{325 \div 25}{100 \div 25} = \frac{13}{4}.$$ So \(3.25\) is really \(\tfrac{13}{4}\) in its simplest form. We want this same amount written over a denominator of \(8\). Since \(4 \times 2 = 8\), multiply the top and bottom by \(2\): $$\frac{13}{4} = \frac{13 \times 2}{4 \times 2} = \frac{26}{8}.$$ Renaming a fraction this way does not change its value, it only changes the size of the pieces we count with. The shelf is \(\tfrac{26}{8}\) feet long, which means it holds \(26\) eighths of a foot. A quick check confirms it, \(26 \div 8 = 3.25\), right back where we started. So there are \(\boxed{26}\) eighths in \(3.25\).
Review 14. A calculator shows exactly \(0.65\). The divisor was less than \(25\) and the fraction was in lowest terms. Reduce \(\tfrac{65}{100}\) to find the divisor. What whole number was divided?
Show solution
The screen shows a terminating decimal, so it must come from a clean fraction. Read \(0.65\) as sixty-five hundredths, which gives \(\frac{65}{100}\). Now simplify. Both \(65\) and \(100\) share a factor of \(5\), since \(65 = 5 \times 13\) and \(100 = 5 \times 20\). Dividing top and bottom by \(5\) leaves \(\frac{13}{20}\). The numbers \(13\) and \(20\) have no common factor bigger than \(1\), so this fraction is in lowest terms, which matches Maya's second clue. The bottom number of a fraction is what you divide by, and \(20\) is indeed smaller than \(25\), so the first clue checks out too. The top number is what gets divided, so the dividend is \(\boxed{13}\).
