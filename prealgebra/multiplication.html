Prealgebra · Lesson 1.3

Multiplication

Solve this lesson — free →All lessons

Multiplication is the art of counting without counting. Nobody finds the number of seats in a stadium by tapping each one; you spot the structure, multiply, and leap straight to the total. Like addition, multiplication hides two freedoms inside it, and it brings one brand-new power of its own: a bridge back to addition that will do more work for you than any other rule in this course. As before, we earn the rules by using them.

A chocolatier packs a gift box with $5$ rows of $8$ truffles. The box sits sideways in the shop window, so a passer-by sees $8$ rows of $5$. The chocolatier computes \(5 \times 8\); the passer-by computes \(8 \times 5\). How many truffles do they each count?
Show solution
5 rows of 8 is \(8 + 8 + 8 + 8 + 8 = 40\); 8 rows of 5 is also 40. It is one box, and tilting it cannot change the count, so the two products are two names for one number.$$5 \times 8 = 8 \times 5 = \boxed{40}.$$Every product is a grid, and a grid counts the same whichever way you sweep it, so order never matters.
An arena has $2$ seating decks. Each deck is divided into $26$ sections, and each section holds $50$ seats. Multiply the three numbers in whatever order is most painless: how many seats does the arena have?
Show solution
Order and grouping are free, so pick the friendly pairing: the 2 and the 50 make 100.$$(2 \times 50) \times 26 = 100 \times 26 = \boxed{2600}.$$Look for a pair that builds a power of ten before multiplying anything.
Key idea
Products enjoy the same two freedoms as sums: any order, any grouping. The winning move is partner-hunting: 2 × 5 = 10, 4 × 25 = 100, 8 × 125 = 1000. Spot a pair like that inside a product and the rest of the problem dissolves.
Go partner-hunting in \(8 \times 73 \times 125\), entirely in your head.
Show solution
Ignore the awkward 73 for a moment and look at its companions, 8 and 125, which are partners:$$8 \times 125 = 1000,$$so \(73 \times 1000 = \boxed{73000}\). Keep the matchmaking table handy, \(2 \times 5 = 10\), \(4 \times 25 = 100\), \(8 \times 125 = 1000\), and scan for couples before computing.

So far multiplication has only borrowed addition’s tricks. Its own character shows when the two operations meet in the same problem: when a sum must be multiplied, or when two products secretly share a factor.

A trail crew is building $9$ identical fence panels. Each panel needs $20$ long boards and $7$ short ones. Count the boards two different ways: find what one panel uses and multiply by 9; then count all the long boards and all the short boards separately and add. Do the two methods agree, and on what total?
Show solution
By panels: one panel uses \(20 + 7 = 27\) boards, so \(9 \times 27 = \boxed{243}\). By type: \(9 \times 20 = 180\) long boards and \(9 \times 7 = 63\) short ones, and \(180 + 63 = 243\). Both count the same lumber, so they must agree.$$9 \times (20 + 7) = (9 \times 20) + (9 \times 7).$$Multiplying a sum is the same as multiplying each part and adding. This is the distributive property, the bridge between \(\times\) and \(+\).
Key idea
The distributive property is the bridge between the two great operations: $a(b+c)=ab+ac$. Read left to right, it splits one hard multiplication into easy pieces. Read right to left, it collapses two products that share a factor into a single one. You will use both directions for the rest of your mathematical life.
The bridge spans subtractions too. Compute $$4 \times 998$$ with nothing written down.
Show solution
998 is 2 short of 1000, so multiply the round part and take back the overpayment.$$4 \times 998 = 4 \times (1000 - 2) = 4000 - 8 = \boxed{3992}.$$The distributive bridge across a subtraction tames any factor sitting just below a round number.
Now run the bridge in reverse: compute $$86 \times 14 - 76 \times 14.$$
Show solution
Read it as eighty-six 14s minus seventy-six 14s, which leaves ten 14s.$$86 \times 14 - 76 \times 14 = (86 - 76) \times 14 = 10 \times 14 = \boxed{140}.$$This is the distributive property read right to left: when two products share a factor, subtract the counts and multiply once.
Lesson 1.1 promised you would one day do this in your head: $$(2025 \times 2030) - (2020 \times 2025).$$ Today is that day.
Show solution
A 2025 sits inside each product, so read it as 2030 copies of 2025 minus 2020 copies, and the subtraction leaves ten copies.$$2025 \times (2030 - 2020) = 2025 \times 10 = \boxed{20250}.$$When look-alike products are subtracted, factor out what they share instead of multiplying.

One door is still closed. The last lesson tamed negative numbers under addition and subtraction. What happens when one walks into a multiplication?

A research submarine descends steadily: its height changes by $-8$ metres every minute. It starts at the surface (height 0) and descends for $5$ minutes. Use repeated addition to find its final height; that is, find $$5 \times (-8).$$
Show solution
Multiplication is repeated addition when the count is positive, so five copies of \(-8\) stack up.$$(-8) + (-8) + (-8) + (-8) + (-8) = \boxed{-40}.$$A positive count of a negative quantity is a stack of debts. A negative count of copies has no such meaning yet, and that takes a different tool.

So one strange case remains: what could (−1) × (−8) possibly mean? “Add −8 to itself −1 times” is gibberish. When the old meaning runs out, mathematicians let patterns decide. Study the ladder below.

The ladder forces \((-1) \times (-8) = 8\); any other answer would shatter the pattern. Extend the ladder one more rung: what must $$(-2) \times (-8)$$ be?
Show solution
Adding \(-8\) to itself \(-2\) times has no meaning, but the ladder keeps its rhythm: each step down, the left factor drops by 1 and the product climbs by 8. From \((-1) \times (-8) = 8\), one more step gives$$(-2) \times (-8) = 8 + 8 = \boxed{16}.$$Keeping the pattern unbroken forces negative times negative to land positive.
Key idea
A minus sign still means “take the opposite”: $(-a)b=-(ab)$, so one negative factor flips a product across zero. A second negative factor flips it back: $(-a)(-b)=ab$. One flip lands negative; two flips land positive.
Settle the score from Lesson 1.1: compute $$(-4) \times (-6),$$ and know why it is not \(-24\).
Show solution
Start from \(4 \times 6 = 24\). One minus sign takes the opposite,$$(-4) \times 6 = -24,$$and the second takes it back.$$(-4) \times (-6) = \boxed{24}.$$Each negative factor is one flip across zero, so a product is negative only when its negative factors come in an odd count.
Key idea
Multiplication is commutative and associative: $ab=ba$ and $(ab)c=a(bc)$. Shuffle and regroup any product, then go partner-hunting: 2 × 5 = 10, 4 × 25 = 100, 8 × 125 = 1000.
Key idea
$a(b+c)=ab+ac$ is the one rule that links × to +. Split an awkward factor across a sum or difference to multiply fast, or factor out a shared factor to collapse a sum of products into one.
Key idea
Each negative factor flips the product across zero, just as each minus sign flipped a number in the last lesson. An odd number of negative factors leaves a product negative; an even number leaves it positive.So (−2) × 7 = −14, while (−2) × (−7) = 14, and (−1) × (−1) × (−1) = −1.

Review problems

Review 1. Compute $$2 \times 87 \times 5.$$
Show solution
Partner the 2 with the 5: \(2 \times 5 = 10\), so the product is \(87 \times 10 = \boxed{870}\).
Review 2. Compute $$4 \times 6 \times 250 \times 5 \times 2.$$
Show solution
Partner-hunt twice: \(4 \times 250 = 1000\) and \(5 \times 2 = 10\). What remains is \(1000 \times 10 \times 6 = \boxed{60000}\).
Review 3. Compute $$5 \times 7 \times 5 \times 2 \times 2.$$
Show solution
Pair each 5 with a 2: \((5 \times 2) \times (5 \times 2) \times 7 = 10 \times 10 \times 7 = \boxed{700}\).
Review 4. Compute $$4 \times 25 \times 4 \times 25 \times 4.$$
Show solution
Two pairs of partners: \((4 \times 25) \times (4 \times 25) = 100 \times 100 = 10000\), with one lonely 4 left over; \(4 \times 10000 = \boxed{40000}\).
Review 5. Compute $$1 \times 1 \times 487 \times 1 \times 1.$$
Show solution
Multiplying by 1 changes nothing: 1 is for \(\times\) what 0 is for \(+\). Strip the 1s away and the product is just \(\boxed{487}\).
Review 6. A licence plate reads $37094$. Multiply its five digits together, the fast way.
Show solution
The third digit is a 0, and a single 0 annihilates a product: \(3 \times 7 \times 0 \times 9 \times 4 = \boxed{0}\). No other digit matters.
Review 7. Compute $$(61 \times 0 \times 88) + 425.$$
Show solution
Resist working out \(61 \times 88\): the 0 annihilates that whole product. The sum is \(0 + 425 = \boxed{425}\). Spotting the zero saves all the work.
Review 8. Compute $$125 + 125 + 125 + 125 + 125 + 125 + 125 + 125.$$
Show solution
Eight copies of 125 is the multiplication \(8 \times 125\), an old friend from partner-hunting: \(\boxed{1000}\). When a sum repeats a single number, switch to \(\times\).
Review 9. Compute $$7 \times 1004.$$
Show solution
Split across the sum: \(7 \times (1000 + 4) = 7000 + 28 = \boxed{7028}\).
Review 10. Compute $$6 \times 997.$$
Show solution
\(997 = 1000 - 3\), so \(6 \times 997 = 6000 - 18 = \boxed{5982}\).
Review 11. Compute $$13 \times 9 + 9 \times 87$$; mind where each 9 sits.
Show solution
One 9 sits to the right of its \(\times\), the other to the left, but order never matters, so both products share the 9: \(9 \times (13 + 87) = 9 \times 100 = \boxed{900}\).
Review 12. Compute $$23 \times 9 + 23.$$
Show solution
The lonely 23 at the end is really \(23 \times 1\). So the sum is \(23 \times (9 + 1) = 23 \times 10 = \boxed{230}\).
Review 13. Compute $$64 \times 35 - 54 \times 35.$$
Show solution
Sixty-four 35s minus fifty-four 35s leaves ten of them: \((64 - 54) \times 35 = 10 \times 35 = \boxed{350}\).
Review 14. Multiplication distributes over addition, but does it distribute over itself? Test the claim \(3 \times (10 \times 4) = (3 \times 10) \times (3 \times 4)\): work out both sides, then enter the right side minus the left side.
Show solution
Left side: \(3 \times (10 \times 4) = 3 \times 40 = 120\). Right side: \((3 \times 10) \times (3 \times 4) = 30 \times 12 = 360\). They differ by \(\boxed{240}\), so the claim is false: the distributive bridge runs between \(\times\) and \(+\) only. Tripling a product once is not the same as tripling both of its factors.
Review 15. Compute $$(-6) \times 7.$$
Show solution
One negative factor means one flip: \(6 \times 7 = 42\), flipped to \(\boxed{-42}\).
Review 16. Compute $$(-9) \times (-12).$$
Show solution
Two negative factors mean two flips, back to positive: \(9 \times 12 = \boxed{108}\).
Review 17. Compute $$(-1) \times (-3) \times (-10)$$; count the negative factors before you multiply.
Show solution
Three negative factors mean three flips: \(1 \times 3 \times 10 = 30\) lands on \(\boxed{-30}\). An odd count of negative factors leaves a product negative; an even count leaves it positive.
Review 18. Compute $$15 \times 12 + 15 \times 38 + 25 \times 12 + 25 \times 38.$$
Show solution
Collapse in stages: \(15 \times (12 + 38) = 15 \times 50\), and \(25 \times (12 + 38) = 25 \times 50\). Then \(15 \times 50 + 25 \times 50 = (15 + 25) \times 50 = 40 \times 50 = \boxed{2000}\). Four multiplications collapsed into one.
Review 19. For thousands of years, everyone multiplied big numbers the same way you do: digit by digit, carrying as you go. In 1956 the great Russian mathematician Andrey Kolmogorov conjectured that nothing fundamentally faster was possible, and in 1960 he said so in his seminar at Moscow State University. In the audience sat a 23-year-old student named Anatoly Karatsuba. Within about a week, he found a genuinely faster method: humanity had been overlooking a multiplication shortcut for four thousand years. Computers still use his idea today when they multiply enormous numbers, and an even faster method was discovered as recently as 2019. Even the “simplest” operations still hold secrets; maybe the next shortcut will have your name on it. 🚀
